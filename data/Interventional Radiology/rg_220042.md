Multiple diseases of the portal system require effective portal vein
access for endovascular management. While percutaneous trans­
hepatic and transjugular approaches remain the standard meth­
ods of portal vein access, transsplenic access (TSA) has gained
recognition as an effective and safe technique to access the portal
system in patients with contraindications to traditional approach­
es. Recently, the utility of percutaneous TSA has grown, with
described treatments including recanalization of chronic portal
vein occlusion, placement of stents for portal vein stenosis, por­
tal vein embolization of the liver, embolization of gastric varices,
placement of complicated transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic
shunts, and interventions after liver transplant. The authors pro­
vide a review of percutaneous TSA, including indications, a sum­
mary of related portal vein diseases, and the different techniques
used for access and closure. In addition, an imaging­based review
of technical considerations of TSA interventions is presented,
with a review of potential procedural complications. With techni­
cal success rates that mirror or rival the standard methods and
reported low rates of major complications, TSA can be a safe and
effective option in clinical scenarios where traditional approaches
are not feasible.

Introduction
Percutaneous transsplenic access (TSA) was first used in 1951 for
splenoportography but was temporarily abandoned because of an
increased incidence of hemorrhagic complications (1). The first
TSA intervention was described by Tsang et al (2) in 1985 when
the approach was used to correct stenosis in a splenorenal shunt.
Since then, TSA has been selectively used in the endovascular
management of multiple diseases of the portal system, particularly
those related to sequelae of portal hypertension or malignancy.
Specific examples include portal vein (PV) recanalization (PVR)
after chronic obliterative occlusion, placement of stents for PV
stenosis (PVS) after a transplant or because of malignancy, PV em­
bolization (PVE) for hepatic hypertrophy, embolization of bleeding
gastric varices, placement of a transjugular intrahepatic portosys­
temic shunt (TIPS), and after liver transplant (3–11).

Access to the portal system has been traditionally accomplished

by means of a percutaneous transhepatic approach or by crossing
from the hepatic vein to the PV through a transjugular approach, as
in placement of a TIPS (12–14). However, several physiologic and
anatomic conditions may limit the feasibility of these two options in
endovascular interventions, resulting in the need for safe alternative
routes of catheterization such as TSA. The purpose of this article is
to review PV diseases amenable to intervention from a TSA approach
and the indications and contraindications for TSA. This article also

Access
Several physiologic and anatomic conditions may
prevent effective endovascular access through
a transhepatic or transjugular route. Risk of
failure of a transhepatic or transjugular route
may increase if no patent intrahepatic venous or
PV branches exist (related to chronic occlusion
or stenosis) or if the main PV is occluded (8).
Additional scenarios include diffusely infiltrat­
ing malignancy in the trajectory of percutaneous
access (ie, risk of tumor seeding), or perihepatic
fluid (ie, ascites or hematoma) (6,8,15–18). In
addition, in patients undergoing liver transplant,
avoidance of direct injury to the allograft from
a percutaneous approach may warrant consid­
eration of a TSA intervention. Before the pro­
cedure, it is pivotal to be aware of the patient’s
preexisting conditions and anatomy. The basic
anatomy of the hepatic venous and portal systems
is shown in Figure 1 (19).

Preprocedural Assessment
In patients who are being considered for per­
cutaneous TSA catheterization, preprocedural

cross­sectional contrast­enhanced imaging is
recommended to delineate the native anatomy and
patency of the portal system, including the splenic
vein (SV)–superior mesenteric vein confluence, the
PV, and associated branches (3,5,6,8,9,11,20,21).
The presence of pathologic varices or coexisting
neoplastic processes should also be assessed pre­
procedurally. The feasibility of TSA to the portal
system is decided by the operator on the basis of
anatomic assessment, including the presence of
vessel tortuosity, diameter, and patency (21).

Percutaneous TSA
In existing studies (5,6,8–11,16,20,21) per­
formed to assess the use of TSA, several com­
mon technical steps are described. The patient
is placed in the supine position or with the left
side of the body slightly raised, and the spleen
is assessed with gray­scale and color Doppler
US. The use of general anesthesia with para­
lytic medication is recommended; the ability to
suspend respiration allows optimal assessment
of the spleen and TSA. Under real­time US
guidance, a central intraparenchymal perihilar
branch of the SV is identified and accessed per­
cutaneously with a 21–22–gauge Chiba needle
(Cook Medical), from a lateral intercostal or
subcostal approach. Care should be taken to
access the vessel in a trajectory that facilitates
in­line access of the central SV with a wire and
vascular sheath, rather than approaching the
vessel at an angle that is too acute. Authors of at
least two studies (9,21) have described injecting
a small amount of iodinated contrast material
under fluoroscopic visualization to confirm SV
branch access. Parenchymal injection of con­
trast material may show opacification of the SV;
gentle advancement of an 0.018­inch guidewire
can allow confirmation of adequate access.
After successful puncture, the needle is ex­
changed over an 0.018­inch guidewire to accom­
modate a 5F catheter. This is commonly per­
formed with an introducer set (Accustick; Boston
Scientific), with upsizing to a 35–45­cm 5F sheath
(8–10, 20). Ohm et al (11) and Ko et al (21) also
described an alternative initial access with the use
of a 6F coaxial dilator (Neff sheath; Cook Medi­
cal) and a 6F–7F sheath (Radifocus Introducer
II sheath; Terumon). Portal venography can then
be performed as needed, with advancement of
a 5F catheter of the operator’s choice over an
0.035­inch guidewire for subsequent endovascular
intervention. Venography often demonstrates pat­
ent native intrahepatic portal vessels secondary to
developed collateral flow. Examples of this process
are provided in Figure 2. Additional images of the
trajectory of needle access are shown in Figures
3B, 4B, and 5A.

Interventions

PV Recanalization for Chronic PV
Thrombosis
Chronic PV thrombosis (PVT) is a common
complication of portal hypertension in patients
with cirrhosis. Patients may present with com­
mon sequelae of portal hypertension, such as
variceal bleeding and ascites (22,23). In some
medical centers, PVT remains an absolute or
relative contraindication to liver transplant; analy­
sis of the United Network for Organ Sharing
registry has shown PVT to be an independent
risk factor for posttransplant death (24). In pa­
tients undergoing a transplant, characterizing the
extent of the thrombus is essential for procedural
planning. The most widely used classification
system was described by Yerdel et al (25) in 2000.
The Yerdel classification system allows grading of
PVT on the basis of involvement of the PV and
the superior mesenteric vein. In grade 1 PVT, less
than 50% of the PV lumen is involved; in grade 2
PVT, greater than 50% of the lumen is involved;
grade 3 is complete PVT with extension into the

proximal superior mesenteric vein; and Grade 4
is complete PVT with occlusion of both the prox­
imal and distal superior mesenteric vein. Yerdel
et al (25) found that patients with grade 1 PVT
who underwent liver transplant had outcomes as
favorable as those of control subjects, with grades
2–4 having increasingly poorer outcomes and
patients of all grades requiring more technically
difficult surgical procedures (25,26). PVR with
placement of a TIPS can be performed in select
cases of PVT, thereby allowing select patients
to regain candidacy to undergo liver transplant
(3,20). PVR has traditionally been performed
with a transhepatic approach; however, in a
recent case series (9) of 61 patients with cirrhosis
and chronic PVT, a TSA approach used in 20
patients was described as technically easier, with
a success rate of 100%.

ment of TIPS have been provided in several
existing articles (9,20). Both Thornburg et al
(9) and Habib et al (20) describe initial internal
jugular vein access, with confirmation of the
presence of PVT reconfirmed with retrograde

Complex TIPS Placement Procedures and
Stent Dysfunction
The TIPS placement procedure is an effective
form of PV decompression and is typically used
to manage the sequelae of portal hypertension.
The primary indications for placement of a TIPS
that have the strongest evidence include second­
ary prevention of esophageal variceal bleeding
and treatment of refractory ascites (27). Addi­
tional indications include hepatorenal and hepa­
topulmonary syndrome, Budd­Chiari syndrome,
or treatment of acute variceal bleeding.

Although it is typically performed with a transhe­

patic transvenous approach, placement of a TIPS
can also be performed using a TSA, if no suitable

hepatic veins are available. Similarly, in patients
with Budd­Chiari syndrome or TIPS occlusion,
recanalization after stent dysfunction with a TSA
is also possible, although to our knowledge, no
large­scale retrospective or prospective trials have
been performed to assess this technique (7,27).
TSA is also helpful in patients who are unable to
tolerate the liver trauma of several passes with sharp
transhepatic canalization, particularly in those with
cirrhosis. In a technique similar to that of PVR, po­
sitioning of a snare into the portal system provides a
fluoroscopic target when the TIPS needle is passed
through the liver parenchyma during placement.
Slow incremental advancement of the needle under
multiple fluoroscopic obliquities allows for snaring

PV Embolization
After a regional injury occurs, the liver can regen­
erate through hyperplasia at a rate proportional
to the degree of injury (37). Regeneration can
be seen after a toxic insult, vascular occlusion,

partial hepatectomy, or direct trauma, with up to
95% of noninjured cells undergoing mitosis after
an insult involving greater than 10% of the liver
(38). In patients with a primary liver neoplasm
or secondary metastatic disease, surgical resec­
tion, embolization, ablation, or a liver transplant
currently are the mainstays of temporizing or
curative management. Although the potential
for liver regeneration allows partial hepatectomy
in select patients, large­volume resections place
patients at risk for postoperative liver insuffi­
ciency (20,37,39). Before partial hepatectomy, it
is essential to estimate the remaining volume of
functioning liver to be retained; this residual tis­
sue is termed the future liver remnant and can be
calculated at cross­sectional imaging.

In patients whose future liver remnant is small

enough to risk liver insufficiency, preoperative
PVE of the diseased liver lobe or segments can
improve future liver remnant volume by inducing
compensatory hyperplasia of the unaffected lobe
(39,40). In patients with normal liver function, a
future liver remnant of greater than 20% has been
associated with reduced postoperative complica­
tions, with a future liver remnant of less than 20%
being considered a standard indication for PVE
(37). Although patients with liver cirrhosis are

PVE has traditionally been performed through

a transhepatic approach, with transileocolic or
transjugular techniques also described. For pa­
tients in whom an ipsilateral transhepatic approach

both safe and feasible (21). In 2016 Ko et al (21)

high­positioned liver. TSA PVE was successful in

Embolization of Varices
Chronic liver disease and portal system abnor­
malities can lead to gastrointestinal varices, which
are dilated submucosal veins that have a potential
to cause life­threatening bleeding (42). These
varices develop secondary to portal hypertension
to decompress the high­pressure portal system.
Approximately 50% of patients with a new diag­
nosis of cirrhosis have gastrointestinal varices (43).
The annual risk of variceal bleeding is approxi­
mately 5%–15%, and the 6­week mortality rate
among patients with variceal bleeding is approxi­
mately 20%. Patient outcome depends greatly on
stabilizing the patient and achieving hemostasis.
Treatment options to help achieve hemostasis
include lowering the portal system pressure, either
pharmacologically with vasoactive medications or
through placement of a TIPS, endoscopic tampon­
ade, endoscopic ligation, sclerotherapy, emboliza­
tion, or surgery (44). In patients with elevated
PV pressure secondary to PV occlusion, throm­
bectomy or placement of a stent can help resolve
bleeding (16).

Pediatric Interventions
TSA of the PV in the pediatric population has been
demonstrated for many of the same indications for
which it is used in the adult population (46,47).
One additional benefit in the pediatric population
is the added value of splenoportography when the
evaluation of the portomesenteric vasculature with
noninvasive imaging is limited by patient size (48).
The safety of TSA PV interventions in pediatric
patients has varied between case series. In 2017,
Pimpalwar et al (47) published their experience
with 44 TSA interventions and reported intraperito­
neal hemorrhage and the need for blood transfusion
at rates higher than those of similar adult series:
up to 27%. In a retrospective review published the
following year, Monroe et al (48) reported their
experience with 26 pediatric patients undergoing
PV interventions with TSA and described intra­
abdominal bleeding rates more in line with those in
the adult series, with 7.7% of patients demonstrat­
ing intraperitoneal hemorrhage and only 3.8% re­
quiring transfusion. Both articles (47,48) reported
perfect technical success rates.

Complications
Reported complication rates and adverse events
related to TSA in adults from prospective and
retrospective studies are provided in Table 2.
Commonly reported complications of TSA have
largely been related to bleeding events, with an
overall low rate of clinically significant hemor­
rhages requiring transfusion. Although most
included studies (8,9,11,49) reported transfusion
requirements in 0%–10% of patients, Gong et al
(5) reported a higher rate of transfusion (28%) in
patients undergoing TSA embolization of esopha­
geal and gastric varices (5,6,8,9,11,20,21,49). In
addition, Zhu et al (8) reported major bleeding
complications in three of 46 patients after TSA
procedures, including intraperitoneal hemorrhage
and splenic subcapsular hemorrhage. Similarly,
hemoperitoneum and intrasplenic hematoma
have also been reported, which may indicate
the need for further technical experience and
improvements to prevent procedural bleeding
complications. Contrast­enhanced CT is recom­
mended to assess for vascular or hemorrhagic
complications in patients with concerning clinical
signs or symptoms. High­attenuation free fluid
may be seen throughout the abdomen in patients
with hemoperitoneum from TSA. In those with
perisplenic hematoma related to TSA or peri­
hepatic hematoma during TIPS needle access,
organized fluid collections of varying attenua­
tion may be identified and are related to evolving
blood products. An example of hemoperitoneum

after TSA is provided in Figure 10, and small
perisplenic or perihepatic hemorrhage after TSA
placement of a TIPS is shown in Figure 7F.

To our knowledge, no current studies have
been performed to asses the optimal closure tech­
niques for TSA or the relationship between bleed­
ing complications and the type of embolic used.
The included studies (4–6,8–11,20,21,49,50)
used primarily coils, glue, and gelfoam; how­
ever, we prefer to use Amplatzer vascular plugs,
which have also been described in a single case
by Dollinger et al (50). Furthermore, wide vari­
ability is reported in the types of access devices,
needle gauges, and sheath sizes used for TSA and
PV intervention. To our knowledge, it remains
unclear if bleeding complications are related to
these variables. Pain after TSA is common and
can be managed with conservative pain control
(11). Additional described complications of TSA
include pneumothorax and pleural effusion,
which are thought to be related to high percuta­
neous access (5,8,49). Other uncommon major
adverse events such as SV dissection and acute
SV rupture have also been observed (11,20,53).
Although multiple factors may be responsible for
such complications, the improvement of maneu­
vering techniques or the avoidance of aggressive
sheath access may be crucial in preventing these
events. Nontarget embolization of tract emboliza­
tion materials has also been described; specifi­
cally, NBCA glue material migration in the SV. A
summary of important technical tips to prevent
pitfalls and complications related to TSA is pro­
vided in Table 3.

Conclusion
TSA is increasingly being used for PV interven­
tions, for recanalization in chronic PV occlusion,
stent placement in PV stenosis, preoperative
PVE, complex TIPS placement procedures, and
variceal embolization. For clinical scenarios in
which a traditional transhepatic approach is not
feasible in patients who require PV intervention,
TSA has been shown to be a generally safe tech­
nique with high technical success rates. Bleeding
complications remain the primary adverse event
in TSA interventions; further research is needed
to clarify if an ideal method of access and closure
exists that may minimize postprocedural hemor­
rhage rates.
