Biliary Leaks: Multidisciplinary Approach
to Diagnosis and Treatment

Philip J. Schertz, DO • Christopher A. Mao, MD • Kade D. Derrick, MD • Federico Galar, BS • Carlos B. Ortiz, MD • John A. Walker, MD • Jorge E. Lopera, MD

Author affiliations, funding, and conflicts of interest are listed at the end of this article.

Bile leaks arise from various causes such as trauma, complications after hepatobiliary surgery, and intrahepatic malignancies
or their associated liver-directed treatments. Bile leaks can result in significant morbidity and mortality. Delayed diagnosis is
not uncommon due to nonspecific manifestations; therefore, a high clinical suspicion is needed. A multidisciplinary approach
for treatment of biliary leaks with prompt referral to tertiary care centers with experienced hepatobiliary surgeons, advanced
endoscopists, and interventional radiologists is needed to address these challenging complications. Management of biliary leaks
can range from conservative management to open surgical repair. Minimally invasive procedures play a crucial role in biliary
leak treatment, and the interventional radiologist can help guide appropriate management on the basis of a clear understanding
of the pathophysiology of biliary leaks and a current knowledge of the armamentarium of treatment options. In most cases, a
simple diversion of bile to decompress the biliary system may prove effective. However, persistent and high-output biliary leaks
require delineation of the source with tailored treatment options to control the leak. This may be done by additional diversions,
occluding the source, reestablishing connections, or using a combination of therapies to bridge to more definitive surgical inter-
ventions. The authors describe the different treatment options and emphasize the role of interventional radiology.

Introduction
Biliary  leaks  are  devastating  iatrogenic  complications  that
can  occur  after  hepatobiliary  surgery  and  liver-directed  in-
terventions, resulting in significant morbidity and mortality
rates,  an  impaired  quality  of  life,  and  financial  burdens  for
affected patients (1). These complications are caused by dam-
age to the bile ducts, unsuccessful bile duct ligation, or failure
to recognize anatomic variants during surgical interventions.
In addition to iatrogenic causes, biliary leaks can be second-
ary to traumatic or neoplastic processes. Early recognition of
a biliary leak is essential to assist in treatment and manage-
ment because biliary leaks can be devastating. Unfortunate-
ly, many biliary leaks are not identified at the patient’s initial
presentation, with many patients having symptoms of vague
abdominal pain and fever similar to those of other postoper-
ative sources of pain.

The  leading  cause  of  biliary  leaks  across  the  world  is
hepatobiliary  surgery,  particularly  surgical  removal  of  the
gallbladder.  Postoperative  leaks  have  a  prevalence  of  1%  for
laparoscopic cholecystectomies and 0.5% for open cholecys-
tectomies  (1–4).  There  was  an  increase  in  biliary  complica-
tions  after  the  introduction  of  laparoscopic  cholecystecto-
mies, likely secondary to the learning curve associated with
laparoscopic  surgery,  but  this  has  decreased  in  recent  de-
cades (3,5). Other hepatobiliary surgeries including hepatec-
tomy,  living  donor  liver  donation,  and  liver  transplantation
can be complicated by biliary leaks. Bile leaks can also result
from  catheter  retraction  or  dislodgement  after  percutane-
ous trans hepatic biliary drainage (PTBD). In addition, biliary
leaks are uncommon complications of liver biopsies and liv-
er-directed  therapies  such  as  transarterial  chemoemboliza-
tion (TACE) and thermal ablation (6). These complications re-
main an important differential consideration for any invasive

interventional radiation therapy, and with modern interven-
tional techniques and awareness of these complications, it is
predicted that the rate of biliary complications will decrease.
Most  biliary  leaks  manifest  clinically  as  bilomas,  with
symptoms that range from asymptomatic and nonspecific ab-
dominal pain to sepsis due to a superimposed infection (1,2).
Bilomas  can  manifest  as  well-circumscribed  collections  ex-
ternal to the biliary tree (Fig 1). In the abdomen, biliary leaks
can result in intra- or extrahepatic bilomas (3). Free spillage of
bile  into  the  peritoneum  can  result  in  bilious  ascites  with  or
without peritonitis. Chest involvement can manifest as bilious
effusions (bilothorax) or even bronchial fistula and can lead to
more  serious  respiratory  complications  such  as  pneumonia,
empyema, and adult respiratory distress syndrome (7) (Fig 2).
Biliary leaks can also result in biliary fistulas between the bili-
ary tree and the skin or other luminal cavities (8).

There is no standardized algorithmic approach to the di-
agnosis  and  treatment  of  biliary  leaks,  although  many  have
been suggested (9). After referral to the interventional radiol-
ogist for evaluation and management, there are many thera-
peutic  approaches  to  treat  biliary  leaks,  including  a  general
stepwise approach and the ability to increase invasiveness as
clinically appropriate.

Etiology, Prevalence, and Pathophysiology
Cholecystectomies,  particularly  with  the  laparoscopic  ap-
proach, are the most common cause of biliary injuries. Con-
certed efforts to prevent postcholecystectomy bile leaks have
led  to  a  prevalence  of  approximately  0.15%–0.46%  (4,9–14).
Given this estimate, cholecystectomies can contribute to sev-
eral  thousand  cases  of  biliary  leaks  per  year  in  the  United
States  (12,13).  Patients  diagnosed  with  a  bile  leak  complica-
tion have a twofold increase in the 1-year mortality ratio (13).
The most commonly reported reasons for biliary leaks after
laparoscopic  cholecystectomy  are  slippage  of  the  cystic  duct
ligature,  gallbladder  bed  leakage  from  hepatic  parenchymal
injury, and anomalous biliary duct anatomy, such as an un-
recognized duct of Luschka or other aberrant ducts (1,4,9).

Hepatic resections contribute to fewer annual cases of bile
leaks.  However,  bile  leaks  are  the  most  reported  postproce-
dural complication from this surgery, with rates ranging from
3.6%  to  20%  (6,8,9,14–16).  Predictors  of  high-risk  resections
for  bile  leaks  have  been  identified  and  include  peripheral
cholangiocarcinoma,  extended  left  hepatectomies,  resection
that involves the main portal fissure, and central resections
(16). Hydatid cyst resections appear to pose the highest risk of
surgical intervention for biliary leaks and have a poor postop-
erative prognosis (17).

Nonsurgical causes of biliary leaks include trauma, under-
lying liver disease, and associated liver-directed therapies. In
the setting of blunt liver trauma, the incidence of biliary leaks
is less than 5% (6,18–20). Perihilar liver injuries may be asso-
ciated with high-output biliary leaks that may require addi-
tional  interventions,  while  more  peripheral  injuries  tend  to
be self-limited with conservative management (20).

rected  interventions  such  as  percutaneous  biopsy,  TACE,
and thermal ablation (21,22). In one study, a higher incidence

Biliary tract damage after liver ablation has been reported,
with an incidence as low as 0.1% and as high as 12%, depend-

may result from damage caused by the needle, direct thermal
injury, or damage to the microvasculature of the biliary tract
(28,29). Please see the “Complications” section for further in-

been reported and include a history of prior TACE and lesions
located close (<10 mm) to the main biliary ducts (28,29).

Infected  bilomas  and  biliary  leaks  after  liver-directed
therapies are particularly common in patients with a history
of previous bilioenteric anastomosis and biliary stent place-
ment and can result in severe complications including sepsis,
liver failure, or death (6,30).

leakage  site  when  not  readily  apparent  at  imaging.  Collec-

location, while collections adjacent to the gallbladder bed af-
ter biliary surgery are suggestive of an extrahepatic leak. Lo-
calization of the biliary leaks, specifically whether the leak is
intra- or extrahepatic, is a key component of treatment plan-
ning.

Diagnostic Imaging
Diagnostic imaging may be indicated after clinical suspicion
for a bile leak is proposed, and the radiologist should use the
patient’s clinical history to integrate this differential consid-
eration.  US  is  often  ordered  during  the  postsurgical  period,
particularly after hepatectomy or liver transplantation, to al-
low assessment of the liver, vasculature, and perihepatic fluid
collections.  Bilomas  may  manifest  as  anechoic  and  well-cir-
cumscribed  collections.  Complex  collections  with  internal
septa can be suggestive of a superimposed infection (31).

CT is frequently performed when there is a higher clinical
suspicion for complications or if the patient is more acutely
ill. CT findings can be nonspecific, often demonstrating only
perihepatic collections (3,6,9,10,31) (Fig 1). When fluid collec-
tions are present, the thickness of the rim may suggest chro-
nicity,  with  thicker  rims  suggesting  a  more  chronic  process
(3,31).  The  attenuation  of  fluid  on  CT  images  can  be  helpful
because bilomas are commonly less than 20 HU (3,31). Over-
all, these findings are nonspecific, and the differential diag-
noses include hematoma, seroma, abscess, cyst, and lympho-
cele  (1,3,10,31).  An  advantage  of  CT,  in  addition  to  its  rapid
availability, is the ability to depict the anatomy and other as-
sociated findings such as peritoneal thickening or enhance-
ment suggestive of biliary peritonitis (3,9,10,31).

Cholescintigraphy  with  technetium  99m  (99mTc)  hepato-
biliary  iminodiacetic  acid  (HIDA)  provides  physiologic  and
functional information. This procedure involves use of  99mTc
HIDA as a radiotracer, which is excreted into the biliary tree
by  hepatocytes.  This  modality  may  also  be  used  to  help  as-
sess the presence of radiotracer in abdominal drains, which
suggests  bile  leak  (3)  (Fig  3).  Unfortunately,  cholescintigra-

Elevated distal biliary pressure may also be caused by ste-

across the common bile duct (CBD) if the duct is accidentally
mistaken for the cystic duct during surgery. Hence, a diversion
through a low-pressure system, such as a stent, facilitates clo-
sure and healing of the biliary injury (10,18,19). Biliary stents
that cross the sphincter of Oddi keep the sphincter open, de-
compressing the entire biliary tree. A similar decompression
can  also  be  achieved  by  placement  of  an  internal-external
percutaneous biliary drainage catheter.

Diagnosis
In approximately 25%–32% of cases, an iatrogenic bile duct in-
jury is recognized intraoperatively (1,6,10), and these biliary
injuries  may  be  repaired  by  the  surgeons  during  the  initial
operation if they have sufficient hepatobiliary surgical expe-
rience, or the bile leak can be drained by placement of a right
upper quadrant surgical drain with additional prompt trans-
fer of the patient to a tertiary care center (10).

Depending  on  the  location  of  the  biliary  leak  and  the
presence  of  suspected  superimposed  infection,  patients
with  biliary  leaks  can  present  clinically  with  acute  symp-
toms such as sepsis, peritonitis, pain, fever, or high output
of bilious fluid through or around indwelling surgical drains
or surgical wounds (3,6,9,10). If the bile leak is slow, there is
often time for fibrosis and encapsulation to occur, followed
by formation of a biloma. If the leak is rapid, biliary perito-
nitis is more likely to occur (3). Jaundice may or may not be
present (10,18).

Diagnostic and Therapeutic Options

Endoscopic  Retrograde  Cholangiopancreatography.—ERCP
is  a  commonly  used  diagnostic  and  therapeutic  modality  in
the  treatment  of  biliary  leaks  by  the  gastroenterologist  in
the  endoscopy  suite.  A  specially  designed  endoscope  with  a
side  camera  and  side  port  is  guided  to  the  duodenum,  and
the sphincter of Oddi is targeted and cannulated. A contrast
agent  is  injected  through  the  sphincter  to  evaluate  the  CBD
and  biliary  tree  to  identify  a  biliary  leak  if  present.  Limited
visualization of a peripheral injury is a limitation of ERCP, and
Strasberg types C and E5 leaks may not be detected with ERCP
(6). The Bismuth-Strasberg classification is described in the
next  section.  A  biliary  stent  can  be  placed  if  needed  during
the same procedure. Of note, some biliary leaks proximal to
the reach of ERCP can still benefit from CBD stent placement,
as the stent allows a low-flow system to divert bile away from
the proximal leak (6,19).

Percutaneous  Transhepatic  Cholangiography.—PTC  is  fre-
quently performed in patients when ERCP is not feasible due
to altered surgical anatomy or in cases where antegrade access
is desired to stop a leak (10). PTC is performed by using a 21-
or 22-gauge Chiba needle to gain access to the biliary system
and injecting a dilute contrast agent to opacify and visualize
the biliary anatomy in real time (3). Often this can be used to
determine the extent and location of the biliary injury. Based
on this information, treatment can be planned, and various
approaches  are  discussed  below.  Since  the  biliary  system  is
often nondilated in patients with biliary leaks, initial PTC can
be technically challenging (2,10).

Classification of Biliary Injuries
The Bismuth-Strasberg classification is commonly used to iden-
tify the location of biliary duct injuries and leaks (1,4,10,32,33)
(Fig  5).  The  Bismuth  classification  was  developed  during  the
era of open surgery, with the injury localized in relation to the
bile duct stump. The Strasberg system was developed after the
introduction of laparoscopic surgery, forming the “E” compo-
nent of injuries (1,4,6,33). The now-combined Bismuth-Stras-
berg classification is a widely used and helpful tool to classify
the type of bile injury and guide treatment (4,6,33).

that drain the liver bed. Type B is occlusion of the biliary tree,
commonly an aberrant right hepatic duct. Type C is transec-
tion without ligation of aberrant right hepatic ducts. Type D

Treatment
Treatment of biliary leaks is unique to every patient situation.
While there is no definitive consensus on the best treatment, the
authors propose a treatment algorithm based on incorporation
of modern minimally invasive techniques and their experience
in treating biliary leaks at a tertiary liver transplant center for
more than 2 decades (Fig 6). Prior to interventional treatment,
medical  optimization  of  the  patient’s  condition  with  fluid  re-
suscitation and aggressive treatment of infections and/or sepsis
with broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy should be performed.

Diversion

Percutaneous  Biloma  Drainage.—Often,  the  first-line  treat-
ment  performed  by  the  interventional  radiologist  focuses  on
diversion of the bile away from the leak. This is usually done
as a temporizing measure. However, this treatment can be ef-
fective in stopping the leak, secondary to reducing the biliary
pressure.

If a collection is of unknown origin, then the aspiration of
fluid  can  help  provide  a  diagnosis  (31).  Biloma  aspiration  is
commonly performed using real-time US, fluoroscopy, or CT
guidance  while  ensuring  that  no  major  adjacent  structures
are injured (1,31). Depending on the size of the collection and
the clinical status of the patient, a drain may be left in place

after  the  aspiration.  A  persistent  and  high  output  of  bilious
material after percutaneous drainage of a biloma or any col-
lection is indicative of a biliary leak and/or fistula.

Percutaneous drains can be placed using CT, fluoroscopy,
or US guidance depending on the location and complexity of
the biliary leak or biloma (1). US guidance offers the operator
more flexibility, real-time imaging, and Doppler US to avoid
vascular structures. CT guidance provides the operator with
improved  details  of  intra-abdominal  anatomy  but  has  the
drawback of radiation exposure to the patient (1,3,31). Drains
may stay in place until the patient’s clinical picture improves,
drainage  output  decreases  significantly  (<10  mL  over  24
hours),  or  follow-up  imaging  demonstrates  a  resolution  or
significant reduction in the size of the biloma. A transhepatic
drain may be required to stay in place for up to 4–6 weeks to
allow the tract to mature.

Endoscopic  Retrograde  Cholangiopancreatography.—As
previously  mentioned,  ERCP  is  diagnostic  and  therapeutic.
Once drainage of a biloma has been performed, or if clinical
suspicion of a biliary leak is high, ERCP can be performed ini-
tially to identify and treat. The success rate is approximately
90% when combined with sphincterotomy (4,9,34,35). Even if
a bile leak is not initially seen during the procedure, sphinc-
terotomy and stent placement are often performed with the
aim of decompressing the biliary system.

Percutaneous Transhepatic Biliary Drainage.—Biliary leaks not
amenable to ERCP enter a new realm of options with individual-
ized care on a case-by-case basis. Percutaneous transhepatic bil-
iary drains or internal-external biliary drains can help to divert
the bile and decompress the biliary tree.

As  the  biliary  system  is  often  nondilated,  multiple  punc-
tures  with  a  21-or  22-gauge  Chiba  needle  under  fluoroscopic
guidance  are  usually  required.  Then  gentle  injection  of  con-
trast agent is performed while slowly removing the needle until
a bile duct is opacified. US may also be used to target triads with
a similar needle withdrawal technique after placement. At this
point, a diagnostic cholangiogram can be obtained followed by
placement of a biliary drain. The use of general anesthesia is
recommended  when  available.  Percutaneous  puncture  of  the
gallbladder can help facilitate opacification of the biliary sys-

Sealing Biliary Leaks
After a period that varies from weeks to months, a bile leak is
expected to resolve through the natural healing processes of

the body. If a biliary leak persists despite diversion and con-
servative management, then additional methods of emboliza-
tion can be considered for definitive management. These are
dependent on the patients’ anatomy, site of the leak, alterna-
tive surgical and endoscopic options, and patient preference.

Biliary Embolization.—Biliary embolization is often performed
in two scenarios. The first scenario is to occlude the injured iso-
lated duct in the setting of a fistula, and the second is to occlude
an  entire  biliary  segment  for  induction  of  hepatic  segmental
atrophy  (8).  There  are  many  embolization  materials  such  as
absolute alcohol, acetic acid, and cyanoacrylate (8). N-butyl cy-
anoacrylate (NBCA), which is used at the authors’ institution, is
a low-viscosity tissue glue monomer that polymerizes and be-
comes solid when in contact with fluids at a neutral pH (2). Due
to the viscosity of the glue, a combination of glue and coils is
sometimes preferred, with coils used as scaffolding for the glue
(2). NBCA is commonly mixed with ethiodized oil to improve
radiopacity and control the polymerization rates.

Biliary  Stents.—Covered  self-expanding  metallic  stents
(SEMS) have been used successfully to seal biliary leaks, es-
pecially  after  liver  transplant  and  when  iatrogenic  injuries
occur after laparoscopic cholecystectomy or other biliary sur-
geries( 4). (Figs 11, 13, 17). SEMS can be placed using ERCP or,
less frequently, a PTBD access. Plastic stents are an option in
addition to metallic stents (35,37). The use of covered SEMS
has the dual purpose of sealing the leaks and treating the dis-
ruption or stenosis of the bile ducts.

Surgical Treatment of Biliary Leaks
There are extensive options regarding surgical management
of biliary leaks beyond the scope of this article. After recog-

nition  of  Strasberg  type  B  through  type  E  injuries,  primary
surgical repair of the leak is typically performed, but effective
nonsurgical options are also available (Fig 6). Many of the sur-
gical treatment options consist of suturing the leak, if visible,
or diverting the bile flow by anastomosing a mobilized loop
of  jejunum  (commonly  Roux-en-Y  hepaticojejunostomy)  to
the biliary system (1,31,38). For complex biliary leaks, surgery
may  be  performed  after  conservative  or  less  invasive  thera-
pies have failed. These surgeries have a risk of high morbidity
(38%–47%)  and  mortality  (2%–9%),  with  the  most  common
long-term  complication  of  a  Roux-en-Y  hepaticojejunosto-
my  being  anastomotic  stricture  in  10%–20%  of  patients  at
11–30  months  after  surgery  (1,3,6,10,11,13,31).  As  previously

Complications
PTBD  is  a  very  safe  procedure  when  performed  with  careful
technique  and  imaging  guidance.  Major  complications  have
an estimated incidence of 2% in PTC and 2.5% in PTBD, with
an overall rate of 3%–10% (1,6,39). Major complications include
but are not limited to sepsis, cholangitis, bile leak, and vascular
injury,  resulting  in  hemobilia,  hemoperitoneum,  hematoma,
adjacent organ injury, and pleural complications (0.8%–2.1%)
(1,6,28,39). Tube migration, bleeding, and accidental transgres-
sion of bowel or pleura are rare complications.

Infectious complications such as cholangitis and sepsis are
well known, and broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy is recom-
mended with adequate coverage of gram-negative and anaer-
obic  bacteria  (40).  At  the  authors’  institution,  the  published
Journal  of  Vascular  and  Interventional  Radiology  guidelines  for
prophylaxis are followed by using 1–2 g of ceftriaxone, 4.5 g of
piperacillin and tazobactam, or 1.5–3 g of ampicillin-sulbactam
intraveneously in patients who have undergone liver transplant
or high-risk patients. These guidelines are used prior to any bil-
iary interventions, including treatment of biliary leaks (40).

For the embolization of biliary leaks or the cystic duct, the
most feared complication is migration of glue or coils into the
central biliary ducts. As previously mentioned, glue or coils
can serve as a nidus of infection, or stone formation or could
create strictures of the bile ducts. Migrated embolic material
can be retrieved with snares or baskets or can be pushed into

the small bowel using Fogarty balloons. Late migration of the
coil was reported in one of seven patients after cystic duct em-
bolization in one study (2).

Outcomes
The  success  rates  for  biliary  drainage  of  small  leaks,  cystic
duct leaks, and traumatic leaks exceed 90% without the need
for  subsequent  surgery  (41).  ERCP  techniques  are  highly  ef-
fective  for  most  postcholecystectomy  leaks  from  the  cystic
duct and duct of Luschka but for only 40% of leaks at the other
locations (42).

The are multiple small series and case reports on the suc-
cessful embolization of refractory biliary leaks. To the best of
our knowledge, there are no large series published given the
rarity of complex biliary leaks refractory to initial diversion.
A recent series of seven patients with iatrogenic biliary leaks
who were treated with a polytetrafluoroethylene-covered mi-
croplug reported a technical and clinical success of 100% (36).
Nezami et al (2) reported that coil embolization of the cystic
duct in seven patients resulted in successful exclusion of the
leak in all patients and late migration of the coil into the CBD
after 2.5 years in one patient. In most of our cases, cessation of
biliary leaks was experienced immediately after intervention
or within a few weeks after the intervention.

Conclusion
Biliary  leaks  can  manifest  in  many  ways  and  are  associated
with significant morbidity and mortality, especially if diagno-
sis and intervention are delayed. There are many options to
address biliary leaks, including biloma drainage, PTBD, ERCP,
embolization,  and  placement  of  plastic  or  metallic  stents.  A
multidisciplinary approach is needed to tailor the most effec-
tive and appropriate treatment method for each specific case.
