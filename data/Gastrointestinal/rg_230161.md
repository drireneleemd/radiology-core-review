The Acute Abdomen: A Case-based Survival
Guide to What the Surgeon Wants to Know

Acute  abdominal  pain  is  a  leading  cause  of  emergency  de-
partment visits, accounting for up to 10% of visits annually.
Both benign and emergent abdominal pathologic conditions
can manifest with similar history and physical examination
findings. Timely diagnoses are crucial for making appropri-
ate management decisions, and imaging is routinely used in
evaluating for underlying causes and potential complications
that require surgical intervention. The radiologist, therefore,
serves a vital role in not only making the diagnosis but also
providing key findings to the clinical and surgical teams that
can influence and improve patient outcomes.

A review of every cause and manifestation of the acute sur-
gical abdomen is impractical. However, the principles that in-
fluence surgical management can be broadly defined to five

categories:  obstruction,  ischemia,  perforation,  active  bleed-
ing,  and  aggressive  infections.  By  understanding  how  these
categories apply to common manifestations, radiologists can
construct their reports with pertinent findings that aid in ur-
gent  management  decisions.  For  example,  reporting  distant
free intrabdominal air and peritonitis in acute diverticulitis
may influence the surgeon to proceed with urgent operative
management (Fig 1).

Conversely, reviewing the classifications used by surgeons
can help the radiologist understand which negative findings
to include. For example, reporting the absence of bowel wall
ischemia, pneumatosis, and perforation in a small bowel ob-
struction  may  encourage  conservative  therapy  and  prevent
unnecessary trips to the operating room (Fig 2).

Figure 1.  Acute sigmoid diverticulitis in a 65-year-old woman presenting with 3 weeks of lower
abdominal pain. Coronal (A) and axial (B) CT images show acute sigmoid diverticulitis (white arrows
in A). A 4.5-cm interloop abscess separate from the sigmoid colon (blue arrow in B) is present in the
central mesentery due to perforation. Additional thickening and hyperenhancement of the peri-
toneum in the right lower quadrant (yellow arrows) is present, consistent with distant peritonitis.
This patient required surgical management.

TEACHING POINTS
	 The acute abdomen is a commonly encountered scenario in the emer-

gent setting.

	 Five major categories of findings can influence emergent surgical inter-
vention: obstruction, ischemia, perforation, infections, and bleeding.
	 By  recognizing  specific  imaging  findings,  radiologists  can  tailor  their
reports  to  provide  the  most  relevant  information  and  guide  surgical
decision making.

The slide presentation reviews the most common causes of
the acute surgical abdomen through a case-based format. Each
section reviews potential complications and provides reporting
recommendations targeted toward radiology residents who are
taking call. By the end of the presentation, the viewer should
feel comfortable providing a focused summary of the pertinent
findings that are important to the surgeon.

In  summary,  the  acute  abdomen  is  a  commonly  encoun-
tered  manifestation  in  the  emergency  setting,  and  imaging
plays a vital role in establishing a timely diagnosis and man-
agement.  Knowledge  of  the  general  principles  influencing
surgical  management  can  allow  radiologists  to  add  value  to
their reports and improve patient outcomes.
