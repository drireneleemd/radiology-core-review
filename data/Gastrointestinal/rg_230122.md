including small bowel adenocarcinoma and
lymphoma, are described.

Introduction
Celiac disease, also known as celiac sprue or gluten-sensitive
enteropathy, is a common inflammatory disease of the small
bowel that is characterized by the loss of villi in the proximal
small bowel. It is mediated by an immune response triggered
by the ingestion of gluten in genetically predisposed individ-
uals. Gluten refers to a family of proteins from wheat, barley,
rye,  and  their  cross-bred  grains  and  also  oats  in  rare  cases
(1,2).  This  process  leads  to  gastrointestinal  malabsorption

with  symptoms  of  diarrhea,  constipation,  and  abdominal
pain and distention (3). The diagnosis of celiac disease must
always be confirmed by biopsy. Typical histologic features are
increased intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs), crypt hyperpla-
sia, and villous atrophy.

At CT and MR enterography, important findings such as an
abnormal fold pattern and mesenteric lymphadenopathy are
characteristic findings. Other imaging features include duode-
nitis, small bowel dilatation, jejunoileal fold pattern reversal,

Epidemiologic Risk Factors
Celiac disease has a prevalence of 1%–2% in the general adult
population (4–6). The prevalence of celiac disease in pediatric
patients, according to studies conducted in the United States,
ranges  from  0.2%  to  1%  (7).  Approximately  3  million  people
are  affected  in  Europe  and  at  least  another  3  million  in  the
United States (1). Celiac disease is also relatively common in
Asia, with a prevalence approaching 1.5% (8). The disease can
occur in patients at any age, but it is more frequently found in
adult women in the 3rd or 4th decade of life, with a female-to-
male ratio from 2:1 to 3:1 (9).

The global distribution of the disease parallels the distri-
bution  of  human  leukocyte  antigen  (HLA)  genotypes  that
predispose an individual to celiac disease, provided that the
population  is  also  exposed  to  gluten  (10).  Population-based
studies  suggest  that  recognized  cases  of  celiac  disease  rep-
resent a small part of the total number of patients with this
disease (10). Experts describe the prevalence of this disease
as akin to an iceberg. The area of the iceberg above water re-
fers to clinically diagnosed celiac disease, while the area of the
iceberg below the water represents the total number of undi-
agnosed cases in a given population at a given time (Fig 1) (11).
Among the groups at high risk are first-and second-degree
relatives, with a prevalence of 8.9% for siblings, 7.9% for chil-
dren, and 3% for parents of the celiac patient (10). Celiac disease
is  associated  with  several  autoimmune  diseases,  especially

type 1 diabetes and Hashimoto thyroiditis. It is less frequently
associated with autoimmune hepatitis infection, primary bil-
iary  cholangitis,  systemic  lupus  erythematosus,  Sjögren  syn-
drome,  systemic  sclerosis,  and  inflammatory  bowel  disease.
Some authors (12) have suggested that gluten may also trigger
autoimmune disorders. It is possible to find immunoglobulin
A (IgA) deficiency in patients with genetic alterations and chro-
mosomopathies  such  as  Down  syndrome,  Turner  syndrome,
and Williams syndrome (12).

There  is  also  a  spectrum  of  gluten  sensitivity  entities,
characterized  by  intestinal  symptoms  related  to  the  inges-
tion of gluten in people who are not affected by celiac disease,
called non-celiac gluten sensitivity (NCGS) or wheat allergy (13).
Unlike  celiac  disease,  NCGS  is  not  an  autoimmune  process
that leads to intestinal epithelial cell damage. Celiac disease
is a common but underdiagnosed disease, so a high index of
suspicion is necessary for diagnosis.

Pathogenesis
Celiac  disease  is  an  autoimmune  inflammatory  condition
that is triggered in genetically predisposed individuals by the
ingestion of the gluten proteins from wheat, barley, rye, and
their cross-bred grains and, in rare cases, oats (14). There is
a close association with the HLA-DR3/DQ2 and HLA-DR4/DQ8
gene locus in patients with celiac disease compared with that
in  the  general  population  (15).  Ingested  gluten  is  partially
digested  by  host  (digestive)  and  microbial  (small  intestinal
microbiota) enzymes and then transported through transcel-
lular and paracellular pathways to the lamina propria. Acti-
vated transglutaminase 2 (TG2), an enzyme secreted during
inflammatory  conditions,  deamidates  glutamine  residues
on gluten peptides, which improves the efficiency of peptide
binding  to  the  HLA-DQ2  or  HLA-DQ8  groove  of  antigen-pre-
senting cells. TG2-gluten complexes activate B cells that pro-
duce  autoantibodies,  including  antibodies  against  TG2  that
are used as a sensitive and specific serologic diagnostic tool.

T-helper cells secrete proinflammatory mediators such as
interferon γ, interleukin (IL)-12, and tumor necrosis factor. B
cells  can  also  internalize  TG2-gluten  complexes  and  release
deamidated  gluten  peptides  that  bind  to  HLA-DQ2  or  HLA-
DQ8,  further  activating  gluten-specific  T  cells.  Infectious  or
inflammatory  signals  upregulate  stress  markers  and  histo-
compatibility antigen HLA class I in intestinal epithelial cells.
IELs interact with intestinal epithelial cells through natural
killer cell receptors. Proinflammatory cytokines such as IL-15
drive IEL cytotoxicity, leading to mucosal destruction and in-
testinal atrophy (Fig 2) (12).

of 2011, there are multiple definitions related to celiac disease
that divide celiac disease into classic, nonclassic, subclinical,
refractory,  latent,  and  potential  manifestations.  The  clas-

Associated Conditions
Celiac disease can be associated with immune, idiopathic, and
oncologic pathologic conditions (Table 2). Intestinal dysfunc-
tion associated with undiagnosed celiac disease increases in-
testinal permeability to certain triggers, which may promote
the onset of other autoimmune disorders. Risk increases with
age and the length of gluten exposure (21,22). Associated auto-
immune diseases include type 1 diabetes mellitus, Hashimo-
to  thyroiditis,  Graves  disease,  primary  biliary  cholangitis,
vitiligo,  Addison  disease,  IgA  deficiency,  and  rheumatologic
diseases  (5).  Other  associated  idiopathic  entities  include  di-
lated  cardiomyopathy,  cerebellar  ataxia,  multiple  myoclonic
seizures, multiple sclerosis, and cerebral atrophy (6,21).

Patients  are  screened  for  celiac  disease  when  clinical  sus-
picion  arises.  In  addition,  the  American  College  of  Gastro-

The  diagnosis  of  celiac  disease  is  established  with  the
combination of pathologic small bowel changes in addition
to serologic test results. The first-line serologic test in adults
is  the  IgA  tissue  transglutaminase  antibody  test,  which
carries  a  95%  sensitivity  and  specificity  (24).  Patients  with
an  underlying  IgA  deficiency  may  produce  a  false-negative
serum  result  (21).  In  such  patients,  an  immunoglobulin-G
antideamidated  gliadin  peptide  test  can  be  performed  (4).
Antigliadin antibody tests are no longer recommended be-
cause  of  the  inferior  results  compared  with  those  of  other
tests.  Serologic  screening  is  recommended  by  the  ACG  in
asymptomatic  first-degree  relatives  of  patients  with  celiac
disease (4).

Adults with positive serologic results often have a diagno-
sis of celiac disease that is confirmed using tissue sampling
performed during endoscopic small bowel biopsy. However,
in the pediatric population, the European Society for Pedi-
atric Gastroenterology, Hepatology and Nutrition guidelines
propose allowing a nonbiopsy diagnosis of celiac disease in
patients with a positive transglutaminase antibody greater
than 10 times the upper limit of a normal and positive endo-
mysial antibody test result and a positive HLA-DQ2 or HLA-
DQ8 test result (25).

In adult and pediatric patients, a negative serologic result
does  not  exclude  celiac  disease,  as  this  result  is  seen  with  a
reduction  or  elimination  of  gluten  from  the  diet,  selective
IgA deficiency, use of corticosteroids, or immunomodulating
medication.  If  there  is  high  clinical  suspicion,  biopsy  of  the
small bowel is often performed (26).

Imaging Features of Celiac Disease
Celiac disease has a wide spectrum of intestinal and extraint-
estinal imaging findings that are related to the stage or sever-
ity of the disease. Table 3 summarizes some advantages and
limitations of CT and MR enterography in evaluation of celiac
disease. Although many of the intestinal findings are nonspe-
cific, the presence of several of these findings should lead to a
more careful examination for other intestinal and extraintes-
tinal findings of celiac disease.

Intestinal Wall Thickening.—Intestinal wall and fold thicken-
ing are often the first radiologic sign of celiac disease, preced-
ing abnormalities in the fold pattern, and are likely the result
of  edema  and  inflammation  from  submucosal  lymphocytic
infiltration.  When  intestinal  wall  thickening  is  present  with
other more specific findings of celiac disease, the likelihood
of celiac disease is increased.

Intestinal wall thickening is defined as a thickness of more
than 3 mm and requires optimal luminal distention (Fig 7).

A  poorly  distended  small  bowel  can  lead  to  a  false-positive
result,  as  it  may  mimic  bowel  wall  thickening  or  mucosal
hyperenhancement. In addition to the inflammation caused
by celiac disease itself, the destruction of villi in the jejunum
predisposes  the  more  proximal  small  bowel  to  become  in-
flamed by gastric, biliary, and pancreatic secretions (14). Gen-
erally, the thickening of the intestinal wall is diffuse and is not
associated with a reduction in intestinal caliber (3).

Intestinal Dilatation.—Dilatation of the small bowel is a non-
specific feature of celiac disease (29). It has a predilection for
the jejunum and is thought to occur secondary to intestinal
hypomotility. Small bowel dilatation at CT or MR enterogra-
phy is defined by a luminal diameter greater than 3 cm, with
preserved wall thickness (Fig 8) (3). In some cases, intestinal
dilatation may cause effacement of the folds. In patients with
celiac  disease,  intestinal  dilatation  does  not  often  cause  ef-
facement of the intestinal folds, and jejunization of the ileum
can still be recognized in dilated ileal loops (30).

Intestinal  dilatation  may  also  simulate  intestinal  obstruc-
tion,  paralytic  ileus,  or  other  rare  entities  such  as  intestinal
scleroderma. In these cases, the patient’s clinical history is cru-
cial to help determine the most likely process. In the absence

Strictures.—Isolated  intestinal  strictures  are  an  uncommon
and  nonspecific  sign  in  celiac  disease  (3).  They  manifest  as
segmental luminal narrowing with or without upstream bow-
el dilatation (Figs 9A, 9B, 10) (14). If there is active inflamma-
tion in the stenotic region, segmental hyperenhancement of
the bowel wall occurs. Enhancement can be stratified (bilami-
nar when it enhances only the mucosa and trilaminar when
it enhances the mucosa and serosa, accompanied by edema
of the submucosa) or homogeneous (28). These types of find-
ings could be confused with Crohn disease, but the strictures

in Crohn disease tend to be multiple and segmental and are
uncommon in celiac disease.

Intussusception.—Intussusception  is  due  to  a  dilated  and
atonic  or  hypotonic  small  bowel  with  abnormal  peristalsis,
resulting in invagination of one segment of small bowel into
another (31). It is a complication that may be incidental and
asymptomatic  but  may  also  be  a  cause  of  severe  abdominal
pain. Intussusception or multiple synchronous intussuscep-
tions  without  lead  point  masses  or  obstruction  should  alert
the radiologist to the possibility of undiagnosed celiac disease
in asymptomatic adults.

At CT and MRI, intussusception is characterized by a double
halo with alternating layers of the intestinal wall. The inner-

vascular engorgement (Fig 12). When multiple adjacent mes-

pearance of a comb (1). The finding of mesenteric hyperemia
is not specific to celiac disease and can be seen in a number of
enteric pathologic conditions.

Extraintestinal Findings

Mesenteric  Adenopathy.—T-  and  B-cell  lymphocyte  stimu-
lation  leads  to  lymph  node  follicular  hyperplasia  (1).  At  im-
aging, this manifests as mesenteric lymphadenopathy (short
axis >2 cm) (Fig 13). Mesenteric lymphadenopathy is the most
frequent extraintestinal finding and is reported in up to 42%
of  patients  (3).  As  a  result  of  chronic  lymphocyte  stimula-
tion,  patients  with  celiac  disease  are  more  likely  to  have  an
increased risk of lymphoma (1).

One  unique  finding  in  patients  with  celiac  disease  is  the
presence  of  hypoattenuating  or  cavitating  lymphadenopa-
thy detected at CT enterography. Cavitated lymph nodes are
found in patients with advanced disease, and their incidence
has been reported in up to 12% of patients (34).

Hyposplenism.—Celiac disease is the most common gastroin-
testinal  entity  associated  with  hyposplenism  (35).  In  chron-
ic  or  severe  cases  of  celiac  disease,  a  decreased  spleen  size
or splenic atrophy may be seen at CT and MR enterography.
Hyposplenism may be present in 30%–50% of adults with ce-
liac  disease  and  mainly  affects  patients  with  chronic  gluten
exposure (Fig 14) (36). It is usually irreversible and does not
normalize after cessation of gluten intake. It is caused by an

Complications

Ulcerative Jejunoileitis
Patients with refractory celiac disease may develop chronic
ulcerations  affecting  the  jejunum  and  ileum,  usually  more
severe  in  the  jejunum.  The  colon  and  duodenum  are  rare-
ly  affected.  Clinically,  these  ulcers  can  produce  abdominal
pain  and  distention,  weight  loss,  fever,  and  malabsorptive
diarrhea with protein loss. Imaging features include jejunal
wall  thickening,  mural  stratification,  and  multiple  lumi-
nal-sided depressions representing mucosal ulcers (3). Ede-
ma and mural hyperenhancement due to inflammation may
also be seen (14). Chronic penetrating ulcers and inflamma-
tion can lead to fibrosis and stricture (Figs 15–17) (3). Ulcer-

Malignancy
Among the rarer complications of inadequately treated celiac
disease is the potential of developing small bowel malignan-
cies,  most  commonly  lymphoma  and  adenocarcinoma  (40).
Therefore, clinical and imaging surveillance in symptomatic
patients with celiac disease may lead to early diagnosis.

Although  the  exact  cause  and  pathophysiology  of  celiac
disease–associated malignancy is not clear, several proposed
theories  include  increased  mucosal  lymphocytes,  increased
permeability  to  oncogenic  factors,  malabsorption  of  protec-
tive substances such as vitamins A and E, and an altered im-
mune system (41).

Enteropathy-associated  T-cell  lymphoma  (EATL)  is  the
most  common  malignancy  associated  with  celiac  disease.
Symptoms  are  often  nonspecific,  and  imaging  and  clinical
features  can  overlap  with  those  of  ulcerative  jejunoileitis.
Although EATL is the most common lymphoma to affect pa-
tients  with  celiac  disease,  diagnosis  often  occurs  late  in  the
disease  course,  and  patients  with  EATL  often  have  a  poor
prognosis. Patients may present with recurrent or persistent
anemia, weight loss, abdominal pain, or obstruction, despite
implementation  of  a  gluten-free  diet.  EATL  often  involves  a
single longer segment of bowel and has a predilection for the
ileum. Involvement of the bowel may be focal or diffuse.

Cavitary Mesenteric Lymph Node Syndrome
Cavitary mesenteric lymph node syndrome (CMLNS) is a rare
complication  with  uncertain  causes  and  incidence,  with  at
least 50 cases reported in the literature (44). CMLNS carries
an extremely poor prognosis, with some studies demonstrat-
ing  a  50%  mortality  rate  (45).  Clinically,  patients  with  this
pathologic condition present with malabsorption symptoms,
even  after  a  prolonged  gluten-free  diet,  including  diarrhea,
anemia, weight loss, and fatigue (46). Histologically, the nodes
have a large central cavity with a lipid or hyaline-type mate-
rial.  At  imaging,  CMLNS  manifests  as  numerous  enlarged
mesenteric lymph nodes that vary in size (2–8 cm in diame-
ter) and are centrally low in attenuation, signifying their cen-
trally lipid or hyaline-type composition. Some lymph nodes
may demonstrate a fat-fluid level, which is highly specific for
CMLNS in celiac disease (Fig 21) (46). Findings of hyposplen-
ism are commonly coexistent with CMLNS (46).

Treatment
Currently the only effective treatment available for celiac dis-
ease is a gluten-free diet, as it improves symptoms,  can de-
crease antibodies, and has a partial protective effect against
several  complications  (6).  Once  treatment  is  instituted,  pa-
tients  should  be  evaluated  4–6  weeks  later  by  measuring
levels of folate; vitamins A, D, E, and B12; copper; zinc; caro-
tene; folic acid; ferritin; and iron and by performing a com-
plete  blood  count,  liver  chemistry  test,  serologic  test,  and
prothrombin time test (measured to detect possible vitamin
K deficiency). Patients should then continue to be monitored
for  new  or  residual  symptoms,  assessment  of  compliance
with  the  gluten-free  diet,  and  detection  of  complications  of
celiac disease (49).

New therapies are focused on immunologic components at
the level of gluten-reactive CD4 T cells rather than on inflam-
matory  events.  Teriflunomide,  which  proliferates  gluten-re-
active  CD4  T  cells,  is  of  special  interest.  Other  drugs  under
investigation  are  nanoparticles  loaded  with  a  gluten  extract
that lead to an inhibition of TG2 and decreased mucosal in-
jury (50). The lifelong absence of wheat, rye, and barley has a
significant psychologic and social impact on the patient, and
dietary transgressions and poor adherence to the diet can oc-
cur (12). Therefore, it is important for these patients to have a
multidisciplinary team that includes nutritionists, gastroen-
terologists, and psychologic support that can be of great help
in the acceptance and management of celiac disease (6).

Conclusion
Patients  with  celiac  disease  present  with  nonspecific  symp-
toms  and  a  broad  spectrum  of  imaging  findings.  However,
the radiologist may help in the diagnosis of celiac disease by
carefully examining the small bowel and extraintestinal find-
ings. The most frequent findings are an inversion in the pat-
tern of the small bowel folds and mesenteric lymphadenopa-
thy. Small bowel imaging is also important in patients with a
poor response or symptomatic recurrence after adherence to
a gluten-free diet to detect complications and associated ma-
lignancies. The presence of ulcerative jejunoileitis, malignan-
cy, cavitated mesenteric lymph nodes, or hyposplenism is an
indicator of advanced disease.
