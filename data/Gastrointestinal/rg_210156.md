Focal nodular hyperplasia (FNH) is a benign lesion occurring in a
background of normal liver. FNH is seen most commonly in young
women and can often be accurately diagnosed at imaging, includ-
ing CT, MRI, or contrast-enhanced US. In the normal liver, FNH
frequently must be differentiated from hepatocellular adenoma,
which although benign, is managed differently because of the risks
of hemorrhage and malignant transformation. When lesions that
are histologically identical to FNH occur in a background of ab-
normal liver, they are termed FNH-like lesions. These lesions can
be a source of diagnostic confusion and must be differentiated
from malignancies. Radiologists’ familiarity with the imaging ap-
pearance of FNH-like lesions and knowledge of the conditions that
predispose a patient to their formation are critical to minimizing the
risks of unnecessary intervention for these lesions, which are rarely
symptomatic and carry no risk for malignant transformation. FNH
is thought to form secondary to an underlying vascular disturbance,
a theory supported by the predilection for formation of FNH-like
lesions in patients with a variety of hepatic vascular abnormalities.
These include abnormalities of hepatic outflow such as Budd-
Chiari syndrome, abnormalities of hepatic inflow such as congenital
absence of the portal vein, and hepatic microvascular disturbances,
such as those that occur after exposure to certain chemotherapeutic
agents. Familiarity with the imaging appearances of these varied
conditions and knowledge of their association with formation of
FNH-like lesions allow radiologists to identify with confidence
these benign lesions that require no intervention.

Introduction
Focal nodular hyperplasia (FNH) is a benign lesion of hepatocytic hy-
perplasia that arises in a background of normal or nearly normal liver.
Histologically and radiologically identical lesions in a background
of an abnormal liver are called FNH-like lesions. Although FNH is
most common in young women, FNH-like lesions are seen in myriad
populations with liver abnormalities who share an underlying cause
of hepatic vascular disturbance, including those with abnormalities
of hepatic inflow and outflow and hepatic microvascular disturbances
(Table 1). Familiarity with the imaging features of FNH and FNH-
like lesions and the various predisposing conditions can allow confi-
dent diagnosis at imaging to avoid undue patient anxiety and the risks
of biopsy or surgery.

Histologically, FNH is hyperplastic growth

as a hyperplastic response to abnormal hepatic

management of symptomatic FNH (6).

Typical Imaging Features of FNH

General Imaging Features.—The imaging
features of FNH correspond well to the histo-
logic features and are similar across modalities.
FNH is typically a solitary well-circumscribed,
unencapsulated, lobulated mass that usually
measures smaller than 5 cm (7). Because FNH
is mainly composed of hepatocytes, it appears
similar to the background liver on unenhanced
images. Accurate diagnosis of FNH at routine
US is only possible in one-third of cases, with
subtle, if any, difference in echogenicity when
compared with the surrounding liver (8). Color
and power Doppler US improve visualization
by demonstrating the central feeding artery and
characteristic “spoke-wheel” pattern of branch-
ing vessels in the radiating septa (9,10) (Fig
E1). Similarly, FNH lesions are isoattenuating
to slightly hypoattenuating on nonenhanced CT
images and may be isointense or mildly T1-
hypointense or slightly T2-hyperintense on MR
images, thus earning the term stealth lesion (7).
The large feeding artery and central branch-

ing vessels explain the characteristic homoge-
neous arterial phase hyperenhancement (APHE)
seen at contrast-enhanced US, CT, and MRI
(7). At contrast-enhanced US, the APHE is in
a characteristic centrifugal pattern with spoke-
wheel enhancement or an eccentric feeding
vessel (9) (Fig 1). Contrast-enhanced US has
diagnostic accuracy of 87% for FNH, and
continuous cine imaging is more sensitive than
other modalities for detecting the early arterial
spoke-wheel sign (10,11). In the portal and later
phases, FNH remains slightly hyperenhancing
or becomes isoenhancing to the adjacent liver
with all modalities (7,9) (Figs 2, 3).

MRI-specific Features.—MRI is generally consid-
ered the most sensitive and specific modality for

detection and diagnosis of FNH, particularly with
hepatocyte-specific contrast agents (HSCAs).
With diffusion-weighted MRI, most FNH lesions
are mildly hyperintense in comparison with the
normal liver on both low– and high–b-weighted
images (12). Although FNH lesions generally
have higher apparent diffusion coefficients than
those of malignant lesions and hepatocellular ad-
enomas (HCAs), substantial overlap in apparent
diffusion coefficients exists, and characteristics at
diffusion-weighted MRI are not a reliable dif-
ferentiating feature in isolation (13). At MRI, the
central scar is characteristically T2 hyperintense,
T1 hypointense, and hyperintense on apparent
diffusion coefficient maps (8) (Fig 3).

HSCAs provide additional diagnostic in-
formation and have dual functions, acting as
extracellular contrast agents up to the venous
phase and transitioning to HSCA uptake over
time. Hepatobiliary phase (HBP) imaging is
typically performed at 20 minutes after admin-
istration of gadoxetate disodium (Eovist, Bayer
Healthcare) or 120 minutes after administra-
tion of gadobenate dimeglumine (MultiHance,
Bracco). HSCA uptake and excretion into bile
is facilitated by hepatocyte-specific membrane
transport proteins that are absent in other cell
types (14). FNH, as a hyperplastic growth of
hepatocytes, almost always shows HBP uptake,
whereas lesions of nonhepatocellular origin,
such as hemangiomas or metastases, do not take
up HSCAs and appear hypointense during the
HBP. Other lesions of hepatocellular origin such
as hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) and HCA
are also usually hypointense during the HBP;
however, these lesions may retain or have up-
regulated hepatocyte-specific membrane trans-
port proteins, and therefore can be isointense
or even hyperintense on images acquired during
the HBP, in a minority of cases (15).

The arterial and portal venous phase en-
hancement pattern of FNH with HSCAs and
extracellular contrast agents is identical. How-
ever, the central scar typically remains hypoin-
tense with gadoxetate enhancement as it begins
to transition from extracellular characteristics to
hepatocyte-specific uptake before the time frame
typical for delayed enhancement of the central
scar (16) (Fig 4).

Four patterns of HBP uptake of HSCA have
been described for FNH: homogeneous hyper-
intensity (Fig 4), heterogeneous hyperintensity,
homogeneous isointensity, and hypointensity
with peripheral ring uptake (17,18) (Fig 5).
Most cases of FNH demonstrate HSCA uptake,
with only 2% reported as hypointense during
the HBP (18). It is important to differentiate
typical FNH HBP uptake patterns from atypical
patterns of HBP enhancement that are associ-
ated with malignancies (Table 2). A targetoid
pattern during the HBP refers to central con-
trast enhancement with a peripheral hypointense
rim, which is secondary to HSCA retention
in tumors with fibrous stroma rather than to
cellular uptake. This pattern is associated with
cholangiocarcinoma and metastatic adenocarci-
noma. One must also differentiate the peripheral
ring uptake pattern of FNH (in which the pe-
ripheral uptake is in the FNH) from peritumoral
retention, where the hyperintense uptake is in a
peripheral rim of reactive peritumoral hepato-
cytic hyperplasia surrounding the lesion and not

Atypical Imaging Features of FNH
Most FNH lesions show typical imaging fea-
tures, allowing confident diagnosis. However, it
is important to be familiar with atypical mani-
festations and their relative frequency to direct
patient treatment most accurately (Table E2).
Most (85%) FNH lesions are smaller than 5
cm, and only a small percentage are larger than 10
cm (29) (Fig E2). Although they were originally
described as static, it is now known that 12%–15%
of FNH lesions grow (Fig E3) and approximately
9% regress over time (30,31) (Fig E4). Growth
may cause clinical uncertainty, but is not associ-
ated with patient symptoms or other adverse
outcomes and should not be considered an indica-
tion for surgical resection; however, referral to a
tertiary care center could be considered to confirm
the accuracy of the imaging diagnosis and to facili-
tate multidisciplinary management (30,31).

Intralesional steatosis is commonly seen histo-
logically in FNH but it is rarely seen at imaging
(2). Intralesional fat, which is identified as areas
of loss of signal intensity at chemical shift MRI, is
much more commonly associated with HCAs and
HCCs, so this finding raises diagnostic uncertainty
when it is present in a patient with FNH. Limited

FNH-like Lesions
FNH-like lesion is the term applied to lesions that
are macroscopically, microscopically, and immu-
nohistochemically identical to FNH but occur in
a background of abnormal liver (Fig E5). Myriad
liver conditions predispose patients to formation
of FNH-like lesions, all of which share a vascular
disturbance as the inciting cause (Table 1) (34).
Because they are histologically identical to FNH,
FNH-like lesions carry no risk for malignant de-
generation. Although they may create diagnostic
confusion, they are otherwise typically clinically
silent.

Congenital Absence of the Portal Vein.—Con-
genital absence of the portal vein, or Abernethy
malformation, is a rare malformation resulting in
bypass of splanchnic venous flow from the liver
directly to the inferior vena cava. Congenital
absence of the portal vein is subtyped on the
basis of the congenital extrahepatic portosystemic
shunt that is present, with the most common type
being the complete absence of the portal vein and
drainage of the splenic and superior mesenteric
veins directly into a systemic vein separately or
through a short common trunk (49).

Although congenital absence of the portal vein
is rare, FNH-like lesions are frequently reported
in this population (Fig E6). In a review of 101
cases of congenital absence of the portal vein,
hepatic masses were seen in almost one-half of
patients. Of these, the majority were FNH-like
lesions (36.7%), followed in frequency by HCCs
(26.5%), NRHs (16.3%), HCAs (10.2%), and
others (50). The absence of portal blood flow
with compensatory increased hepatic arterial

Cavernous Transformation.—Cavernous trans-
formation refers to the development of numerous
collateral veins in the hepatic hilum as a response
to restoring flow after portal vein thrombosis.
This substantial alteration in hepatic hemody-
namics leads to increased arterial perfusion and
an accompanying predilection for formation of
FNH-like lesions. In a study of 58 patients with
cavernous transformation, 21% were found to
have at least one FNH-like lesion. In this setting,
imaging features are typical and are like those in
the other predisposing conditions we have de-
scribed, including small size, peripheral location,
and homogeneous APHE (52).

Hereditary Hemorrhagic Telangiectasia.—HHT,
also known as Osler-Weber-Rendu syndrome,
is an autosomal-dominant disorder that is char-
acterized by telangiectasia, or abnormal com-
munication between an arteriole and a dilated
postcapillary venule. Arteriovenous malformations
in patients with HHT increase with age and can
involve the skin, mucous membranes, lungs, liver,

gastrointestinal tract, and central nervous system.
Depending on the distribution of arteriovenous
malformations, affected individuals can have hem-
orrhage or can develop high-output cardiac failure
from hepatic shunting. Diagnosis of HHT requires
three of four findings: (a) recurrent epistaxis since
childhood, (b) cutaneous or mucosal telangiecta-
ses, (c) visceral involvement, and (d) family his-
tory of HHT (53).

The liver is the most commonly involved
abdominopelvic organ in patients with HHT,
and liver involvement in HHT often develops
during adulthood. Although most patients are
asymptomatic, with preserved liver function, up
to 8% become symptomatic as a result of the
arterial steal phenomenon, from arterioportal or
arteriovenous shunts progressing to ischemic
cholangiopathy, increased portal flow from
shunting evolving into portal hypertension, or
portovenous shunts resulting in high-output
cardiac failure (53).

Hepatic imaging findings of HHT include
enlarged hepatic arteries (diameter, ≥6 mm),
intrahepatic arteriovenous or portovenous shunts,
perfusion anomalies, telangiectases, large conflu-
ent vascular masses, and FNH-like lesions (54–57)
(Fig 12). While some literature describes the large
confluent vascular masses of HHT separately from
FNH-like lesions, studies (56,57) with specific
imaging criteria for FNH-like lesions or histo-
logic evaluation support that these masses are
also FNH-like lesions. Patients with HHT often

Hepatic Microvascular Disturbances

Cirrhosis.—Cirrhosis of the liver is the pathologic
result of chronic liver damage with necroinflam-
mation and progressive fibrosis (60). Accurate
imaging evaluation is of the utmost importance
in patients with cirrhosis because of the substan-
tially increased risk of malignancy, most com-
monly HCC. Because HCC is usually diagnosed
on the basis of imaging, without the need for
pathologic confirmation, radiologists must be
well informed regarding its imaging appearance
and those of its mimics, including FNH-like le-
sions, which have been reported in 3%–15% of
patients with cirrhotic livers (19,61).

FNH-like lesions are thought to form because

of the altered vascular dynamics of cirrhosis,
including sinusoidal remodeling, local ischemia
and angiogenesis, and formation of intrahe-
patic shunts (34,60). FNH-like lesions, unlike
dysplastic nodules, do not undergo malignant
transformation and are therefore considered “do
not touch” lesions (34,42,61). Histologically,
they can be challenging to distinguish from
well-differentiated HCCs, with one study (42)
reporting a 6.5% rate of misdiagnosis as HCC
at percutaneous core needle biopsy.

nodules that are readily identified with imaging
in patients with NRH is confusing. Although
one study (67) of 36 patients with NRH dem-

other studies (15,19,68–70) report enhancing
masses that have variably been termed large
regenerative nodules but are very likely FNH-like

is thought to cause NRH also predisposes pa-
tients to formation of FNH-like lesions or that
this coappearance is related to an overlap in the
underlying populations that are predisposed to
both conditions, such as in those with a congen-

arterial hyperenhancement and are more likely

has been suggested (71). MRI with HSCAs
may be helpful to characterize masses found at
screening, because HSCA uptake during the
HBP is a strong predictor of benignity regardless
of the nomenclature used for the benign nodules
discovered in these patients.

Conclusion
FNH and FNH-like lesions are benign lesions that
can often be diagnosed on the basis of imaging.
Knowledge of the imaging features of FNH and of
the numerous conditions that predispose a patient
to the formation of FNH-like lesions and their
characteristic imaging appearance can help radi-
ologists to guide appropriate conservative manage-
ment of these benign, clinically silent liver lesions.
