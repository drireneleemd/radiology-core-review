Stercoral Colitis: Review of Imaging Features
and Complications

Nikitha Karkala, MD • Bertin Mathai, MD • John J. Hines Jr, MD • Sarah Byun, BS • Douglas S. Katz, MD

Author affiliations, funding, and conflicts of interest are listed at the end of this article.

Stercoral colitis is an inflammatory reaction sec-
ondary to fecal impaction and almost always occurs
in the setting of chronic constipation. Luminal
distention caused by dense and dehydrated stool
compresses the vascular supply of the distal colon,
resulting in bowel ischemia and ulceration. Stercoral
colitis primarily affects elderly patients, but it can
be seen in any patient with decreased bowel motil-
ity, with risk factors including neurodegenerative
disorders, chronic medical diseases, malignancy,
immobility, and the use of narcotic or anticho-
linergic medications. Patients most often present
with abdominal pain and tenderness. However, the
presentation is often nonspecific and can include
nongastrointestinal symptoms. Due to the common
presence of comorbidities, a thorough history and
physical examination findings may be difficult to
obtain. Imaging, especially CT, plays a vital role in
the diagnosis of stercoral colitis, demonstrating
hallmark features such as fecal impaction and a
large colorectal stool burden. Mural thickening (>3
mm) and other CT signs of inflammation aid in
diagnosis, although findings including perirectal fat
stranding can be nonspecific. Signs of perforation,
including mural discontinuity, extraluminal air, and
extraluminal stool collections, can also be identified.
Other potential complications include obstruction,
bleeding, fistulas, and urinary tract involvement
secondary to mass effect. The overlap of findings
between stercoral colitis and other colonic diseases,
particularly diverticulitis and malignancy, can some-
times make diagnosis challenging. Identification of
fecal impaction and associated inflammatory changes helps in distinguishing stercoral colitis from other pathologic conditions.
Prompt diagnosis of stercoral colitis and its complications allows appropriate management, which can range from preventive
measures to emergent surgical treatment.

Introduction
Stercoral  colitis  is  an  inflammatory  reaction  secondary  to
fecal  impaction.  Impacted  stool  can  compress  the  vascular
supply of the colon and rectum, causing ischemia and muco-
sal ulceration. It typically involves the rectosigmoid colon, al-
though it can occasionally occur elsewhere in the colon (1–6).
Stercoral  colitis  is  associated  with  chronic  constipation  and

fecal  impaction  particularly  among  the  elderly,  in  patients
who receive opioids and other medications causing constipa-
tion, and in those with comorbidities including neurodegen-
erative disorders and chronic medical diseases.

Pathophysiology
The responsibility of the colon is to absorb water from waste
to produce stool. Consequently, as fecal matter traverses the
colon,  it  progressively  becomes  more  inspissated  and  solid,
collecting in the rectosigmoid region due to its narrower lu-
minal caliber compared with that of the proximal colon (8,9).
The short segment of the colon between the branches of the
inferior mesenteric and superior rectal arteries, known as the
Sudeck point, marks the junction of the rectum and sigmoid
colon. This region is a watershed territory with a relatively re-
duced blood supply and is susceptible to ischemia (10). The

The pathophysiology of stercoral colitis is primarily driven
by  bowel  wall  ischemia  caused  by  luminal  stool  impaction.
Impacted  fecal  material  in  the  colon  causes  compression  of
the colonic vasculature due to increased pressure on the wall
(11). This results in ischemic injury and can lead to necrosis,
ulceration (Fig 3C), and colonic wall perforation in a stepwise
fashion (9,10,12). At pathologic examination, stercoral ulcers
are  described  as  irregular  and  “geographic,”  matching  the
contour of the impacted stool (9). Histologic examination re-
veals ischemic pressure necrosis with thinning of the colonic
wall  and  an  inflammatory  reaction,  with  transmural  necro-
sis seen at perforation sites (8,9). The perforations are round
or ovoid and typically occur at the center of the ulcers, where
wall thinning is most pronounced (9).

Epidemiology
Stercoral  colitis  most  commonly  occurs  in  patients  with
chronic  constipation,  most  often  in  the  elderly,  with  a  re-
ported median age of 60–70 years (1,6,9,12,13). In addition to
age, end-stage renal disease is a risk factor for constipation,
possibly due to associated medications, fluid restriction, and
decreased physical activity (14). Dietary issues  can also be a
contributing  factor,  particularly  low-fiber  diets,  inadequate

hydration, and malnutrition (10,13,15). While stercoral colitis
is relatively uncommon in younger patients, it can result from
constipation associated with hospitalization and pain medi-
cation use, particularly opioids (16). Psychiatric medications
including tricyclic antidepressants and antipsychotic medica-
tions are also linked to stercoral colitis, possibly due to their
anticholinergic properties (9,17,18).

Comorbidities such as diabetes, hypertension, malignan-
cy,  immobility,  neurologic  disorders,  metabolic  abnormal-
ities,  and  recent  surgery  may  increase  the  risk  for  stercoral
colitis through decreased physical mobility and gastrointes-
tinal  motility  (1,4,5,7,9,15).  Although  the  majority  of  stercor-
al  colitis  cases  occur  in  elderly  patients,  cases  in  pediatric
patients must not be overlooked. The rare pediatric cases of
stercoral  colitis  may  be  due  to  chronic  constipation,  autism
spectrum disorder, congenital anorectal malformations, and
mental health disorders (19).

Clinical Presentation
Patients  with  stercoral  colitis  often  present  with  nonspecif-
ic  signs  and  symptoms.  Abdominal  pain  and/or  distention
are the most common chief patient concerns. However, these
symptoms  are  reported  to  be  absent  in  over  60%  of  affected
patients (13). Approximately one-third of patients do not have
abdominal  tenderness  at  physical  examination  (13).  Signs  of
peritonitis may arise if perforation occurs, along with leukocy-
tosis and elevated lactate levels in cases of ischemia (3,5,7,10).
However, a recent study shows that many patients with stercor-
al colitis have a normal white blood cell count and lactate lev-
els, so normal values do not necessarily exclude ischemia (13).

Imaging Findings
Stercoral colitis is characterized by a large conglomerate stool
burden, which is often visible as rectal and/or colonic distention
on plain radiographs. However, this finding is nonspecific, and
radiographs alone are insufficient to confirm the diagnosis. CT
is the primary imaging modality to aid in diagnosing stercoral
colitis and its potential complications. On CT images, stool typ-
ically has a “soap bubble” appearance. In stercoral colitis, in-
spissated stool forms hard masses known as fecalomas. These
fecalomas are more dense compared with normal stool and of-
ten contain calcifications or have a laminated appearance (21).
The impacted stool collects in the affected colon and rectum,
resulting  in  the  characteristic  fecal  loading  observed  in  ster-
coral colitis (Fig S1). Distention of the rectal diameter beyond
6–6.5 cm is generally considered abnormal, but this should be
compared with that of the proximal portions of the colon (5,22).
While fecal impaction and large stool burden are prerequi-
site CT findings, the diagnosis of stercoral colitis can be made
only in the presence of mural abnormalities. Bowel wall thick-
ening (>3 mm) at the site of stool impaction is an indicator of
inflammation (12). The degree of bowel wall thickening may
vary depending on the amount of distention, often appearing
more subtle in cases of substantial colonic or rectal distention.
Other signs of inflammation include perirectal fat stranding
and presacral edema (Figs 2, 4), although these signs may be
nonspecific  because  fluid  and  edema  tend  to  accumulate  in
this dependent space. These findings should be used in con-
junction with the presence of fecal impaction to help establish
the diagnosis of stercoral colitis at CT.

Aside  from  mural  thickening,  there  are  other  CT  ab-
normalities  reported  in  patients  with  stercoral  colitis.  The
“dense  mucosa”  sign,  characterized  by  a  high-attenuation
colonic  wall  due  to  intramural  hemorrhage,  is  best  appre-
ciated on noncontrast images and has been reported to be a
sign of ischemic bowel (12,23) (Fig S2). Another sign is dis-
continuous  mural  enhancement  that  is  manifested  by  low
attenuation in the colonic wall secondary to hypoperfusion
and  may  portend  impending  bowel  perforation  (5,12,24).
Mucosal  sloughing,  visualized  as  a  narrow  irregular  layer
of bowel wall protruding into the colon, is thought to indi-
cate the progression of bowel ischemia to infarction (4,24).
Long-segment  involvement  of  the  colon  (>40  cm)  and  the
presence  of  free  fluid  have  also  been  linked  to  increased
mortality (5,24) (Fig 5).

Potential Complications
Extramural  findings  are  associated  with  complications  and
are usually manifestations of ischemia leading to perforation.
Stercoral  perforation  is  associated  with  a  mortality  rate  of
32%–59% due to extraluminal spillage of feces and the often
rapid onset of feculent peritonitis (9). CT is particularly use-
ful in the identification of direct sites of transmural injury or
secondary signs of perforation, including mural discontinui-
ty, extraluminal air, and perirectal fluid collection or abscess.
(Figs 6–8). Extraluminal gas is easily identifiable on noncon-
trast CT images, but mural discontinuity may not be evident.
Intravenous contrast agents can aid in the detection of sites of
mural discontinuity, although sometimes this feature is dis-
cernible on noncontrast CT images (Fig 9). Ascites is a non-
specific  finding.  However,  it  is  concerning  in  the  absence  of
an obvious reason, including cardiac, liver, or kidney disease,
or if it is a new finding in a patient with persistent symptoms
or laboratory abnormalities (5). Pneumatosis can be seen as

Extraluminal stool has the appearance of a mottled gas and

Mural  discontinuities  and  extramural  gas  are  virtually
diagnostic of perforation. Extraluminal air can collect in the
intraperitoneal and/or extraperitoneal spaces, depending on
the involved segment of the colon. In the authors’ experience
and  based  on  the  limited  literature,  stercoral  rectal  perfo-
rations are usually walled off and localized, which in theory
may allow a better prognosis than an intraperitoneal perfora-
tion, which often leads to the rapid onset of frank sepsis (Fig
S3) (7–9,26). However, the largest single-institution study that
identified patients with stercoral colitis at CT showed that ap-
proximately 95% of stercoral perforation involved the sigmoid

colon,  with  only  5%  of  cases  involving  the  rectum  alone  (6).
This increased risk of perforation in the sigmoid colon is pos-
sibly related to the decreased luminal capacity and distensi-
bility compared with that of the rectum (6). There are limit-
ed data regarding the incidence of perforation involving the
more  proximal  colon;  recent  review  articles  have  indicated
an incidence of 11.1%–19.2% (27,28). The cecum, which has a
significantly higher distensibility than the sigmoid colon, was
implicated in less than 5.2% of cases (27,28).

Stercoral colitis is a potential cause for complications that
intrinsically involve the large bowel or affect other structures
in the pelvis. Large bowel obstruction due to a sigmoid fecal-
oma is a relatively common finding and should be suspected
when there is marked colonic dilatation proximal to the site of
fecal  impaction  (30,31).  Potential  uncommon  complications
include  lower  gastrointestinal  tract  bleeding,  fistulization,
and urinary tract obstruction from mass effect on the bladder
neck or ureters. Bleeding from nonperforated stercoral ulcers
is a rare cause of hematochezia (32,33) (Fig 13). Although rare,
fistulization  to  the  adjacent  structures  from  a  focal  perfora-
tion can also occur. In patients with constipation with bloody
and feculent vaginal discharge, a rectovaginal fistula should
be suspected (34) (Fig 14). Rectocutaneous fistulas have also
been described in the literature (34). The large stool burden

associated with stercoral colitis can compress the bladder, re-
sulting in urinary retention and hydronephrosis (2) (Fig 15).

rized in the Table. Many of these imaging findings are non-
specific and can overlap with those of other pathologic bowel
conditions. The key point is that identification of fecal impac-
tion  is  essential  for  diagnosis.  Inflammatory  changes  such
as wall thickening and fat stranding typically are associated
with the area affected by fecal impaction. Understanding the
underlying pathologic cause of stercoral colitis will aid in dis-
tinguishing it from other forms of colitis.

Differential Diagnosis
Diagnosis  of  stercoral  colitis  at  CT  is  often  straightforward.
However,  findings  from  other  colonic  diseases  may  overlap.
Some disease entities that may share similar findings include
diverticulitis,  ischemic  colitis,  large  bowel  obstruction,  in-
flammatory  bowel  disease,  pseudo-obstruction  (Ogilvie  dis-
ease), and colorectal malignancy.

Diverticulitis  is  an  important  differential  consideration.
There  is  an  overlap  in  patient  demographics,  as  it  is  most
commonly  seen  in  older  patients  with  chronic  constipation.
In diverticulitis, wall thickening and fat stranding are usually
centered  around  a  single  diverticulum,  while  the  inflamma-
tion associated with stercoral colitis may be more diffuse, with

Management
Early recognition of stercoral colitis allows identification of at-
risk patients and early intervention to reduce potential mor-

bidity  and  mortality.  It  is  common  for  stercoral  colitis  to  be
diagnosed incidentally while a patient is seeking acute med-
ical care (6). Therefore, appropriate and timely management
can halt disease progression and prevent potential complica-
tions. Some of the imaging findings associated with a wors-
ened prognosis include long-segment (>40 cm) involvement
of the colon and the presence of free fluid, extraluminal air,
and extraluminal stool (5,9,24).

Treatment is based on the severity of the disease. With un-
complicated  stercoral  colitis,  a  bowel  regimen  is  used,  which
includes  preventive  measures  such  as  a  high-fiber  diet  and
prophylactic use of laxatives and/or stool motility agents (3). It
is advisable to discontinue anticholinergics or opioid medica-
tions when clinically feasible. Enemas and manual disimpac-
tion are also recommended. Contrast agent enemas serve as an
additional method for the diagnosis and treatment of stercoral
colitis, particularly when large bowel obstruction is suspected
(31). While their use is primarily documented in the older lit-
erature, they continue to be performed in an attempt to disim-
pact the fecaloma and mitigate the need for surgery (9) (Fig 3B).
Endoscopic  disimpaction  is  often  performed  when  the
fecaloma  is  unreachable  with  digital  disimpaction  and  en-
emas  are  not  successful  (10,11,13,19).  Management  can  be
challenging,  with  patients  often  requiring  multiple  proce-

Conclusion
Stercoral  colitis  is  essentially  a  form  of  ischemic  colitis  that
is  precipitated  by  chronic  constipation.  Although  stercoral
colitis is considered to be relatively rare in the literature, the
authors  believe  this  may  be  related  to  underdiagnosis  due
to  nonspecific  clinical  presentations  and  overlap  with  other
inflammatory bowel disorders. Stercoral colitis primarily af-
