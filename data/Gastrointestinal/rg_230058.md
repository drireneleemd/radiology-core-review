The Portal Vein:
A Comprehensive Review

Benjamin M. Layton, BSc, MSc, BMBS, FRCR • Snehal K. Lapsia, MBChB, FRCR

Author affiliations, funding, and conflicts of interest are listed at the end of this article.

Radiologists are familiar with the appearances of a
normal portal vein; variations in its anatomy are com-
monplace and require careful consideration due to the
implications for surgery. These alterations in portal
vein anatomy have characteristic appearances that are
clearly depicted on CT, MR, and US images. Similarly,
there are numerous congenital and acquired disorders
of the portal vein that are deleterious to its function
and can be diagnosed by using imaging alone. Some
of these conditions have subtle imaging features, and
some are conspicuous at imaging but poorly under-
stood or underrecognized. The authors examine im-
aging appearances of the portal vein, first by outlining
the classic and variant anatomy and then by describing
each of the disorders that impact portal vein function.
The imaging appearances of portal vein abnormalities
discussed in this review include (a) occlusion from
and differentiation between bland thrombus and tu-
mor in vein and the changes associated with resultant
hepatic artery buffer response changes, cavernous
transformation of the portal vein, and portal biliopa-
thy; (b) ascending thrombophlebitis of the portal vein
(pylephlebitis); (c) portal hypertension and its causes
and sequelae; (d) the newly described disease entity
portosinusoidal vascular disorder; and (e) intra- and
extrahepatic shunts of the portal system, both congeni-
tal and acquired (including Abernethy malformations),
and the associated risks. Current understanding of the
pathophysiologic processes of each of these disorders
is considered to aid the approach to reporting.

Introduction
The portal vein (PV) delivers 75% of the blood supply to the
liver, transporting toxins and nutrients from the gut for pro-
cessing (1,2). Although the PV transports partially deoxygen-
ated blood, it is responsible for approximately 50% of the ox-
ygen supply to the liver, with the remainder being supplied
by  the  hepatic  artery  (3).  Normal  physiology  requires  that
PV function is unrestricted; however, a number of disorders
exist,  both  congenital  and  acquired,  that  can  directly  and
indirectly affect PV flow. Some of these conditions—for ex-
ample, portal hypertension (PH) secondary to cirrhosis—are
common and well understood, and some, such as Abernethy

malformations,  are  seldom  seen  at  imaging.  Knowledge  of
the full spectrum of diseases of the PV is important because
of  the  significant  associated  morbidity  and  mortality.  The
PV  is  readily  and  routinely  assessed  with  imaging  modali-
ties  such  as  US,  CT,  and  MRI  of  the  abdomen,  and  a  good
understanding of the associated diseases will help to avoid
incorrect or delayed diagnosis of important diseases.

This article also highlights PV anatomy and common and
uncommon variants, both of which are paramount to success-
ful surgical planning. Finally, to aid understanding of the ra-
diologic appearances, some of the proposed pathophysiologic
processes  behind  the  conditions  discussed  are  explained.  In

Anatomy

Intrahepatic Anatomy
Knowledge  of  PV  branching  patterns  is  critical  for  the  suc-
cess  of  hepatic  interventions  such  as  liver  resection  and  PV
embolization.  Misunderstanding  of  PV  anatomy  can  result
in suboptimal outcomes or even death. Variations in intrahe-
patic PV arrangements are common, and differentiating one
variant from another can be difficult.

The classic anatomy of the PV, accounting for approximately
65% of cases, involves a bifurcation into the left PV (LPV), which
subdivides to supply the left lateral and medial segments, and
into  the  right  PV  (RPV),  with  further  subdivisions  into  the
right-to-anterior PV (RAPV) and right-to-posterior PV (RPPV)
(Fig 1) (5). The most common variant is an initial division of
the main PV into the LPV and RPPV, which accounts for 13%
of  cases.  Within  this  category,  the  RAPV  may  arise  from  the
LPV (Fig 2), or vice versa (Fig 3), and in some studies, these two
configurations have been grouped under the same umbrella of
Z-type PV (5–7). To assist in differentiating these two variants

on images, it can be useful to consider that the normal LPV has
an anteroposterior or north-south orientation on axial images
(Fig 1). A trifurcating PV is the next most common variant (9%
of cases) , whereby the RAPV, RPPV, and LPV form within 1 cm
of each other (Fig 4); in 6% of cases, a branch arising from the
RPV to exclusively supply liver segment VI occurs (5).

Finally,  multiple  rare  divergent  arrangements,  which  ac-
count for less than 1% of the reported anatomy, exist and in-
clude  a  nonbranching  PV,  whereby  each  Couinaud  segment
receives a branch directly from a single PV (Fig 5).

Rotation.—The  position  and  size  of  some  intrahepatic  lesions
can be such that no liver parenchyma is visible around them.
Likewise, some extrahepatic lesions exert such a mass effect on
the  liver  and  are  in  such  close  proximity  that  no  fat  plane  is
discernible between the lesion and the liver tissue; therefore,
it can be challenging to determine the organ of origin (8). The
orientation of the LPV is a valuable clue in such circumstances.
The north-south orientation is standard for the LPV and will
not be altered in the presence of a large right intrahepatic mass.
However, an extrahepatic mass in the right upper quadrant will
cause the liver to rotate clockwise, resulting in the LPV rotating
in an east-west orientation (Fig 6) (8).

Variant  Minor  Tributaries.—Occasionally,  the  smaller  tribu-
taries to the PV can drain directly into the liver, and this can
result in abnormalities that are visible on images and may be
misinterpreted.

In the classic anatomy of the PV, the left gastric vein (LGV)
and right gastric vein (RGV) drain into the PV. Aberrant drain-
age directly to the liver can occur, typically with the LGV drain-
ing to the posterior aspects of liver segments II and III and the
RGV draining to the posterior aspects of segment IV, resulting
in  radiologically  visible  alterations  (9,10).  These  alterations
manifest as areas of focal fatty sparing or focal fatty change (Fig
7). These changes are considered to be dependent on the con-
centration  of  insulin  in  the  aberrant  vessel,  as  insulin  stimu-
lates the conversion of glucose into fat (11).

In patients with alcohol addiction, the focal area of the liver
receiving aberrant drainage may atrophy at a slower rate, giving
the appearance of relative hypertrophy (9,12). Finally, aberrant
gastric veins provide a potential direct route of spread for a gas-
tric tumor into the liver (10).

Extrahepatic Anatomy
The  PV  forms  when  the  splenic  vein  and  superior  mesen-
teric vein (SMV) join posteriorly to the neck of the pancreas.
The inferior mesenteric vein is the other major contributor,
draining into either the splenic vein or the SMV, or the PV
confluence  in  about  20%  of  cases  (1).  In  addition  to  these
three  major  contributors,  there  are  multiple  smaller  veins
that drain into the PV. These include the gastric veins, pos-
terior superior pancreaticoduodenal vein, and the plexus of
veins draining the gallbladder and biliary tree.

The PV travels from its origin to the liver within the hepato-
duodenal ligament. Variations of the extrahepatic PV anatomy
include exceedingly rare anomalies such as duplicated PV and
PV receiving a total anomalous pulmonary venous return (13)

Acute PVT
US  is  the  first  line  of  assessment  for  PVT,  with  a  specificity
of  between  80%  and  100%  and  a  sensitivity  of  between  88%
and 98%, which is highest in cases of complete occlusion (26).
CT  is  not  required  to  confirm  PVT  unless  sonographic  as-
sessment is limited or the acquired images are suboptimal.
However, once PVT is diagnosed, a CT examination should
be  performed  to  assess  the  extent  of  the  PVT,  map  collat-
eral  flow,  and  rule  out  complications  (26).  The  normal  PV
lumen is anechoic on US images and exhibits a hepatopetal
color flow Doppler signal. The sonographic appearances of
PVT are hyperechoic luminal content and an expanded PV,
with an experienced operator unable to detect typical color
flow (Fig 10) (4). This results in a distinct filling defect that
is devoid of enhancement at contrast-enhanced US (Fig 11).
Contrast-enhanced US has a sensitivity higher than 95% at
PVT detection, which is higher than that of CT (26–28).

Acute  PVT  is  commonly  diagnosed  by  using  contrast-en-
hanced CT, which is very sensitive for demonstrating the cause
and extent of and any complication associated with PVT (26).
PVT  causes  expansion  of  the  PV,  with  high  attenuation  at
noncontrast  CT  and  filling  defect  in  the  PV  at  PV  phase  im-
aging, with resultant poor liver enhancement (29). The major
tributaries and branches of the PV should be assessed to delin-
eate the extent of thrombosis (Fig 10) (4).

Another  CT  feature  of  PVT  is  a  hyperenhancing  vessel
wall  (Fig  10),  the  pathophysiology  of  which  is  not  fully  un-
derstood. It has been variously attributed to undisturbed en-
hancement of the PV wall through patent vasa vasorum, sec-
ondary inflammatory response of the wall, and blood flowing
around the periphery of the acute thrombus (30). Care should
be  taken  to  avoid  misdiagnosing  laminar  flow  of  contrast
material in the PV as thrombus (so-called pseudothrombus),
which resolves during later contrast phases (2).

The  depiction  of  acute  PVT  on  MR  images  is  variable.
Noncontrast T1-weighted MR images can help differentiate
acute from chronic PVT, with reported hyperintensity of the
liver for at least 5 weeks and a reduction in signal intensity
over time such that at 2 months following the acute event,
PVT is hypointense relative to the liver and muscle (31). The
signal  intensity  characteristics  on  T2-weighted  MR  images
have not been found to be a reliable tool for PVT diagnosis
(32).  Thrombus  may  become  conspicuous  on  susceptibili-
ty-weighted  MR  images;  this  finding  is  comparable  to  that
seen with CT in terms of sensitivity (26).

The most dangerous manifestations of PVT are upper gas-
trointestinal bleeding and venous ischemia of the small bowel.
Upper gastrointestinal bleeding usually manifests as clinically
apparent hematemesis, anemia, or perhaps a patient in extre-
mis, and it may be the reason for imaging. The clinical diagno-
sis of venous ischemia of the small bowel is more challenging,
but the presence of PVT should lead to the assessment for ve-
nous ischemia of the bowel and vice versa. Venous ischemia of
the bowel causes mesenteric venous engorgement, mesenteric
congestion,  ascites,  and,  most  important,  edematous  bowel
wall thickening with poorly enhancing walls.

A THAD can be seen on CT images and is typically a wedge-
shaped relatively hyperenhancing region located peripherally
in the liver. It represents early enhancement of otherwise nor-
mal hepatic parenchyma, which is explained by the adenosine
washout hypothesis: Interrupted PV flow allows the buildup
of adenosine in the liver, which causes vasodilatation of the
hepatic arteries and compensatory arterial perfusion to a re-
gion of the liver that has just lost its portal supply (33).

Therefore, with occlusion of a small PV branch, the intra-
hepatic arterial supply compensates, and this change from a
portal to arterial supply is vividly depicted on CT scans as a
THAD or on MR images as a transient hepatic signal intensity
difference (THID). The discovery of a THAD should prompt
assessment of its apex for the cause, as a subtle PVT may be
visible (Fig 12).

If  no  thrombus  is  found  following  careful  scrutiny  of  the
apex of a THAD, further investigation can be guided by the in-
dex of suspicion for an obstructing tumor or thrombus. THADs
and THIDs are commonly seen in cirrhotic livers, where appli-
cation of the Liver Imaging Reporting and Data System criteria
(34)  can  mitigate  a  misdiagnosis  of  arterializing  tumors  and
provide a basis for planning follow-up imaging.

Hepatic Artery Buffer Response
The  hepatic  artery  buffer  response  provides  an  explanation
for  an  important  indirect  radiologic  indicator  of  PVT:  the
transient hepatic attenuation difference (THAD).

Chronic PVT
Without  recanalization  following  acute  PVT,  stigmata  of
chronic  PVT  develop.  These  changes  can  be  assessed  and
categorized under three broad umbrella categories: cavern-
ous transformation of the PV (CTPV), liver morphology, and
altered PV appearance.

Cavernous Transformation of the PV.—The hepatic artery buf-
fer response provides no alternative route for the 800–1200
mL of blood that flows every minute through the PV; there-
fore,  venous  collaterals  are  required  and  are  established
by way of CTPV. This occurs as soon as 6–20 days after the
thrombotic event (9,37).

CTPV involves gross dilatation of otherwise tiny, usually
radiologically  occult,  veins  such  that  they  may  assume  the
role  of  the  PV. This  principally  involves  the  venous  plexus
of the common bile duct (CBD): the epicholedochal plexus
of  Saint,  a  network  of  veins  within  the  CBD  wall,  and  the
paracholedochal  plexus  of  Petren,  two  parallel  veins  run-
ning in tandem outside the CBD wall toward the liver (Fig
14) (38). Proximal insertion into the PV allows these veins to
bypass extrahepatic thrombosis (38,39).

Liver Morphology.—A chronic lack of portal supply causes at-
rophy of the affected segment (Fig 17). This finding at imaging
may be incidental and should lead the radiologist to search for
a cause of the segmental atrophy, potentially revealing features
of subtle chronic PVT (discussed in the following “Altered PV
Appearance” section). This is an important finding, as it may
suggest an undiagnosed underlying hematologic disorder (Ta-
ble). Occlusion of the PV supply to one region of the liver also
causes  compensatory  hypertrophy  of  the  remaining  liver,  a
condition that is exploited in hepatic surgery (4).

Altered PV Appearance.—Over time, the PV can sclerose and
become difficult to appreciate with imaging. This is particu-

larly  the  case  when  only  intrahepatic  branches  are  affected.
Prominence of the smaller PV tributaries such as the gastric
veins  and  posterior-superior  pancreaticoduodenal  vein  can
be indicative of subtle PVT, and the intrahepatic PV branches
should  be  scrutinized.  Thrombus  in  the  PV  may  eventually
become calcified, and this condition is conspicuous on images
(29).  In  addition,  occlusion  results  in  increased  portal  pres-
sures, so features of PH may be visible.

Portal Hypertension
A pressure gradient of more than 5 mm Hg between a hepatic
vein and the PV suggests PH; this becomes clinically signifi-
cant if the pressure gradient rises to over 10 mm Hg (44). This
sustained  increased  pressure  eventually  results  in  radiologi-
cally visible sequelae, and one of the most important of these
is  the  formation  of  extrahepatic  portosystemic  shunts.  The
clinical ramifications of this include hepatic encephalopathy
and gastrointestinal bleeding (45).

Stigmata of PH
Sustained PH results in dilatation of the PV and its tributar-
ies.  Eventually,  the  resistance  is  such  that  blood  is  shunted
from  the  portal  system  to  some  of  the  weaker  veins  of  the
caval system (46). The most useful modality for detection of
these varices is contrast-enhanced CT (47). This is probably
due in part to the comprehensive scope of CT coupled with
the unpredictability of shunt locations, which have been re-
ported  at  more  than  20  sites  throughout  the  abdomen  (Fig
21) (6).

Other  features  of  PH  include  splenomegaly,  ascites,  and
congestion of the walls of various parts of the gastrointestinal
tract, most commonly the right colon (portal colopathy) but
also the stomach (gastropathy).

Shunts
Shunts can be intra- or extrahepatic, acquired or congenital,
and  portosystemic  or  arterioportal.  Acquired  extrahepatic
portosystemic shunts are the most common type and are dis-
cussed as sequelae of PH in the earlier “Portal Hypertension”
section.

Portosystemic Shunts
Allowing splanchnic blood into the systemic circulation can
have  serious  consequences,  the  most  important  and  most
common ones being hepatopulmonary syndrome and hepatic
encephalopathy–like syndromes.

With  hepatopulmonary  syndrome,  vasoactive  mediators
bypass the liver and cause marked vasodilatation in the lungs,
allowing blood to flow too rapidly for effective gas exchange
and thus resulting in clinical deoxygenation (57,58). Hepatic
encephalopathy involves neurotoxins such as ammonia that
reach the brain but would otherwise be filtered by the liver.
These neurotoxins interfere with neural activity, resulting in
encephalopathy  (58).  Children  appear  to  be  more  resistant
to hepatic encephalopathy than adults; this is why portosys-
temic shunts tend to be discovered incidentally in the pediat-
ric population (59).

Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunts.—Intrahepatic portosystemic
shunts are venovenous communications between the portal and
systemic systems, at least partially within the liver, and measur-
ing more than 1 mm in diameter. Four types of intrahepatic por-
tosystemic shunts exist and are illustrated and described in Fig-
ure 23 (60,61). A patent ductus venosus is sometimes considered
a fifth type, although it is exceedingly rare (61).

Arterioportal Shunts
Arterioportal shunts can be congenital (eg, hereditary hemor-
rhagic telangiectasia) or acquired (eg, with cirrhosis or malig-
nancy), and form by way of various pathophysiologic mech-
anisms  (63,67).  They  can  appear  as  grossly  dilated  shunts
through proximal branches of the hepatic arteries and PV, to
tiny perfusional changes, implying microscopic shunts (63).

If an arterioportal shunt involves larger vessels, then clear
opacification of the PV is visible during the arterial phase of im-
aging. This type of macroscopic fistula tends to be the result of
intervention, trauma, or malignancy (classically an HCC) (Fig
20) (67). In these instances, color flow Doppler US can show an
arterial waveform in the PV or even flow reversal (63,68).

Peripheral arterioportal shunts tend to be microscopic, as
the portal and arterial systems decrease in size considerably

The  afferent  and  efferent  vessels  should  be  readily  trace-
able on images (Fig 24), with the efferent vessel demonstrat-
ing earlier enhancement than the adjacent hepatic veins (6).
Awareness of the types of shunt can be of value because when
a shunt is discovered, more shunts may be visible (type 4), and
type 2 shunts may evolve into type 3 shunts and these, which
include a varix, may be mistaken for cystic lesions or solid le-
sions if the varix becomes thrombosed (62).

Spectral Doppler US shows altered waveforms on both sides
of  the  shunt. The  portal  side  will  show  a  triphasic  waveform
due to cardiac pulsatility being transmitted directly through the
shunt, and the hepatic side produces a flattening of the usual
triphasic waveform due to continuous portal inflow (63).

PV Aneurysms
PV aneurysm might be more correctly termed varix, although
the term PV aneurysm has become the standard. The normal
PV  diameter  varies  considerably,  and  a  strict  diameter  limit
that defines a PV aneurysm does not exist. However, an upper
limit of 20 mm has been suggested in the medical literature
(71,72). PV aneurysms can also have a saccular morphology,
appearing  as  exophytic  structures.  These  tend  to  affect  the
extrahepatic PV and are most commonly caused by cirrhotic
PH, although congenital PV aneurysms also exist (72).

PV aneurysms can cause vague upper abdominal pain or
occur  due  to  symptoms  and  signs  from  the  mass  effect  that
they  exert—for  example,  a  palpable  right  upper  quadrant
structure or compression of the biliary tree causing portal bil-
iopathy and jaundice (73). Rupture and upper gastrointestinal
bleeding occur but are not common complications (72).

PV Gas
PV gas is a poor prognostic indicator, with a related mortality
rate of about 30%, primarily due to its association with bowel

ischemia  (75).  Other  more  benign  causes  exist  and  include
diverticulitis, pylephlebitis, trauma, and inflammatory bowel
disease (2).

CT  findings  of  PV  gas  can  be  mistaken  for  pneumobilia,
particularly since on cross-sectional images, both PV gas and
pneumobilia  predominate  in  the  nondependent  left  liver.
However,  differentiating  these  two  entities  is  straightfor-
ward:  the  flow  of  bile  is  hepatofugal,  theoretically  keeping
the gas in the biliary tree away from the liver periphery, but
PV gas tends to extend all the way to the liver edge (2). The
physical presence of gas in the PV system disrupts perfusion
and therefore results in decreased enhancement of the liver
parenchyma,  a  finding  not  compatible  with  pneumobilia
(Fig 29).

The  sonographic  appearances  of  PV  gas  are  distinct  and
unique. Highly echogenic foci of PV gas are conspicuous, flow-
ing toward the liver within the PV. This is a rare but unmistak-
able finding. Spectral Doppler US is equally characteristic, with
momentary high-amplitude bidirectional vertical spikes as the
bubbles pass on the background normal PV waveform (76).

Conclusion
The PV is seen at routine abdominal imaging with multiple mo-
dalities and is therefore very commonly reviewed. Radiologists
should be comfortable with the anatomy of the PV and its vari-
ant anatomy to aid successful surgical planning and should have
a sound understanding of the multitude of disorders that affect
the PV. Although numerous more rare PV disorders exist and
have distinctive radiologic features, they are commonly misdi-
agnosed or missed altogether. Awareness of these rare entities is
important because in many cases, early radiologic diagnosis can
help  avoid  unnecessary  interventions  and  drastically  alter  the
direction of management, improving patient outcomes.
