High-frequency  (HF;
12  MHz)  US  can  be  used  in  routine
hepatobiliary imaging examinations. The accompanying slide
presentation  demonstrates  our  use  of  HF  US  to  evaluate  the
liver  surface  and  parenchyma,  peripheral  focal  liver  lesions,
gallbladder  wall,  and  bile  ducts  to  make  diagnoses  that  were
either  questionable  or  not  appreciated  at  standard  lower-fre-
quency US. We discuss our routine HF US protocol, correlate
HF  US  and  histologic  findings,  and  highlight  the  modality’s
shortfalls.

HF US is an established method to evaluate the contour and
margins of the liver for diagnosis of cirrhosis. While it cannot
usually penetrate the full liver, HF US allows improved resolu-
tion of the superficial hepatic parenchyma. Thus, with diffuse
liver disease, subtle findings are often more easily appreciated,
especially for less experienced interpreters. In addition to sev-
eral static HF images, our protocol requires HF US cine clips
of the right and left lobes, allowing the interpreter to make an
independent assessment of the hepatic contour and texture of
the parenchyma. Instead of the commonly used description of
coarse hepatic parenchyma seen on low-frequency images, HF
US  images  directly  depict  increased  numbers  of  hyperechoic
reticular echoes felt to represent fibrosis and distinct 1–3-mm
regenerating nodules that define cirrhosis (Fig 1).

Although US is typically the initial imaging modality used
to  evaluate  the  gallbladder,  its  high  sensitivity  for  acute  cho-
lecystitis,  especially  as  compared  with  that  of  CT,  has  been
questioned  in  recent  years.  Since  the  gallbladder  is  relatively
superficial, it is well suited to HF US. We have found that sub-
tle  signs  of  acute  cholecystitis  are  also  easily  appreciated,  in-
cluding  slight  wall  thickening,  tensile  shape,  small  amounts
of  fluid,  and  adjacent  echogenic  fat.  Demonstration  of  these
overt findings is much more useful for diagnosis than the sub-
jective sonographic Murphy sign (Fig 2). The gallbladder wall
can be scrutinized for subtle defects or trace amounts of gas to
make the diagnosis of gangrenous cholecystitis. In addition to
gallbladder wall edema in acute cholecystitis, HF US can help
more confidently differentiate other benign gallbladder condi-
tions from malignant gallbladder conditions. In focal adenomy-
omatosis,  one  may  directly  visualize  the  dilated  crystal-filled
Rokitansky-Aschoff sinuses and avoid the need for follow-up or
alternative imaging to exclude gallbladder carcinoma.

While lower-frequency imaging is excellent to evaluate bile
ducts and vessels in the central liver, the smaller branches in the
periphery of the liver are well visualized at HF US. Thus, one
may more easily distinguish between periportal edema and bil-
iary sludge and differentiate benign from malignant portal vein

TEACHING POINTS
	 Beware of making or excluding a diagnosis of cirrhosis based solely on the

liver contour; corroborate with confirmatory textural findings at HF US.

	 The  tensile  shape  and  subtle  wall  thickening  of  an  inflamed  gallbladder  in

acute cholecystitis can be overlooked at routine low-frequency US.

	 Direct  visualization  of  dilated  Rokitansky-Aschoff  sinuses  using  HF  imaging

can help distinguish focal adenomyomatosis from a gallbladder mass.

thrombosis. Using HF US helps delineate the various causes of
punctate and linear echogenic foci, including pneumobilia, von
Meyenburg complexes, and hepatic cysts.

Last, we provide examples of situations in which HF US is
an impediment rather than an improvement, including severe
steatosis and large volumes of air or gas.
