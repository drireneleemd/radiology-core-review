The duodenum can be affected by a variety of abnormalities be-
cause of its development during embryogenesis and its dual in-
tra- and retroperitoneal location. If small bowel embryogenesis
is disturbed, congenital errors occur. Although some congenital
variants may be asymptomatic and inconsequential to the patient,
other anomalies can result in life-threatening emergencies such as
malrotation, leading to midgut volvulus. Many infectious processes
affect the duodenum, including duodenal ulcers and opportunistic
infection in patients with HIV/AIDS or Crohn disease. Small bowel
malignancies are uncommon but important to recognize, because
the duodenum can be involved in polyposis syndromes or the de-
velopment of primary adenocarcinoma, neuroendocrine tumors,
lymphoma, and metastasis. Although endoscopy is currently the
most used diagnostic method to assess the lumen of the upper gas-
trointestinal tract, fluoroscopy is a valuable adjunct technique and
the study of choice for many diseases, specifically those for which
anatomic and functional information is required. Fluoroscopy is
also commonly used postoperatively to assess for complications
such as obstruction and extraluminal leaks. Compared with endos-
copy, fluoroscopy is an inexpensive and noninvasive technique that
provides salient anatomic information and allows delineation of the
duodenal mucosa and assessment of real-time duodenal motility.
The authors examine the broad spectrum of conditions that can
involve the duodenum, including congenital, infectious, inflamma-
tory, and neoplastic abnormalities, and review their typical appear-
ances at fluoroscopy.

Introduction
The duodenum is uniquely positioned in the intraperitoneal and retro-
peritoneal abdomen and is close to multiple other intra-abdominal
structures. As a result, it can be affected by many primary and sec-
ondary diseases including traumatic, infectious, inflammatory, and
neoplastic abnormalities (Table). Because development of the duode-
num involves counterclockwise rotation of the midgut during em-
bryogenesis, numerous congenital variants can occur if this process is
interrupted. Although endoscopy, CT, and MR enterography are used
for evaluation of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, fluoroscopy is a nonin-
vasive and inexpensive method to examine the duodenum. Thorough
knowledge of optimal fluoroscopic techniques, normal anatomy, and

Embryology and Anatomy
The GI tract develops from the foregut, midgut,
or hindgut. The duodenum is derived from the
foregut, which forms the esophagus, stomach,
and duodenum up to the ampulla of Vater, and
the midgut, which forms the distal duodenum
through the proximal two-thirds of the trans-
verse colon. During weeks 4 and 5 of develop-
ment, the small and large intestines rapidly
outgrow the available space in the abdominal
cavity and eventually herniate outside the abdo-
men and into the umbilical cord. The herniated
intestine rotates 90° counterclockwise around
the mesentery, and the proximal bowel moves to
the right while the distal bowel moves to the left.
At 10 weeks of gestation, the bowel returns to
the abdominal cavity and rotates an additional
180° counterclockwise (1,2).

The duodenum is the first part of the small

intestine and connects the stomach with the
second part of the small intestine, the jejunum.
The duodenum begins at the duodenal bulb and
ends at the ligament of Treitz, which is some-
times referred to as the duodenojejunal junction.

The duodenum is divided into four parts on the
basis of anatomic orientation: the duodenal bulb
(D1), descending duodenum (D2), transverse
duodenum (D3), and ascending duodenum (D4)
(Fig 1A). D1 is intraperitoneal for its first few
centimeters before curving superiorly and poste-
riorly into the retroperitoneal abdomen (3).The
remainder of the duodenum is retroperitoneal.
The duodenojejunal junction is located to the left
of the vertebral column, posterior to the stomach,
and at the level of D1 (4).

At fluoroscopic examination, D1 has either
smooth nearly featureless mucosa or a fine reticu-
lar mucosal pattern (Fig 1B) (5). D2–D4 have
numerous parallel mucosal folds, also known as
valvulae conniventes or plicae circulares. These
mucosal folds are characteristic of the small bowel
and extend from D2 through the jejunum before
disappearing in the distal ileum (6).

Fluoroscopic Technique

A fluoroscopic upper GI series is an x-ray ex-
amination to evaluate the structure and function
of the esophagus, stomach, and duodenum, for
which the patient ingests barium or water-sol-
uble contrast material. Barium, which is highly
attenuating, is used unless there is concern for
perforation, in which case, a water-soluble con-
trast agent is used. The upper GI fluoroscopic
series can be performed with a “single-contrast”
technique (contrast material alone) or a “dou-
ble-contrast” technique (contrast material and
effervescent granules). A single-contrast tech-
nique may be preferable in certain clinical situ-
ations such as in patients with limited mobility
or a large body habitus and in those who have
undergone restrictive gastric surgery. If the pur-
pose of the study is assessment for perforation,
obstruction, or dysmotility, a single-contrast
technique may be sufficient.

A double-contrast technique is preferred in
most scenarios. The patient first swallows effer-
vescent granules with a small sip of water, then
rapidly gulps barium to distend the esophagus.
When the effervescent granules contact the
stomach, they release gas and cause distention,
which effaces the normal bowel folds and al-
lows evaluation of gastric and bowel mucosa.
Fluoroscopic evaluation of the esophagus and
stomach is performed with a technique described
by Levine et al (5,7), with the patient in a supine
left posterior oblique position. Spot air contrast
images of the duodenum are acquired with the
patient in the left posterior oblique and left
lateral positions. The patient then moves into the
prone right anterior oblique position to acquire

Congenital Variants

Intestinal Malrotation
Intestinal malrotation is a congenital anomaly
that results from abnormal rotation of the mid-
gut as it returns to the abdominal cavity during
embryogenesis. Any deviation from the normal
270° counterclockwise rotation of the midgut
is considered malrotation, although the degree
of deviation is on a spectrum and can be com-
plete or incomplete (9). The ligament of Treitz is
normally positioned directly anterior to or to the
left of the vertebral column at the level of the
D1 apex. In malrotation, the ligament of Treitz
is abnormally positioned (Fig 2). In infants with
bilious emesis, the most concerning diagnostic
consideration is midgut volvulus, which is a
life-threatening condition that is associated with
malrotation and can result in bowel necrosis
if it is not treated with urgent surgical repair
(10). At fluoroscopy, midgut volvulus appears as
proximal bowel dilatation to the level of D2 or
D3, with an associated spiral or corkscrew ap-
pearance of bowel loops just beyond the dilated
bowel. In a patient with bilious vomiting, mal-
rotation without this imaging appearance may
represent intermittent volvulus.

Duodenum Inversum
Duodenum inversum is a rare congenital anomaly
in which the normal curvature of the duode-
num is reversed. Instead of D3 turning left and
crossing the midline, it turns right and courses
superiorly before curving to the left and cross-
ing the midline above the pancreas (Figs 3, E1)
(11). Unlike its position in patients with malro-

Duodenal Diverticula
Diverticula occur when mucosal and submucosal
layers herniate through a muscular defect in the
bowel wall (13). After the colon, the duodenum

is the most common location for diverticula,
which usually occur on the mesenteric side
along the medial wall of D2 or the superior wall
of D3. Ninety percent of cases of diverticula are
asymptomatic, but rare complications include
diverticulitis or incompetence of the biliary
sphincter if the diverticulum is located near the
papilla of Vater (14). At fluoroscopy, duodenal
diverticula appear as extraluminal collections of
barium that are contiguous with the duodenal
lumen and can change configuration throughout
the examination (Fig 4A). At CT, they appear
as saccular outpouchings from the duodenum
and may contain air, fluid, or food material (Fig
4B) (15). Although it is rare, the duodenum may
develop numerous diverticula with an imaging
appearance that is comparable to that of colonic
diverticulosis (Fig E2).

Intraluminal Diverticula
An intraluminal diverticulum is a sac-like projec-
tion typically in D2 that develops from gradually
increasing protrusion of a congenital mucosal
diaphragm or web (16). As the diverticulum
enlarges because of repetitive peristalsis, patients
can develop symptoms of nausea, vomiting,
and postprandial pain. The classic appearance
on fluoroscopic images of the upper GI is the
“windsock” sign, which is a contrast material–
filled sac in the duodenal lumen that is sur-
rounded by a narrow lucent line that represents
the mucosa of the diaphragm or the web (Figs 5,
E3). The fundus of the diverticulum can extend
as far as D4 (17).

Visceral Myopathy
Visceral myopathy is a rare neuromuscular
condition that is characterized by muscle cell
degeneration and fibrosis, leading to impaired
intestinal function and motility. This can result
in chronic dilatation and pseudo-obstruction of
the GI tract. Clinical manifestations of visceral
myopathy range from asymptomatic to repeated
episodes of bowel obstruction (18). Evaluation
of affected patients with barium contrast mate-
rial demonstrates marked duodenal dilatation
(megaduodenum), with decreased motility and
contrast material stasis (Fig 6) (19).

of pancreatic tissue completely or incompletely
encircling D2 because of incomplete rotation of
the ventral bud of the pancreas during embryo-
genesis (20). Approximately 50% of cases mani-
fest during infancy, with symptoms of gastric
outlet obstruction. The remaining 50% are di-

Inflammatory and Infectious
Conditions

Aphthoid Lesions
Aphthoid lesions are submucosal ulcers on the
surface of hyperplastic intestinal lymphoid fol-
licles. They result from a variety of conditions
including infectious enterocolitis, Crohn dis-
ease, polyarteritis nodosa, and Bechet disease.
At fluoroscopy, the aphthae appear as small
oval areas of lucency, with a central collec-
tion of contrast material that represents focal
erosion surrounded by a halo of inflamed and
edematous mucosa (Fig 10) (25). Because of the
superficial mucosal character of these erosions,
the double-contrast (barium and air) technique
is needed to optimize luminal distention and
mucosal coating.

Nodular Lymphoid Hyperplasia
Nodular lymphoid hyperplasia is a rare and benign
type of lymphoproliferative disease that forms
from lymphoid aggregates in the bowel mucosa.
It is most frequently found in the terminal ileum,
which contains the highest concentration of lym-
phoid follicles. In pediatric patients, it is associated
with viral infections or food allergies. In adults,
it has been associated with common variable im-
mune deficiency and Giardia or Helicobacter pylori
infection (26,27). Lymphoid hyperplasia can also
be a normal variant or incidental finding, although
some patients experience weight loss, diarrhea,

Duodenal Ulcer
Duodenal ulcers are two to three times more
common than gastric ulcers, but unlike gastric
ulcers, they are almost always benign. Common
associations include H. pylori infection and use of
nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs),
alcohol, steroids, and tobacco. Approximately
95% of duodenal ulcers form in D1. They appear
as round or ovoid collections of contrast material
projecting beyond the expected bowel wall, with
radiating folds converging at the edge of these
collections (Fig 12) (28).

Perforated Ulcer
Duodenal ulcers can be asymptomatic or result
in burning or postprandial pain that is relieved
by eating. Complications include hemorrhage,
perforation, obstruction, or formation of a
fistula. If there is strong clinical suspicion for
perforation of a duodenal ulcer, patients typi-
cally undergo CT to assess for pneumoperito-
neum, extraluminal leakage of water-soluble
oral contrast material, or fluid collections. An
upper GI series with water-soluble contrast ma-
terial may be performed to evaluate the location
and extent of the perforation and to determine
if the perforation has sealed. The presence of
extraluminal contrast material during the upper
GI examination allows confirmation of bowel
perforation and identification of the site of the
perforation (Fig 15).

Duodenitis
Duodenitis is inflammation of the duodenum,
most commonly caused by infection with H. py-
lori or the use of NSAIDs. Other causes include
pancreatitis, Crohn disease, radiation, viral or
bacterial infections, atypical infections including
fungal infection and mycobacterium avium-in-
tracellulare infection, and granulomatous dis-
ease. The fluoroscopic appearance of duodenitis
is generally similar for all causes and includes
thickening of the mucosal folds, erosions, and
bulb deformity (Figs 16, E5). Thickening of the
mucosal folds is the most common imaging find-
ing in patients with duodenitis but is also less
specific than erosions for diagnosis of duodenitis,
because fold thickening can also occur in patients
with hemorrhage and edema. Very small erosions
and mucosal nodularity are best shown with the
double-contrast technique (33).

The terminal ileum and proximal colon are most
frequently involved, and the duodenum is only
affected in 0.5%–4% of cases (34). Most patients
with duodenal Crohn disease also have concurrent
involvement of the terminal ileum or colon. The
most common pattern of involvement is con-

Neoplasms

Lipomas
GI lipomas are benign tumors composed of ma-
ture adipocytes, which can be found in the small
and large bowel. Although GI lipomas are usually
asymptomatic and only diagnosed incidentally, a
pedunculated lipoma can act as a lead point for
intussusception or obstruction (43). At fluoros-
copy, lipomas appear as smoothly marginated
and compressible masses that are usually found
in D3 (Fig 21) (44).

Lymphoma
Lymphoma is the most common malignancy
of the small bowel and includes B-cell, T-cell,
Burkitt, mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue, and
Hodgkin lymphoma. At fluoroscopy, lymphoma
can have many different manifestations, includ-
ing diffuse or segmental ulceration or nodularity,
aneurysmal dilatation, or intussusception. It can
also appear as a single or multiple polypoid in-
traluminal masses. Mucosa-associated lymphoid
tissue lymphoma is a low-grade non-Hodgkin
lymphoma that typically arises in the stomach.
Fewer than 30% of cases arise in the small intes-
tine (51). Primary duodenal mucosa-associated
lymphoid tissue lymphoma is rare and has similar
imaging features to those of most other subtypes
of lymphoma (Fig 27) (52). Of all lymphomas,
T-cell lymphoma is most common in the small
bowel and is more likely to have multifocal in-
volvement and cause perforation (53).

Metastasis
Metastasis to the duodenum is rare and typically
occurs in patients with a known primary malig-
nancy and widespread metastatic disease (Fig 28).
The imaging features of metastasis at fluoroscopy
are nonspecific, overlap with those of other previ-
ously described malignant neoplasms, and include
an exophytic or intramural mass, central necrosis,
or focal ulceration (55). Metastasis to the duode-
num usually occurs by means of direct extension
and intraperitoneal seeding from pancreatic, gas-
tric, colonic, and gynecologic malignancies, with
far fewer instances of hematogenous spread from
primary renal cell carcinoma, melanoma, breast
cancer, and lung cancer (56).

sarcoma may initially be asymptomatic, but hem-

Hematoma
Anticoagulant medications, trauma, and bleeding
disorders are the most common causes of a duo-

a duodenal hematoma may raise suspicion for
nonaccidental injury. At fluoroscopy, a duodenal

the lumen with thickening of the folds or a focal
area of scalloped luminal narrowing with fold
effacement, most commonly affecting D3. Suf-

obstruction of the gastric outlet or proximal small
bowel (Figs 31, E7). The “stack of coins sign”
describes smooth and uniform thickening of the
small bowel folds at fluoroscopy and CT that can
be seen in patients with small bowel hemorrhage.

Conclusion
Many duodenal abnormalities can be diagnosed
with fluoroscopy. In other cases, fluoroscopy may
suggest a group of diseases such as malignancy or
widespread inflammation and help to guide patient
care. Although the fluoroscopic imaging features of
many duodenal abnormalities may overlap, clinical
context and subsequent cross-sectional imaging
or evaluation with endoscopy can further narrow
the differential diagnosis. Fluoroscopy is especially
helpful in the postoperative abdomen because it
allows delineation of the altered anatomy and iden-
tification of common postoperative complications.
The anatomic and functional information elicited
from fluoroscopy is a valuable adjunct to endos-
copy and cross-sectional imaging. Radiologists’
familiarity with fluoroscopic techniques, normal
anatomy, and imaging features of common duo-
