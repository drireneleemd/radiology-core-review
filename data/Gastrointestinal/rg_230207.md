How to Make Meaningful Diagnoses with
Abdominal Radiographs: Clues from Natural
“Contrast Agents”

David J. DiSantis, MD • Deborah A. Baumgarten, MD, MPH • Raymond B. Dyer, MD • Joseph G. Cernigliaro, MD

With the rightful ascendance of advanced imaging modalities
such as CT, US, and MRI, abdominal radiographs have been
relegated to a secondary diagnostic role. Hence, there is a per-
ception of them as quaint relics that offer no useful diagnostic
information, deserving only a cursory “nasogastric tube tip in
the stomach.”

Yet,  as  recently  as  2021,  over  8  million  abdominal  radio-
graph  studies  were  performed  in  the  United  States  alone
(Centers  for  Medicare  &  Medicaid  Services  data,  courtesy
of  Jennifer  Hemingway  and  Chi-Mei  Liu,  Harvey  L.  Neiman
Health  Policy  Institute).  Low  cost  and  universal  availability
likely play a role, particularly in less affluent health care set-
tings.  Consequently,  radiologists’  ability  to  interpret  these
studies  in  a  manner  that  adds  value  to  patient  care  will  re-
main  a  part  of  standard  clinical  practice  for  years  to  come.
Abdominal radiographs indeed can help to provide valuable
clues to diagnosis if the interpreter knows what to look for.

Over the decades, the common appellation “plain film of
the  abdomen”  came  to  mean  a  radiograph  with  no  exoge-
nous contrast agents in the gastrointestinal or genitourinary
tracts  or  in  blood  vessels.  But  that  categorization  ignores
three intrinsic “contrast agents” available on all abdominal
radiographs: gas, calcium, and fat. These intrinsic contrast
agents  offer  insights  into  varied  clinically  relevant  diagno-
ses (among them, small bowel obstruction, colonic volvulus,
bowel infarction, extraluminal gas, and sites of infection [Fig
1]), and help to identify and characterize calcified (Fig 2) or
fat-containing benign and malignant pathologic conditions.
The  accompanying  slide  presentation  is  designed  as  a
guide for using these “contrast agents” to recognize clinically
important  findings  on  abdominal  radiographs.  Some  key
points to be gleaned from the presentation include (a) how to
develop an orderly approach to analyzing the bowel gas pat-
tern, (b) where to look for pneumoperitoneum when a supine

frontal view is all that you have, and (c) how to use calcifica-
tions and fat to find and analyze pathologic conditions.

The goal of the presentation is to provide illustrative diag-
nostic  examples  that  encourage  an  orderly  and  thoughtful
approach  to  interpretation  of  abdominal  radiographs,  just  as
would be expected with a patient’s CT or MRI study. The hum-
ble  “kidney,  ureter,  and  bladder”  (KUB)  study  still  holds  im-
portant diagnostic clues that it will reveal to the discerning eye.

TEACHING POINTS
	 Gas, calcium, and fat provide clues to specific diagnoses on abdominal

radiographs.

	 Clues from gas, for example, can indicate bowel obstruction or perforation.
	 Intra-abdominal fat and calcifications can reveal organomegaly, inflam-

matory processes, and masses.
