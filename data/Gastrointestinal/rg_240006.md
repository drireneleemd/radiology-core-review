Hepatic sinusoids are highly specialized microcirculatory conduits within the hepatic lobules that facilitate liver functions.
The sinusoids can be affected by various disorders, including sinusoidal dilatation, sinusoidal obstruction syndrome (SOS),
sinusoidal cellular infiltration, perisinusoidal infiltration, and endothelial neoplasms, such as hemangioendothelioma and
angiosarcoma. While these disorders, particularly SOS and neoplasms, can be life threatening, their clinical manifestation
is often nonspecific. Patients may present with right upper quadrant pain, jaundice, hepatomegaly, ascites, splenomegaly,
and unexplained weight gain, although the exact manifestation depends on the cause, severity, and duration of the disease.
Ultimately, invasive tests may be necessary to establish the diagnosis. A comprehensive understanding of imaging manifes-
tations of various sinusoidal disorders contributes to early diagnosis and can help radiologists detect subclinical disease. Ad-

Introduction
Hepatic sinusoids are microvascular conduits lined by liver si-
nusoidal endothelial cells (LSECs). Sinusoids are closely asso-
ciated with hepatocytes, separated by the perisinusoidal space
of Disse. Dynamic fenestrations in the LSECs facilitate the ex-
change of nutrients and metabolites between hepatocytes and
sinusoidal blood. In addition, LSECs play a role in toxin elim-
ination,  the  regulation  of  sinusoidal  pressure,  antigen  mani-
festation, and liver regeneration (1). Sinusoids can be affected

by several disorders including sinusoidal dilatation, sinusoidal
obstruction  syndrome  (SOS),  sinusoidal  cellular  infiltration,
perisinusoidal  disorders,  and  endothelial  neoplasms.  While
sinusoidal  dilatation  may  manifest  in  nearly  all  sinusoidal
disorders, certain etiologies have distinct clinical features and
imaging appearances. Knowledge of different sinusoidal disor-
ders and their clinical features and imaging characteristics is
crucial for early detection, given that sinusoidal disorders often
remain asymptomatic in the initial stages.

In the context of treatment of various primary cancers and
metastatic liver disease, the escalating use of chemotherapeu-
tic drugs and their various combinations, multiple regimens,
and frequent combination with radiation therapy have all con-
tributed to an increased incidence of SOS. This surge is of sig-
nificant concern, as SOS can adversely impact overall patient
outcomes. This article provides an overview of the pathogen-
esis, clinical features, and imaging appearances of various he-
patic sinusoidal disorders, with a special emphasis on SOS.

Normal Hepatic Sinusoid
Hepatic  sinusoids  are  unique,  dynamic,  and  low-pressure
vascular  channels  situated  between  rows  of  hepatocytes
within a hepatic lobule (Fig 1). The hepatic lobule is a micro-
structural  unit  of  liver  that  is  classically  described  as  con-
sisting of hexagonal plates of hepatocytes bounded by portal
tracts at the corners in the periphery and the terminal he-
patic  vein  (centrilobular  vein)  in  the  center.  The  sinusoids
transport  blood  from  the  terminal  portal  vein  and  hepatic
arterioles  to  the  centrilobular  vein.  These  sinusoids  are
about 300-μm long and have an average diameter of about
5–10  μm,  with  periportal  sinusoids  being  more  tortuous
than centrilobular sinusoids (1). Specialized LSECs line the
sinusoids, and they are separated from hepatocytes by the
perisinusoidal space of Disse.

Within  the  sinusoidal  lumen,  Kupffer  cells  and  the  im-
mune  natural  killer  cells  are  also  present  (2).  Functioning

as  a  barrier  to  blood  cells  under  normal  conditions,  LSECs
feature  dynamic  fenestrations.  These  fenestrations,  cou-
pled with the absence of intercellular junctions, sinusoidal
diaphragms,  and  basement  membranes,  allow  the  pas-
sage  of  macromolecules  across  the  perisinusoidal  space
for exchange with hepatocytes (1). The fenestrations in the
periportal region are larger and fewer, whereas in the cen-
trilobular region, they are smaller and more numerous. Fen-
estrations  are  dynamic  structures  that  respond  to  changes
in  sinusoidal  pressure,  age,  inflammatory  mediators,  ox-
ygen  levels,  pharmacologic  agents,  and  the  composition  of
matrix in the perisinusoidal space (3).

The perisinusoidal space constitutes about 2%–4% of liver
parenchyma  volume  and  contains  a  small  amount  of  base-
ment membrane, collagen, and other structural proteins (1).
Within this perisinusoidal space, hepatic stellate cells (HSCs)
are  present,  along  with  various  immune  cells  including  liv-
er-associated  lymphocytes  and  unmyelinated  nerve  fiber
endings.  HSCs,  also  known  as  Ito  cells,  store  vitamin  A  and
possess multiple long cytoplasmic processes that envelop the
sinusoids. These cytoplasmic processes can contract to regu-
late sinusoidal flow, particularly when activated by endothe-
lin-1  or  other  agonists  (1).  When  activated  by  inflammation
and cytokine factors, HSCs undergo transformation into my-
ofibroblasts and deposit collagen fibers around sinusoids and
hepatocytes, leading to perisinusoidal fibrosis (1).

The  sinusoids  receive  dual  blood  supply  from  the  hepatic
artery and portal vein, with blood mixing within the sinusoi-
dal lumen and flowing from the portal triad toward the centri-
lobular vein (Fig 1). The hepatic lobule shows physiologic and
metabolic heterogeneity known as metabolic “zones” centered
between two portal triads and extending outward toward the
centrilobular  veins  (Fig  1).  Periportal  zone  I  is  nearest  to  the
porta and receives the most oxygenated blood, and centrilob-
ular zone 3 receives the least oxygenated blood. The perisinu-
soidal space may also serve as a lymphatic pathway, directing
fluid to the lymphatics running in the portal tract toward the
liver  hilum  (1).  The  dual-source  blood  supply  to  the  sinusoid
and the potential lymphatic role of the perisinusoidal space are
important for maintaining liver function.

Overview of Hepatic Sinusoidal Disorders
Hepatic  sinusoids  can  be  affected  by  various  disorders  that
can  be  broadly  classified  into  categories  such  as  sinusoidal
dilatation,  sinusoidal  cellular  infiltration,  perisinusoidal  in-
filtration, and neoplasms (Fig 1). The specific causes that fall
under each broad category are summarized in Table 1.

The  primary  manifestations  of  sinusoidal  disorders  are
circulatory changes, specifically alteration of sinusoidal blood
flow. This alteration of flow may increase portal venous pres-
sure,  ultimately  resulting  in  portal  hypertension.  Severe  SOS
and perisinusoidal infiltration can result in lymphatic obstruc-
tion and ascites. Severe or chronic sinusoidal disorders, along
with portal hypertension, may result in ischemia of centrilob-
ular hepatocytes and lead to hepatic cord atrophy or hepato-
cyte necrosis. Reactive hepatocyte hypertrophy adjacent to the
areas of atrophy or necrosis may give rise to a nodular appear-
ance—nodular  regenerative  hyperplasia.  In  cases  of  chronic

The  clinical  manifestations  of  sinusoidal  disorders  are
variable depending on the cause, severity, and duration of the
disease.  In  acute  manifestations,  hepatomegaly  is  common
and frequently causes right upper quadrant pain or discom-
fort.  Ascites  may  be  a  symptom  at  the  initial  presentation,
leading to abdominal distention. Hepatocyte dysfunction se-
vere  enough  to  result  in  hyperbilirubinemia  is  typically  ob-
served  in  the  later  phases  of  sinusoidal  disorders,  with  the
exception  of  severe  acute  cases.  Often,  these  disorders  are
clinically silent and may be discovered only when abnormal
liver  function  test  results  prompt  further  investigations,  in-
cluding imaging.

Currently, the role of imaging in sinusoidal disorders is to
confirm the clinical suspicions and clinical findings, includ-
ing hepatomegaly, ascites, and splenomegaly. Furthermore,
imaging plays a crucial role in excluding alternative causes
of  liver  dysfunction,  such  as  biliary  obstruction  and  portal
vein  thrombosis.  Additionally,  imaging  can  offer  valuable
insights  for  the  specific  diagnosis  of  sinusoidal  disorders,
as will be further detailed below. In addition, the radiologist

may be the first person to suggest the diagnosis based on im-
aging findings.

Sinusoidal Dilatation
Sinusoidal dilatation may be seen in various sinusoidal dis-
orders  as  listed  in  Table  1.  Contrast-enhanced  CT  and  MR
images of sinusoidal dilatation show a mosaic pattern of en-
hancement of the parenchyma (Fig 2) and mottled, heteroge-
neous,  and  reticular  enhancement  of  the  liver  parenchyma,
which is often more prominent in peripheral and subcapsular
regions (4,5). The mosaic pattern is seen during the arterial
and/or portal venous phase, and parenchymal enhancement
becomes homogeneous at a later phase.

Sinusoidal Obstruction Syndrome
SOS  primarily  develops  from  toxic  injury  or  activation  of
LSECs from myeloablative regimens used to prepare patients
for  hematopoietic  stem  cell  transplant  (HSCT),  ingestion  of
pyrrolizidine  alkaloids  (herbal  teas  or  food  source  contami-
nation), metabolites from chemotherapeutic drugs, radiation
therapy, and liver transplant (6,7). Pyrrolizidine alkaloids and
radiation cause direct injury to LSECs (9). Toxic metabolites
of chemotherapeutic agents and myeloablative agents, secret-
ed  by  hepatocytes  that  first  enter  the  perisinusoidal  space,
expose  the  LSECs  to  a  higher  concentration  than  other  en-
dothelial cells that line the central venules and hepatic veins
downstream.  This  toxin-mediated  injury  initiates  a  cascade
of events that lead to SOS.

Injured LSECs undergo a transition from their normal flat-
tened shape to a swollen and rounded form, creating spaces be-
tween them (Fig 3) (8). These widened intercellular spaces per-
mit an ingress of plasma contents into the perisinusoidal space,
subsequently expanding it and enabling the entry of platelets,

SOS after Myeloablative Conditioning
Myeloablative  conditioning  regimens  prepare  patients  for
HSCT.  Previously,  the  incidence  of  SOS  in  patients  receiving
HSCT was reported to be as high as 50%, depending on pa-
tient risk factors and the choice of conditioning regimen (7).
However,  with  the  adoption  of  reduced-intensity  condition-
ing regimens, the incidence of SOS has significantly declined
to  5%–15%  (10–12).  Clinically,  SOS  after  HSCT  manifests  as
weight  gain  (with  or  without  ascites),  right  upper  quadrant
pain from hepatomegaly, jaundice, and, in severe cases, mul-
tiorgan  failure  (8).  These  manifestations  typically  develop
within 1–3 weeks after HSCT (13).

The  diagnosis  is  clinical  with  several  diagnostic  criteria
existing, including the Seattle criteria (14), Baltimore criteria
(15), and European Society for Blood and Marrow Transplan-
tation (EBMT) criteria (16) (Table 2). SOS after myeloablative

conditioning is a life-threatening condition and is clinically
graded  as  mild,  moderate,  or  severe  based  on  the  necessi-
ty  for  treatment  and  probability  of  recovery.  Mild  SOS  has
a self-limiting course and therefore does not require treat-
ment of fluid excess, aside for pain management. Moderate
SOS  necessitates  treatment  of  fluid  excess  or  hepatic  pain
but resolves completely in most cases. Severe SOS is charac-
terized by disease that does not resolve by day 100 or results
in patient death; severe SOS is associated with a high mor-
tality rate of up to 80% (7,17).

Imaging serves to confirm the diagnostic criteria for SOS,
such as hepatomegaly (craniocaudal length >16 cm) (18) and
ascites  that  may  not  be  clinically  apparent  initially.  US,  CT,
and  MR  images  show  the  same  features  of  hepatomegaly,
periportal  edema,  ascites,  and  gallbladder  wall  thickening
(19,20).  Imaging  also  plays  a  crucial  role  in  excluding  other

SOS after Cytoreductive Chemotherapy
Oxaliplatin,  a  chemotherapeutic  agent  frequently  used  as
a  first-line  treatment  of  pancreatic  and  colon  cancer,  is  a
well-established  cause  of  SOS  (27).  Various  chemotherapy
regimens  incorporating  oxaliplatin,  performed  alone  or  in
combination with other agents, can potentially induce SOS.
The use of multiple chemotherapeutic agents or in combina-
tion with chemotherapy and radiation therapy increases the
likelihood of SOS compared with the use of any single agent
(28).

Patients with SOS after cytoreductive chemotherapy may
be  asymptomatic  or  have  mildly  elevated  serum  liver  en-
zymes  (29).  However,  imaging  indicates  characteristic  find-
ings even when SOS is subclinical. Manifestations are more
commonly observed at CT and MRI performed for treatment
assessment  of  primary  and  metastatic  tumors.  The  US  and
CT manifestations are similar to those of SOS from myeloab-
lative  regimens,  including  reduced  or  reversed  portal  flow
seen  on  Doppler  US  images  as  well  as  hepatomegaly  and
heterogeneous  hypoattenuation  with  patchy  enhancement
of parenchyma on CT images. (Fig 6) Splenomegaly or an in-
crease in the size of the spleen from the baseline is common
(Fig 7).

At MRI, subtle parenchymal signal intensity abnormalities
on  conventional  T2-weighted  images  and  diffusion-weight-
ed  images  and  heterogeneous  parenchymal  enhancement
on contrast-enhanced T1-weighted images may be noted (Fig
8).  At  hepatobiliary  phase  (HBP)  MRI,  areas  of  reticular  hy-
pointensities,  often  in  a  peripheral  distribution,  are  seen  in
patients with SOS (Figs 7, 8); this phase is often the only and/or

best sequence to demonstrate abnormal liver parenchyma. The
reticular hypointensity is 75% sensitive and 96%–100% specific
for SOS after cytoreductive chemotherapy (30). The finding of
reticular hypointensity is due to reduced organic anion trans-
porter protein (OATP1B3) expression that is normally found in
centrilobular hepatocytes and responsible for the uptake of ga-
doxetate (31).

The severity of imaging features is related to the number
of  cycles  and  duration  of  chemotherapy  (Fig  9).  Some  au-
thors  have  proposed  grading  the  severity  of  SOS  based  on
the extent of reticular hypointensities in the HBP (30,32,33).
Subjective grading characterized as definitely not present to
definitely present on a 5-point scale or as absent, mild, mod-
erate, or severe on a 4-point scale were used (30,32,33). Two
studies  indicated  excellent  interobserver  agreement  and  a
significant  association  of  elevated  serum  liver  enzymes  or
platelet  counts  with  grading  (30,32).  However,  one  study
found  poor  reproducibility  of  grading  and  that  the  overall
outcome did not correlate with the severity (33). More stud-
ies are needed to evaluate the grading of HBP findings in SOS
for diagnostic and prognostic purposes. The degree of sple-
nomegaly may prove to be useful for grading the severity of
SOS, as the increase in spleen size correlates with the cumu-
lative amount of oxaliplatin administered. In one study, an
increase of greater than or equal to 50% of spleen size from
the baseline correlated with severe hepatic sinusoidal injury
in 55% of livers at the time of resection (34).

MRI  is  the  preferred  imaging  modality  for  SOS  evalua-

cilitating  treatment  response  assessment  for  primary  and
metastatic  liver  tumors.  Quantitative  imaging  techniques
such  as  MR  elastography  and  T1  maps  are  emerging  for
SOS evaluation (36). Increased parenchymal stiffness at MR

Whole-body  radiation  therapy  is  associated  with  SOS.  With
newer  conformative  radiation  therapy,  focal  changes  of  SOS
are limited to the zone of radiation (Figs 12, 13) (49). Histologic
findings include central vein occlusion, dilated congested si-
nusoids filled with loose edematous stroma, and hepatocyte
atrophy (Fig 13) (50). The degree of sinusoidal congestion im-
proves over time after radiation therapy. As the changes are
focal and limited to the zone of radiation, these changes may
not cause clinically significant liver dysfunction (51).

SOS after Ingestion of Toxins
Exposure  to  hepatotoxic  herbal  medicines  and  health  prod-
ucts,  particularly  pyrrolizidine  alkaloids,  is  associated  with
SOS.  The  patient’s  clinical  presentation  is  nonspecific,  with

symptoms of fatigue, loss of appetite, and sometimes jaundice
(25). Patchy liver enhancement, ascites, and narrowing main
hepatic veins are the most frequent signs in herbal medicine–
induced SOS (53). The grade of patchy enhancement is associ-
ated with the clinical severity (53). Correlation with a history
of exposure to toxins is important, and sometimes liver biop-
sy is needed to establish the diagnosis.

Other Causes of Sinusoidal Dilatation

gestive heart failure, inferior vena cava obstruction, BCS, and
isolated hepatic vein obstruction due to thrombosis or a mass
that  compresses  the  hepatic  vein.  At  CT  and  MRI,  congestive

inferior vena cava, along with mosaic-pattern enhancement of
liver parenchyma (Fig 14). Reflux of intravenous contrast agent
into  the  hepatic  veins  may  also  be  seen  during  the  arterial
phase (54).

Sinusoidal Cellular Infiltration
Infiltration of sinusoids results from the increased accumu-
lation  of  systemically  circulating  benign  or  malignant  cells.
Benign causes include lymphocytosis secondary to infections
(eg, Epstein-Barr virus) or drug-induced granulomatous in-
flammation  and  eosinophilia  (3,67).  Langerhans  cell  histio-
cytosis  rarely  involves  sinusoids.  Malignant  causes  include
lymphoproliferative  disorders  such  as  hepatosplenic  T-cell
lymphoma, hairy cell leukemia, and megalokaryocytic leuke-
mia (Fig S3); myeloproliferative disorders (eg, mastocytosis)
(Fig S4); and metastatic disease (67).

Perisinusoidal Infiltration
Perisinusoidal fibrosis is the most common cause of perisinu-
soidal  infiltration.  The  perisinusoidal  fibrosis  is  due  to  acti-

vated HSCs and typically found first around the centrilobular
region  in  alcoholic  steatohepatitis  and  metabolic  dysfunc-
tion–associated steatotic liver disease, with progression of fi-
brosis to involve portal tracts in advanced stages. In contrast,
in methotrexate-induced injury, the early fibrosis tends to be
located  in  the  periportal  region  (67).  Perisinusoidal  fibrosis
also occurs in PSVD and diabetic hepatosclerosis (3).

Systemic amyloidosis involves the perisinusoidal space with
acellular  and  dense  deposition  of  eosinophilic  material  (3).
Amyloid  deposits  can  also  be  seen  in  the  portal  tract  stroma
and vessel walls and around nerves in the liver hilum. Amyloid
deposition can be diffuse and homogeneous, causing hepato-
megaly, or can be heterogeneous or even focal (Fig S6). At MR
elastography,  amyloidosis  typically  causes  markedly  elevated
liver stiffness that is often greater than 15 kPa in the affected
region of liver parenchyma (69).

Endothelial Cell Neoplasms
Neoplasms  that  arise  from  LSECs  include  benign  infantile
hemangiomas and malignant neoplasms such as epithelioid
hemangioendotheliomas  (EHEs)  (Fig  S7)  and  angiosarco-
mas (Fig S8) (72). The demographics and imaging features
of infantile hemangioma, EHE, and angiosarcoma are sum-
marized in Table 3. Infantile hemangioma is an uncommon
neoplasm found in infants and young children, with similar
enhancement imaging features as those seen in cavernous
hemangioma,  but  it  is  not  fluorodeoxyglucose  avid  on  PET
images,  unlike  EHE  and  angiosarcomas.  EHE  is  a  rare  ma-
lignant  tumor  in  adults  and  is  more  common  in  women,
whereas angiosarcomas are also rare but more common in
men (73,74). EHE shows growth along sinusoids beyond the
grossly  visible  tumor.  Angiosarcomas  may  rarely  exhibit  a
sinusoidal growth pattern that is difficult to detect at imag-
ing and histologic examination, as no discrete mass may be
found (75).

Approach to Sinusoidal Disorders
A  patient’s  clinical  history  is  of  utmost  importance  for  the
diagnosis  of  sinusoidal  disorders.  Patients  with  a  history  of
a myeloablative regimen, cytoreductive chemotherapy (often
used  in  colon  and  pancreatic  cancers),  radiation  therapy,  or
ingestion of pyrrolizidine toxins are at high risk for SOS. Im-
aging  findings  of  parenchymal  heterogeneity  at  CT  or  MRI,
periportal edema, hemodynamic changes in portal flow at US,
and findings of portal hypertension (splenomegaly and collat-
erals) should raise suspicion for SOS. MRI with hepatobiliary

gradient (HVPG). SOS and sinusoidal infiltration are common
indications  for  transjugular  hepatic  venography  and  biopsy,
which offer a definitive diagnosis (70). An HVPG greater than
10 mm Hg is considered diagnostic of SOS after HSCT (13,71).
However, a recent study indicated that approximately 40%
of patients with SOS had an HVPG less than 10 mm Hg and
recommended  that  HVPG  be  combined  with  a  liver  biopsy
(70). However, HVPG is not accurate in postsinusoidal diseas-
es, as wedged hepatic and free hepatic pressures are elevated.
As HVPG is an invasive technique and available only in cen-
ters with radiologists who have special expertise, it is indicat-
ed only in cases of suspected and unclear cause for SOS (19).

Reticular hypointensity seen at HBP MRI is characteristic
of SOS even when the disease is subclinical. HBP MRI is there-
fore  indicated  for  follow-up  imaging  after  chemotherapy,
particularly for patients who are undergoing liver resections.
HBP  MRI  is  useful  for  assessment  of  treatment  response  in
hepatic metastases and also for detection of subclinical SOS,
if present. The radiologic diagnosis of SOS is useful informa-
tion for surgeons to plan for surgery, consider a modification
or alternative chemotherapy regimen to prevent progression
of SOS, or wait until a patient recovers from severe SOS to re-
duce perioperative and postoperative complications.

In patients with myeloproliferative or lymphoproliferative
disorders  or  primary  malignancy  who  present  with  hepato-
megaly (with or without heterogeneous liver) and evidence of
disease elsewhere (splenomegaly, lymphadenopathy, or bone
metastases), suspicion should be raised for sinusoidal cellular
infiltration. In these patients, multiple discrete small hyper-
intensities or confluent hyperintensities on diffusion-weight-
ed images are suggestive of diffuse liver metastases.

Atypical clinical features or incidental imaging findings of
sinusoidal dilatation or diffuse hepatomegaly with or without
portal hypertension present diagnostic challenges. Transjugu-
lar hepatic venography and/or liver biopsy is required for the
confirmation of portal hypertension and the etiologic diagno-
sis. Endothelial cell neoplasms have variable imaging appear-
ances, and histologic confirmation is necessary.

A systematic approach to common causes of heterogeneous
or mosaic enhancement of liver parenchyma may be accom-
plished by reviewing other imaging features (Fig 23). Obstruc-

tive causes of sinusoidal dilatation should be excluded first.
If HBP MRI findings indicate reticular hypointensities, then a
diagnosis of SOS should be considered. When all obstructive
causes of sinusoidal dilatation are excluded, nonobstructive
causes should be considered. The etiologic diagnosis of non-
obstructive causes of SOS requires a correlation with clinical
features and supportive findings from laboratory tests.

Conclusion
Hepatic  sinusoids  are  involved  by  a  variety  of  pathologic
conditions,  with  sinusoidal  dilatation  being  the  common
manifestation. SOS is an important complication in patients
undergoing  oxaliplatin-based  chemotherapy  for  colorectal
and pancreatic malignancies. Imaging serves dual purposes:
detecting  SOS  and  demonstrating  the  patency  of  the  portal
vein, hepatic veins, and inferior vena cava. HBP MRI is par-
ticularly valuable in helping to identify SOS in asymptomatic
patients  undergoing  cytoreductive  chemotherapy.  Further-
more, a diagnosis of SOS determined from imaging findings
may  aid  in  predicting  perioperative  and  postoperative  out-
comes.  Sinusoidal  cellular  infiltration  by  malignancy  shows
similar  imaging  manifestations  to  those  of  SOS.  However,
diffusion-weighted imaging may be useful in showing small
metastases  throughout  the  liver.  Endothelial  cell  malignan-
cies are uncommon and require biopsy for confirmation.
