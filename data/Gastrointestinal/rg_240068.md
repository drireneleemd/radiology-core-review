Tumor biologic characteristics encompassing histopathologic, immune microenvironmental, genetic, and molecular aspects
are becoming indispensable factors to be considered in treatment strategies for patients with cancer. Innovations in oncologic
treatment have broadened the range of therapeutic approaches and now hold promise for treatments personalized according
to tumor biologic characteristics. Particularly for hepatobiliary and pancreatic neoplasms, the advent of cytostatic agents such
as molecularly targeted agents and immune checkpoint inhibitors, which differ markedly from conventional cytotoxic agents,
has contributed to advances in clinical practice. These cytostatic agents increase the potential for curative-intent treatment of
unresectable cancers by reducing tumor volume. Radiologic examinations are of more interest than ever to noninvasively obtain
information about tumor biologic features. Radiomics represents an invaluable research method for elucidating associations
between tumor biologic characteristics and radiologic imaging findings, but their applicability in daily clinical practice remains
challenging. Various radiologic predictive findings for tumor biologic characteristics have already been proposed for hepatobi-
liary and pancreatic neoplasms. Radiologists must gain familiarity with these findings and the roles they have in predicting the
clinical prognosis and treatment efficacy. In addition, radiologists should explore the potential applications of these imaging
findings to current treatment strategies for the coming era of personalized medicine. The authors describe predictive findings
using CT and MRI for diagnosis of hepatocellular carcinoma, colorectal liver metastases, intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma, and
pancreatic adenocarcinoma, with correlations to pathologic, immunologic, molecular, and genetic background factors.

Introduction
Treatment  strategies  for  patients  with  cancer  have  become
more  diverse,  with  continuing  advances  in  curative-intent
and  systemic  therapies.  In  particular,  molecularly  targeted
agents  and  immune  checkpoint  inhibitors  have  been  game
changers in oncologic treatment. Conversion surgery, which is
defined as changing the status of a tumor from unresectable
to resectable using systemic therapies, is becoming an estab-
lished  treatment  for  a  variety  of  abdominal  malignancies.
Furthermore, the combination of curative-intent and system-
ic therapies has been explored to achieve better clinical out-

comes for patients with unresectable malignancies (1). How-
ever, even patients with the same clinical stage of abdominal
malignancy do not always respond to a therapy in the same
manner because each malignancy has diverse biologic prop-
erties. As oncologic treatment strategies continue to progress,
prognostic  stratification  based  on  tumor  biologic  character-
istics is becoming more and more important for maximizing
the clinical benefit to patients with cancer.

properties, including genetic, molecular, histopathologic, and

Standard and Advanced Imaging Methods to
Evaluate Tumor Biologic Characteristics
Multiphasic contrast-enhanced CT and MRI have been widely
used as standard imaging modalities to evaluate tumor bio-
logic  characteristics.  In  addition  to  qualitative  assessments,
quantitative  assessments  of  imaging  findings  (eg,  apparent
diffusion  coefficients  [ADCs])  have  been  correlated  with  tu-
mor  biologic  characteristics  to  identify  more  objective  fea-
tures. Advances in current imaging modalities such as perfu-
sion CT, dual-energy CT, and MR elastography have provided
new insights into tumor biologic characteristics. For example,
MR  elastography  enables  quantitative  measurements  of  tu-
mor stiffness and a more objective assessment of microvascu-
lar invasion in HCC (3).

Hepatocellular Carcinoma

Expectations for Predictive Imaging based on Cur-
rent Treatment Strategies
Unlike other abdominal malignancies, HCC can be diagnosed
on the basis of imaging criteria, with high accuracy and with-
out  biopsy.  Although  HCC  has  many  treatment  options  de-
pending on clinical staging, molecular, genetic, and histologic
factors can influence treatment efficacy and clinical outcomes
in patients with HCC who are treated with each therapeutic
option (4). In addition to conventional staging systems such
as  TNM  staging,  pretreatment  prediction  of  tumor  biologic
characteristics may contribute to better treatment in patients
with HCC.

The landscape of HCC treatment has changed dramatical-
ly,  with  advances  in  the  use  of  immune  checkpoint  inhibi-
tors.  Atezolizumab  plus  bevacizumab  and  durvalumab  plus
tremelimumab  have  demonstrated  marked  efficacy  as  first-
line  treatments  for  unresectable  HCC,  surpassing  the  clini-
cal outcomes of the use of sorafenib (5,6). Atezolizumab plus
bevacizumab  is  also  recommended  as  a  first-line  treatment
for some groups of intermediate HCCs, for which transcath-
eter arterial chemoembolization was previously the standard
of care treatment (7). However, objective treatment response
remains  low,  at  approximately  30%,  even  for  this  promising
first-line treatment. Authors of a recent article (8) have advo-
cated the immunologic classification of HCC, linking genom-
ic,  molecular,  and  pathologic  features.  Radiologic  prediction

Pathologic and Genetic Molecular Classifications
The current integrated classification system based on histo-
logic, molecular, and genetic mutation characteristics divides
HCCs into two classes: proliferative and nonproliferative (9).
Histologically,  the  proliferative  class  includes  poorly  differ-
entiated tumors, whereas the nonproliferative class includes
well-differentiated tumors. Scirrhous, macrotrabecular-mas-
sive,  and  progenitor  subtypes  are  classified  as  proliferative.
The  proliferative  class,  genetically  characterized  by  chro-
mosomal instability and TP53 mutations, is subdivided into
G1–G3  molecular  subclasses,  whereas  the  nonproliferative
class, genetically characterized by chromosomal stability and
CTNNB1 gene mutations, is subdivided into G4–G6 subclass-
es.  Vascular  invasion  is  frequently  observed  in  proliferative
HCCs, with a poor clinical prognosis.

Predictive Imaging and Relevant Biologic Background

Morphologic Appearance
Tumor size in patients with HCC is well known to be relevant
to clinical prognosis and has been incorporated into various
staging systems. Tumor size is associated with the presence
of  vascular  invasion,  which  is  frequently  observed  in  HCCs
measuring  larger  than  5  cm  in  diameter  as  evaluated  with
MRI (10). Intratumoral bleeding is also often observed in large
HCCs.  A  2020  study  (11)  showed  correlations  between  mor-
phologic and genetic aspects, indicating that the ratio of the
long to short diameter of the HCC (cutoff, 1.2) may be a predic-
tor of TP53 mutation status. Regarding prognostic indications,
MRI criteria based on tumor size and vascular invasion have
been reported (12) as useful for predicting disease-free sur-
vival after resection. Intratumoral bleeding is also a predictor
of recurrence after resection (13).

An enhancing capsule is defined as a smooth, sharp border
around a tumor depicted during the portal venous, delayed, or
transitional phase, reflecting a fibrous capsule or pseudocap-
sule at pathologic examination. Capsular continuity is related
to tumor aggressiveness and has important implications for
clinical prognosis. In HCCs larger than 5 cm in diameter, com-
plete encapsulation is significantly associated with favorable
overall survival after locoregional therapy compared with in-
complete or no encapsulation (14).

Signal Intensity Decrease
Intratumoral fat or steatosis is evaluated as a decrease in signal
intensity at chemical shift imaging. A signal intensity decrease
at  chemical  shift  imaging  is  frequently  observed  in  patients
with steatohepatitic HCC, a histologic World Health Organiza-
tion  classification  system  subtype  of  HCC  (15).  The  histologic
distribution of fat deposits in the tumor changes from diffuse
to focal as the tumor increases in size (16). In a previous report
(17), most HCCs with diffuse fat deposition were found to rep-
resent well-differentiated HCC. Fatty changes in HCC, caused
by  insufficient  arterial  blood  supply,  occur  most  frequently

at tumor diameters of 1.1–1.5 cm, suggesting this as a charac-
teristic of early-stage HCC. Intratumoral fat is associated with
better  clinicopathologic  features  in  patients  with  HCC,  with
the exception of one report (17), the results of which showed fat
deposition in poorly differentiated HCCs due to the hypoxic en-
vironment associated with decreased arterial blood flow. A re-
cent study (18) provided insights into the relationship between
the  immune  microenvironment  and  tumor  steatosis.  Tumor
steatosis in a patient with HCC is associated with high expres-
sion of PD-L1 protein, which may lead to an immunosuppres-
sive tumor microenvironment.

HCCs showing a signal intensity decrease on chemical shift
images, reflecting favorable tumor histologic characteristics,
have  a  longer  time  to  tumor  progression,  particularly  after
resection, percutaneous ablation, or transarterial chemoem-
bolization (19). Intratumoral fat detected with chemical shift
imaging  in  patients  with  HCC  also  represents  an  important
prognostic  factor  for  progression-free  survival  after  therapy
with  atezolizumab  plus  bevacizumab  (18).  The  fat  fraction
(FF), defined as:

FF = SIIP– SIOP(2 × SIIP) × 100,
where SI is signal intensity, IP is in phase, and OP is out of
phase,  is  a  quantitative  value  that  reflects  intratumoral  fat.
HCC with a cutoff fat fraction value greater than 10% reflects
a favorable prognosis after chemotherapy with atezolizumab
plus  bevacizumab  (Fig  1).  A  PD-L1  inhibitor,  in  the  form  of
atezolizumab, as a first-line agent for an unresectable HCC,
binds to the PD-L1 ligand on tumor cells, thereby activating
CD8  T  cells  without  exhaustion,  resulting  in  antitumor  ef-
fects.

Apparent Diffusion Coefficients
ADC is a quantitative value obtained from diffusion-weighted
MRI and is calculated through placement of a region of inter-
est on the tumor. Various ADC calculations have been report-
ed, including mean, minimum, and volumetric ADC. ADC is
basically lower in malignant tumors than in benign tumors,
reflecting histopathologic features such as cellularity. Patho-
logically, ADC is significantly associated with tumor grade and
microvascular invasion in HCCs (20). ADC is an independent
factor for tumor recurrence after resection, percutaneous ab-
lation, and transplants (21,22).

revealed that high expression of epithelial cell adhesion mol-
ecule was an indicator of unfavorable clinical prognosis after
percutaneous ablation or resection of HCC. CK19-positive HCC

Dilated Vasculature during the Arterial Phase.—Dilated vas-
culature  during  the  arterial  phase  of  contrast-enhanced  CT
is a reported concept in imaging. The dilated vasculature is
defined  as  a  thick-branched  structure,  with  a  maximum  di-
ameter  exceeding  2  mm,  and  correlates  significantly  with
P53-mutated HCC (28). P53 is a protein coded by the TP53 gene

and  is  relevant  to  aggressive  histologic  phenotypes  such  as
the macrotrabecular-massive pattern and microvascular in-
vasion and poor clinical prognosis in patients with HCC (Fig
3). In vitro studies have shown that lenvatinib and cabozan-
tinib provide better efficacy against moderately to poorly dif-
ferentiated liver cancer cells with mutated P53 (29). This im-
aging finding might be an indicator relevant to the selection
of systemic therapy for HCC.

Hyperintensity  in  the  HBP.—Hepatic  tumors  show  hyperin-
tensity in the HBP due to intrinsic hyperintensity at precon-
trast T1-weighted MRI (eg, steatosis, hemorrhage) and the up-
take of gadoxetic acid disodium into tumor cells via OATP1B3.
The  definition  of  hyperintensity  remains  contentious.  As  a
quantitative  indicator  of  tumor  signal  intensity,  the  relative
enhancement ratio (RER) is defined as:

RER = SINOD-post/SIPAR-post ÷ SINOD-pre/SIPAR-pre,
where NOD is nodular, PAR is parenchyma, pre is precontrast,
post is postcontrast, and SI is signal intensity.

Determination of the relative enhancement ratio is a well-
known  method  of  assessing  tumor  signal  intensity  in  HCC,
with a previous report (33) revealing a significant correlation
between an relative enhancement ratio greater than or equal
to 0.9 in the HBP and HCC. Hyperintense HCC in the HBP has

been pathologically surmised to reflect a less-aggressive type
of  HCC,  specifically  characterized  by  better  differentiation,  a
greater frequency of a pseudoglandular proliferation pattern,
formation of a bile plug, and a lower rate of microvascular in-
vasion (34). Wnt/β-catenin activation in HCCs causes overex-
pression of OATP1B3, leading to hyperintensity in the HBP and
reduced infiltration of CD8+ T cells into tumor tissues (35).

Hyperintensity  in  the  HBP  has  been  reported  as  a  good
prognostic predictor of locoregional therapy, associated with
better recurrence-free survival and overall survival after sur-
gical resection and a lower recurrence rate after transarterial
chemoembolization (34,36) (Figs 6, 7). Some anticancer drugs
such as cisplatin are taken up in cells by means of this same
uptake  transporter  (37).  Hyperintensity  in  the  HBP  for  HCC
may thus reflect increased intracellular uptake of anticancer
drugs and a good response to transarterial chemoemboliza-
tion.  Meanwhile,  hyperintensity  in  the  HBP,  referred  to  as
“immune exclusion,” “noninflamed phenotype,” or “cold tu-
mor,” is a predictor of a poor prognosis and is associated with
short progression-free survival after immunotherapy (Fig 8)
(38).

Prognostic Values of LI-RADS
LI-RADS is a standardized diagnostic algorithm used with CT
and MRI that was proposed by the American College of Radiol-
ogy for patients at risk for HCC. A growing trend is being seen
for the evaluation of associations between the LI-RADS score
and clinical prognosis in relation to tumor biologic character-
istics (45,46). The LR-M category, associated with aggressive
histologic  features,  is  an  independent  predictor  of  overall
survival  and  recurrence-free  survival  after  resection.  Fur-
thermore,  the  LR-M  category  consists  of  targetoid  and  non-
targetoid features, and the nontargetoid features (ie, an infil-
trative appearance, necrosis or severe ischemia, and marked
diffusion restriction) are more important than the targetoid
features in predicting clinical prognosis (46).

Clinical  trials  (58,59)  have  shown  promise  regarding  the  in-
troduction  of  immunotherapy  and  molecularly  targeted
agents into clinical practice. Favorable results were reported
from the TOPAZ-1 trial (58) for the combination of cytotoxic
agents and immunotherapy in patients with locally advanced
or  metastatic  disease.  The  PROOF  301  trial  (59)  comparing
first-line  treatment  with  infiglatinib  (ie,  a  fibroblast  growth
factor receptor inhibitor) versus cytotoxic agents in advanced
or  metastatic  cholangiocarcinoma  with  FGFR2  mutations  is
ongoing.  Understanding  the  tumor  biologic  characteristics
will  be  even  more  important  for  improving  the  efficacy  of
these treatment options.

ICC is histopathologically classified into perihilar and pe-
ripheral types that have different genetic backgrounds. While
isocitrate  dehydrogenase  mutations  and  FGFR2  transloca-
tion  are  associated  with  peripheral-type  ICCs,  KRAS  genetic
mutations are frequently related to perihilar-type ICCs (60).
Predictive images reflecting pathologic and genetic factors in
ICCs  could  contribute  to  the  identification  of  optimal  treat-
ment strategies.

Predictive Imaging and Relevant Biologic Back-
ground

Volumetric ADC.—The volumetric ADC reflects restriction of
diffusion in the entire tumor as measured with imaging soft-

ware. Volumetric ADC is more indicative of intratumoral het-
erogeneity such as necrosis, hemorrhage, and tumor cellular-
ity, than is conventional ADC (61).

Volumetric  ADC  before  treatment  has  been  identified  as
an independent predictor of overall survival in unresectable
ICCs  treated  with  systemic  chemotherapy  and  transarterial
chemoembolization  (61,62).  Tumors  showing  extensive  ne-
crosis,  which  is  associated  with  a  high  volumetric  ADC,  dis-
play a poor response to chemotherapy and intra-arterial ther-
apy.

Arterial  Hyperenhancement.—Arterial  enhancement  pat-
terns are mainly classified into rim enhancement and hyper-
enhancement  patterns  (Fig  16).  The  arterial  enhancement
pattern  reflects  pathologic  classification.  ICCs  with  arterial
hyperenhancement  are  more  commonly  of  the  peripheral
type than of the perihilar type. Peripheral-type ICCs are as-
sociated with a lower frequency of lymphatic metastases and
perineural  invasion,  which  are  findings  consistent  with  the
previously reported radiologic aspects (63).

The  arterial  enhancement  patterns  are  associated  with
the clinical prognosis in ICCs. With both resectable and un-
resectable ICCs, patients with tumors showing arterial hyper-
enhancement achieve better overall survival and disease-free
survival  than  those  with  ICCs  showing  a  typical  hypoen-
hancement type (63,64).

Delayed  Enhancement  at  Contrast-enhanced  CT.—Delayed
enhancement  pathologically  corresponds  to  dense  fibrous
stroma  with  scattered  neoplastic  cells.  ICCs  with  abundant
fibrous  stroma,  known  as  the  “scirrhous  type,”  are  known
to display a worse prognosis than “nonscirrhous-type” ICCs.
Scirrhous-type ICCs have more frequent perineural invasion
and a higher MIB-1 index. ICCs with visual enhancement in
more than two-thirds of the tumor during the delayed phase
show poor overall survival after resection (Fig 17) (65).

Pancreatic Ductal Adenocarcinoma

Expectations for Predictive Imaging based on Cur-
rent Treatment Strategies
Surgery  is  the  only  potentially  curative  treatment  of  PDAC.
However, even for patients who have undergone surgery, the
5-year survival rate is unsatisfactory due to local recurrence
and distant metastases. On the other hand, in recent years, fa-
vorable outcomes for patients with PDAC have been reported,
with no recurrence for greater than 5 years after surgery (67).
The latest National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN)

guideline proposes two treatment strategies (ie, upfront sur-
gery or neoadjuvant chemotherapy followed by surgery) and
incorporates  recommendations  for  treatment  of  patients
with high-risk resectable PDAC. Neoadjuvant chemotherapy
is recommended for patients with high-risk resectable PDAC,
although criteria to identify populations at high risk are not
explicitly defined (68). A system of risk stratification is needed
to incorporate biologic factors, along with the assessment of
resectability based on images that refer to the anatomic loca-
tions of the tumor and vessels.

Predictive Imaging and Relevant Biologic Background

Isoattenuation  during  the  Arterial  and  Portal  Venous
Phases.—PDACs  showing  isoattenuation  to  the  background
pancreatic  parenchyma  in  the  arterial  and  portal  venous
phases  of  contrast-enhanced  CT  account  for  5.5%–14%  of
PDACs (69). Dual-energy CT may improve the accuracy of di-
agnosing  isoattenuating  PDACs.  Monoenergetic  images  and
iodine maps obtained from dual-energy CT can more clearly
depict isoattenuating PDAC than conventional CT images do
(70).

Pathologically,  isoattenuating  PDACs  contain  lower  per-

brous stroma in the tumor. Lymphovascular invasion is less
frequently observed in isoattenuating PDACs. A high rate of
occurrence is also seen for isoattenuating PDACs in well-dif-
ferentiated  and  small  (less  than  2  cm  in  diameter)  PDACs.
This imaging finding is associated with better overall survival
after  curative-intent  surgery  than  those  of  conventional  hy-

Conclusion
Predictive imaging features for hepatobiliary and pancreatic
neoplasms reflect the background biologic characteristics of
the tumor and are relevant to clinical benefits. Imaging phe-
notypes  based  on  predictive  images  may  hold  independent
prognostic value and merit incorporation into subclassifica-
tions along with pathologic, molecular, and genetic categories.
Predictive  imaging  features  associated  with  important  and
still-unknown biologic factors as revealed by developments in
treatment strategies should be further explored in the future.
In addition, the features reported to date have been obtained
from cohorts including different stages of hepatobiliary and
pancreatic neoplasms. Tumor stage must be noted when con-
sidering the utility of these features.
