Lymphatic abnormalities encompass a wide range of disorders
spanning solitary common cystic lymphatic malformations (LMs)
to entities involving multiple organ systems such as lymphangioleio-
myomatosis. Many of these disorders are rare, yet some, such as
secondary lymphedema from the treatment of malignancy (radia-
tion therapy and/or lymph node dissection), affect millions of pa-
tients worldwide. Owing to complex and variable anatomy, the lym-
phatics are not as well understood as other organ systems. Further
complicating this is the variability in the description of lymphatic
disease processes and their nomenclature in the medical literature.
In recent years, medical imaging has begun to facilitate a deeper
understanding of the physiology and pathologic processes that in-
volve the lymphatic system. Radiology is playing an important and
growing role in the diagnosis and treatment of many lymphatic
conditions. The authors describe both normal and common variant
lymphatic anatomy. Various imaging modalities including nuclear
medicine lymphoscintigraphy, conventional lymphangiography, and
MR lymphangiography used in the diagnosis and treatment of lym-
phatic disorders are highlighted. The authors discuss imaging many
of the common and uncommon lymphatic disorders, including
primary LMs described by the International Society for the Study
of Vascular Anomalies 2018 classification system (microcystic,
mixed, and macrocystic LMs; primary lymphedema). Secondary
central lymphatic disorders are also detailed, including secondary
lymphedema and chylous leaks, as well as lymphatic disorders not
otherwise easily classified. The authors aim to provide the reader
with an overview of the anatomy, pathology, imaging findings, and
treatment of a wide variety of lymphatic conditions.

Introduction
The lymphatic system performs multiple important functions in the
human body, including maintenance of fluid balance, absorption of
fat from the digestive tract, and immune surveillance for infection
and cancer. The lymphatics extend throughout the body, ultimately
connecting to the central venous system. Yet, despite the critical
importance and extensive presence throughout the body, lymphat-
ics are often overlooked on many imaging examinations owing to
small vessel size and variable anatomy, and the methods for imaging
lymphatic tracts and lymphatic disease processes remain underde-
veloped. With the emergence of new imaging techniques, including
MR lymphangiography, as well as a renewed interest in lymphatic
treatment approaches, our understanding of the radiologic appear-
ances of this system and the pathologic processes that may affect this
system is growing (1–3).

some of the major conditions outlined in the

Anomalies (ISSVA) 2018 classification system
(4). Additional pathologic processes not included

Lymphatic Physiology and Anatomy
Lymphatic vessels allow fluid balance, absorption
of digested fats, and immune surveillance. Small
lymphatic capillaries join to form lymphatic
vessels that drain into filtering lymph nodes and
then into central lymphatics, ultimately draining
into the venous system.

The central lymphatic anatomy is highly vari-
able. In general, lymphatics from the pelvis and
lower extremities along with abdominal lymphatics
from the bowel and liver drain into the cisterna
chyli (Fig 1). Chyle (a milky odorless fluid) is a
mixture of chylomicrons (absorbed from the intes-
tines) and clear lymph from the pelvis and lower
extremities. The cisterna chyli is located behind the
right diaphragmatic crus at the T12–L2 level (Fig
2) and gives rise to the thoracic duct. It is often
identifiable at cross-sectional imaging as an ovoid
structure with fluid attenuation or signal intensity
and may be mistaken for a lymph node, especially
when large. The characteristic location, fluid at-
tenuation or signal intensity, and connection to a
tubular structure superiorly (thoracic duct) can
help differentiate it from a pathologic finding.

The thoracic duct is the largest lymphatic

channel in the body, collecting lymph from
everywhere in the body except the right upper
hemithorax, right upper extremity, and right side
of the neck. Most commonly, the thoracic duct
enters the thorax through the aortic hiatus and is
located between the azygos vein and aorta. Once
in the thorax, it crosses to the left at the T5–T7
level and then ascends to the level of C7. It finally
drains into the venous system, posterior to the left
internal jugular vein at its junction with the left
subclavian vein, commonly referred to as the left
venous angle (Fig 3). However, the anatomy of the
thoracic duct is highly variable, with a single trunk
reported in only 16% of cadavers (5). Multiple
additional anatomic variants have been described
(Fig 3B) (6). In patients with congenital heart
disease, who may have abnormal situs, anatomic
variability in the appearance of the thoracic duct
and cisterna chyli is more common (7).

A smaller right lymphatic duct drains the right
hemithorax, right upper extremity, and right side of
the neck. It most commonly begins with the union
of the right jugular, right subclavian, and right
bronchomediastinal ducts, but again, this anatomy
is highly variable (8). Mirroring the thoracic duct,
the right lymphatic duct typically joins the venous
system at the confluence of the right subclavian
vein and right internal jugular vein (Fig 3).

In the lungs, lymphatics are present in the
interlobular septa and subpleura and along the
bronchovascular structures (9). Disease processes
that affect the lymphatics can therefore lead to
thickening of the interlobular septa and peribron-
chovascular interstitium. From here, lymphatics
coalesce and drain to the hila and then into the
mediastinal lymphatics (Fig 4). In comparison,

Imaging Evaluation of Lymphatic Flow
Abnormalities

Nuclear Medicine: Lymphoscintigraphy
Lymphoscintigraphy can be performed to help
detect abnormal lymphatic flow. In this proce-
dure, a technetium-99m–labeled probe, often sul-
fur colloid, is injected in the subcutaneous tissues
between the toes of both lower extremities. This
radiotracer is taken up by the lymphatics, and
serial images are acquired to assess the progres-
sion of the radiotracer in the lymphatic system by

using a γ camera (11). Abnormal movement of
radiotracer can be seen in a variety of conditions
including lymphedema, chyle leak, etc (Fig 5).
This technique is similar to sentinel lymph node
mapping except that the injection site in sentinel
node mapping is near the cancerous lesion.

Conventional Lymphangiography
In one common method to perform conventional
lymphangiography, the interventional radiologist
first performs intranodal or pedal lymphangiog-
raphy (usually with ethiodized oil) to help iden-
tify the cisterna chyli. Oil-based contrast agents
are viscous and radiopaque and allow effective
targeting of the cisterna chyli. When performed for
assessment of chyle leak, this procedure is often
therapeutic as well as diagnostic; ethiodized oil
frequently leads to an intralymphatic inflammatory
reaction, which may by itself result in chyle leak
cessation (12). A drawback to the use of an oil-
based contrast agent for lymphangiography is the

who undergo lymphangiography will usually have

although this is not clinically significant in low
doses. For this reason, high volumes of oil-based
contrast agents should be avoided in patients, es-

such as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease

hence this procedure is relatively contraindicated
in this scenario (14).

Conventional liver lymphangiography can be

performed by positioning a needle under US
guidance in the periportal space of a branch of
the left or right portal vein. Opacification of the
liver lymphatics can then be performed by the
injection of a water-soluble contrast agent (1,15).
A similar technique has also been used to opacify
the mesenteric lymphatics by transabdominally
placing a needle adjacent to an anterior superior
mesenteric vein or mesenteric lymph node under
US guidance (2).

MRI Techniques
Both the peripheral and central lymphatics can
be imaged by using MRI. Two basic sequences
are used: a heavily T2-weighted acquisition and
precontrast and dynamic postcontrast breath-
hold three-dimensional (3D) fat-saturated T1-
weighted gradient-echo (GRE) acquisitions after
the administration of a gadolinium-based contrast
agent (GBCA). Generally, the heavily T2-weighted
sequence covers the anatomy from the cervical
to inguinal regions. This allows the depiction of
larger central lymphatics and generalized edema
(dermal, mesenteric, and mediastinal) and the
localization and severity of lymphedema, ascites,
and pleural effusion. This sequence also helps in
the identification of cystic lymphatic malforma-
tions (LMs). It has been shown that T2-weighted
imaging can be used to map lymphatic abnormali-
ties to help predict outcomes and hospital stays in
single-ventricle palliation (16). However, smaller
lymphatics within organs and in the extremities
frequently cannot be resolved with this sequence
(17). Furthermore, T2-weighted imaging does
not provide information relating to lymphatic
flow and cannot localize regions of active chyle
leak; for such information, dynamic postcontrast
T1-weighted images must be obtained.

Lymphatic Disorders
There is a wide spectrum of lymphatic disorders
ranging from congenital malformations to other
processes leading to lymph and/or chyle accumu-
lation or lymphatic flow abnormalities. These are
summarized in Table 3. The ISSVA classifies the
various LMs into common cystic LM, general-
ized lymphatic anomaly (GLA), Gorham-Stout
disease (GSD), channel-type LM (also known as
central conducting lymphatic anomaly), acquired
progressive lymphatic anomaly, and primary
lymphedema (Table 4) (4). We also discuss some
lymphatic diseases not classified in this system (eg,
lymphangioleiomyomatosis [LAM]) and various
other conditions such as chyle leaks and impaired
lymphatic flow that can manifest as chylothorax,
chylopericardium, chylous ascites, chyluria, plastic
bronchitis, and secondary lymphedema.

When fluid collection in body cavities is identi-

fied, its chylous nature is diagnosed on the basis
of fluid analysis results demonstrating triglyceride
levels greater than 110 mg/dL or by the pres-
ence of chylomicrons. To differentiate a chylous
effusion from a pseudochylothorax or cholesterol
effusion, a cholesterol level may be obtained with
chylous effusions having a ratio of cholesterol to
triglycerides of less than 1. The loss of chyle from
the lymphatic system results in electrolyte altera-
tions, nutritional deficiency, and immune sup-
pression. Therefore, identification and treatment
of the underlying lymphatic abnormality is crucial
(25). Given the heterogeneous conditions that can
lead to chylous fluid accumulation, the precise
workup and imaging manifestations depend on
the underlying disorder and clinical context, for
example trauma, malignancy, or multisystem
involvement in GLA and GSD. Imaging allows
the identification of the leak or obstruction lead-
ing to the extralymphatic accumulation of chy-
lous fluid and assessment of anatomic lymphatic
variations for pretreatment planning, especially
when considering interventional approaches
like embolization or stent placement. Likewise,

Lymphatic Malformations

Common Cystic LMs
The common cystic LMs in the 2018 ISSVA
classification system are subdivided into macro-
cystic, microcystic, and mixed types (4). These
lesions are defined as low-flow vascular malfor-
mations due to abnormal development of lym-
phatic channels (26). They do not communicate
with normal lymphatics and typically manifest in
childhood. Often these lesions are transspatial,
crossing through anatomic planes, and are best
evaluated by using cross-sectional imaging (CT
and MRI) that can provide a 3D perspective of
the extent of these lesions.

the mylohyoid and contains cysts larger than 1–2

GSD, also known as vanishing bone disease,

compared with GLA (39). The underlying cause

Channel-type LM
The diagnosis of channel-type LM (also known as
central conducting lymphatic anomaly) is typically
made when there is dysfunction or obstruction of
lymphatic flow in the thoracic duct or cisterna chyli
(41). Because of the impaired lymphatic drainage,
patients are diagnosed with chylothorax, chylous
ascites, PLE, and distention of the lymphatic struc-
tures, as well as swelling due to lymph seepage (Fig
12). At dynamic lymphatic imaging, impaired cen-
tral lymphatic flow as well as lymphatic reflux can
be seen (42). Distinction of channel-type LM from
other lymphatic anomalies is difficult owing to the
variability in presentation and overlap in clinical
manifestations of these conditions (35).

Acquired Progressive Lymphangioma
Case reports of acquired progressive lymphan-
gioma have most commonly been reported in
younger patients who present with a slow-growing
erthymatous plaque, typically in an extremity (43).
This skin manifestation is due to the proliferation
of lymphatics in the dermis.

Chyle leaks can be due to disruption or occlu-

Pulmonary Lymphatic Perfusion Syndrome
Pulmonary lymphatic perfusion syndrome oc-
curs when abnormal lymphatic flow develops
in the lungs or pleural space. This can result in
the development of a chylothorax and/or plastic
bronchitis. While chylothorax is diagnosed by the
results of pleural fluid analysis, plastic bronchitis
is diagnosed by the removal (either by self-ex-
pectoration or bronchoscopy) of branching casts
made of cross-linked mucin (51). This condition
has been described in congenital heart disease,
specifically in patients with a single ventricle after
Fontan palliation, where flow reversal is due to
increased central venous pressures secondary to
Fontan physiology (52). MR lymphangiography
can be performed to demonstrate the abnormal
lymphatic flow (Fig 15). To review, in the normal
state lymphatic flow occurs from the lungs to the
hilum and then into the central veins. However,
when there is impaired flow, whether that be
functional or anatomic impedance to flow, this
pattern of flow reverses. Specifically, flow goes
from the hilum into the lungs (Fig 16), which can
be seen at dynamic imaging of the lymphatics.

Protein-losing Enteropathy
PLE can be another manifestation of impaired
lymphatic function due to either gastrointestinal
causes (eg, primary intestinal lymphangiecta-
sia) or cardiac causes leading to elevated central

venous pressure (such as in patients with conges-
tive cardiac failure) (53). This is a well-described
although uncommon complication of the Fon-
tan procedure and contributes to an increase
in mortality and morbidity in these patients
(54). Patients present with chronic diarrhea and
peripheral edema, which are due to the loss of
proteins through the duodenum through abnor-
mal hepatoduodenal lymphatic communications
(55,56). The abnormal lymphatic flow can be
demonstrated at MR lymphangiography by the
progression of lymphatically injected contrast
agents into the bowel, which is best observed
during dynamic imaging to assess contrast agent
movement during peristalsis (Fig 17).

Secondary Lymphedema
Surgical or radiation therapy–related chronic
inflammation and scarring in an extremity leads
to disruption of lymphatic channels and sub-
sequently to abnormalities in lymph drainage.
Secondary lymphedema is an under-recognized
disease with an estimated 200 million people
worldwide with this condition (44). In the United
States, lymph node dissection and radiation
therapy, used for the treatment of malignancy,
is the most common cause of lymphedema. The
skin may ulcerate and recurrent infections can
occur. Traditionally, first-line approaches for
treatment included skin care, manual lymphatic

Lymphangioleiomyomatosis
Some lymphatic disorders are not covered in any
of the categories previously discussed. One of
these (LAM) is discussed in this section.

There are a variety of manifestations of LAM,

including pulmonary cysts and renal angiomyo-
lipomas. Two forms exist, sporadic and tuber-
ous sclerosis complex (TSC)–associated forms,
both of which are due to mutations in one of
the tuberous sclerosis genes (TSC1 and TSC2);
a mutation in the TSC2 gene is the most com-
mon (60). The condition occurs predominately
in women. The mutation allows the proliferation
of LAM cells, which are considered a low-grade
metastasizing neoplasm that spreads via the lym-
phatics (61). Diagnostic guidelines outline that in
addition to the characteristic findings of thin uni-
formly distributed pulmonary cysts, an additional
one or more feature(s) are required to make the

diagnosis, including (a) an underlying diagnosis
of TSC, (b) angiomyolipomas, (c) chylous effu-
sion, (d) elevated vascular endothelial growth fac-
tor (VEGF)–D level greater than or equal to 800
pg/mL, and/or (e) lymphangioleiomyomas (Fig
19) (62). Clinical practice guidelines recommend
treatment with sirolimus if there is declining or
abnormal lung function (63).

Therapeutics
Interventional radiology plays an integral part
in the treatment of both acquired and inherited
lymphatic abnormalities. Several approaches
may be used depending on the clinical scenario
and underlying pathologic condition, including
selective embolization, percutaneous or cath-
eter-based sclerosis, or more novel techniques
such as thoracic duct stent placement.

Thoracic Duct Embolization
Thoracic duct embolization is an effective
minimally invasive procedure for treatment of
chylothorax and other chyle leaks. Although
most cases of chyle leak occur in the setting of
trauma or surgery, this technique has also shown
efficacy in idiopathic nontraumatic causes where
a leak might not be identified at lymphangiogra-
phy. Thoracic duct embolization has a reported
success rate greater than 60% for effective tho-
racic duct embolization, with a low risk of major
complication (<2%) (64). Multiple techniques
have been described to obtain access to the tho-
racic duct (65).

Thoracic Duct Stent Placement
There is renewed interest in the use of stent
placement for treatment of chylous effusions
and other disorders that lead to chyle accumula-

tion. Case reports and small series of patients
have been described in whom stent grafts have
been placed in the thoracic duct at a site of
impaired drainage or leakage (74). Through the
use of stent grafts rather than embolization, it is
hypothesized that continued patency of the cen-
tral lymphatics can avoid delayed complications
including lymphedema or PLE.

Conclusion
The complex anatomic and physiologic role of
the lymphatic system continues to be an area
of evolving knowledge. Imaging plays a central
role in improving our understanding of this
