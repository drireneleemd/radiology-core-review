Patients often have symptoms due to the mass
effect of a neoplasm on surrounding tissues or
the development of distant metastases. However,
some patients may present with clinical symptoms
that are not attributable to direct tumor invasion.
In particular, certain tumors may release sub-
stances such as hormones or cytokines or trigger
an immune cross-reactivity between malignant
and normal body cells, resulting in characteristic
clinical features that are broadly referred to as
paraneoplastic syndromes (PNSs). Recent advanc-
es in medicine have improved the understanding
of the pathogenesis of PNSs and enhanced their
diagnosis and treatment. It is estimated that 8% of
patients with cancer develop a PNS. Diverse organ
systems may be involved, most notably the neuro-
logic, musculoskeletal, endocrinologic, dermato-
logic, gastrointestinal, and cardiovascular systems.
Knowledge of various PNSs is necessary, as these
syndromes may precede tumor development, com-
plicate the patient’s clinical presentation, indicate
tumor prognosis, or be mistaken for metastatic
spread. Radiologists should be familiar with the
clinical presentations of common PNSs and the
selection of appropriate imaging examinations.
Many of these PNSs have imaging features that
can assist with arriving at the correct diagnosis.
Therefore, the key radiographic findings associ-
ated with these PNSs and the diagnostic pitfalls
that can be encountered during imaging are
important, as their detection can facilitate early
identification of the underlying tumor, reveal
early recurrence, and enable monitoring of the
patient’s response to therapy.

Introduction
Paraneoplastic  syndromes  (PNSs)  are  a  constellation  of  dis-
ease  manifestations  that  are  caused  by  an  underlying  neo-
plasm. However, they are not directly related to invasion by a
primary or metastatic tumor (1). Immunologic or biochemical
tumor-derived substances are often identified as a cause, al-
though in many cases, the true causative factor may remain
unknown. PNSs are thought to affect up to 8% of patients with
cancer (2), and this prevalence will likely increase as cancer
survival durations become longer (1).

In  many  malignant  neoplasms,  PNSs  may  manifest  before
a cancer diagnosis. Therefore, the timely recognition of a PNS
may  allow  early  detection  and  treatment  of  a  previously  un-
suspected  tumor,  leading  to  improved  clinical  outcomes  (1).
In  some  patients,  PNSs  are  the  initial  sign  of  an  underlying
tumor,  prompting  cancer  screening  and  further  workup  (1).
PNSs may also serve as the first sign of disease recurrence or
progression. Thus, they can be used to monitor the evolution
of  the  underlying  tumor  (2).  PNSs  should  be  included  in  the
differential diagnosis in patients with constitutional symptoms
(eg, weight loss), advanced age, subacute presentation, and pro-
gressive deterioration that is not explained by other causes or is
not responsive to treatment (3,4), particularly if associated with
other neuropathies (eg, somatic sensory neuropathy) (5), with
evidence of known cancer risk factors (eg, smoking), or in the
presence of supportive laboratory findings (eg, tumor markers
and antibodies commonly associated with tumors) (6).

PNSs  may  involve  diverse  organ  systems,  including  but
not limited to the neurologic, musculoskeletal, dermatologic,
endocrinologic, gastrointestinal, and cardiovascular systems.

Radiologists should be aware of the commonly encountered
PNSs, familiar with their imaging features, and experienced
in selecting the appropriate imaging examinations for patients
with PNS (7). We discuss the imaging features of PNSs, asso-
ciated causative malignant neoplasms (Table 1), and common
diagnostic pearls and pitfalls that can help the radiologist to
suggest a PNS diagnosis. We also review the European Feder-
ation of Neurological Societies’ imaging recommendations for
patients with PNS and review the imaging strategy for investi-
gation in patients with unknown primary tumors (8).

Neurologic PNSs
In  contrast  to  some  other  PNSs  (eg,  endocrinologic),  neuro-
logic  PNSs  develop  before  cancer  detection  in  80%  of  cases
(1,9).  Tumor-directed  antibodies  (ie,  onconeural  antibodies)
are produced in response to a growing cancer and can be clas-
sified into two groups: those with a strong cancer association,
such  as  anti-Hu  (antineuronal  nuclear  antibody  [ANNA]
type 1), anti-CV2 (CRMP5), and antiamphiphysin, and those
with partial association to a specific cancer, such as anti-me-
tabotropic  glutamate  receptor  (mGluR)  1  and  ANNA  type  3
(ANNA-3) (1). Onconeural antibodies and their common as-
sociated PNSs are summarized in Table 2.

Detection of onconeural antibodies in cases of a possible
PNS  strongly  indicates  an  underlying  malignant  neoplasm
and helps in diagnosing a neurologic condition as a true PNS.
However, the absence of antibodies in the presence of a neu-
rologic  syndrome  should  not  be  a  reason  to  stop  searching
for  an  underlying  tumor,  as  some  antibodies  might  not  be
detected. If a tumor is identified but is not known to be asso-
ciated with a PNS or onconeural antibodies, a search for an
additional tumor should be performed (4).

Screening  imaging  examinations  are  usually  indicated
when a PNS is detected without an identifiable tumor (10). To
detect  an  underlying  tumor,  CT  of  the  chest,  abdomen,  and
pelvis should be performed, followed by fluorodeoxyglucose
(FDG)  PET  or  PET/CT  if  the  initial  CT  examination  yields
negative results. Neurologic PNSs can affect the central and
peripheral  nervous  systems,  as  well  as  neuromuscular  junc-
tions, as described in the following sections.

PNSs of the Central Nervous System

Encephalitis.—Limbic  encephalitis  (LE)  is  a  PNS  that  usually
manifests  with  seizures,  short-term  memory  loss,  confusion,
and  psychologic  dysfunctions  that  rapidly  progress  over  3
months (4). The most common underlying tumor is small cell
lung  cancer  (SCLC),  which  is  found  in  an  average  of  55%  of
patients  with  LE  and  is  commonly  associated  with  anti-Hu
antibodies (4). LE has also been reported to develop in up to
13%  of  patients  with  testicular  tumors  with  the  presence  of
anti-Ta  antibodies  and  5%  of  patients  with  breast  cancer  (7).
Anti–N-methyl-D-aspartate  (NMDA)–receptor  antibodies  are
found in LE in young women between 20 and 50 years of age
and  are  strongly  associated  with  tumors  (59%),  particularly
with mature and immature ovarian teratomas (Fig E1) (11,12).

Cerebellar  Degeneration.—Paraneoplastic  cerebellar  degener-
ation (PCD) is one of the most devastating examples of a re-

mote  neurologic  complication  of  a  tumor  (18).  Most  patients
develop a rapidly progressive course limiting daily living activi-
ties within 3 months (4). Gait ataxia is usually the initial symp-
tom, followed by truncal and limb involvement. PCD may also
include ataxic dysarthria or diplopia (18). Neurologic impair-
ments are bilateral and often show asymmetry (19).

Early  in  the  disease  course,  brain  MRI  findings  are  often
unremarkable. However, cerebellar edema has been reported
in  some  cases.  In  the  late  stages,  cerebellar  atrophy  corre-
sponding to permanent neural loss can be seen (18,20). Signal
intensity abnormalities in the brain stem may be noted when
PCD coexists with another paraneoplastic disorder (eg, brain
stem encephalitis or LE) (18).

PCD  is  a  rare  finding  that  mimics  other  causes  of  cere-
bellar  degeneration,  such  as  alcoholic  cerebellar  degenera-
tion, infectious or postinfectious cerebellitis, antineoplastic
medications (particularly phenytoin), and ataxia associated
with anti–glutamic acid decarboxylase (22). Alcoholic ataxia
shows vermis atrophy on brain images. In addition, speech
and ocular motility are usually preserved, while symptoms
of peripheral neuropathy and imaging findings of posterior
column degeneration are commonly seen (23). Unlike PCD,
ataxia associated with anti–glutamic acid decarboxylase has
a  slower  progression  and  is  associated  with  polyendocrine
dysfunction  (eg,  pernicious  anemia,  thyroiditis,  or  insu-
lin-dependent diabetes mellitus) (22).

Opsoclonus-Myoclonus  Syndrome.—Opsoclonus-myoclonus
syndrome is characterized by saccadic eye movement with ac-
tion myoclonus of the trunk, limbs, and head (4). It is closely
associated  with  neuroblastoma  among  children,  while  it  is
more  commonly  associated  with  SCLC  or  breast  cancer  in
adults (14). Most cases have no onconeural antibodies. How-
ever,  patients  with  underlying  breast  cancer  and  opsoclo-
nus-myoclonus syndrome may have anti-Ri antibodies (7).

Brain  MRI  findings  are  usually  normal.  Nevertheless,  in
chronic  cases,  cerebellar  atrophy  with  pontine  tegmental  ab-
normalities has been described (24). According to the European
Federation of Neurological Societies Task Force guidelines, im-
aging with chest and abdominopelvic CT is recommended to
identify  pulmonary,  gynecologic,  and  other  visceral  primary
malignant  neoplasms  (7,14).  Furthermore,  mammography  is
indicated in women with opsoclonus-myoclonus syndrome. In
children, whole-body scintigraphy with radiolabeled metaiodo-
benzylguanidine may be used to identify an occult neuroblas-
toma when conventional imaging results are negative (25).

Paraneoplastic Myelitis.—Paraneoplastic myelitis is a rare en-
tity and may occur in isolation or with involvement of other
areas  (eg,  encephalomyelitis)  (26).  Paraneoplastic  myelitis
symptoms  include  flaccid  or  spastic  tetraparesis  or  parapa-
resis,  bowel  or  bladder  dysfunction,  and  sensory  loss  (27).
Patients  may  develop  cerebral  symptoms  (eg,  headache,  sei-
zures) in the case of associated intracranial involvement (26).
Paraneoplastic  myelitis  demonstrates  longitudinally  exten-
sive hyperintensity at T2-weighted imaging and enhancement
at contrast-enhanced T1-weighted imaging, which makes MRI
of the entire spine particularly helpful (Fig 5) (26). Lateral col-
umn  involvement  is  a  characteristic  finding,  best  seen  in  the
axial plane (26). Abnormal FDG uptake in the involved spinal
cord  area  has  also  been  described  and  could  be  mistaken  for
tumor metastasis (28). Imaging of the brain is usually indicated
to identify associated encephalitis, which is commonly seen in
patients with positive results for anti-CRMP5 antibodies (26).

Patients with paraneoplastic myelitis frequently have an un-
derlying SCLC or breast cancer, and the most common associ-
ated antibodies are anti-Hu, anti-CRMP5, and anti-Ri (27). Ce-
rebrospinal fluid analysis may confirm elevated protein levels,
pleocytosis, and oligoclonal bands. Despite treatment, most pa-
tients have a poor outcome and require the use of a wheelchair.
Differential considerations for paraneoplastic myelitis are
broad and include transverse myelitis, radiation myelitis, spi-
nal  dural  arteriovenous  fistula,  spinal  cord  infarct,  and  de-
myelinating disease, such as neuromyelitis optica or multiple
sclerosis. Evidence of an enlarged vessel with a vascular flow

PNSs of the Peripheral Nervous System
The  peripheral  nervous  system  is  the  initial  site  of  involve-
ment in one-third of patients diagnosed with PNS (29). Sub-
acute  sensory  neuropathy  is  the  most  common  pattern  of
paraneoplastic neuropathy and may occur along with motor

weakness or autonomic involvement (29). In other cases, only
symptoms of autonomic neuropathy are noted (30). Patients
with subacute sensory neuropathy present with either pain-
ful dysesthesia and mechanical hyperalgesia or sensory ataxia
and loss of proprioceptive sensation.

Paraneoplastic cranial neuropathy is rare but has been de-
scribed in several reports. Symptoms depend on the affected
nerve,  such  as  balance  disorder  and  sensorineural  hearing
loss involving the cochlear nerve. Moreover, involvement of
multiple cranial nerves has rarely been reported (31).

PNSs of the Neuromuscular Junction

Myasthenia  Gravis.—Patients  with  myasthenia  gravis  report
ocular and oropharyngeal symptoms that progress to proximal
limb weakness and are commonly associated with thymoma.
Chest CT is advised in patients with myasthenia gravis, espe-
cially  those  with  positive  findings  for  antiacetylcholine  and
ryanodine  receptor  autoantibodies.  Thymoma  and  thymic
hyperplasia are both associated with myasthenia gravis. Even
patients with hyperplasia may undergo thymectomy (33).

Eosinophilic Myocarditis
Eosinophilic  myocarditis  is  a  rare  type  of  myocardial  in-
flammation  characterized  by  eosinophilic  infiltration  of  the
cardiac  muscles  that  may  result  in  cardiogenic  shock  (35).

eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis, or it can be a

Venous Thromboembolism
Venous  thromboembolism  in  the  absence  of  a  conventional
risk factor is a significant indicator of an occult tumor (42). It
has notably been observed with tumors of the pancreas, brain,
and ovary and with hepatocellular carcinoma. Physical exam-
ination, blood tests, and chest radiography have been shown
to be helpful in identifying most cancers in patients with ve-
nous thromboembolism. Other extensive screening by using
US and CT is not cost-effective and not justified except in the
presence  of  symptoms  concerning  for  an  underlying  malig-
nancy (eg, change in bowel habits and weight loss) (7,42).

Trousseau Syndrome
Trousseau syndrome is a recurrent cancer-induced migratory
venous thrombosis. This condition is characterized by a com-
bination  of  migratory  thrombophlebitis  that  causes  painful
erythematous rash and blood hypercoagulability that leads to
development of venous and arterial thromboses. An underly-
ing malignant neoplasm is diagnosed in 85%–95% of patients
with Trousseau syndrome (43). Mucin-secreting adenocarci-
nomas are the most common malignant neoplasms associated
with  Trousseau  syndrome,  including  those  of  the  pancreas
(50%), lung, stomach, prostate, and colon (44). Batsis et al (43)
suggested the use of PET to identify occult tumors in patients
with  Trousseau  syndrome  because  it  has  higher  sensitivity
and specificity than those of conventional imaging and could

cost less than extensive conventional investigations (such as
multiple  CT  examinations,  gastrointestinal  endoscopy,  and
biopsy). Stroke in the setting of a three-territory involvement
pattern at diffusion-weighted MRI (ie, three-territory sign) is
highly specific for Trousseau syndrome and is six times more
frequently observed in malignancy-associated stroke than in
atrial fibrillation–associated stroke (45,46).

Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone
Secretion
Syndrome  of  inappropriate  antidiuretic  hormone  secretion
(SIADH) is characterized by euvolemic hyponatremia and oc-
curs in 1%–2% of all patients with malignant tumors (48). The
symptoms of SIADH depend on the rapidity of onset and the
degree of hyponatremia, which may result in weakness, head-
ache, and memory difficulties. In severe cases, patients may
develop seizures, coma, and respiratory collapse (52).

Approximately  10%–45%  of  patients  with  SCLC  develop
SIADH (Fig 11) (53). A wide variety of other tumors have also
been found in conjunction with SIADH, including head and
neck, breast, and prostate tumors (48). Benign conditions un-
derlying  SIADH  include  intrathoracic  infection  and  central
nervous system disorders.

In  patients  with  biochemically  confirmed  unexplained
SIADH, chest radiography and CT are essential for identify-
ing both malignant and nonmalignant pulmonary causes. If
chest imaging examinations yield negative results, depending
on  clinical  presentation,  neuroimaging  may  be  indicated  to
identify a benign intracranial cause (7,54).

Hypercalcemia
Humoral hypercalcemia of malignancy (HHM) occurs in up
to  10%  of  all  patients  with  advanced  tumors  and  generally
signals  a  poor  prognosis  (1).  Hypercalcemia  manifestations
include  nausea,  vomiting,  confusion,  polyuria,  renal  failure,
and coma (55). Symptom severity is determined by the degree
of hypercalcemia, rapidity of onset, and the patient’s baseline

thyroidism should first be excluded.

Dermatomyositis
Dermatomyositis  is  inflammatory  proximal  myopathy  with
multiple skin changes (60). Approximately 10%–25% of cases
are  paraneoplastic,  with  the  commonly  associated  tumors
being breast, lung, ovarian, and prostate cancers (1). For an
acute presentation of dermatomyositis, early investigation of
an underlying malignant neoplasm with use of chest and ab-
dominopelvic CT or mammography and breast US is justified.
However,  extensive  screening  is  not  warranted  in  patients
with long-standing dermatomyositis (7).

Radiographs  of  dermatomyositis  show  increased  superfi-
cial skin radiopacity (calcinosis cutis) as well as increased ra-
diopacity around joints that encounter frequent microtrauma,
such as the elbows (calcinosis circumscripta) or deep fascial

planes along the musculature (calcinosis universalis) (Fig 14).
Patterns of calcinosis described are clumplike, sheetlike, and
reticular calcifications (61). In the chronic phase, characteris-
tic calcifications appear in 25%–50% of cases, which may lead
to  localized  muscle  atrophy  (62).  MRI  is  the  best  modality
for detecting soft-tissue abnormality (eg, active myositis and
subcutis inflammation) with use of coronal and axial short τ
inversion-recovery or T2-weighted sequences, especially with
fat suppression. In contrast, CT is less accurate than MRI for
the evaluation of muscle inflammation but is more sensitive
than MRI in calcinosis detection (62).

Palmar Fasciitis and Polyarthritis
Palmar  fasciitis  and  polyarthritis  (PFPA)  syndrome  usually
occurs in women and is characterized by a nonerosive polyar-
thritis of the metacarpophalangeal and interphalangeal joints
(66).  Additional  features  consist  of  rapid  inflammation  and
fibrosis of the palmar fascia leading to contracture of the hand
(66). PFPA is usually associated with an underlying malignant
tumor, such as that of the breast, fallopian tube, ovary, or en-
dometrium.  Prostate,  stomach,  and  pancreatic  cancers  are
other possibilities (3).

Imaging  findings  include  narrowing  of  a  joint  compart-
ment without erosions, as well as flexion deformities of the
hand  or  fingers  (Fig  16).  Unlike  Dupuytren  disease,  PFPA
has a rapid progression and a higher severity of hand con-
tracture  (67).  The  noticeable  induration  of  subcutaneous
tissues  seen  in  PFPA  has  resulted  in  the  descriptive  term

“woody  hands”  (67).  Scleroderma  may  also  mimic  PFPA.
However, patients with PFPA lack evidence of sclerodactyly,
have  rapid  development  of  symptoms  and  hand  disability,
and  less  often  have  multiple  additional  affected  joints  (eg,
knees and ankles) (67).

Porphyria Cutanea Tarda
Porphyria cutanea tarda is characterized by bullae and vesi-
cles that form in response to minor trauma or light sensitiv-
ity. Baravelli et al (70) showed that patients with porphyria
cutanea  tarda  have  a  20-fold  increased  risk  of  hepatocel-
lular  carcinoma  and  a  sevenfold  increased  risk  of  biliary
tract  and  gallbladder  cancer.  Patients  with  porphyria  cu-
tanea tarda should undergo screening with abdominal US
and serum α-fetoprotein testing. A suspected hepatic lesion
can  be  further  evaluated  with  a  dedicated  CECT  or  MRI
examination (71).

Role of PET/CT in PNSs
Patients  with  suspected  PNSs  may  have  an  underlying  ear-
ly-stage cancer that is difficult to detect at conventional imag-
ing (72). PET/CT has shown high sensitivity and negative pre-
dictive value and may serve as a whole-body screening tool for
the  underlying  tumors,  particularly  in  patients  with  negative
conventional  imaging  findings  (73).  Pena  Pardo  et  al  (6)  sug-
gested a clinical pretest risk stratification system to select can-
didates with PNSs to undergo PET. Meanwhile, Vaidyanathan
et al (74) concluded that a low clinical score does not exclude an
underlying tumor, and PET findings can be concordant with,
upgrade, or downgrade the clinical risk scoring. Nevertheless,
the proportion of new malignant neoplasms identified at PET
increases as the clinical risk of PNSs increases (6,74).

Although a few studies showed that PET could be used to
identify  tumors  in  PNS  settings  that  were  missed  at  conven-
tional imaging (74,75), not enough data are present to evaluate
the additional screening benefit of PET over conventional mo-
dalities and investigate its cost-effective value (72,73). Similarly,
more research is needed to follow up on patients with negative

Conclusion
PNSs are a heterogeneous set of conditions that indicate the
presence or precede the development of a tumor. They are rel-
atively rare disorders that require exclusion of more common
conditions.  Knowledge  of  the  spectrum  of  the  underlying
tumors of PNSs substantially helps tailor diagnostic imaging
for patients. Radiologists should be aware of imaging findings
and  potential  diagnostic  pitfalls  of  PNSs,  as  this  can  enable
timely diagnosis of underlying tumors and positively impact
clinical outcomes for these patients.
