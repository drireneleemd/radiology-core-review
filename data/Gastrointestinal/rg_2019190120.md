Diagnostic imaging after orthotopic liver transplant focuses primar-
ily on depicting complications related to surgical hepatic vascular
and biliary anastomoses. Less common preexisting vascular condi-
tions include congenital anatomic variants, atherosclerosis, chronic
portal venous thrombosis, splenic artery and variceal steal phenom-
ena, and transarterial embolization (TAE) for hepatocellular car-
cinoma (HCC). If unappreciated or left untreated preoperatively,
these conditions negatively impact the transplant by impairing
hepatic arterial or portal vascular inflow. Many of the complications
related to preexisting vascular conditions can be prevented or miti-
gated by proper performance and careful evaluation of preoperative
imaging studies. The authors describe the diagnosis and treatment
of complications arising from narrowing of the celiac axis by ath-
erosclerosis and the median arcuate ligament, variant anatomy of
the hepatic artery, insufficiency of the portal vein requiring surgical
conduits, and large varices or an enlarged splenic artery and spleen
that may steal blood and compromise hepatic arterial or venous in-
flow. While preoperative evaluation primarily involves CT and MRI,
postoperative diagnosis involves screening with sonography and
confirmation with other modalities. We propose the use of a pre-
operative checklist of vascular status and measurements in patients
undergoing liver transplant. Reports of imaging studies in recipients
after transplant should include details of surgical vascular anasto-
moses and conduits, any history of HCC and preoperative TAE, de-
tails of the preoperative α-fetoprotein levels, and any unusual pro-
cedures or pathologic findings in the explanted liver that may affect
postoperative surveillance. The authors review the pretransplant
imaging evaluation of vascular and HCC issues that may affect sur-
gical outcomes and methods to help recognize complications after
transplant that can arise from these conditions.

Introduction
The liver is the second most commonly transplanted organ after
the kidneys in the United States. According to the United Network
for Organ Sharing (UNOS), 163 450 liver transplants were per-
formed between January 1988 and November 2018 (1). At least
6800 liver transplants are performed each year. In 2016, 7841 liver
transplants were performed, and 95% of these transplants involved
whole liver grafts from deceased donors. Also in 2016, 14 046 candi-
dates were on the waiting list for a liver transplant (2), indicating an
ongoing undersupply of available organs.

Despite the trend of more severe disease
in recipients, as evidenced by higher MELD
(Model for End-Stage Liver Disease) scores at
the time of transplant, the 1-year survival rate
has steadily increased, and it now ranges between
86.9% and 93.8%, with the 3-year survival rate
between 78.2% and 90.4% (3). While improve-
ment in choice of recipients, surgical technique,
and perioperative care has had a major effect on
these outcomes, imaging plays a crucial role for
these patients. Optimal diagnostic imaging and
interpretation are crucial before and after surgery
to depict, characterize, and help treat vascular
complications to help maintain the allograft.

Postoperative imaging of the orthotopic trans-
planted liver, performed immediately after surgery
and at follow-up, emphasizes the depiction of
complications related to the surgical anastomoses,
particularly stenosis and thrombosis of the hepatic
artery and portal vein.

Several vascular complications due to underly-
ing nonanastomotic vascular issues are less com-
mon and less well appreciated. Hepatic arterial
inflow may be compromised by narrowing at the
celiac axis or by competition for arterial flow by
a large splenic artery secondary to splenomegaly,
resulting in splenic artery steal syndrome (SASS).
Preexisting varices that do not involute can com-
pete for portal flow and cause a hemodynamically
significant steal and ultimately result in portal vein
compromise and thrombosis.

Variant anatomy in the recipient hepatic artery

and preexisting portal vein insufficiency due

to partial or complete thrombosis may require
nonstandard surgical approaches such as arterial
and portal conduits or complex arterial anasto-
moses. While the surgeon will decide on the types
and variations of anastomoses on the basis of the
size and integrity of the vessels at the time of the
procedure, foreknowledge of potential problems
aids preoperative planning. These surgical tech-
niques can result in complications and unusual
findings at US, and other imaging such as CT
angiography and conventional angiography may
be required for complete evaluation.

Other vascular issues, particularly varices,
may be in blind spots that are not in the routine
surgical purview. If these vascular issues become
symptomatic after surgery, diagnostic imaging and
possibly interventional treatment are required.

Last, a variety of issues may arise related to he-

patocellular carcinoma (HCC) in the explanted
liver. Various reports describe the increased fre-
quency of arterial complications such as hepatic
artery stenosis in patients treated preoperatively
with transarterial embolization (TAE), which
comprises chemotherapy, radiotherapy, and bland
particulate therapy. Surveillance imaging for
recurrent tumor is also required in these patients.
Awareness of the histologic findings in the ex-
plant regarding the extent of residual tumor and
any vascular invasion should increase the level of
suspicion for recurrent tumor when unusual find-
ings are seen at imaging.

This article describes our imaging approach to

this group of unusual liver transplant complica-
tions with an emphasis on Doppler US as an initial
screening study after transplant complemented by
CT, MRI, and conventional angiography. Exam-
ples of catheter-directed treatments are included.
We describe an optimal preoperative screening ap-
proach and propose a checklist to help in surgical
planning (Table).

Vascular Anastomoses
and Doppler US

Orthotopic liver transplant requires multiple
anastomoses (Fig 1). The hepatic artery anasto-
mosis is made between the donor celiac axis or
the branch point of the donor common hepatic
artery and the splenic artery to the recipient
proper hepatic artery. The donor and recipient
portal veins are joined in an end-to-end ap-
proach. Most commonly, a “piggyback” anas-
tomosis is created from the donor hepatic veins
to the recipient suprahepatic inferior vena cava
(IVC). The bile duct is fashioned by an end-to-
end anastomosis after cholecystectomy unless
a Roux-en-Y hepaticojejunostomy is necessary,
such as in patients with underlying primary scle-
rosing cholangitis.

US is used for immediate postoperative imaging

of these anastomoses and frequently for subse-
quent surveillance of the liver. Thus, familiarity
with the normal appearance of the hepatic vessels
at spectral Doppler US is essential. Our imag-
ing template includes routine gray-scale imaging
of the liver and spleen and color and spectral
Doppler US of the hepatic arteries, portal veins,
hepatic veins, IVC, splenic vein, and splenic artery
with angle-corrected velocities and arterial resis-
tive indexes (RIs) (Fig 2).

Hepatic arterial imaging includes the proper
hepatic artery at the porta hepatis and the central
intrahepatic right and left branches. The hepatic
artery waveform should have a prompt systolic
upstroke and an RI ranging from 0.55 to 0.8 (4).
While imaging of the arterial anastomosis and
celiac axis is desirable, these regions are often ob-
scured by gas, especially in the period immediately
after surgery.

Spectral Doppler US of the splenic artery at
the splenic hilum is always included in posttrans-
plant evaluation for comparison with the hepatic
artery. It also serves as an internal control, since
the arterial waveforms and RIs may be affected by
systemic hemodynamics. Portal imaging includes
the main portal vein, the right and left intrahepatic
portal vein branches, and the splenic vein. The
intrahepatic IVC and the three main hepatic veins
are also interrogated (Fig 2).

The Doppler US findings seen in the complica-
tions described are often subtle and nonspecific and
may evolve over time. Thus, the radiologist must
be aware of these possibilities and maintain a high
index of suspicion for them. Review of preoperative
imaging studies is crucial, with appropriate recom-
mendations for dedicated diagnostic vascular imag-
ing and—potentially—interventional treatment.

Arterial Inflow

Celiac Artery Stenosis
Unlike the native liver, the liver graft has no col-
lateral arterial supply immediately after transplant
and relies completely on inflow from the celiac
artery. Thus, celiac artery stenosis can result in
hepatic arterial insufficiency in the graft and is a
risk factor for hepatic arterial thrombosis.

Two causes of celiac artery stenosis are (a) ath -

erosclerotic stenosis or (b) compression by the
median arcuate ligament (MAL). These entities
are differentiated by the location and appearance
of the narrowing of the celiac axis at sagittal CT or
MRI. Atherosclerotic narrowing from calcified or
noncalcified plaque appears at imaging as an ostial
narrowing without a fishhook configuration (Fig
3a). Compression by the MAL is characterized
by focal narrowing of the proximal celiac axis 5

The MAL is a fibrous arch located superior

imaging is recommended (8) (Fig 4).

CT or MRI performed at end expiration can

suggest MAL narrowing of the celiac artery,
which may be confirmed at Doppler US (Fig 5)
and catheter angiography (Fig 6). In the set-
ting of a normal celiac axis at end inspiration,
identification of collateral arterial vessels and
poststenotic dilatation of the celiac axis can help
decrease the number of false-negative diagnoses.
Doppler US findings include a greater than two-
fold increase of peak systolic velocity in the celiac
artery compared with the aorta or a peak systolic
velocity of greater than 200 cm/sec with varia-
tion during respiration (9). In addition, real-time
imaging can help confirm a more acute angle
between the celiac axis and the aorta at expira-
tion (Fig 5b).

Before undergoing transplant, patients with

celiac artery stenosis are often asymptomatic

because of a rich collateral blood supply, includ-
ing retrograde flow from the superior mesenteric
artery through the pancreaticoduodenal system.
After transplant, the celiac artery provides the
only arterial inflow to the liver because of the
division of the collateral arteries, including the
gastroduodenal artery.

In patients with celiac axis narrowing caused
by the MAL during expiration, hepatic arterial
blood flow can be compromised over the major-
ity of the respiratory cycle, given that expiration
comprises two-thirds of the respiratory cycle. Of
164 consecutive liver transplants performed at
one center, 10% of patients were symptomatic of
MAL compression of the celiac axis intraopera-
tively after the hepatic arterial construction when
there was decreased blood flow through the he-
patic artery depicted during expiration. Hepatic
arterial blood flow improved in 15 patients after
surgical transection of the MAL, whereas two pa-
tients required an aortohepatic artery graft (10).
Intraoperative evaluation of the hepatic artery
during liver transplant with clinical palpation or
a blood flow meter can help elucidate a celiac
axis narrowing. After the hepatic arterial anasto-
mosis is created and after ligation or temporary
interruption of the gastroduodenal artery (which
ceases flow from collateral arteries), if the hepatic
artery blood flow decreases during expiration,
this suggests compression of the celiac axis by the
MAL (11,12). Surgical division of the MAL can
improve blood flow to the hepatic artery, or an
aortohepatic conduit can be created.

Splenic Artery Steal Syndrome
SASS is a poorly understood and still controversial
cause of allograft ischemia in which hepatic arte-
rial hypoperfusion occurs with shunting of blood
to the splenic artery. An alternative theory is that
increased portal venous flow causes hepatic arte-
rial hypoperfusion on the basis of the hepatic arte-
rial buffer response, which washes out adenosine,
a vasodilator, resulting in hepatic arterial vasocon-
striction. The result in either case is hepatic arte-
rial hypoperfusion. The reported incidence after
liver transplant is 3%–8% (13). In a study of 44
patients with SASS, the syndrome usually mani-
fested within the first 3 months after transplant,
with a range of 3 weeks to 5.5 years (14).

The clinical findings of transaminitis are
nonspecific, and liver biopsy, if performed,
reveals inflammation rather than rejection.
Doppler US findings of SASS are also non-
specific, consisting of high-resistance hepatic
arterial waveforms with an RI greater than 0.8
(Fig 9) and often low peak systolic velocities
of less than 35 cm/sec. Portal velocities may be
elevated secondary to portal hyperperfusion.
Frequently, it is more difficult to visualize the
hepatic artery at color Doppler US, especially
the intrahepatic branches.

Portal Inflow

Preexisting Insufficiency
Nontumoral portal vein thrombosis is frequent
in patients with cirrhosis, with a prevalence as
high as 25% in patients before transplant, and is
frequently undiagnosed until surgery. While US
is a good screening study, particularly in the main

portal vein and central intrahepatic portal veins,
the mesenteric vessels are often obscured by gas.
CT and MRI provide a more complete delineation
of the integrity of the portal system and help dif-
ferentiate tumor in vein, bland venous thrombus,
and chronic portal vein thrombosis and depict the
size and location of collateral vessels (20,21).

tion therapy or a transjugular intrahepatic porto-
systemic shunt (TIPS), can result in portal vein

Varices and Shunts
In cirrhosis, resistance to portal venous flow
increases over time as normal hepatic sinusoidal
flow is disrupted by the patient’s underlying liver
disease. As a means of decompression, collateral

vessels develop between the high-pressure portal
and low-pressure systemic venous circulation.
This collateral circulation takes various forms,
including esophageal varices, gastric varices,
splenorenal shunts, and portocaval shunts.

Typically, these abnormal connections involute

after liver transplant as the recipient’s diseased
high-resistance hepatic sinusoidal circulation is
replaced with the donor liver’s low-resistance he-
patic sinusoids, and normal portal venous blood
flow patterns are restored. Occasionally, varices
do not involute after transplant and continue
to divert portal flow from the liver, resulting in
persistent slow hepatopetal or hepatofugal flow,
which in turn can lead to graft dysfunction or
failure. This state of portal hypoperfusion is com-
monly referred to as portal steal. Treatment in-
cludes endovascular or open surgical techniques
such as embolization or ligation of the collateral
vessels (Figs 12, 13).

Native Liver with HCC: Posttransplant
Surveillance, Recurrence, and
Complications

Several well-known factors have been identified
as increasing the risk of recurrent HCC after liver
transplant, including micro- and macrovascular
invasion, tumors greater than 5 cm, higher tumor
grades, disease in both lobes, and a multiplicity of
lesions. UNOS data show a lower 5-year survival
rate of 48.2% for patients with HCC compared
with 74.7% in those without (26). Although criteria
in selecting which patients with HCC are transplant
candidates have become stricter, the inaccuracy of
preoperative staging is well known, with the discor-
dance rate not significantly changed since the early
1990s despite advances in imaging techniques.
A recent study of 318 patients who under-
went liver transplant for HCC reported a 22%
discordance between preoperative imaging and
results of histologic examination of the explant

Proposed Pretransplant Imaging
Imaging before orthotopic liver transplant must
allow the radiologist to address vascular and
HCC-related issues. We feel that optimal imaging
consists of contrast-enhanced multiphasic CT or
MRI with arterial and portal venous phases.

The arterial phase images should depict the
hepatic and splenic arteries and demonstrate size
and any anatomic variations. Particular attention
should be given to the celiac axis with respect to
any intrinsic stenosis, anatomic variation, or the
presence of narrowing because of an MAL, and
the phase of respiration should be considered.

Portal phase imaging should delineate the size

and patency of the main portal vein and portal
confluence and depict acute or chronic throm-
bus, cavernous transformation, and location of a
TIPS, if present. The location and size of varices
must be described. Finally, one should include
information regarding any underlying HCC, both
treated and viable, and if the treatment included
TAE. When information regarding residual HCC
in the explant becomes available, it is useful to
include such information with any postoperative
surveillance imaging.

Conclusion

Advances in medical and surgical care of pa-
tients undergoing orthotopic liver transplant
have significantly improved patient outcomes.
Diagnostic and therapeutic imaging also have a
significant role in treatment of these patients. A
small subset of these patients will have recipient
vascular issues related to arterial inflow or portal
insufficiency and varices that have the potential
to compromise the transplant.

Preoperative imaging has a significant role in
helping predict these potential problems, which
include the following: narrowing of the celiac
axis from atherosclerotic disease or an MAL and
anatomic variations of the hepatic artery that may
require extra anastomoses or arterial conduits;
splenic artery enlargement and splenomegaly that
can cause SASS; and large varices that have the
potential to steal portal flow from the liver.

After transplant, the radiologist must appreci-
ate imaging findings that suggest the possibility

of one of these complications. Since US is typi-
cally used as a screening study, subtle changes in
hepatic artery waveforms and RIs, increasing or
decreasing portal vein velocity, or any change in
direction of intra- or extrahepatic portal vein flow
must be noted, with a low threshold to perform
contrast-enhanced CT or catheter angiography to
help confirm the US findings. If the patient was
treated with TAE, the incidence of classic hepatic
arterial complications such as stenosis and pseu-
doaneurysm may be increased.

In addition, information about preexisting
HCC and any additional HCC findings in the
explant should be included in postoperative im-
aging reports to help alert the radiologist to the
possibility that a subtle abnormality in an un-
usual location may represent recurrent tumor.
