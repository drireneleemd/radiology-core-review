![](/cms/10.1148/rg.281075092/asset/images/g08ja33c03x.jpeg)

**Figure 3.**  Intraoperative photograph shows the transition zone of the intussusception, which was caused by a lead-point Peutz-Jeghers polyp. The intussusceptum (thick arrow) is dilating and obstructing the intussuscipiens (thin arrow).

* [Download as PowerPoint](/action/downloadFigures?doi=10.1148%2Frg.281075092&id=F3)

![](/cms/10.1148/rg.281075092/asset/images/g08ja33c04x.jpeg)

**Figure 4.**  Photograph of the gross bowel specimen demonstrates the lead-point polyp (thick arrow) with several smaller polyps aggregated at the base of the dominant polyp (thin arrows).

* [Download as PowerPoint](/action/downloadFigures?doi=10.1148%2Frg.281075092&id=F4)

![](/cms/10.1148/rg.281075092/asset/images/g08ja33c05x.jpeg)

**Figure 5.**  Photomicrograph (original magnification, ×100; hematoxylin-eosin stain) demonstrates that Peutz-Jeghers polyps characteristically contain a core of arborizing smooth muscle (thick arrow), upon which rests mucosa that is native to the site of the polyp, in this case small bowel mucosa. Note that although the surface is hemorrhagic (thin arrow), in other areas the surface is entirely necrotic.

* [Download as PowerPoint](/action/downloadFigures?doi=10.1148%2Frg.281075092&id=F5)

![](/cms/10.1148/rg.281075092/asset/images/g08ja33c06x.jpeg)

**Figure 6.**  Photomicrograph (original magnification, ×100; hematoxylin-eosin stain) shows that this large Peutz-Jeghers polyp includes nests of benign mucosa (arrows) that are located in the submucosa and muscularis propria, which is called epithelial misplacement or “pseudoinvasion.” This is a relatively uncommon but well-described finding in Peutz-Jeghers polyps and should not be mistaken for invasive carcinoma.
