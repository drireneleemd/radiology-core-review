Early Postoperative Gastrointestinal Tract
Evaluation: Expected Findings and Complications

Postoperative  imaging  evaluation  following  gastrointestinal
surgery is a critical concern for radiologists and surgeons. The
early postoperative period can be challenging for radiologists
because  the  imaging  appearances  of  expected  findings  and
complications are often similar. The slide presentation high-
lights  the  importance  of  correctly  identifying  imaging  find-
ings that require further investigation.

Free intraperitoneal air and fluid, fat stranding, and bowel
dilatation are commonly expected in the early postoperative
period. However, these findings can also serve as indicators
of  postoperative  complications.  Understanding  the  specific
imaging characteristics and their evolution over time is cru-
cial  for  distinguishing  expected  findings  from  potential
complications.

Contrast-enhanced CT is the most commonly used imaging
modality for evaluation of postoperative patients. The CT pro-
tocol must be individually tailored to each patient based on
the surgical procedure performed and the clinical suspicions.
For  example,  administration  of  positive  oral  contrast  agent
aids in detection of complications such as anastomotic leak-
age (Fig 1). Use of the appropriate CT protocol ensures early
detection of complications and leads to timely intervention.

The  slide  presentation  enhances  the  reader’s  abilities  in
the  following  areas:  (a)  differentiating  between  expected
postsurgical imaging findings and surgical complications in
the  early  postoperative  period,  (b)  selecting  the  appropriate
CT  protocol  for  assessing  different  suspected  complications
in the early postoperative period, and (c) identifying specific
imaging  patterns  associated  with  intraperitoneal  air,  intra-
peritoneal fluid, fat stranding, and bowel dilatation that will
aid in diagnosis.

By understanding the expected postoperative findings and
commonly seen complications and by effectively performing
contrast-enhanced  CT,  radiologists  can  confidently  evalu-
ate patients after gastrointestinal surgery, promptly identify
problems  such  as  active  bleeding  (Fig  2),  and  contribute  to
optimized patient care and outcomes.

TEACHING POINTS
	 Contrast-enhanced  CT  is  a  commonly  used  imaging  modality  for  eval-
uating  postoperative  patients  because  it  accurately  characterizes  gas
and fluid collections. Administration of positive oral and rectal contrast
material can be beneficial in patients suspected of having anastomotic
leakage or bowel perforations.

	 Free intraperitoneal air and fluid, fat stranding, and bowel dilatation are
expected in the early postoperative period. However, these findings can
also serve as indicators of postoperative complications.

	 Unexpected  postsurgical  imaging  findings  include  venous  thrombosis,
peripheral  wedge-shaped  hypoenhancement,  and  foreign  body  reac-
tion.
