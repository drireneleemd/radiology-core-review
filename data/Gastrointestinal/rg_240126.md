Several autoimmune diseases (primary and secondary)
can affect the liver and bile ducts. While the exact cause
remains unclear, early diagnosis is crucial to prevent
complications. The authors’ main objective is to review
imaging features of various autoimmune disorders, in-
cluding autoimmune hepatitis, primary biliary cholan-
gitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis, immunoglobulin
G4 (IgG4)–related diseases, and drug-induced auto-
immune injury. These disorders are chronic inflam-
matory conditions causing destruction of hepatocytes
or cholangiocytes, destruction of the latter potentially
leading to cholestasis and associated ductopenia. Com-
plications related to untreated autoimmune disorders
include sequelae of chronic liver failure or cirrhosis,
such as portal hypertension and ascites. Neoplasms
arising in the setting of cirrhosis related to autoim-
mune diseases include hepatocellular carcinoma,
cholangiocarcinoma, and gallbladder cancer. As these
autoimmune disorders of the liver and biliary tract
characteristically involve bile ducts and cause cholesta-
sis, MRI or MR cholangiopancreatography (MRCP) is the
preferred imaging modality, given its ability to provide
excellent anatomic details of the bile ducts and demon-
strate changes in the liver parenchyma. Understanding
the imaging appearance of each of the autoimmune
disorders affecting the liver and biliary tract allows a
particular diagnosis to be suggested. Imaging studies
often provide the first clues to an autoimmune disorder
of the liver and bile ducts, enabling early diagnosis to
halt progression and prevent complications. In addi-
tion, imaging studies are also useful for monitoring
progression of disease, assessing treatment response,
and detecting complications during follow-up.

Introduction
Once considered rare, autoimmune diseases are now known
to affect 3%–5% of the population, with almost 100 distinct au-
toimmune diseases recognized (1). The current understand-
ing  suggests  a  multifactorial  cause  for  autoimmune  diseas-
es.  In  genetically  susceptible  individuals,  an  environmental
stimulus  or  trigger  such  as  an  infection,  toxins  (eg,  tobacco

smoke), or medication can disrupt immune tolerance, lead-
ing to dysregulated immune response and subsequent tissue
damage (1,2).

Overview of Autoimmune Disorders of the Liver
and Biliary Tract
The liver, receiving blood from the gut via the portal system,
encounters a constant stream of antigens and pathogens (3).
Unsurprisingly, the liver contains one of the largest popula-
tions of immune cells, strategically positioned near the por-
tal triads and within the sinusoids, where the portal venous
blood  first  comes  in  contact  with  hepatic  parenchyma.  As
such, autoimmune disorders of the liver and biliary tract tend
to  predominantly  involve  the  portal  tracts,  given  the  abun-
dance of immune cells in this location.

AIH targets hepatocytes, with the inflammation starting in
the periportal region (4). In contrast, autoimmune biliary dis-
eases (PBC, PSC, and IgG4-RD) cause progressive damage to

Diagnosing autoimmune disorders of the liver and biliary
tract often involves combining clinical features, results of au-
toantibody tests, serum liver enzyme levels, and findings of
imaging  and  sometimes  liver  biopsy.  While  AIH,  PBC,  PSC,
and  IgG4-RD  share  some  features,  each  has  distinct  demo-
graphics,  laboratory  results,  and  specific  imaging  features
that aid differentiation (Table 2). As mentioned earlier, auto-
immune diseases target specific bile ducts (eg, large vs small)
(Fig 1). This pattern can be seen at imaging, aiding diagnosis.
While  US  and  CT  are  commonly  used  for  initial  evaluation,
their findings may be normal or nonspecific.

Involvement of the biliary tree makes MRI or MR cholan-
giopancreatography (MRCP) the preferred imaging modality
due  to  its  noninvasive  and  detailed  visualization  of  the  bile
ducts (Fig 2). A normal MRCP study without artifacts typical-
ly  shows  only  up  to  medium-sized  (third-  or  fourth-order)
ducts. Visualization of more-peripheral ducts should suggest
an  abnormality,  while  seeing  only  first-  and  second-order
branches should suggest pruning.

Ideally, MRI and MRCP should be performed with a 3-T im-
aging unit with meticulous artifact-reduction techniques em-
ployed, as subtle changes are crucial for diagnosis (7). The MRI-
MRCP protocol should include contrast-enhanced T1-weighted
dynamic sequences for demonstration of heterogeneous paren-
chymal enhancement, bile duct thickening, and enhancement;
detection  of  vascular  complications;  and  characterization  of
focal lesions. A detailed discussion of the MRI-MRCP protocol
is beyond the scope of this focused review. Readers are referred
to detailed MRI-MRCP protocols and assessment of the quality
of MRI and MRCP studies in earlier publications (7,8).

There are two types of AIH based on which serum autoan-
tibody is present. In type 1 AIH, antinuclear antibody (ANA)
or  smooth  muscle  antibody  (SMA)  is  present.  Type  1  AIH  is
most commonly diagnosed in adult women (27–60 years old)
(13,14).  In  type  2  AIH,  anti–liver-kidney  microsomal  (LKM)
antibodies or anti–liver cytosol type 1 antigen antibody (an-
ti-LC1) is present. The peak incidence of type 2 AIH occurs in
children and adolescents (<16 years old) (13–15).

Treatment  and  prognosis  do  not  significantly  differ  be-
tween  type  1  and  type  2  AIH,  and  there  are  no  imaging  or
histologic distinguishing features between the two AIH types
(16,17). A small subset of patients with biopsy-proven AIH do
not  have  identifiable  circulating  antibodies  and  are  classed
as having autoantibody-negative AIH or cryptogenic chronic
hepatitis (11,18).

Histologic  analysis  shows  a  hepatitic  pattern  character-
ized  by  lobular  hepatitis  and  portal  inflammation  rich  with
lymphocytes  and  plasma  cells.  Inflammation  of  zone  1  he-
patocytes  is  called  interface  hepatitis  and  is  a  common  but
nonspecific finding (19). The plasma cells in AIH are predomi-
nantly immunoglobulin G (IgG) positive. The severity of lobu-
lar inflammation ranges from mildly active hepatitis, through
bridging necrosis, to massive hepatic necrosis (12,19).

Early in the course of AIH, imaging may demonstrate a nor-
mal  liver  and  biliary  tree.  Heterogeneous  parenchymal  en-
hancement in the late arterial and portal venous phase may
be seen and is suggestive of underlying inflammation (Fig 3);
however, this is not specific for AIH. Heterogeneous enhance-
ment may improve after treatment (20). As the disease pro-
gresses to fibrosis, hepatic surface nodularity and heteroge-
neous parenchymal MRI signal intensity and heterogeneous
enhancement at both CT and MRI may increase (Fig 4).

Fibrosis is most commonly seen in a reticular pattern, de-
fined as fine lines of T1-hypointense signal that enhance in the
delayed phase, often giving rise to a lacelike pattern of enhance-
ment  (21).  As  AIH  is  often  detected  in  later  stages,  the  most
common imaging feature of AIH is hepatic surface nodularity,
which is typically indicative of fibrosis or cirrhosis, but rarely
may be seen in fulminant hepatitis (16,21). Lymphadenopathy
is less common compared to other chole static diseases. In ad-
vanced stages, global atrophy of the liver is common (22).

While  the  imaging  features  in  AIH  are  nonspecific,  they
are  useful  to  help  rule  out  other  cholestatic  disease  such  as
PBC, PSC, and IgG4-RD, with AIH most likely to show a nor-
mal biliary tree at MRCP. However, cholestatic diseases in the
very early stages, particularly PBC, may also show a normal
biliary tree. Any bile duct injury in AIH is typically focal and
mild and rarely causes bile duct loss (16). However, in chronic
AIH, there may be pruning of the distal biliary tree at imag-
ing, although this does not correlate with loss of bile ducts at
histologic analysis.

Autoimmune  disorders  of  the  liver  are  typically  chron-
ic  and  sometimes  relapsing,  often  progressing  silently  until
complications arise or liver tests are performed for other clin-
ical  reasons.  Untreated,  they  can  progress  to  cirrhosis  with
complications.  Chronic  autoimmune  diseases  are  also  asso-
ciated with development of neoplasms, including hepatocel-
lular carcinoma (HCC), cholangiocarcinoma, and gallbladder
cancer. While the American Association for the Study of Liver
Diseases (AASLD) and the Liver Imaging Reporting and Data
System  (LI-RADS)  recommend  US  screening  for  HCC  in  any
patient  with  Child-Pugh  A-B  cirrhosis  or  with  Child-Pugh  C
cirrhosis  awaiting  transplant  with  or  without  measurement
of α-fetoprotein (AFP) level every 6 months (9), MRI or MRCP
is likely more beneficial in the setting of autoimmune disor-
ders,  due  to  associated  biliary  complications  and  increased
risk of biliary cancers in addition to HCC.

Autoimmune Hepatitis
AIH  is  thought  to  be  caused  by  environmental  exposure  to
viruses or xenobiotics in a genetically susceptible individual,
which triggers a dysregulated adaptive immune attack against
hepatocyte autoantigens, leading to destruction of hepatic pa-
renchyma, inflammation, and eventually fibrosis and related
sequelae (4,10,11). AIH involves liver parenchyma more than
bile  ducts,  as  compared  with  other  cholestatic  autoimmune
diseases discussed later. AIH is female predominant (male to
female ratio, 1:3) (12). The clinical spectrum of AIH is variable,
ranging from mild subclinical disease to fulminant hepatitis.

Overlap Syndromes
Concurrent  or  sequential  occurrence  of  more  than  one  au-
toimmune disease is termed overlap syndrome (14). In typical
AIH, cholestatic features are uncommon; however, cholesta-
sis may be seen in a small proportion (<10%) of atypical vari-
ants, which are called overlap syndromes or cholestatic phe-
notypes  of  AIH  (26).  AIH  is  the  predominant  disease,  with
clinical, laboratory, histologic, and imaging findings suggest-
ing the overlapping conditions (27,28).

Imaging  findings  are  often  a  combination  of  AIH  and  the
overlapping condition (PSC or PBC), as discussed in those sec-

tions. The AIH+PBC variants have antimitochondrial antibod-
ies (AMAs) and histologic features of PBC. The PSC+AIH variant
shows cholangiographic abnormalities of PSC (Fig 8) and ob-
struction-type bile ductular proliferation at histologic analysis.
When  inflammatory  bowel  disease  is  present  in  patients
with AIH, MRCP should be performed to rule out intrahepatic
biliary  strictures  suggestive  of  sclerosing  cholangitis,  which
can manifest in AIH+PSC overlap syndrome. The third type of
overlap syndrome has cholestatic features at histologic anal-
ysis  without  positive  AMAs  or  abnormal  cholangiographic
findings and has been referred to as AIH and undefined choles-
tatic syndrome (26).

Cholestatic Autoimmune Biliary Disorders
PBC, PSC, and IgG4-RD are immune-mediated cholangiopa-
thies that have a common feature of inflammation and pro-
gressive destruction of bile ducts leading to cholestasis, with
fibrosis and cirrhosis developing over time. These cholangiop-
athies  are  asymptomatic  in  the  early  stages  and  are  usually
detected with routine laboratory tests. The diagnostic workup
includes patient history, clinical findings, serologic tests, and
imaging with MRI and MRCP for evaluation of bile ducts. Liver
biopsy and genetic testing may be required in some cases.

Primary Biliary Cholangitis
PBC is the most common autoimmune biliary disorder of the
liver, predominantly affecting women (9:1) and with a marked
White  predominance  (>90%).  It  typically  manifests  in  indi-
viduals aged 50–60 years (14,29). In PBC, there is loss of im-
mune tolerance against mitochondrial antigens expressed on
the bile duct epithelium with production of antimitochondri-
al antibodies (AMAs). The AMAs attack on bile ducts triggers
inflammation, initially in the portal areas, and damages small
and medium-sized bile ducts, which are eventually destroyed.
A specific finding at histologic analysis is a florid duct le-
sion,  characterized  by  a  collection  of  histiocytes  around  the
duct, which is identified in less than 40% of PBC biopsy sam-
ple but can be diagnostic when present (30,31). Well-defined
noncaseating granulomas can be seen in up to 50% of cases
(31). The end result is chronic cholestasis, which over time re-
sults in progressive fibrosis and cirrhosis (30,32,33).

In very early disease, the bile duct damage and portal in-
flammation are distributed heterogeneously within the liver.
The loss of interlobular bile ducts leads to the finding of un-
paired arteries, also known as vanishing bile duct or ducto-
penia.  Nodular  regenerative  hyperplasia  (NRH)  can  be  seen
in PBC; this is a process composed of regenerating nodules in

Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis
PSC is characterized by progressive obliterative inflammation
and  fibrosis  of  intrahepatic  and  extrahepatic  ducts,  result-
ing in short segmental strictures of the bile ducts alternating
with normal or dilated segments. The median age at onset is
30–40 years, with a male predilection (2:1) and an association
with  inflammatory  bowel  disease  in  up  to  80%  of  patients
(45–47).  PSC  is  classified  as  an  immune-mediated  disease
versus a traditional autoimmune disease, as it has male pre-
dilection and often lacks a response to immunosuppression
(45). Typical findings at histologic analysis are periductal fi-
brosis and fibro-obliterative duct lesions around medium or
large  bile  ducts,  with  varying  degrees  of  periductal  inflam-
mation. As the disease advances, there is progressive luminal
narrowing and eventual complete replacement of ducts (46).
The imaging features of PSC are variable, depending on the
disease  stage  (7,8,48).  Early  on,  normal  imaging  findings  or
short segmental strictures alternating with normal or mildly
dilated  ducts  with  wall  thickening  and  mural  enhancement
may be seen. Later on, diffuse multifocal strictures involving
the intrahepatic or extrahepatic bile ducts give the character-
istic and more specific beaded appearance (Fig 14).

IgG4-related Sclerosing Cholangitis
IgG4-RD is an immune-mediated fibroinflammatory condi-
tion that may involve multiple organ systems, commonly the
pancreas  and  biliary  tract.  Involvement  of  the  biliary  tract
with IgG4-RD is referred to as IgG4-SC (57–60). IgG4-SC usu-
ally occurs in association with other organ involvement in at
least 95% of patients, with the pancreas most commonly in-
volved in about 60% of patients (60). Other associated man-
ifestations include sialadenitis, tubulointerstitial nephritis,
dacryoadenitis, and periaortitis (60). Pancreas involvement
is referred to as autoimmune pancreatitis and is the hallmark
of IgG4-RD. The presence of both pancreatic and biliary tract
disease  is  nearly  diagnostic  of  IgG4-RD.  IgG4-SC  rarely  oc-
curs in the absence of pancreatic involvement (10% of cases)
(61).

The  most  common  clinical  manifestation  of  IgG4-SC  is
chronic  or  recurrent  cholestatic  jaundice.  IgG4-SC  occurs
most  often  in  middle-aged  or  older  patients  (>60  years  old)
and has a male predilection (male to female ratio, 4:1–8:1) (61).
At  histologic  analysis,  IgG4-SC  shows  a  lymphoplasmacytic
infiltration around the bile duct, IgG4-positive cells, and vari-
able degrees of characteristic storiform fibrosis (62).

Understanding the imaging features of IgG4-SC is import-
ant to suggest the diagnosis, identify other systems involved
with  IgG4-RD,  and  monitor  treatment  response.  Imaging
features of IgG4-SC can overlap with those of PSC, so under-
standing key differences is important to increase confidence
in  one  diagnosis  versus  the  other.  While  intrahepatic  ducts
can be involved, IgG4-SC has a predilection for the extrahe-
patic and hilar ducts, with the most common site of involve-
ment being the intrapancreatic segment of the common bile
duct (59). IgG4-SC is characterized by long-segment and cir-
cumferential symmetric wall thickening of the bile ducts, re-
sulting in luminal narrowing or stricture with smooth outer
and inner margins (61), with characteristic persistent visual-
ization of the lumen at the site of luminal narrowing or stric-
ture. Strictures in IgG4-SC typically affect long segments and
are smooth (61) (Fig 25).

Systemic  rheumatic  diseases  (SRDs)  are  chronic  inflamma-
tory  autoimmune  disorders.  Different  autoantibodies  are
present  depending  on  the  SRD,  and  these  autoantibodies
can  affect  any  organ  system.  SRDs  include  systemic  lupus
erythematosus  (SLE),  Sjögren  syndrome,  systemic  sclerosis,
and  rheumatoid  arthritis.  Liver  dysfunction  in  SRDs  can  be
secondary to medications, viral hepatitis, or coexisting auto-
immune liver diseases. Secondary causes of liver dysfunction
must be excluded before considering liver involvement due to
the SRD (71). Significant liver injury is rare.

Nearly 70% of patients with rheumatoid arthritis have ab-
normal results on liver function tests (72). NRH and portal hy-
pertension can occur in patients with Felty syndrome, SLE, or
antiphospholipid  syndrome.  In  antiphospholipid  syndrome,
Budd-Chiari syndrome may develop in addition to NRH. PBC
is  associated  with  Sjögren  syndrome  and  systemic  sclerosis
(72). Imaging findings may include a normal biliary tree or a
mild form of sclerosing cholangitis (Fig 29) and mild patchy
enhancement  of  liver  parenchyma.  Liver  biopsy  may  be  re-
quired to exclude a primary autoimmune liver disease.

Graft-versus-Host Disease
Graft-versus-host  disease  (GVHD)  is  a  serious  multiorgan
complication  often  encountered  after  hematopoietic  stem
cell transplant, with an incidence of 39%–59% (73). The most
common organs involved are the skin, gastrointestinal tract,
and  liver.  Typically,  liver  involvement  manifests  after  skin
and gastrointestinal involvement. Skin involvement consists
of  a  diffuse  pruritic  rash,  and  gastrointestinal  involvement
consists of an enterocolitis. In terms of liver disease, patients
typically present with elevated levels of liver enzymes with or
without jaundice (74).

Imaging  findings  are  often  nonspecific,  with  biliary  wall
thickening and enhancement, biliary dilatation, and gallblad-
der  thickening;  periportal  edema  may  be  seen  in  the  acute
phase (Fig 30). In the chronic phase, imaging findings are typ-
ically similar, although patients can start developing a pruned
appearance of the intrahepatic ducts, similar to that of PSC (75).

Diagnostic Approach to Autoimmune
 Hepatobiliary Diseases
Knowledge of the imaging appearance of autoimmune disor-
ders of the liver and biliary tract is important to suggest them
as a diagnostic possibility and to provide a weighted differen-
tial diagnosis. Table 2 provides a comparison of the main au-
toimmune disorders.

Involvement of the biliary tree is more common with the im-
mune-mediated  cholangiopathies—PSC  and  IgG4-SC—where
stricturing  of  the  medium  and  large  ducts  is  readily  visual-
ized with MRI and MRCP. While both have a male predilection,
IgG4-SC is seen in older patients (>60 years old) and often with
imaging findings of IgG4-RD in other organs, particularly the
pancreas. PSC most commonly involves both the intrahepatic
and extrahepatic ducts, whereas IgG4-SC usually involves pre-
dominantly extrahepatic ducts, most commonly the intrapan-

creatic segment of the common bile duct. The strictures in PSC
are commonly short segmental strictures, whereas in IgG4-SC,
the strictures involve long segments of the bile ducts.

Also, IgG4-SC is highly steroid responsive whereas PSC is
not, so if diagnostic uncertainty exists between them, a ste-
roid trial may be considered. Small-duct PSC should be con-
sidered  in  a  patient  with  a  history  of  inflammatory  bowel
disease, a cholestatic profile, and a normal cholangiographic
appearance. The diagnosis can be established with a liver bi-
opsy. Small-duct PSC cases should be followed up, as some of
them develop features of large-duct PSC.

In  PBC,  the  bile  ducts  are  often  normal  early  in  the  dis-
ease course, with irregularity or destruction of the peripheral
ducts seen later. This pruned-tree appearance at MRCP, along
with the heterogeneous lacelike appearance of the liver, adja-
cent lympadenopathy, and the periportal halo sign, suggests
the diagnosis of PBC.

Imaging  features  of  AIH  are  nonspecific;  therefore  biop-
sy is required for diagnosis. Hepatic surface nodularity, het-
erogeneous arterial phase hyperenhancement, and elevated
levels of serum liver enzymes and serum autoantibodies are
suggestive of AIH. For drug-induced AIH, the medication his-
tory is most important, with the onset of imaging findings af-
ter starting an offending medication being crucial.

Diagnosis  of  overlap  syndrome  and  liver  involvement  in
systemic  autoimmune  disorders  requires  a  full  diagnostic
workup for all autoimmune diseases and often includes a liv-
er biopsy.

geneous group of inflammatory disorders that should be con-
sidered in the differential diagnosis of chronic cholestasis and
