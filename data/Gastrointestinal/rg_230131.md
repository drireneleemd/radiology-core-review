MR Enterography in Ulcerative Colitis:
Beyond Endoscopy

Amir Reza Radmard, MD • Mehrnam Amouei, MD • Ala Torabi, MD • Ali Reza Sima, MD • Hiva Saffar, MD • Amine Geahchan, MD
Amir H. Davarpanah, MD • Bachir Taouli, MD

Author affiliations, funding, and conflicts of interest are listed at the end of this article.

Ulcerative colitis (UC) is a chronic idiopathic inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) that progressively affects mucosa and sub-
muccosa of the colon and rectum in a continual pattern. In comparison, Crohn disease (CD), the other type of IBD, is a chronic
transmural inflammatory disorder that can involve any part of the gastrointestinal tract. MR enterography (MRE) has emerged
as an important imaging modality for the diagnosis and detection of disease activity and complications in CD, with comparable
results to those of endoscopy. But MRE has been underused for assessment of UC in recent years, and clinicians heavily rely on
endoscopic findings for management of UC. Despite UC being considered an endoscopically assessable disease, MRE can pro-
vide useful information beyond that obtained with endoscopy about mural or extramural abnormalities, inaccessible parts of
the colonic lumen, associated extraintestinal diseases, and superimposed pathologic conditions. Moreover, endoscopy might be
contraindicated in some clinical settings due to the risk of colonic perforation. In addition to depicting the features of UC activity
in different phases, MRE demonstrates findings of disease chronicity that cannot be achieved with endoscopy, particularly in a
patient with colitis of unknown cause. The valuable diagnostic role of MRE to exclude undiagnosed CD in patients with UC who
have refractory disease or those with postproctocolectomy complications is also emphasized. Radiologists can play a crucial role
in the management of UC with MRE by addressing what is beyond endoscopy.

Introduction
Ulcerative colitis (UC) is the most common form of inflamma-
tory bowel disease (IBD) and is characterized as a chronic pro-
gressive disorder affecting the colon and rectum (1). Compared
with  the  transmural  involvement  in  the  other  form  of  IBD,
Crohn disease (CD), only mucosal and submucosal layers are
affected in UC, which starts in the rectum and can continually
extend proximally to other segments of the colon. The effect
of UC on various aspects of patients’ lives has been demon-
strated in several studies (1,2), given the relapsing-remitting
pattern  of  the  disease,  known  complications  and  comorbid-
ities, and the need for lifelong treatment and follow-up. The

exact pathogenesis of UC is still unclear, but several factors,
including genetic background, altered gut microbiota, muco-
sal immune dysregulation, and environmental triggers, have
been  suggested.  Patients  usually  present  with  characteristic
symptoms including bloody diarrhea and tenesmus, but they
may also have fever or weight loss (3).

In  recent  years,  MR  enterography  (MRE)  has  become  an
important  imaging  modality  as  an  adjunct  to  endoscopy  in
assessing the small bowel in patients with IBD and has been
shown to correlate with endoscopic and histopathologic find-
ings (4). The pivotal role of MRE in the diagnosis and evalua-
tion of disease activity, complications, and treatment response

UC to explore what is beyond endoscopy. Technical consider-
ations; variants of the disease and indicators of disease activ-
ity, severity, and chronicity are also discussed.

Technical Considerations
The 2017 joint consensus statement of the European Society of
Gastrointestinal  and  Abdominal  Radiology  (ESGAR)  and  the
European Society of Pediatric Radiology (ESPR) has described
the technical performance of cross-sectional small bowel and
colonic  imaging  (13).  Five  main  additional  technical  points

should be considered as part of a routine MRE protocol (Table
S1) when optimal colonic visualization is needed, particularly
in patients with UC: (a) colonic preparation the day before the
examination for proper assessment of the colonic lumen and
mucosal surface; (b) acquisition of MR images in the axial plane
from the subdiaphragmatic region down to the deep pelvis to
entirely  cover  the  colon  (including  the  splenic  flexure)  and
rectum;  (c)  acquisition  of  additional  postcontrast  coronal  T2-
weighted MR images at the end of the examination to confirm
or exclude colonic stricture or spasm detected in the earlier se-
quences; (d) acquisition of sagittal T2-weighted MR images of
the abdomen and pelvis to display the ileocecal valve, rectum,
and  ileal  pouch–anal  anastomosis;  and  (e)  acquisition  of  one
thin-section axial fat-suppressed T2-weighted image from the
anal canal in patients suspected of having perianal disease.

UC Variants
The extent of UC disease is divided into three subgroups on
the basis of the Montreal classification system: (a) ulcerative
proctitis, (b) left-sided UC, and (c) extensive UC (ie, UC pan-
colitis) (Figs 1–3). UC variants should be addressed with either
endoscopic or radiologic examinations. The extent of the dis-
ease is one of the most determinant factors included in dis-
ease severity indices and prognostic assessment (14).

Patients with UC tend to present with a continuous pat-
tern  of  involvement  in  the  colon,  with  a  relatively  sharp
border between diseased and normal mucosa (Figs S1, S2).
This  proximal  clear  demarcation,  which  has  remained  un-
explained  by  current  hypotheses  of  the  disease,  may  be
disrupted by treatment, causing disease remission in some
discontinuous segments (4). The early endoscopic manifes-
tations of UC include mucosal edema, hyperemia, and gran-
ularity,  which  may  cause  blunting  of  the  colonic  haustra.
From the histopathologic point of view, the mucosal edema
in the early stage represents an inflammatory infiltrate su-
perficial  to  the  muscularis  mucosa.  The  inflammatory  in-
filtrate then collects within the crypt lumen, resulting in a
crypt  abscess  and,  eventually,  glandular  destruction.  The
submucosal layer may be involved when inflammatory infil-
trates extend deeply secondary to the progression of inflam-
mation (15). Features of acute UC at MRE may only be mu-
cosal  or  inner-wall  hyperenhancement  in  the  early  phase,
while colonic wall thickening, submucosal edema, and mu-
cosal ulcerations occur by means of progressive inflamma-
tion. Clear differentiation between the mucosal-submucosal
complex and the muscularis propria and a smooth outer co-
lonic contour are other useful features that aid in differenti-
ation of UC from CD colitis (16).

classically  known  as  backwash  ileitis,  which  could  cause  a

the hypothesis of ileocecal valve inflammation and incompe-
tency resulting in the reflux of inflammatory colonic material
into the terminal ileum. But some uncommon presentations
of  backwash  ileitis  in  left-sided  UC  have  been  reported  (17),

Perianal  disease  encompasses  various  forms  of  involve-
ment  including  perianal  fistulas,  abscesses,  fissures,  and
hemorrhoids,  which  are  commonly  associated  with  CD.  Al-
though  perianal  disease  is  relatively  uncommon  in  patients
with  UC,  its  diagnosis  has  been  underestimated.  The  esti-
mated  occurrence  of  perianal  disease  in  patients  with  UC  is
approximately  5%  (22).  The  incidence  has  been  reported  to
be threefold higher than that in the general population (23).
There  is  a  higher  frequency  of  extensive  colitis  in  patients
with  UC  who  have  perianal  disease  that  substantially  affects
the treatment strategies and disease outcomes (23). Further-
more, care should be taken to consider the possibility of un-
diagnosed  CD.  Therefore,  the  diagnosis  of  UC  with  perianal
disease is of great clinical significance.

Perianal disease can be suspected on the pelvic images of
an  MRE  examination,  especially  when  a  single  thin-section
axial  fat-suppressed  T2-weighted  imaging  sequence  of  the
anal canal is included. Positive findings may prompt further
evaluation  with  a  dedicated  perianal  MRI  protocol  to  deter-
mine the disease extent and activity (Fig 7).

Disease Activity and Severity
Disease  activity  is  defined  as  the  degree  of  inflammation  at
a certain time. The severity of the disease refers to the over-
all concept of disease burden, with assessment of the longi-
tudinal  factors  in  addition  to  the  disease  activity,  including
the patient’s history of hospitalization, treatment failure, and
quality-of-life metrics (24).

Several  scoring  systems  have  been  introduced  for  assess-
ment  of  the  disease  activity  using  clinical  and  endoscopic
variables. The Mayo Clinic Score disease activity index is one
of the most favored scoring tools clinicians frequently use, and
it  includes  four  descriptors:  stool  frequency;  rectal  bleeding;
findings at flexible sigmoidoscopy; and the physician’s global
assessment.  The  index  is  used  to  categorize  disease  activity

into one of four grades for each descriptor: 0, normal; 1, mild; 2,
moderate; and 3, severe. Of these, the Mayo endoscopic score is
widely applied in clinical practice, although it has significant in-
terobserver variability. To increase the inter- and intraobserver
agreement, the Ulcerative Colitis Endoscopic Index of Severity
(UCEIS) was developed and demonstrated that only three en-
doscopic descriptors (vascular pattern, bleeding, and erosions
or ulcers) can be combined to account for 90% of the overall
assessment of endoscopic activity (25). The UCEIS scores range
from  0  to  8  and  are  linked  to  the  four  clinical  disease  states:
remission (scores 0 and 1); mild (scores 2 through 4); moderate
(scores 5 and 6); and severe (scores 7 and 8) (26).

The MRE descriptors of bowel wall inflammation based on
the recent literature include mural edema, mucosal hyperen-
hancement, diffusion restriction, mural ulceration, pericolic
lymphadenopathies, and an engorged perienteric mesentery.
Authors of several studies (16,27,28) have investigated the ac-
curacy of noninvasive MRI techniques for the assessment of
UC activity. Ordás et al (28) demonstrated sensitivity of 87%
and specificity of 88% for detecting endoscopic inflammation
in  patients  with  UC  by  using  a  simplified  index  (MRC-S  ≥  1)
derived from MR colonography (MRC), which included rela-
tive  contrast  enhancement,  mural  edema,  enlarged  lymph
nodes,  and  the  “comb  sign.”  They  showed  that  MR  colonog-
raphy  has  high  accuracy  for  the  assessment  of  disease  ac-
tivity  and  severity  in  patients  with  UC.  Authors  of  another
single-center  study  (16)  used  diffusion-weighted  MRI/MRC
without  oral  or  rectal  preparation  to  assess  disease  activity
in  35  patients  with  UC  and  61  patients  with  CD.  They  intro-
duced  a  segmental  MRI  score  (MR-score-S)  using  six  radio-
logic signs at diffusion-weighted imaging in patients with UC:
hyperintensity, rapid enhancement, differentiation between
the mucosa-submucosa complex and the muscularis propria,
bowel wall thickness, parietal edema, and ulceration. An MR-
score-S (ie, a “Nancy” score) greater than 1 was determined to
indicate  endoscopic  inflammation  in  patients  with  UC,  with
sensitivity  and  specificity  of  89.4%  and  86.7%,  respectively.

Disease Chronicity
UC is an intestinal barrier disease initially caused by an ep-
ithelial barrier defect or cell dysfunction. The barrier might
also be disrupted by strong inflammatory cells and mediators
in the lamina propria. These two mechanisms accelerate in-
testinal barrier impairment and cause an inflammatory cas-
cade that eventually results in disease chronicity (3).

Fibrosis  in  chronic  UC  has  remained  widely  unexplored
endoscopically.  Gordon  et  al  (32)  demonstrated  submucosal
fibrosis in 100% of UC colectomy specimens, but only in ar-
eas affected by inflammation. A significant amount of fibrosis
and  muscularis  mucosal  thickening  undiagnosed  by  means
of  endoscopic  mucosal  sampling  should  be  taken  into  ac-
count  as  a  common  complication  of  chronic  progressive  UC
(32). The “lead pipe” sign describes a shortened and stiff colon
in patients with chronic UC, with a smooth and ahaustral con-
tour that is caused by hypertrophy of the muscularis mucosa
and fibrosis (32,33) (Figs S4, S5). This sign is not specific to UC
and may also be found in other conditions such as infectious
colitides (tuberculosis, amebiasis), or even CD or cathartic co-
lon  (33)  (Fig  11).  Submucosal  fat  deposition  is  also  a  marker
of chronic involvement and is particularly seen in the rectum
and can be detected with MRE. The role of fat-suppressed T2-
weighted MRI sequences to differentiate submucosal fat from
edema is crucial (Fig 12). Although fibrofatty proliferation is a
typical sign of CD, pericolic fat proliferation can be frequently
seen at MRE of patients with chronic UC, mostly in the peri-
rectal and perisigmoid area (34) (Fig S6). A constellation of fi-
brosis, muscle hypertrophy, submucosal fat, and pericolic fat
widening results in luminal narrowing in long-standing UC.

Acute Complications
MRE is not widely applied in emergency conditions, but some
acute  complications  of  UC  can  be  assessed  with  MRE  when
endoscopy is contraindicated.

Toxic  megacolon  is  defined  as  acute  nonobstructive  dila-
tation  of  the  colon  associated  with  systemic  toxicity.  It  is  an
uncommon, potentially fatal condition that can be superim-
posed on inflammatory or infectious colitis. IBD is an import-
ant  predisposing  factor  for  developing  toxic  megacolon  (12).
Megacolon may also occur in conditions without definite clin-
ical or laboratory signs of toxicity, termed nontoxic megacolon.
IBD, UC in particular, may be complicated by toxic or nontoxic
megacolon and may manifest as acute abdominal distention.
However, the absence of systemic toxicity favors diagnosis of a
nontoxic condition (35). MRE allows evaluation of the disease
activity, extent, and superimposed complications in a mark-
edly  distended  colon  in  which  endoscopy  is  contraindicated
(Fig 13).

The estimated global incidence of colonoscopic perforation
is 0.016%–0.8% for diagnostic and 0.02%–8% for therapeutic
colonoscopy  (36).  Navaneethan  et  al  (37)  reported  a  higher
risk  of  colonoscopy-associated  perforation  in  patients  with
IBD than that in control subjects without IBD in a nationwide
study. Detection of pneumoperitoneum at MRE is a challenge,
and it can be easily overlooked by the radiologist. Care must
be taken not to miss pneumoperitoneum at MRE in the set-
ting of a recently incomplete, technically difficult colonoscopy
in a patient with IBD. The underlying immunocompromised
status  of  these  patients  may  mask  signs  of  peritonitis.  It  is
also difficult to detect colonic mural defects at MRE in such
conditions (Fig 14).

dence of colonic diverticulosis in UC. The decreased muscular
tone of the colon, which is the consequence of chronic mural
inflammation in UC, could play a protective role in develop-
ing diverticulosis (38). Although uncommon, concurrent di-
verticulosis with acute diverticulitis in UC has been reported,

Extraintestinal Manifestations
A wide range of extraintestinal manifestations associated with
IBD, particularly UC, have been described. The factors relating
to the pathogenesis of the extraintestinal manifestations are as-
sumed to be similar to those contributing to intestinal inflam-
mation, with a dominant role of immune-related mechanisms.
However, the exact pathogenesis has not been well explained
(10,40). The frequency of extraintestinal manifestations of IBD
is estimated to be 21%–47% (41). Given the field of coverage at
MRE, the hepatobiliary system, pancreas, mesenteric vessels,
urinary  system,  and  musculoskeletal  structures  may  show
related  manifestations  in  the  MRE  examination.  Detection  of
these  abnormalities  is  helpful  to  avoid  treatment  delay  and
perform further diagnostic procedures, as necessary. Primary
sclerosing cholangitis and ankylosing spondylitis usually occur
independently of IBD activity (40).

Pneumatosis Intestinalis
Pneumatosis intestinalis reflects the presence of gas in the wall
of the small or large bowel that can be either benign or related
to transmural necrosis. The circular form of pneumatosis in-
testinalis is usually benign, while a linear or bubblelike form
can  be  due  to  benign  or  life-threatening  conditions.  Benign
pneumatosis intestinalis in the colon (ie, pneumatosis coli) is
usually characterized by multiple gas-containing submucosal
or subserosal cysts (56). Although it is easily diagnosed at CT, it
mimics a submucosal mass or external impression at colonos-
copy.  The  occurrence  of  pneumatosis  intestinalis  in  patients
with IBD has been rarely reported. The only case series (57) on
UC and pneumatosis intestinalis, to our knowledge, included
four  patients  and  revealed  no  major  complications  or  differ-
ences in treatment. The low signal intensity of intramural gas
during  all  sequences,  with  susceptibility  artifacts  on  images
acquired  with  gradient-echo  techniques,  can  help  to  detect
pneumatosis intestinalis at MRE (Fig 23).

Lymphoma
The link between immunosuppressive therapy in UC and the
development of lymphoma has been frequently studied (63).
Monotherapy of thiopurines or combination therapy of anti–
tumor necrosis factor
 and thiopurines has been associated
with an increased risk of lymphoma in patients with IBD (64–
66). Study results on the association between monotherapy of
anti–tumor necrosis factor agents and lymphoma incidence
have  been  controversial;  some  studies  supported  it  (65,66)
and  some  did  not  demonstrate  it  (67,68).  Although  future
large  prospective  studies  are  needed  to  address  the  risk  of
anti–tumor necrosis factor monotherapy and lymphoma, the
therapeutic  effectiveness  outweighs  the  probably  increased
risk of developing lymphoma.

The most common histologic subtype has been reported
to  be  non-Hodgkin  lymphoma,  which  is  similar  to  lym-
phoma in patients without IBD. On the other hand, primary

intestinal lymphoma constitutes a large proportion of the af-
fected patients with IBD compared with those with de novo
lymphoma (22%–75%). Patients with lymphoma and under-
lying  IBD  have  shown  equivalent  survival  to  those  with  de
novo lymphoma (69).

Data on imaging features of colonic lymphoma in patients
with IBD are scarce, to our knowledge. Overall, extension to
the terminal ileum, a well-defined outer margin, the absence
of an obstructive pattern, and the lack of desmoplastic reac-
tion  or  invasion  to  adjacent  structures  can  help  to  differen-
tiate colonic lymphoma from adenocarcinoma, regardless of
underlying IBD (70). Moreover, attention must be paid to de-
tect  signs  of  extraintestinal  lymphoma  in  patients  with  IBD
(eg,  organomegaly,  solid  organ  or  bone  marrow  infiltration,
or para-aortic and parailiac lymphadenopathy). Imaging fea-
tures of colonic or systemic lymphoma superimposed on UC
can  be  assessed  with  MRE,  especially  in  patients  who  have
undergone  long-standing  immunosuppressive  therapy  who
present with systemic complaints (eg, fever or weight loss). In
such patients, signs of lymphoma might be overlooked at the
endoscopic examination (Fig 25).

Mimics of UC
There is a broad spectrum of conditions to consider in the dif-
ferential  diagnosis  of  UC.  Various  inflammatory,  infectious,
vascular, drug-induced, and immune-mediated diseases can
mimic  UC  and  merit  a  multimodality  diagnostic  approach.
The most challenging diagnosis to differentiate is colonic CD.
The incidence of isolated CD colitis is approximately 14%–32%
at  the  onset  of  disease  (77).  Discontinuous  colonic  involve-
ment, asymmetric mural thickening, transmural disease, for-
mation of a sinus or fistula, and strictures are typical descrip-
tors of CD colitis (Fig S11). In comparison with CD, any colonic
stricture  development  in  a  patient  with  UC  necessitates  the
exclusion of a superimposed malignancy.

When to Consider MRE in UC
UC manifests as a disease of the colonic mucosa that is often
entirely  accessible  with  colonoscopy;  therefore,  radiologists
have  traditionally  underused  advanced  intestinal  imaging
modalities  in  the  management  of  UC  compared  with  their
use in CD. MRC was introduced for the assessment of colonic
polyps and inflammation, but it is not widely applied because
of a lack of patient acceptance and the superiority of CT colo-
nography  for  the  detection  of  polyps  (79,80).  MRC  requires
retrograde filling of the entire colon with fluid using a rectal
tube or Foley catheter and is an unpleasant procedure for the
patient. MRC also does not allow imaging of the small bowel.

MRE  with  colonic  preparation  can  provide  valuable  data
about small- and large-bowel involvement in a patient with IBD

Conclusion
MRE has an important role in the assessment of UC and has
been underused compared with its use for CD. Although UC
is characterized as an inflammatory disease of the colorectal
mucosa,  various  related  abnormalities  might  be  underdiag-
nosed with endoscopic examination. The radiologist can play
a pivotal role in the management of UC with MRE by exploring
what is beyond endoscopy.
