Cross-sectional and fluoroscopic imaging play a pivotal role in evaluat-
ing the jejunum and ileum because of less accessibility for endoscopic
evaluation  compared  with  the  stomach,  duodenum,  or  colon.  For  the
radiologist in training, assessment of the small bowel can be challeng-
ing, in large part due to the overlapping imaging features of many small
bowel diseases. A pattern-based approach to small bowel imaging can
be useful in potentially narrowing the differential diagnosis and allow-
ing the radiologist to offer a more useful impression.

The  slide  presentation  comprises  an  extensive  and  illustrative  re-
view of clinical and radiologic aspects of multiple small bowel diseases.
These are classified, for didactic purposes, into seven main categories:
(a) benign diseases: diverticulitis, celiac sprue, Crohn disease, sclero-
derma, and infection; (b) trauma and hemorrhage: blunt trauma, pen-
etrating  trauma,  luminal  hemorrhage,  and  intramural  hemorrhage;
(c) vascular disease: ischemia and vasculitis; (d) obstruction: mechani-
cal and closed-loop obstruction; (e) treatment related: graft versus host
disease,  nonsteroidal  anti-inflammatory  drug  (NSAID)  enteropathy,
angioedema, radiation enteritis, and lymphangiectasia; (f) benign neo-
plasms: polyposis, gastrointestinal stromal tumor (GIST), and lipoma;
and (g) malignant neoplasms: adenocarcinoma, GIST, carcinoid, lym-
phoma, and metastatic disease.

Although cross-sectional imaging is emphasized in the slide presen-
tation,  fluoroscopic  examples  are  presented  when  available.  Features
shared across disease entities, such as aneurysmal luminal dilatation
of masses exhibiting an exoenteric growth pattern (Fig 1) and features
specific to a disease, such as multiple short-segment strictures or dia-
phragms in NSAID enteropathy (Fig 2), are shown.

The radiologist should be aware of the spectrum and typical imag-
ing  appearances  of  small  bowel  diseases  to  accurately  guide  clinical
management.

TEACHING POINTS
	 The jejunum and ileum are less accessible than the stomach, duodenum, and
colon  for  endoscopic  evaluation.  Cross-sectional  and  fluoroscopic  imaging
play a key role in detection and diagnosis of small bowel disease.

	 A pattern-based approach integrating the appearance of wall thickening, de-
gree and symmetry of thickening, length and segment of involvement, and
clinical history can hone an otherwise broad differential diagnosis into a more
useful impression.

	 Awareness of the spectrum and typical appearance of small bowel diseases

facilitates the radiologist guiding clinical management.
