Mesotheliomas and Benign Mesothelial Tumors:
Update on Pathologic and Imaging Findings

Apurva Bonde, MD • Ramandeep Singh, MD • Srinivasa R. Prasad, MD • Dhiraj Kamireddy • Aarushi Aggarwal, MD • Nisha Ramani, MD
Sachin Saboo, MD • Krishna Shanbhogue, MD • Anil K. Dasyam, MD • Venkata S. Katabathina, MD

Author affiliations, funding, and conflicts of interest are listed at the end of this article.

A diverse spectrum of benign entities and malignant neoplasms originate
from the monotonous mesothelium that lines the serosal membranes of
the pleural, pericardial, and peritoneal cavities. The mesothelium of myriad
sites shows a common origin from the lateral plate mesoderm; primary me-
sothelial tumors thus demonstrate similar pathogenesis, imaging findings,
and treatment options. Significant changes have been made in the 2021
World Health Organization (WHO) classification schemata of the pleural
and pericardial tumors on the basis of recent advances in pathology and
genetics. While malignant mesotheliomas are biologically aggressive malig-
nancies that occur primarily in patients exposed to asbestos with attendant
poor survival rates, well-differentiated papillary mesothelial tumors and
adenomatoid tumors charter a benign clinical course with an excellent
prognosis. Mesothelioma in situ is a newly characterized entity represented
by recurrent unexplained pleural effusions without any identifiable mass at
imaging or thoracoscopy. Immunohistochemical markers based on BAP1,
MTAP, CDKN2A, and TRAF7 gene mutations help differentiate diffuse
mesotheliomas from benign mesothelial proliferations and localized meso-
theliomas. Cross-sectional imaging modalities, including US, CT, MRI, and
fluorine 18–fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) PET/CT, permit diagnosis and play
a major role in staging and assessing surgical resectability. Imaging studies
are invaluable in providing noninvasive and quantitative assessment of
tumor response in patients with unresectable disease. Owing to significant
overlap in patient characteristics and pathomorphology, accurate diagnosis
based on advanced histopathology techniques and genetic abnormalities is
imperative for optimal management and prognostication. While patients
with nonepithelioid pleural mesotheliomas benefit from immunotherapy,
novel targeted therapies for CDKN2A-, NF2-, and BAP1–altered mesothelio-
mas are under consideration.

TEACHING POINTS
	 The 2021 WHO classification schemata for the pleura and pericardium rec-
ognize a wide spectrum of benign, preinvasive, and malignant mesothelial
tumors on the basis of histopathologic features, tumor genetics, and clini-
cal characteristics as well as imaging features. The term malignant as a pre-
fix is no longer included for diffuse and localized mesotheliomas, indicating
that all of them are malignant.

	 The imaging modality of choice is CT, with classic imaging findings includ-
ing unilateral pleural effusion, irregular or nodular pleural thickening (>1 cm
thick),  infiltration  of  adjacent  tissues,  mediastinal  lymphadenopathy,  and
intrathoracic or extrathoracic metastases.

	 Imaging  findings  may  vary  on  the  basis  of  the  severity  of  the  disease.
Characteristic imaging findings include peritoneal thickening with perito-
neal or mesenteric soft-tissue nodules or masses or omental caking, with
or without ascites. Three morphologic subtypes of peritoneal mesothelio-
ma are described: (a) wet-type peritoneal mesothelioma with predominant
ascites and associated peritoneal thickening or plaques or small nodules;
(b) dry-type peritoneal mesothelioma, when peritoneal masses are seen
without ascites; and (c) mixed-type peritoneal mesothelioma, when fea-
tures of both are present.

	 Cisplatin with pemetrexed has been a standard chemotherapeutic treat-
ment  over  the  years  for  pleural  and  peritoneal  mesothelioma;  the  addi-
tion of bevacizumab to this regimen is shown to improve overall survival.
Combination  immunotherapy  with  nivolumab  and  ipili mumab  was  ap-
proved as first-line systemic therapy for pleural mesothelioma in 2020, with
improved quality of life and survival rates. The survival benefits of immuno-
therapy are proved to be better for nonepithelioid pleural mesothelioma.
	 At imaging, a nonenhancing thin-walled multilocular cystic lesion or cluster
of multiple thin-walled cysts is the characteristic appearance of peritoneal
inclusion cyst. These lesions may be free floating or adherent to underlying
viscera and often are large with odd shapes conforming to the pelvic cav-
ity and organs. The finding of a normal ovary suspended within a web of
adhesions, referred to as the spiderweb appearance, is typical at imaging.

Introduction
The  pleural,  pericardial,  and  peritoneal  cavities  and  the  tu-
nica vaginalis of the testes embryologically develop from co-
alescence and resegmentation of primitive coelomic spaces of
mesodermal origin (1,2). These coelomic cavities are lined by
a monolayered mesothelium. It is remarkable that a plethora
of developmental disorders and neoplasms with variable tu-
mor genetics, histopathologic features, and biology originate
from the histologically “bland” mesothelium (Table 1) (3,4).

While pleural, pericardial, peritoneal, and tunica vagina-
lis mesotheliomas comprise malignant tumors, adenomatoid
tumor,  well-differentiated  papillary  mesothelial
tumor
(WDPMT),  and  peritoneal  inclusion  cyst  represent  benign
entities (3). In the 2021 World Health Organization (WHO)
classification for the pleural and pericardial tumors of meso-
thelial origin, substantial changes have been made on the ba-
sis of updated information on tumor pathogenesis (3,4). Im-
munotherapy with ipilimumab and nivolumab was recently
approved for select mesotheliomas, and novel therapies are
being evaluated to target select genetic abnormalities.

Mesotheliomas  and  benign  mesothelial  lesions  demon-
strate characteristic imaging findings at radiography, US, CT,
MRI,  and  fluorine  18  (18F)–fluorodeoxyglucose  (FDG)  PET/
CT,  which  help  in  appropriate  diagnosis.  Additionally,  im-
aging plays a pivotal role in monitoring treatment follow-up
and long-term surveillance of mesothelial tumors (5–7). It is
important to note that select tumorlike lesions and secondary
neoplasms  involving  the  mesothelium  may  mimic  primary
tumors;  imaging  findings  and  image-guided  biopsy  help  in
further characterization of diverse entities.

In  this  article,  we  describe  recent  advances  in  genetics,
histopathology, the 2021 WHO classification, and the tumor
biology of a diverse spectrum of malignant and benign me-
sothelial  neoplasms  with  implications  for  diagnosis,  man-
agement, prognostication, and surveillance.

Mesothelium: Histologic, Physiologic, and
Embryologic Features
The mesothelium, consisting of simple squamous epithelium,
overlies  the  elastic  lamina  and  specialized  submesothelial
connective  tissue  of  mesenchymal  origin  (1,2).  The  parietal
mesothelium is the outer layer of the body cavities, while vis-
ceral mesothelium is the inner layer of body cavities envelop-
ing the viscera. Both parietal and visceral mesothelium have
distinctive somatic and splanchnic vascular supply, innerva-
tion, and lymphatics (1,2).

Mesothelial cells primarily provide a nonadhesive surface
for movement of organs through secretion of glycosamino-
glycans and surfactants and mesothelial microvilli reducing
friction (2). The microvilli also play a role in solute absorp-
tion  and  fluid  regulation,  actively  transporting  water  and
other  exudates  to  maintain  cavity  pressures  and  volumes
(2). Mesothelial cells are involved in antigen recognition of
pathogens,  leading  to  activation  of  the  inflammatory  cas-
cade and microbial clearance (8).

Understanding  embryology  is  vital  in  demonstrating  the
genetic  overlap  between  the  mesothelium  found  at  different
anatomic  locations.  Formation  of  the  mesothelium  from  the
lateral plate mesoderm begins during the 3rd week of life (Fig
1) (9,10). The lateral plate mesoderm is formed during gastru-
lation; subsequent clefts within the lateral plate mesoderm lead

2021 WHO Classification of Tumors of
Mesothelium
The  2021  WHO  classification  schemata  for  the  pleura  and
pericardium  recognize  a  wide  spectrum  of  benign,  preinva-
sive, and malignant mesothelial tumors on the basis of histo-
pathologic features, tumor genetics, and clinical characteris-
tics as well as imaging features (3,4). The term malignant as a
prefix is no longer included for diffuse and localized mesothe-
liomas, indicating that all of them are malignant (3,4). Diffuse
mesotheliomas, the most common and most lethal mesothe-
lial malignancies, are characterized by somatic copy number
abnormalities  and  primarily  deletions.  Mesotheliomas  fre-
quently harbor BAP1, NF2, TP53, and SETD2 gene mutations
(3,11).  Localized  mesotheliomas  are  solitary,  circumscribed,
indolent tumors that portend a better prognosis (than diffuse
mesotheliomas)  after  surgical  resection;  these  show  genetic
changes  that  may  overlap  with  those  of  diffuse  mesothelio-
mas and adenomatoid tumors (12).

A newly defined entity, mesothelioma in situ, manifesting as
recurrent pleural effusions, has been recently described on the
basis of strict clinicopathologic criteria (13). WDPMT, a benign
neoplasm with indolent behavior and excellent prognosis, re-
places the previously used term of well-differentiated papillary

mesothelioma  (3,14). These  tumors  occur  more  commonly  in
the  peritoneum  than  in  the  pleura.  Adenomatoid  tumors  are
benign neoplasms with indolent behavior that show a predilec-
tion to involve the paratesticular region and the serosal surface
of the uterus and fallopian tube (15). Genital tract adenomatoid
tumors are defined by TRAF7 mutations, which lead to aber-
rant activation of the nuclear factor kappa light chain enhancer
of activated B cells (NF-kB) pathway (16).

Pathogenesis and Pathologic Features of
Mesotheliomas: Recent Updates
Germline mutations in cancer predisposition genes are respon-
sible  for  up  to  12%  of  pleural  mesotheliomas;  BRCA1-associ-
ated protein (BAP1) is the most commonly mutated gene (17).
Pleural mesotheliomas with germline mutations in cancer pre-
disposition  genes  are  more  common  in  younger  patients  and
women, those without a history of asbestos exposure, and those
with a family history of cancer or individual history of cancer
(melanoma,  mesothelioma,  breast  cancer)  (17).  CDKN2A  de-
letion is a specific marker for malignant mesothelial prolifera-
tion. It has sensitivity of 50%–65% for pleural mesothelioma, up
to 80%–100% for the sarcomatoid type of pleural mesothelioma,
and 25%–29% for peritoneal mesothelioma (18). While meso-
theliomas in younger adults may also rarely harbor recurrent
EWSR1- or FUS-ATF1 fusions (19), a subset of mesotheliomas
found  in  young  women  shows  TP53  and  SETDB1  commuta-
tions with “genomic near-haploidization” (20).

BAP1  tumor  predisposition  syndrome  is  an  autosomal
dominant  syndrome  that  predisposes  patients  to  early  de-
velopment  of  a  plethora  of  malignancies,  including  uveal
melanoma,  mesotheliomas,  cutaneous  melanomas,  clear
cell  renal  cell  carcinoma,  and  basal  cell  carcinoma  (in  de-
creasing order of frequency) (21). Mesotheliomas related to
BAP1 germline mutations account for approximately 1%–5%
of all cases (21). The BAP1 tumor suppressor gene encodes
ubiquitin hydrolase with multiple functions, including DNA
repair,  chromatin  remodeling,  cellular  proliferation,  and
differentiation (22). Inactivating BAP1 mutations are postu-
lated to confer increased sensitivity to the carcinogenicity of
asbestos even at low levels of exposure (21).

A plethora of pathogenic events at the molecular level are
responsible for the carcinogenesis of mesothelioma after as-
bestos exposure (Fig 2) (17). As an initial response, the mac-
rophages try to phagocytize and clear asbestos fibers without
effect; in this process, they release reactive oxygen and nitro-
gen  species  (ROS/RNS),  which  promote  genotoxic  damage

and  recruit  other  immune  and  inflammatory  cells  (17,23).
Repeated DNA damage to mesothelial cells results in multi-
ple oncogenic mutations of various genes involved in DNA
repair (BAP1/TP53), the Hippo pathway (NF2 and LATS1/2),
the  mTOR  pathway  (TSC1/2  and  PI3K),  DNA  methylation
(SETD2),  and  cell  cycle  control  (CDKN2A/B)  (17).  The  in-
flammatory mediators promote cell survival and ultimately
mesothelioma  growth  (17).  Uncontrolled  regulation  of  ep-
ithelial  to  mesenchymal  transition  also  plays  a  pivotal  role
in the pathogenesis, tumor progression, and immune system
evasion of mesotheliomas; this may be an appropriate target
for potential cancer therapies (24,25).

disease (3,26,27). Mesotheliomas are histopathologically clas-

Histopathologic  analysis,  immunohistochemistry,  and
genetic testing with fluorescent in situ hybridization (FISH)
or  next-generation  sequencing  (NGS)  help  differentiate
diffuse  mesotheliomas  from  localized  mesotheliomas  and
benign  or  reactive  mesothelial  proliferations  (3,26,28).  A
tumefactive  or  haphazard  growth  pattern  and  frank  stro-
mal invasion into subserosal mesenchymal fat are the most
reliable histologic criteria for mesotheliomas (3,17). Loss of
BAP1 or MTAP at immunohistochemistry; loss of CDKN2A
at FISH; or BAP1,  CDKN2A, or NF2 mutations detected at
NGS  allow  differentiation  of  mesotheliomas  from  benign
mesothelial proliferations (3).

Localized  mesotheliomas  are  characterized  by  lack  of
invasion beyond the circumscribed borders of the tumor in
surgical  resection  specimens,  with  histologic  features  and
immunophenotypes  similar  to  those  of  diffuse  mesotheli-
omas  (3,29).  Select  localized  mesotheliomas  demonstrate
distinctive  genetic  features  such  as  TRAF7  mutations  and
genomic  near-haploidization  with  a  good  prognosis  (12).
Mesothelioma  in  situ  is  identified  as  a  single  layer  of  rela-
tively bland mesothelial cells growing along the pleural sur-
face with loss of BAP1 or MTAP at immunohistochemistry
or homozygous deletion of CDKN2A at FISH (3,13).

Role of Multimodality Imaging
A  multitude  of  imaging  techniques,  including  radiography,
US,  CT,  MRI,  and  18F-FDG  PET/CT,  play  an  important  role
in diagnosis, staging, treatment follow-up, detecting compli-
cations, and long-term surveillance (Tables 2, 3) (5,30). Chest

radiography is usually the initial imaging modality performed
for  suspected  thoracic  abnormalities  and  may  demonstrate
several findings, including pleural thickening, calcifications,
masses, and effusions (Fig 4). CT is required for accurate tu-
mor localization, differentiation of cystic from solid tumors,
local  staging,  and  assessment  for  metastatic  disease  (Fig  4)
(5,31).  MRI  is  particularly  useful  for  evaluating  pleural  and
pericardial mesotheliomas, for determining tumor origin, lo-
cal-regional staging, and cardiac function assessment (Fig 5)
(5,31). 18F-FDG PET/CT is usually performed to detect recur-
rent or residual and metastatic disease during initial staging
and surveillance for malignant tumors.

CT  of  the  abdomen  and  pelvis  is  the  primary  imaging
modality  for  assessing  peritoneal  mesothelioma;  MRI  and
18F-FDG  PET/CT  play  a  supplemental  role  to  CT  in  staging
and surveillance (Fig 6) (6,30,32,33). Scrotal US is the initial
imaging  modality  performed  to  evaluate  scrotal  masses.  US
readily allows differentiation of testicular masses from extra-
testicular  lesions  (Fig  7).  While  scrotal  MRI  allows  confir-
mation of the diagnosis of lipoma, liposarcoma, and fibrous
pseudotumor, other paratesticular masses are difficult to dif-
ferentiate at MRI and are often diagnosed at histopathologic
analysis after surgical resection (34).

Mesotheliomas
Mesotheliomas are rare biologically aggressive malignancies
that predominantly affect the pleura (~90%). The peritoneum
(10%–15%),  pericardium  (<1%),  and  tunica  vaginalis  testis
(<1%)  are  uncommon  target  sites  of  involvement  (17,35).
Mesotheliomas are usually diagnosed late, when radical sur-
gery is not feasible. Patients typically present with nonspecific
symptoms and signs, leading to delayed diagnosis (26,36).

While pleural mesotheliomas predominantly afflict older
men (male-to-female ratio = 3–4:1), peritoneal mesothelio-
mas can occur in women and at an earlier median age (63
years) than pleural mesotheliomas (71 years) (36). Chronic
asbestos  exposure  is  seen  in  greater  than  90%  of  patients

Pleural Mesothelioma
Pleural mesothelioma originates from the mesothelial cells of
the parieta or visceral pleura and is the most common primary
pleural neoplasm and the second most common pleural ma-

lignancy after pleural metastases (5). Diffuse mesothelioma is
the most common form, with a poor prognosis; early diagno-
sis and timely therapy significantly improve overall survival
(5,37). Imaging plays a pivotal role in TNM staging by allow-
ing accurate assessment of the local extent of the primary tu-
mor, lymph node involvement, and metastatic disease (5,38).

Chest radiography is usually the initial imaging modality
and  commonly  shows  unilateral  pleural  effusion  or  a  pleu-
ra-based mass (5,37) (Fig 4). Lung metastases and mediastinal
lymphadenopathy  manifest  as  lung  nodules  or  masses  and
irregular mediastinal contours (5,38). The imaging modality
of choice is CT, with classic imaging findings including uni-
lateral  pleural  effusion,  irregular  or  nodular  pleural  thick-
ening  (>1  cm  thick),  infiltration  of  adjacent  tissues,  medi-
astinal  lymphadenopathy,  and  intrathoracic  or  extrathoracic

Pericardial Mesothelioma
Pericardial mesothelioma is the most common primary pericar-
dial malignancy, occurring commonly in men during the 5th to
7th decades (43,44). The clinical manifestations are nonspecific
and may include conduction abnormalities, acute pericarditis,
cardiac tamponade, constrictive pericarditis, and heart failure
(43,44). Frequently, the clinical manifestation of the tumor is
in the advanced stages, associated with a dismal prognosis (44).

Imaging findings of pericardial mesothelioma are nonspe-
cific and mimic those of other pericardial pathologic condi-
tions  (31,45).  The  echocardiographic  findings  include  peri-
cardial  effusion,  pericardial  thickening,  cardiac  tamponade,
and  a  mass;  US-guided  pericardiocentesis  commonly  yields
hemorrhagic fluid (31,44,45). Chest radiography may demon-
strate cardiac or mediastinal enlargement and irregular car-
diac contour (44). The typical findings at contrast-enhanced
CT are a heterogeneously enhancing (predominantly periph-
eral) pericardial mass or irregular thickening with invasion of
the adjacent mediastinal structures and associated pericardial
effusion (Fig 11) (31,44,45).

MRI offers better soft-tissue evaluation and can be partic-
ularly useful for determining the degree of cardiac constric-
tion  and  diastolic  cardiac  impairment  using  real-time  cine
steady-state  free-precession  (SSFP)  imaging  and  cine-grid
and  gated  short-axis  cine  SSFP  sequences  (31,46).  As  peri-
cardial mesothelioma is frequently hypermetabolic, intense
FDG uptake is seen, particularly in areas of nodularity (Fig
11)  (31,44,45).  Imaging  reveals  metastatic  disease,  com-

Peritoneal Mesothelioma
Peritoneal  mesothelioma  is  a  rare  lethal  malignancy  of  the
peritoneum (36). Patients commonly present with nonspecific
symptoms; aggressive subtypes can manifest with rapidly pro-
gressive symptoms, including bowel obstruction (36).

Estimating  the  overall  disease  burden  is  the  primary
goal  of  imaging  in  peritoneal  mesothelioma.  The  perito-
neal  carcinomatosis  index  (PCI)  score  is  helpful  in  quan-
titative  assessment  of  the  peritoneal  disease  burden  (47).
CT is the initial modality of choice for peritoneal mesothe-
lioma  (6,30).  CT  is  useful  for  diagnosis,  determining  the
extent of disease and surgical resectability, staging, and fol-
low-up (48).

MRI has better spatial resolution than CT and hence more
sensitivity  for  smaller  tumors.  Diffusion-weighted  imaging
(DWI)  and  gadolinium-enhanced  MRI  improve  the  detect-
ability  of  smaller  tumors  (47,48).  18F-FDG  PET  is  comple-
mentary  to  CT  for  detecting  peritoneal  lesions  and  distant
metastatic  disease  (48).  There  is  lack  of  uniformity  and  a
standardized  approach  to  imaging  evaluation  of  peritoneal
mesothelioma;  more  research  is needed for standardization
(47,48).  Gallium  68  (68Ga)–fibroblast-activating  protein-spe-
cific  inhibitor  (FAPI)  is  a  newer  radiopharmaceutical  that
allows better detection rates than 18F-FDG PET (49).

Imaging  findings  may  vary  on  the  basis  of  the  severity
of the disease (47). Characteristic imaging findings include
peritoneal thickening with peritoneal or mesenteric soft-tis-
sue  nodules  or  masses  or  omental  caking,  with  or  without
ascites (Fig 12) (30,32). Three morphologic subtypes of peri-
toneal  mesothelioma  are  described:  (a)  wet-type  peritoneal
mesothelioma, with predominant ascites and associated peri-
toneal thickening or plaques or small nodules; (b) dry-type
peritoneal  mesothelioma,  when  peritoneal  masses  are  seen
without ascites; and (c) mixed-type peritoneal mesothelioma,
when  features  of  both  are  present  (Fig  12)  (30).  Calcifica-
tions are rare in peritoneal mesothelioma, although calcified
pleural plaques are widespread in pleural counterparts (6).
Localized  peritoneal  mesothelioma  manifests  as  a  heteroge-
neous solid mass and may invade other viscera (6).

Epithelioid  mesothelioma  is  the  most  common  histologic
subtype,  often  demonstrating  peritoneal  thickening,  nodules,
and  ascites,  while  sarcomatoid  mesothelioma  is  the  most  ag-
gressive subtype, manifesting as single or multiple large perito-
neal masses (32,47). Correlation between CT features and the
histologic type of peritoneal mesothelioma is noted with more
aggressive visceral organ infiltration, commonly the liver and
other solid organs. The sarcomatoid type is more frequently as-
sociated with liver metastases followed by lung metastases than
the epithelioid type (32). Discrete masses were more commonly
found  with  the  sarcomatoid  subtype,  while  ascites  was  more
widely reported with the epithelioid subtype (32).

Management
A multidisciplinary team approach with surgery, chemother-
apy, and radiation therapy is required for treatment planning
and  management  of  mesotheliomas,  considering  tumor  re-
sectability, histologic subtype, and radiologic staging (52,53).
Image-guided or thoracoscopic or laparoscopic biopsy is re-
quired for pathologic diagnosis and subtyping to allow plan-
ning of optimal treatment (53).

Pleurectomy and decortication or en bloc resection of the
lung with the visceral and parietal pleura can be performed
for  resectable  pleural  mesothelioma  (52,53).  Surgical  resec-
tion of pericardial mesothelioma is curative in patients with
localized  tumors;  palliative  pericardiectomy  is  performed  to
relieve  symptoms  of  constrictive  morphology  and  prevent
cardiac  tamponade  in  advanced  disease  (44).  Cytoreductive
surgery  with  hyperthermic  intraperitoneal  chemotherapy
(HIPEC) is the standard of reference for treatment of perito-
neal mesothelioma (54). Radical orchiectomy is the treatment
of choice for localized tunica vaginalis mesothelioma; if there
is  suspicion  for  metastases,  then  staging  lymphadenectomy
must be performed (50).

For patients with mesothelioma who are poor candidates for
surgery or with unresectable disease, a combination of systemic

Benign and Preinvasive Mesothelial Tumors

Mesothelioma in Situ
Mesothelioma  in  situ  should  be  suspected  in  patients  with
recurrent unilateral pleural effusions with no apparent cause
and without any identifiable mass at imaging or thoracoscopy
to  suggest  malignancy  (13).  Pleural  or  peritoneal  biopsy  is
recommended even in clinically benign-appearing asbestosis
effusion or ascites, and BAP1 testing is recommended of even
bland-appearing  mesothelial  cells  (13).  This  approach  can
help  identify  early  mesothelioma  and  significantly  improve
overall survival.

Adenomatoid Tumor
Previously referred to as benign mesothelioma, adenomatoid
tumor is a benign localized mesothelial proliferation that pri-
marily involves the male and female genital tract (15). Adeno-
matoid tumor involving the mesentery, omentum, or other ab-

dominopelvic organs is rare (60). Adenomatoid tumor of the
testes and epididymis originating from the tunica vaginalis or
tunica albuginea is common, constitutes one-third of parates-
ticular tumors, and is commonly seen to involve the epididy-
mal tail (15). At histopathologic analysis, adenomatoid tumor
consists of proliferation of epithelioid mesothelial cells with
fibrotic  or  smooth  muscle  stroma;  the  mesothelial  cells  are
arranged  in  sheets,  trabeculae,  or  glandlike  structures  (15).
Most  adenomatoid  tumors  are  asymptomatic,  and  30%  may
manifest as scrotal pain (34,61).

At US, adenomatoid tumor appears as a well-circumscribed
solid iso- to hypoechoic lesion abutting the inferior pole of the
testis  or  tail  of  the  epididymis,  ranging  from  0.5  to  5  cm  in
size  (34,62)  (Fig  15).  Occasionally,  adenomatoid  tumor  may
appear as a cystic lesion. At MRI, this extratesticular lesion is
hypointense to testicular parenchyma on T2-weighted images
and hypoenhancing to testicular parenchyma on contrast-en-
hanced images (61,62). After histologic confirmation with in-
traoperative frozen section, surgical resection is curative, with
no reported recurrences (62).

Well-Differentiated Papillary Mesothelial Tumor
Previously referred to as well-differentiated papillary mesothe-
lioma, this tumor is now renamed well-differentiated papillary
mesothelial  tumor  (WDPMT)  according  to  the  2021  WHO
classification  schemata  (3,14).  This  rare  tumor,  considered
a benign to low-grade indolent malignancy, is typically seen
arising  from  the  peritoneum  of  women  of  reproductive  age
who lack asbestos exposure (60,63). The tunica vaginalis tes-
tis,  pleura,  and  pericardium  are  rare  target  sites  of  involve-
ment. Most cases occur in asymptomatic patients and are inci-
dentally diagnosed. However, long-term follow-up is required
to exclude malignant transformation (63).

WDPMT  is  histopathologically  characterized  by  papillary
architecture with a fibromyxoid core lined by a monolayer of
bland mesothelial cells that lack stromal invasion (Fig 16) (60).
WDPMT is genetically distinct from mesothelioma; the classic
genetic  abnormalities  associated  with  malignant  tumors  are
typically absent. A recent study found characteristic mutations
involving EHD1, FBXO10, ATM, and TP73 in this tumor (64).
Another study found that WDPMT harbored mutually exclu-
sive missense mutations involving TRAF7 or CDC42 (65).

There is scant literature on the radiology of WDPMT ow-
ing to lack of specific imaging findings. CT or MRI can show
normal findings, single or multiple small peritoneal nodules,
or  scattered  areas  of  plaquelike  peritoneal  thickening  with
or without calcifications. Most of these tumors are less than
1 cm and can be up to 3 cm. Sometimes these tiny nodules
are challenging to visualize at CT or MRI. Diffuse or nodular
peritoneal  calcifications  without  a  mass  at  CT  can  also  be
seen (Figs 16, 17) (30,47,66).

WDPMT  commonly  manifests  as  multifocal  disease;  re-
currence is rare. Patients with localized tumors have a better
prognosis  than  those  with  multifocal  or  multicavitary  dis-
ease.  Complete  surgical  resection  is  considered  the  treat-
ment of choice (66). Because of significant overlap in histo-
pathologic  findings,  overtreatment  with  debulking  surgery

Developmental Conditions of the Mesothelium

Pericardial Cyst
Pericardial  cyst  arises  owing  to  congenital  aberration  in  the
mediastinal mesothelium during formation of coelomic cav-
ities  (71–73).  It  commonly  manifests  as  a  unilocular,  thin-
walled, mesothelium-lined cyst in the anterior cardiophrenic
angle (on the right more than on the left) (73). Pericardial cyst
is  frequently  asymptomatic;  symptoms  such  as  chest  pain,
cough, fever, arrhythmia, or compression secondary to adja-
cent structures may occur (71,72).

Pericardial cyst is seen at routine chest radiography as an
incidental  well-defined  smoothly  marginated  mass  in  the
anterior mediastinum and is cystic and thin walled at echo-
cardiography (71,73). CT shows a thin-walled nonenhancing
unilocular cystic structure abutting the pericardium (Fig 20)
(72).  MRI  demonstrates  a  T1-hypointense  and  T2-hyperin-
tense unilocular cystic lesion (Fig 20). Variable attenuation or
signal intensity at CT or MRI may be seen with proteinaceous
or hemorrhagic contents (71,72). Asymptomatic cases require
no  treatment,  while  symptomatic  patients  or  those  with  in-
conclusive  imaging  results  require  surgical  resection  of  the
cyst or percutaneous image-guided drainage (71–73).

Peritoneal Mesothelial Cyst
Peritoneal mesothelial cyst is a benign cystic lesion of the peri-
toneal cavity resulting from incomplete fusion of mesothelial
peritoneal surfaces (70). It is commonly seen in children and
young adults but can also be found in older adults. The most
common locations include the small-bowel mesentery, meso-
colon, omentum, and paraovarian region in women (70). Most
peritoneal mesothelial cysts are asymptomatic; however, they
may become symptomatic from rupture, infection, torsion, or
hemorrhage (33,70).

Pathologically,  this  lesion  is  a  thin-walled  unilocular  cyst
lined  by  mesothelial  cells  with  an  outer  wall  of  fibrosis.  At
imaging, it appears as a well-defined simple cystic lesion (Fig
21)  (33,70).  Complete  surgical  resection  is  the  treatment  of
choice  for  symptomatic  patients  (33,70).  Loculated  ascites,
enteric duplication cyst, unilocular peritoneal inclusion cyst,
and unilocular cystic lymphangioma mimic mesothelial cyst
at imaging (Fig S7).

nonspecific  symptoms,  this  lesion  is  most  commonly  dis-
covered at surgery or imaging for unrelated conditions. The
exact etiopathogenesis is unknown; chronic peritoneal irri-
tation is postulated as the primary etiologic mechanism (47).
This lesion shows a distinct predilection for involving the
pelvic peritoneum of young to middle-aged women owing to
progressively increased entrapment of fluid produced by the
ovaries  during  ovulation;  a  history  of  asbestos  exposure  is
absent (67). It is hormone sensitive and shows a reduction in
size with hormonal treatment. This lesion is associated with
local recurrence or a progressive clinical course akin to that
of a low-grade or borderline malignant neoplasm. Although
solitary lesions can occur, it typically manifests as multiple
thin-walled cysts containing serous fluid.

Histopathologically, the cyst is lined by epithelioid meso-
thelial cells without significant atypia. The cyst wall is com-
posed of fibrovascular stroma and may show hemorrhagic,
inflammatory,  or  granulation  tissue  changes  (Fig  18)  (67).
This lesion consists of reactive mesothelial hyperplastic pro-
liferation and demonstrates indolent behavior, occurring in
patients  with  antecedent  pelvic  surgery  (with  adhesions),
endometriosis, or pelvic inflammatory disease (60).

Pelvic  US  may  be  performed  as  the  initial  imaging  mo-
dality, as this lesion is seen in reproductive-age women and
is commonly present in the pelvis. CT or MRI allows deter-
mination of extent and complexity. Pelvic MRI provides bet-
ter evaluation of the involvement of adjacent pelvic organs
(33,70).  Common  locations  include  the  pelvic  peritoneum,
including the peritoneal surface of the rectum or uterus, cul-
de-sac, and paracolic gutter (30,33). Rarely, diffuse cystic le-
sions are seen throughout the peritoneal cavity (47).

the characteristic appearance of peritoneal inclusion cyst.
