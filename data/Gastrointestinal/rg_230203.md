Anatomic Basis of Rectal Cancer Staging:
Clarifying Controversies and Misconceptions

Harmeet Kaur, MD* • Helena Gabriel, MD* • Muhammad O. Awiwi, MD • Ekta Maheshwari, MD • Camila Lopes Vendrami, MD
Tsuyoshi Konishi, MD, PhD • Melissa W. Taggart, MD • Michael Magnetta, MD, MS • Linda C. Kelahan, MD • Sonia Lee, MD

*H.K. and H.G. contributed equally to this work.
Author affiliations, funding, and conflicts of interest are listed at the end of this article.

Rectal MRI provides a detailed depiction of pelvic anatomy; specifically, the relationship of the tumor to key anatomic struc-
tures, including the mesorectal fascia, anterior peritoneal reflection, and sphincter complex. However, anatomic inconsisten-
cies, pitfalls, and confusion exist, which can have a strong impact on interpretation and treatment. These areas of confusion
include the definition of the rectum itself, specifically differentiation of the rectum from the anal canal and the sigmoid colon,
and delineation of the high versus low rectum. Other areas of confusion include the relative locations of the mesorectal fascia
and peritoneum and their significance in staging and treatment, the difference between the mesorectal fascia and circumfer-
ential resection margin, involvement of the sphincter complex, and evaluation of lateral pelvic lymph nodes. The impact of
these anatomic inconsistencies and sources of confusion is significant, given the importance of MRI in depicting the anatomic
relationship of the tumor to critical pelvic structures, to triage surgical resection and neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy with the
goal of minimizing local recurrence. Evolving treatment paradigms also place MRI central in management of rectal cancer.

Introduction
As  use  of  high-resolution  rectal  MRI  in  treatment  of  rectal
cancer  continues  to  grow  in  popularity,  a  detailed  under-
standing of interpretive pitfalls, controversies related to rectal
and pelvic anatomy, staging, and new treatment paradigms is
essential for radiologists.

The  evolving  treatment  paradigms  appear  to  be  moving
management of rectal cancer in divergent directions: upfront
total  neoadjuvant  therapy  (TNT)—where  chemotherapy  is
administered in the neoadjuvant setting along with neoadju-
vant chemoradiotherapy (nCRT)—for a subset of lower rectal
tumors, while stage II and III upper rectal tumors above the
anterior  peritoneal  reflection  (APR)  are  increasingly  being
managed with only surgery. These approaches can be appreci-
ated by understanding two distinct but overlapping oncologic

perspectives in treatment of rectal cancer: prevention of local
recurrence (LR) and improvement in disease-free and over-
all survival. High-resolution MRI, with its excellent depiction
of the relationship of the primary tumor to pelvic structures,
has had a particularly significant impact—through appropri-
ate deployment of nCRT and accurate surgical planning—in
reducing LR.

The distinction between treatment of the upper and lower
rectum is related to a growing appreciation of the fact that ana-
tomic factors—primarily involvement of the potential circum-
ferential resection margin (CRM)—rather than T and N catego-
ries are the key determinants of LR and surgical management.
The current treatment paradigms are based on T and N status,
which while important for predicting prognostic outcomes—
including overall and disease-free survival—have less impact

The excellent depiction with MRI of the relationship of the
primary  tumor  to  key  pelvic  anatomic  structures—enabling
accurate  prediction  of  clear  surgical  margins—in  conjunc-
tion with improvement of surgical techniques and nCRT has
produced phenomenal results, with a decline in the LR rate
to less than 5% (1). Although currently neoadjuvant radiation
therapy  is  deployed  based  on  T  and  N  category,  its  primary
impact  is  reduction  of  LR  by  50%,  with  little  or  no  effect  on
overall survival or disease-free survival (2).

A number of recent studies where upper or mid T3N+ tu-
mors with a widely clear mesorectal fascia (MRF) were treated
with upfront surgery showed LR rates of 2.3%–3%, compara-

ble to those of tumors treated with both nCRT and surgery (3–
5). Most of the tumors in which nCRT could be avoided were
located above or at the APR. In contrast, most tumors below
the  APR  are  near  the  MRF  and  sphincter  and  consequently
require nCRT or total neoadjuvant therapy. In summary, an-
atomic factors such as the location of the tumor in the upper
or lower rectum, the relationship of the tumor to the potential
surgical margin and anal sphincter, and high-risk prognostic
factors  (ie,  T4  tumors,  extramural  vascular  invasion,  tumor
deposits, and LPLN metastasis) are the prime determinants
of  LR  and  the  need  for  nCRT  or  total  neoadjuvant  therapy.
This  is  a  radical  departure  from  previous  treatment  guide-
lines, where nCRT was suggested for all stage II and III rectal
cancers.

In terms of mesorectal lymph nodes, the Optimierte Chi-
rurgie und MRT (OCUM), QuickSilver, and MERCURY studies
have shown that lymph nodes assessed at MRI have no impact
on LR and can in large part be excluded in determination of
the need for nCRT (5,6). Since it is well established that lymph
node  involvement  in  rectal  cancer  causes  a  significant  de-
cline in overall and disease-free survival rates, this approach
seemingly  contradicts  well-established  oncologic  principles.
Lymph  node  involvement  in  rectal  cancer  unequivocally
causes a decline in overall survival, but with rare exceptions
has no impact on MRF involvement and hence on LR rates.

These evolving treatment paradigms require specific ana-
tomic delineation of the tumor relative to adjacent anatomic
landmarks, thereby placing MRI—with its exquisite depiction
of  detailed  anatomy—at  the  forefront  of  rectal  cancer  eval-
uation.  Yet,  anatomic  nuances  and  inconsistencies  exist  in
the  literature  and  in  practice,  which  can  confound  accurate
interpretation. This article attempts to address these anatom-
ic areas of confusion in staging rectal MRI using a consistent
format, which defines the issue and its impact on interpreta-
tion and staging followed by a discussion clarifying the issue.
In areas where the inconsistencies have yet to be resolved, the
authors also provide reasonable approaches to various dilem-
mas based on the literature and experiences at tertiary-care
cancer centers.

Issue 1: Defining the Anal Canal
There are confusing and overlapping definitions of the ana-
tomic anal canal, surgical anal canal, and lower rectum.

Impact
Clarity  and  consistency  in  terminology  are  important  for
communication, clinical management, and comparability of
trial populations.

Discussion
Inconsistencies in defining the anatomy in this region often
stem from the lens through which various clinicians and spe-
cialties view and use the anatomic information. The surgeon’s
assessment  is  geared  toward  the  type  of  surgical  resection,
while radiation oncologists and medical oncologists focus on
the propensity for development of peritoneal carcinomatosis
and LPLN metastasis. All these factors depend on the location
of the tumor within the rectum, as different portions of the

The anatomic anal canal lined by stratified squamous epi-
thelium arises from the embryologic ectoderm, with vascular
supply from the middle and inferior rectal vessels and lym-
phatic drainage to the superficial inguinal nodes. Its superior
border is the dentate line, which is the junction between the
columnar epithelium of the lower rectum and the squamous
epithelium  of  the  anal  canal.  The  anal  canal  extends  to  the
anatomic anal verge, defined as the junction of the anal and
perianal skin (Fig 1) (7,8).

In  contrast,  the  surgical  anal  canal  is  a  functional—not
anatomic—unit defined by surgeons, which extends from the
top  of  the  puborectalis  sling  (palpable  as  the  anorectal  ring
at rectal examination) to the anal verge and is essential from
the perspective of maintaining continence. It is defined by the
sphincter  complex,  composed  of  (a)  the  internal  sphincter,
which is the continuation of the circular muscle layer of the
muscularis  propria  separated  by  the  intersphincteric  plane,
which is a continuation of the longitudinal muscle layer of the
muscularis propria, and (b) the external sphincter, composed
of  distal  fibers  of  the  levator  ani  muscle,  puborectalis  mus-
cle, and small external sphincter muscles. The surgical anal
canal extends to the anal verge, clinically defined as the in-
ferior-most aspect of the intersphincteric groove. This is the

preferred  and  most  reproducible  landmark  for  defining  the
anal verge at MRI and by clinical palpation. It should be noted
that this is different from the anatomic definition of the anal
verge (Fig 1) (7).

The surgical anal canal contains the anatomic anal canal
(lower one-third to one-half ) and a portion of the anatomic
rectum, which lies above the dentate line (upper one-third
to one-half of surgical anal canal). This explains how rectal
ade nocarcinomas may occur within the surgical anal canal
(8).

The dentate line is occasionally seen at MRI as the junction
of vertical T2-hyperintense columnar epithelium (columns of
Morgagni) with T2-hypointense squamous epithelium (Fig 1)
(9). The anorectal ring and its relationship to the lower edge
of a rectal tumor is critical in determining the possibility of
sphincter-saving surgery. On coronal T2-weighted MR imag-
es, the puborectalis arises from the inferior aspect of the pu-
bic symphysis, extends on either side of the rectum, and fuses
posteriorly,  creating  the  anorectal  angle  (Fig  2).  On  sagittal
T2-weighted images, the plane of the anorectal ring is defined
by a line drawn from the anorectal angle to the inferior aspect
of the pubic symphysis (Fig 2).

The current norm at MRI and endoscopy and in some clin-
ical societies (Table 1) is to define the lower 0–5 cm from the
anal verge as the lower rectum. However, this overlaps with
the anatomic and surgical anal canals, and these variable ter-
minologies are the source of considerable confusion. The use
of the surgical anal canal for this region may be the most clin-
ically relevant definition, as suggested by the American Joint
Committee  on  Cancer  (AJCC)  and  National  Comprehensive
Cancer Network (Table 1).

Issue 2: Defining the Rectum
Definitions of the rectum vary, related to different approach-
es  in  defining  the  rectum,  using  either  anatomic,  measure-
ment-based, or landmark-based criteria.

Impact
Inconsistent definitions of the rectum can lead to miscatego-
rization of anal, rectal, and sigmoid cancers, impacting indi-
vidual treatment and comparison of clinical trial outcomes.

Discussion
The definition of the rectum varies between anatomists and
clinicians and among clinical specialties and societies (Table
2) (10–17).

The  anatomic  rectum  extends  superiorly  from  the  point
of fusion of the taenia coli—which are present in the sigmoid
colon  and  coalesce  to  form  the  circumferential  longitudinal
muscle of the rectum—to the dentate line inferiorly.

The sigmoid colon and rectum have different vascular sup-
plies  and  pathways  of  lymphatic  drainage  and  lymph  node
spread. The sigmoid receives its vascular supply from the sig-
moid arteries, with lymphatic drainage and metastatic lymph
nodes  following  these  vessels,  located  in  the  sigmoid  meso-
colon. Conversely, the entire rectum gets its vascular supply
from  the  superior  rectal  arteries,  with  the  lower  rectum  re-
ceiving additional vascular supply from the middle rectal ves-
sels;  lymphatic  drainage  and  metastatic  lymph  nodes  from
the rectum follow these vessels (Fig 3).

While  the  anatomic  definition  of  the  rectum  is  unchang-
ing,  there  are  numerous  surgical  and  clinical  definitions
(Table 2). There is even significant variability among individ-

ual  surgeons  for  defining  rectal  boundaries:  for  the  inferior
boundary, 43% used the anal verge, 19% the dentate line, and
15% the anorectal ring; for the superior boundary, 35% used
the peritoneal reflection, 21% the rectosigmoid, and 30% the
distance from the verge (Fig 4) (18).

The rectum can be defined relative to adjacent pelvic land-
marks (eg, the sacral promontory or anorectal ring) or by us-
ing measurements from the anal verge. However, there is no
agreement on which landmarks should be used to define the
borders of the rectum; furthermore, the landmark-based and
measurement-based definitions do not align. In an editorial
referencing  unpublished  data,  Mathis  and  Nelson  (19)  com-
pared endoscopic measurement-based criteria with different
landmarks and found discrepancies. For example, measure-
ment-based criteria place the upper rectal limit 12–15 cm from
the anal verge. Landmark-based criteria for the upper rectal
border, like the sacral promontory, were found to be located
21–23 cm from the anal verge, and coalescence of the taeniae
coli was located 21 cm from the verge. This would result in tu-
mors classified as sigmoid based on measurement criteria to
be localized to the upper rectum based on landmarks.

Another  limitation  of  measurement-based  criteria  in  de-
fining  the  rectum  is  related  to  significant  variation  in  the
length  of  the  rectum  between  individuals.  A  study  during
open proctectomy found significant variation based on gen-
der and body habitus in the distance from the anal verge to
the dentate line (P = .003), puborectalis muscle (P = .03), or
peritoneal  reflection  (P  =  .02).  Consequently,  categorization
of rectal tumor location based on fixed measurements would
result in variable localization of tumors relative to these clini-
cally and anatomically relevant landmarks (Fig 5) (20).

ic; however, clinicians have modified the definition based on

have limitations, MRI reports should include—in addition to

Issue 3: Defining the APR and Its Significance
1. The APR subdivides the rectum into two clinically and ana-
tomically distinct regions.

2. The distinction between the APR and the visceral perito-

neum requires clarification.

Impact
The subdivision into the high and low rectum should be based
on the relationship of the APR to the rectum.

Discussion
The APR simply defines a point of contact or reflection of the
visceral peritoneum from the bladder and seminal vesicles or
uterus  onto  the  anterior  rectal  wall  and  is  not  interchange-
able with the visceral peritoneum.

The  APR  divides  the  rectum  into  extraperitoneal  (lower
rectum) and intraperitoneal (upper rectum) segments, with
the section of the rectum bridging the APR representing the
midrectum. It also separates two distinct lymphatic drainage
zones  of  the  rectum,  with  only  the  extraperitoneal  rectum
draining to lateral pelvic nodes, while the rectum above the
APR drains superiorly to the superior rectal and inferior mes-
enteric nodes (Fig 3).

The APR can be defined in 81.6%–88.5% of rectal MRI stud-
ies  (27).  On  axial  T2-weighted  images,  the  APR  is  seen  as  a
V  configuration—likened  to  a  seagull  configuration—at  the
point where the peritoneum reflects onto the anterior rectum
(Fig 8). The seminal vesicles and uterocervical angle serve as
reliable  landmarks  for  defining  the  APR  (27).  The  distance
from  the  APR  to  the  anorectal  ring  and  the  relationship  of
rectal tumors to the APR has excellent interreader agreement
(27).

Impact

Discussion
The MRF circumferentially surrounds the lower rectum, runs
over the surface of the levators, terminates at the top of the
puborectalis sling, fuses with the APR anteriorly, and ascends
in the lateral and presacral region above the APR.

In terms of the primary tumor, MRF assessment is applica-
ble only in T3 tumors, not in T1 or T2 tumors. In low rectal tu-
mors extending into the anal canal, MRF involvement should
be assessed only for the component of the tumor in the lower
rectum, as the MRF terminates at the top of the puborectalis
sling. MRF involvement by the primary tumor is best assessed
at the base of the tumor, which is the most likely point of mus-
cularis propria transgression (Fig 15).

With respect to EMVI, a study by Birbeck et al (35) reported
that MRF involvement by EMVI resulted in  a 30.4% LR rate,
while  tumor  deposit  involvement  of  the  MRF  resulted  in  a
40.9% LR rate. On the basis of such data, a recent expert con-
sensus recommended that EMVI and tumor deposits within
1 mm of the MRF should be reported as MRF involvement at
MRI (Figs 16, 17) (31). Additionally, EMVI or tumor deposits at
MRI  have  significant  prognostic  impact  and  are  associated
with  a  decline  in  overall  survival  and  disease-free  survival
(36).

In  terms  of  mesorectal  lymph  nodes,  the  Colorectal  and
Anal Disease-focused Panel of the Society of Abdominal Ra-
diology  has  recommended  that  lymph  nodes  in  proximity
to  the  MRF  be  described  but  not  reported  as  MRF  involve-
ment  (37).  This  recommendation  recognizes  that  most  visi-
ble lymph nodes are benign and that radiologic evaluation of
lymph nodes is limited in its accuracy. Interestingly, a study
by Nagtegaal et al (38) found that even tumor-bearing lymph
nodes involving the CRM did not confer any increased risk of
LR. Shihab et al (39) found that it is uncommon for a mesorec-
tal lymph node to be the only factor responsible for a positive
CRM at histopathologic analysis.

The  eighth  edition  of  the  AJCC  manual  defines  tumor  de-
posits  as  discontinuous  nodules  of  tumor  in  the  mesorectal
fat, with no identifiable nodal or vascular tissue. The definition
of  tumor  deposits  at  TNM  staging  has  changed  between  the
fifth  and  eighth  editions  and  continues  to  evolve,  presenting
challenges  in  their  reporting.  Tumor  deposits  were  initially
described 90 years ago by Gabriel (40) to occur in conjunction
with EMVI as in-transit tumor thrombi forming a nodular mass
with a spiculated border contiguous with EMVI, an appearance
described at MRI as the comet tail sign (41).

Tumor  deposits  may  also  be  completely  replaced  me-
sorectal  lymph  nodes.  However,  the  distinction  between  a
metastatic partially replaced lymph node and a lymph node
entirely replaced by tumor is a pathologic diagnosis that can-
not  be  made  with  imaging.  To  accommodate  this  limitation

of imaging, a recent multidisciplinary expert consensus rec-
ommended  that  heterogeneous  spiculated  nodules  in  the
mesorectal fat could be either lymph nodes or tumor deposits
and  should  be  treated  differently  from  smooth  well-encap-
sulated mesorectal lymph nodes. The MRF should be consid-
ered as involved if a heterogeneous spiculated lymph node or
tumor deposit is in proximity to the MRF, but not a smooth
encapsulated  lymph  node  even  if  heterogeneous  in  appear-
ance and potentially metastatic (Fig 18) (31) (Table 1).

Accurately defining involvement of the MRF by the prima-
ry tumor and EMVI or tumor deposits requires adherence to
a few key steps:

1. MRF involvement should be assessed in T3 tumors at the
base of the tumor, the most common site of transgression of
the  muscularis  propria  (Fig  15).  Assessment  of  the  point  of
breach  of  the  muscularis  propria  by  tumor  signal  intensity
extending  beyond  the  muscularis  propria  and  approaching
the  MRF,  ideally  confirmed  on  multiplanar  high-resolution
T2-weighted images, is essential. Occasionally, the intact rec-
tal wall adjacent to the tumor approximates the MRF and is
misdiagnosed  as  MRF  involvement.  Tumor  confined  within
the muscularis propria at the point of abutment does not in-
dicate MRF involvement (29).

2. In terms of measurement from the MRF, the closest dis-
tance from the most penetrating component of the primary
tumor,  EMVI,  or  spiculated  lymph  node  or  tumor  deposit
should be obtained. MRF is described as involved when any
of these structures are less than 1 mm from the MRF (Figs 15,
16).  Counterintuitively  increasing  the  cutoff  from  1  mm  to  5
mm reduced accuracy due to higher false-positive results, re-
sulting in patients receiving unnecessary neoadjuvant ther-
apy (6).

3. MRI has high interobserver agreement and negative pre-
dictive  value  of  94%–98.1%  in  excluding  MRF  involvement.
The  positive  predictive  value  is  only  slightly  lower  at  86.5%.
This has been shown consistently in multiple trials and stud-
ies over the decades and is a significant contributor to the suc-
cess of MRI in reducing LR (4,42).

the LNSC color-coded CT-MRI atlas (Fig 21):

Internal Iliac Lymph Node Compartment.—All lymph nodes
abutting  the  internal  iliac  artery  and  its  branches  from  its
origin  up  to  the  point  where  the  terminal  arterial  branches
exit the infrapiriformis foramen are internal iliac nodes. The
internal pudendal and inferior gluteal artery are the terminal
branches of the internal iliac artery that exit through the in-
frapiriformis foramen (Figs 21–23).

Obturator Lymph Node Compartment.—Lymph nodes lateral
to the internal iliac vessels at the pelvic brim, posterior to the
external iliac vessels at the midpelvis, and inferior to the in-
frapiriformis foramen are obturator nodes. We propose place-
ment of the following landmark planes:

1. Pelvic brim: A line drawn along the lateral aspect of the
internal iliac vessels separates the cephalic portion of the in-
ternal iliac compartment and the obturator compartment.

In terms of prognosis and staging, internal iliac nodes are
categorized  by  the  AJCC  as  regional  or  N  category  for  rectal
cancer (13). The overall survival for internal iliac node metas-
tasis is comparable to that of N2 category mesorectal lymph
nodes. Obturator node involvement is more controversial, as
these lymph nodes have a worse overall survival than inter-
nal iliac nodes but a superior overall survival to that of oth-
er  LPLN  compartments.  However,  resection  of  these  lymph
nodes has been associated with excellent survival outcomes,
supporting  classification  of  both  as  regional  lymph  nodes
(54). Distinction of obturator nodes from M category external
iliac nodes is exceedingly important, as there is a significant
difference in overall survival between these two lymph node
groups and because external iliac lymph nodes—unlike obtu-
rator nodes—are not candidates for LPLN dissection (55).

incidence of metastatic LPLNs and which localize primarily to
internal iliac nodes and obturator nodes (51). LPLN size crite-

Conclusion
Rectal  MRI  has  transformed  staging  and  management  of
rectal cancer. Despite great strides in performance and im-
age interpretation, numerous anatomic nuances and areas
of confusion exist, including the definitions of the anal ca-
nal,  rectum,  APR,  and  upper  and  lower  rectum;  the  loca-
tion  of  the  MRF  in  relation  to  the  upper  rectum;  anatomic
structures to be assessed for MRF involvement; distinction
between  the  CRM  and  MRF;  and  definition  of  boundaries
of  LPLN  compartments  most  commonly  involved  in  rectal
cancer at CT and MRI. This article attempts to identify and
address these areas of confusion and provide clarifying ex-
planations to promote more accurate rectal MRI interpreta-
tions.
