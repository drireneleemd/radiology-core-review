Diffusely Infiltrative Small Bowel Disease

Multiple infiltrative disorders can affect the small bowel,
often resulting in diffuse small bowel wall thickening.
These infiltrative disorders can manifest owing to vari-
ous factors such as an influx of immunologic or neoplas-
tic cells or the accumulation of substances within one or
more layers of the intestinal wall. Although there can be
considerable overlap in the appearances of infiltrative
diseases on cross-sectional images, a comprehensive
understanding of more specific ancillary imaging fea-
tures and clinicopathologic correlation can substantially
narrow the differential diagnosis. The radiologist can be
instrumental in synthesizing the clinical and imaging
information and guiding subsequent workup. The au-
thors present a comprehensive review of the infiltrative
disorders that commonly involve the small bowel. These
disorders are organized on the basis of their pathophys-
iologic features, with multiple illustrative case examples
to enhance understanding of these entities. CT and MRI
are currently the most commonly used imaging modali-
ties for evaluating small bowel disorders, and this review
is focused on these two modalities. Detailed information
regarding the pathologic features, clinical presentation,
and imaging findings of these infiltrative disorders is
provided to aid radiologists in recognizing and differen-
tiating these conditions.

Introduction
The  small  bowel  is  the  longest  part  of  the  gastrointestinal
(GI) tract, and its primary functions are breakdown and ab-
sorption  of  nutrients,  extraction  of  water,  and  movement  of
intestinal  contents  toward  the  large  intestine.  The  intestine
is  a major site of entry for food-borne antigens  and various
pathogens,  as  well  as  a  home  to  beneficial  microbes,  which
are maintained by defenses provided by gastric and pancreat-
ic juices, the mucus layer overlying the intestinal mucosa, and
immune cells in the bowel wall.

Infiltrative disorders of the small bowel result from an ex-
cessive  influx  of  cells  (inflammatory  or  neoplastic)  or  accu-
mulation of substances in one or more layers of the intestinal
wall.  This  influx  often  leads  to  thickening  of  the  predomi-
nantly involved wall layer or the entire wall thickness, which
can  adversely  affect  bowel  function.  The  infiltration  can  re-
sult in complete or incomplete circumferential thickening of
the small bowel wall, with the length of involvement ranging
from one segment to the entire small bowel.

These  disorders  often  manifest  with  nonspecific  clinical
symptoms, posing diagnostic challenges. This article provides
an overview of the clinical presentations, radiologic findings,
and histopathologic features of diffuse infiltrative disease of
the  small  bowel  and  the  appropriate  diagnostic  tests  where
applicable.  A  multidisciplinary  approach  with  involvement
of  gastroenterologists,  radiologists,  and  surgeons  is  crucial
for accurate diagnosis and appropriate management of these
complex conditions.

Normal Small Bowel Wall Layers and Their
Components
The small intestine is composed of four distinct layers: the
mucosa, submucosa, muscularis layer, and serosa. The mu-
cosal  layer  consists  of  the  surface  epithelium,  lamina  pro-
pria mucosae (or lamina propria), and muscularis mucosae
(1). The surface epithelium comprises absorptive tall colum-
nar  cells  and  goblet  cells  that  line  the  villi  and  the  crypts
of  Lieberkuhn  and  secrete  mucus  to  protect  the  intestinal
epithelium (2). Neuroendocrine cells  are also  found in the
villi and crypts. The lamina propria contains loose connec-
tive tissue, blood vessels, lymphatics, nerves, and immune
cells (2). Lymphoid follicles and Peyer patches can be found
in  the  lamina  propria,  particularly  in  the  distal  ileum  (2).
The muscularis mucosae, a narrow band of smooth muscle
fibers, forms the boundary between the lamina propria and
the submucosa (1).

The  submucosa  is  predominantly  composed  of  fibrocon-
nective  tissue  and  contains  blood  vessels,  lymphatics,  and
nerves (ie, Meissner plexus) (1). Lymphoid follicles and Peyer

patches can protrude into the submucosa through the mus-
cularis mucosae (2).

The  muscularis  layer  comprises  an  inner  circular  layer
and  an  outer  longitudinal  layer  of  smooth  muscle,  with  the
myenteric neural plexus (ie, Auerbach plexus) between them
(1). The serosa is the outermost layer of the bowel and consists
of a continuous sheet of squamous epithelial cells (mesothe-
lium)  over  a  thin  layer  of  connective  tissue  (subserosa)  that
overlies the muscularis layer (2).

Infiltrative Bowel Diseases
Infiltrative bowel diseases may involve the small bowel ex-
clusively,  involve  the  GI  tract  more  generally,  or  be  a  com-
ponent of multisystem infiltrative disease. Depending on its
cause,  the  infiltrative  process  may  predominantly  involve
only one layer of the bowel, or it may start in one layer and
spread  to  involve  other  adjacent  layers  or  the  entire  bowel
wall  (Table  1).  The  subsequent  sections  of  this  article  de-
scribe  various  infiltrative  diseases  on  the  basis  of  the  pre-
dominant or initially involved bowel layer (Fig 1A).

Both CT and MRI are excellent tools for evaluating diffuse-
ly infiltrative diseases of the small bowel by imaging the small
bowel, mesentery, and other abdominopelvic organs that may
be  involved  and  provide  clues  to  the  diagnosis.  Small  bowel
wall  thickening  and  fold  thickening,  abnormal  mural  en-
hancement, and luminal narrowing or distention, in addition
to  other  extraintestinal  findings  such  as  mesenteric  lymph-
adenopathy,  ascites,  and  complications  associated  with  the
disease, can be identified on cross-sectional images. For some
disease processes, two or more bowel wall layers can be dis-
tinctly visualized; this phenomenon is commonly referred to
as  mural  stratification  (Fig  1B).  Dynamic  contrast-enhanced
and cine MRI sequences can provide insight into bowel perfu-
sion and functionality, respectively.

Disease Processes Predominantly Involving or
Beginning in the Mucosal Layer
Several  infiltrative  diseases  begin  in  the  mucosal  layer  of
the small bowel (Table 2), with infectious and immunologic
processes  being  the  most  common.  Infectious  enteritis  of-
ten results from pathogens directly invading from the bowel
lumen into the mucosa and invoking an immune response.
These  pathogens  include  various  viruses,  bacteria,  myco-
bacteria, and occasionally protozoan flagellates such as Giar-
dia intestinalis. Fungal infections are the notable exception;
they  are  usually  disseminated  hematogenously  and  begin
in the submucosal layer (3). Given their different bowel wall
layer  involvement,  fungal  infections  are  discussed  in  fur-
ther detail later in this review. At imaging, most infectious
processes appear with mural thickening and edema that are
almost completely circumferential. However, many entities
may display other characteristic imaging findings, including
extraintestinal findings, which can aid in differentiation.

Many immunologic disorders, such as graft versus host dis-
ease  (GVHD),  mastocytosis,  Crohn  disease,  and  eosinophilic
enteritis,  involve  the  mucosa  owing  to  the  infiltration  of  im-
mune cells into the lamina propria. While Crohn disease can
manifest  with  nearly  pathognomonic  imaging  findings,  the

Infectious Enteritis
Infectious enteritis is often associated with recent travel or
recent exposure to or ingestion of an infecting organism. De-
pending on the pathogen, activation of the immune system
leads  to  macrophages,  neutrophils,  lymphocytes,  or  eosin-
ophils infiltrating the mucosa. Whipple disease (Fig 2) is a
bacterial  infection  that  tends  to  be  more  diffusely  infiltra-
tive. It is usually a multisystem infection by the gram-pos-
itive  bacillus  Tropheryma  whipplei.  This  is  a  rare  infection,
with an incidence of one to three of every one million peo-
ple overall (4). The bacilli penetrate the mucosal lining and
then  sometimes  infiltrate  toward  the  serosa.  An  immune
response is triggered in the lamina propria layer, with infil-
tration by macrophages. Under the microscope, characteris-
tic “foaming” macrophages containing periodic acid–Schiff
(PAS)  stain–positive  granules  are  visualized  (5).  Patients
typically present with abdominal cramping, loss of appetite,
diarrhea,  signs  of  malabsorption,  and  weight  loss,  with  an
additional less common symptom of GI bleeding or migra-
tory polyarthralgia if the infection is disseminated (4). Im-
aging features that are suggestive of Whipple disease include
diffuse  or  patchy  micronodules  along  the  bowel  wall  with
nodular or bulbous folds. In addition, lymphangiectasia can
be  present,  and  lymph  nodes  can  be  hypoattenuating  on
CT images (5). The diagnosis is confirmed with polymerase
chain  reaction  testing  and  visualization  of  PAS  stain–posi-
tive macrophages at small bowel biopsy (4).

When Mycobacterium avium–intracellulare complex (MAC)
infection (Fig 3) involves the GI tract, the pathogen infiltrates
from the lumen through the mucosa, with macrophages in-
filtrating the lamina propria (6). Infection of the GI tract is
seen almost exclusively in disseminated disease, particular-
ly in severely immunocompromised patients, such as those

with  AIDS  (7).  Patients  present  with  abdominal  pain  and
diarrhea  in  addition  to  constitutional  symptoms  including
fever,  night  sweats,  and  malaise  (6).  MAC  infection  has  a
predilection for the proximal small bowel. At imaging, bow-
el  folds  are  diffusely  thickened  and  may  show  nodularity.
Low-attenuation caseating lymph nodes may also be present
in the mesentery (7). It should be noted that MAC infection
can be differentiated from Mycobacterium tuberculosis infec-
tion, as M tuberculosis predominantly involves the ileocecal
region, in contrast to MAC, which has a more proximal dis-
tribution (6). Diagnosis of MAC infection is made by using a
combination of the presenting symptoms, pulmonary imag-
ing findings, and sputum culture. Blood culture analysis can
be  used  to  confirm  infection  dissemination,  and  stool  cul-
ture testing can be used to confirm spread to the GI tract (8).
G  intestinalis  infection  (Fig  4)  is  the  most  common  flag-
ellated  protozoan  intestinal  pathogen  worldwide  (9).  This
organism  has  a  two-stage  life  cycle.  In  stage  1,  the  cyst  is
transmitted via a fecal and/or oral route, typically by means
of  contaminated  water.  In  the  second  (feeding)  stage,  the
trophozoite  form  of  the  organism  infects  the  small  intes-
tine. The flagellated protozoan attaches to the mucosa and
usually remains within the lumen; however, occasionally in
the  setting  of  immunocompromise,  it  can  invade  through
the  lumen  to  the  submucosal  layer  (10).  Neutrophilic  and
mast  cell  infiltration  of  the  mucosal  layer  and  blunted  vil-
li are seen at microscopy (11). The classic clinical presenta-
tion is greasy foul-smelling diarrhea accompanied by signs
of  malabsorption  due  to  villous  atrophy  and  unintentional
weight  loss  (9).  Some  patients  later  develop  postinfectious
irritable  bowel  syndrome,  and  it  takes  up  to  several  weeks
for their bowel habits to normalize (11). Cross-sectional im-
aging  shows  diffuse  bowel  wall  thickening  similar  to  that
with other infections; however, giardiasis manifests with a
lesser degree of submucosal edema (12). Another character-
istic feature of giardiasis is the predilection for the duode-
num  and  jejunum  (9).  Moreover,  multiple  fluid-filled  and

Viruses  including  norovirus  frequently  cause  infec-
tious enteritis. Norovirus infection leads to nonspecific dif-
fuse bowel wall thickening and submucosal edema seen at
cross-sectional imaging (13). Although imaging findings are
not specific for identifying this pathogen, they can exclude
certain complications (12). For example, many children with
norovirus  infections  present  with  appendicitis-like  symp-
toms,  and  CT  findings  can  rule  out  appendicitis  when  the
results  of  sonographic  evaluation  of  the  appendix  are  in-
conclusive (13). Cytomegalovirus (CMV) enteritis is a note-
worthy condition, as it is most commonly seen in the colon;

however,  it  can  occur  anywhere  along  the  GI  tract  (Fig  5),
with infection of the small bowel seen in 10% of cases (14).

CMV  is  a  double-stranded  DNA  virus  that  is  present  in
the gut biome in up to 83% of the global population (15). Af-
ter the initial infection, CMV becomes latent in monocytes
and endothelial cells within the mucosa (16). Reactivation is
triggered by immunosuppression, and an immune response
results  in  mucosal  ulcer  formation  (16).  At  histopatholog-
ic analysis, CMV inclusion bodies, which have an “owl eye”
appearance, are visualized (15). Most cases of CMV enteritis
are subclinical, with symptoms arising in immunocompro-
mised  individuals  (16).  The  most  common  symptoms  are
fever, abdominal pain, diarrhea, and GI bleeding (15). Non-
specific long-segment bowel wall thickening, similar to in-
flammatory bowel disease, is the most common feature (17).

Immune-mediated Disease Processes
GVHD (Fig 6) affecting the bowel occurs after bone marrow
or stem cell transplant. Donor T lymphocytes, or T cells,
cause an immune response against mismatched antigens on
recipient epithelial cells, triggering cytotoxic T-cell infiltra-
tion of the mucosa and cytokine release, which results in im-
mune-mediated damage of the GI tract, skin, liver, and lung
mucosa (19). Clinically, GVHD can be subclassified as acute
GVHD (10–40 days, usually <100 days, after transplant) or
chronic GVHD (>100 days and <3 years after transplant) (20).
The focus here is on the diffusely infiltrative acute phase.

With acute GVHD, the classic patient presentation includes
diffuse maculopapular rash, pruritis, secretory diarrhea,
vomiting, abdominal pain, weight loss, and jaundice result-
ing from cholestatic hyperbilirubinemia (21). The character-
istic findings in acute GVHD are bowel wall thickening and
mural stratification due to avidly enhancing mucosa and
serosa, with hypoattenuating intramural bowel wall edema
(22,23). Diffuse involvement of the entire GI tract may be
seen. Additional imaging findings can be hepatomegaly, asci-
tes, and ground-glass opacities in the visualized lung bases if
the lungs are involved (22). Definitive diagnosis is made with
skin or GI tract biopsy, as opportunistic infections can mimic
acute GVHD (24). Patients are then treated with steroids and
advanced immunosuppressive therapy (21).

Systemic mastocytosis (Fig 7) is a rare disorder character-
ized by abnormal proliferation of mast cells in the skin, bone
marrow, and abdominal viscera, with the GI tract involved in
80%  of  patients  (25).  Histopathologic  analysis  reveals  dense
mast cell infiltration of the lamina propria arranged in sheets,
aggregates, or pericryptal whorls. Prominent eosinophilic in-
filtration is seen throughout the mucosa as well (26). Patients

Carcinomatosis
Primary  bowel  malignancy  and  metastases  to  the  bowel  typi-
cally are focal or multifocal; however, advanced peritoneal car-
cinomatosis  can  involve  the  small  bowel  diffusely.  Peritoneal
carcinomatosis (Fig 18) refers to the spread of metastatic malig-
nant cells to the peritoneum and is associated with a very poor
prognosis (55). Metastatic malignant cells can reach the perito-
neum  through  the  lymphatic  system  or  directly  spread  within
the peritoneal cavity (56). To seed the mesentery, the malignant
cells  must  gain  access  to  the  subperitoneal  lymphatic  spaces;
this  is  achieved  through  lymphatic  stomata.  These  lymphatic
stomata are found in higher concentrations in the small bowel
mesentery, as well as in the appendix, greater omentum, pouch
of Douglas, and inferior surface of the diaphragm (57). Once the
peritoneal cavity has been accessed, metastatic cells disperse in
all  directions  in  search  of  viable  tissue  to  replicate  (58).  When
metastatic cells implant on the serosal surface of the small bowel
and multiply profusely, diffuse serosal disease can be seen. Peri-
toneal metastases are found in advanced stages of malignancy.

or on the serosa, patients can have bowel obstruction and pres-
ent with abdominal pain and distention, nausea, vomiting, and
unintentional weight loss (55). Imaging reveals focal to diffuse
soft-tissue  thickening  along  the  serosa;  this  thickening  can  be
nodular or a smooth involvement of long segments (59). The di-
agnosis is made with imaging (CT or PET/CT) or surgical explo-
ration (56).

Hemorrhage.—Intramural hemorrhage (Fig 22) of the small
bowel results from deposition of red blood cells predominant-
ly in the submucosal layer. There are several causes of intra-
mural hemorrhage, including but not limited to trauma, vas-
culitis,  coagulopathy,  ischemia,  amyloidosis,  and  Dieulafoy
lesions (44). Patients present in a variety of ways, depending
on the cause of the hemorrhage, but abdominal pain and GI
bleeding are common symptoms (68). Increased attenuation
and thickening of the bowel wall, which can be diffuse or focal,
are visualized on CT images. The high attenuation is helpful
for differentiating hemorrhage from other inflammatory and
infectious causes of bowel wall thickening. A large hematoma
can lead to luminal narrowing and bowel obstruction (44).
