MRI is the reference standard modality for evaluation of perianal fistulas, providing high-spatial-resolution mapping of the tract
origin, course, and secondary extensions to aid classification and treatment planning. Cryptoglandular fistulas most commonly
follow intersphincteric or transsphincteric pathways and may be complicated by formation of abscesses. In comparison, fistulas
related to perianal fistulizing Crohn disease (PFCD) often arise near the anorectal junction, frequently extending beyond con-
ventional classifications due to branching, high-origin involvement, and associated proctitis. In patients with PFCD, MRI facili-
tates serial evaluation, allowing stable tracts to be distinguished from new branching or progressive fibrosis. MRI allows differ-
entiation of fluid-persistent tracts, which carry a higher risk of symptom relapse, from fibrosis-predominant tracts, which reflect
a favorable response to therapy and a reduced risk of recurrence. Although most perianal fistulas are benign, chronic tracts can
undergo malignant transformation. In PFCD, prolonged inflammation and nonhealing fistulas increase the risk of mucinous
adenocarcinoma. The authors present an MRI-based approach to interpretation of fistulas, focusing on tract origin and escape
pathways, and using the Parks and St James classification systems to improve radiologic assessment. Core components include
defining tract trajectory, identifying internal and external openings, and recognizing secondary extensions such as abscesses
or supralevator spreading. Radiologists should also assess for proctitis and monitor changes in fistula composition over time.
High-spatial-resolution MRI, combined with mapping of the escape pathway and evaluation of the serial tract, improves charac-
terization of fistulas and supports long-term monitoring in patients with cryptoglandular and PFCD fistulas.

Introduction
Perianal fistulas are abnormal tracts between the anal canal
and the perineal skin that typically exit in the gluteal cleft.
MRI is the primary modality for confirming and classifying
perianal fistulas, particularly for surgical planning (1,2). His-
torically,  perianal  MRI  was  a  single  time  point  assessment,
providing a static snapshot of disease anatomy. However, in
patients  with  perianal  fistulizing  Crohn  disease  (PFCD),  in
whom fistulas persist or progress, serial MRI is routine, re-
quiring  interpretation  beyond  simple  detection.  Serial  MRI
allows radiologists to assess whether a tract is stable, improv-
ing, or worsening, distinctions that directly affect treatment
decisions  (3).  This  shift  parallels  that  of  MR  enterography,
which evolved from a tool for single time point assessment to
one for serial monitoring of small bowel inflammation and
fibrosis (4).

Perianal fistulas follow predictable escape routes, forming
the  basis  of  classification  systems.  Cryptoglandular  fistulas,
driven by anal gland infection, generally follow intersphinc-
teric  or  transsphincteric  escape  routes  (1).  In  comparison,
PFCD fistulas arise from chronic inflammation, often branch
extensively, and may extend beyond the perianal region to the
vagina, the scrotum, or the mesorectal fascia. Proctitis further
complicates PFCD, and high-origin tracts near the anorectal
junction frequently do not meet strict classification require-
ments for perianal versus rectal classification (3,5).

Interpretation of MRI examinations benefits from a repro-
ducible  search  pattern  for  evaluating  the  origin,  path,  and
complications of fistulas. Radiologists assessing perianal fis-
tulas  should  document  the  origin,  path,  and  external  open-
ing  of  the  tract,  determine  whether  setons  are  present,  and
evaluate changes in the composition of the fistula over time
(6,7). The Parks and St James classification systems are used
to classify the complexity of fistulas (1). High-spatial-resolu-
tion, small-field-of-view T2-weighted MRI best allows the po-
sition of the fistula to be defined in relation to the sphincters,
the  intersphincteric  plane,  and  the  levator  ani.  This  review
details an approach to MRI interpretation of perianal fistulas,
emphasizing escape pathways, tract complexity, serial imag-
ing,  and  reporting  strategies  for  cryptoglandular  and  PFCD
fistulas.  To  support  a  reproducible  interpretive  framework,
this review is organized around the dichotomy between cryp-
toglandular and PFCD fistulas. Although the imaging features
and tract behavior of these two types of fistulas overlap, this
separation  facilitates  radiologic  teaching,  highlights  differ-
ences  between  fistula  types,  and  emphasizes  practical  dis-
tinctions in complexity, chronicity, and treatment response.

Anatomy of the Anal Canal
The anal canal extends from the anorectal junction to the anal
verge, although its boundaries differ based on histologic, surgi-
cal, and radiologic definitions. Histologically, it spans from the
dentate line to the external opening at the hair-bearing perianal
skin.  Surgically  and  radiologically,  the  anorectal  junction,  de-
fined by the puborectalis muscle, is the preferred proximal land-
mark  because  the  dentate  line  is  inconsistently  visualized  on
MR images (Fig 1). The puborectalis muscle encircles the distal
rectum, forming a sling that narrows anteriorly toward the pubic
symphysis and serves as a key MRI reference point (8,9).

The internal and external sphincters and the intersphinc-
teric space are key MRI landmarks for assessment of fistulas.
The internal sphincter, a continuation of rectal smooth muscle,
appears as a T2-isointense to -hypointense ring. The external
sphincter, composed of skeletal muscle, is T2 isointense to the
adjacent pelvic muscles and forms a ring or triangular config-
uration around the anal canal (8,10). Sex-based differences in
the  external  sphincter  influence  fistula  pathways.  In  women,
the anterior external sphincter is shorter than that in men and
terminates at the vaginal canal, creating an open interface with
the intersphincteric space (11) (Fig 2). This anatomic configura-
tion increases the likelihood of anterior fistulas tracking toward
the posterior vaginal wall, elevating the risk of anovaginal fistu-
las. The anatomy of the anterior external sphincter in women
allows intersphincteric tracts from the high anal canal to reach
the posterior vagina, predisposing women to anovaginal fistu-
las. In men, the external sphincter forms a continuous ring and
typically directs fistulas posteriorly toward the ischioanal fossa
rather than anteriorly (11). The intersphincteric space, a poten-
tial space between the sphincters, appears as a thin interface
or fat-filled compartment in healthy patients and serves as the
primary site for fistula formation (10,12).

Inflammation from fistulas or abscesses expands the inter-
sphincteric  space,  replacing  fat  with  fluid  and  edema,  caus-
ing  hyperintensity  on  T2-weighted  MR  images.  Horseshoe
abscesses and complex spiraling fistula tracts can distort this

Types and Pathophysiologic Characteristics of
Perianal Fistulas
Perianal  fistulas  are  classified  as  either  cryptoglandular  or
PFCD,  with  the  cryptoglandular  type  being  more  common
(13).  Cryptoglandular  fistulas  originate  from  infected  anal
glands  and  typically  follow  intersphincteric  or  transsphinc-
teric pathways (14). In comparison, PFCD-related fistulas de-
velop due to chronic inflammation at the anorectal junction
or rectum, often exhibiting high-origin branching tracts that
extend beyond conventional fascial planes (15). Less common
causes  of  perianal  fistulas  include  trauma,  tuberculosis,  hi-
dradenitis  suppurativa,  immunosuppression  (eg,  HIV  infec-
tion), lymphogranuloma venereum, rectal duplication, sacro-
coccygeal teratoma, and actinomycosis (13).

The American Gastroenterological Association defines sim-
ple fistulas as low-origin tracts with a single external opening
and  no  associated  abscess  (16).  Complex  fistulas,  more  com-
mon in patients with PFCD, include high-origin or branching

tracts, multiple external openings, abscesses, anovaginal fistu-
las, anorectal strictures, and proctitis (16). In PFCD, many fistu-
las fail to undergo fibrotic closure, remaining fluid-persistent
despite  long-term  follow-up.  Some  tracts  epithelialize  rather
than  fully  heal,  resulting  in  stable  chronic  fistulas  (17).  Their
proximity to chronic proctitis increases the risk of recurrence
and limits the success of medical and surgical treatment. The
St James classification system allows categorization of fistulas
based on MRI appearance, with lower grades assigned to sim-
ple linear tracts and higher grades to those with abscesses, sec-
ondary branches, or supralevator extension (1,2).

Cryptoglandular Theory
Described  by  Parks  (18)  in  1961,  the  cryptoglandular  theory
attributes  formation  of  fistulas  to  anal  gland  infection  and
obstruction.  Obstructed  secretions  accumulate  in  the  inter-
sphincteric  space,  forming  a  fluid  collection  that  is  initially
not visible with MRI. If the collection decompresses into the
anal  canal,  it  resolves.  Otherwise,  it  follows  an  alternative
escape  pathway,  forming  a  fistula,  most  commonly  an  in-
tersphincteric  or  transsphincteric  fistula  (19).  Persistent  ob-
struction  can  lead  to  further  fluid  accumulation,  eventually
forming an abscess that is detectable with MRI (Fig 4) (1,2).

although its accuracy is debated due to the lack of a true serosa in
the anal canal (8). Many PFCD fistulas follow a “proctitis tracking

Perianal Crohn Disease
Crohn disease is a chronic immune-mediated disorder char-
acterized  by  segmental  transmural  inflammation,  affecting
approximately  0.3%  of  North  Americans  and  Europeans,
with  increasing  global  prevalence  (27,28).  Perianal  manifes-
tations including skin tags, fissures, hemorrhoids, abscesses,
and fistulas occur in 15%–30% of patients with Crohn disease,
(29,30).  Perianal  disease  is  strongly  associated  with  rectal
inflammation,  with  up  to  92%  of  patients  with  rectal  Crohn
disease developing perianal fistulas. However, perianal man-
ifestations  are  not  limited  to  colonic  disease:  approximately
12% of patients with isolated ileal Crohn disease and 5% with-
out  detectable  luminal  inflammation  also  develop  perianal
fistulas. Perianal symptoms may precede bowel involvement
or  occur  in  isolation  (21,22).  Symptomatic  fistulas  indicate
a  more  severe  phenotype  of  Crohn  disease,  correlating  with
higher dependence on steroids, higher hospitalization rates,
and more surgical interventions (31).

PFCD causes symptoms such as anal pain, purulent drain-
age,  and  bleeding,  all  of  which  substantially  reduce  quality
of life (32). Despite treatment, many cases remain refractory,
with some patients requiring fecal diversion or proctectomy.
Colonic involvement and proctitis further complicate disease
progression and impair treatment response (33). Long-stand-
ing PFCD increases the risk of anal and rectal cancer, particu-
larly fistula-related mucinous adenocarcinoma (34).

Management strategies depend on the complexity of the
fistula,  disease  activity,  and  prior  interventions.  A  multi-
disciplinary  approach  incorporating  clinical  examination,
endoscopy,  imaging,  and  examination  under  anesthesia

guides treatment selection (33,35). Placement of a seton is a
key component of initial therapy to maintain drainage and
prevent premature closure that could lead to formation of an
abscess (26). Removal of a seton may lead to recurrence of
or new abscesses, often requiring placement of new setons
over time to maintain drainage (Fig 6).

Anti–tumor necrosis factor (TNF) agents are the first-line
treatment  for  moderate  to  severe  PFCD;  infliximab  achieves
closure in 68% of patients (vs 26% of those taking a placebo)
(36). Despite undergoing optimized therapy, more than 50%
of patients have a recurrence of anal drainage, reflecting the
chronic  relapsing  nature  of  PFCD  (33,35).  MRI  allows  differ-
entiation of fluid-persistent tracts from fibrotic closure, and
the latter is correlated with sustained remission (Fig 7) (23).
Patients with persistent fluid-filled tracts on MR images carry
a higher risk of recurrence, reinforcing the need for serial im-
aging in treatment evaluation (24).

Antibiotics such as ciprofloxacin provide temporary relief
but are primarily used as a bridge to biologic therapy or sur-
gery (37). Second-line therapies interleuken-23 (IL-23) inhibi-
tors (eg, risankizumab) and Janus kinase (JAK) inhibitors (eg,
upadacitinib)  are  increasingly  used  in  patients  with  PFCD
who  are  unresponsive  to  anti–tumor  necrosis  factor  thera-
py, although long-term data remain limited (38–40). Future
studies with standardized MRI endpoints will help to further
clarify the role of these therapies.

Perianal Fistulizing Crohn Disease
PFCD differs from cryptoglandular disease due to prolonged
cyclical  inflammation  and  fibrosis  (20,25).  Rather  than  only
mapping anatomy, MRI should be used to track longitudinal
changes,  such  as  whether  a  fistula  remains  stable  or  devel-
ops  new  branches.  Fluid-filled  active  tracts,  which  appear
hyperintense  on  T2-weighted  MR  images,  should  be  distin-
guished  from  fibrotic  low-signal-intensity  tracts.  Postcon-
trast  T1-weighted  MRI  allows  confirmation  of  patent  tracts
(rim enhancement) versus chronic remodeling (minimal en-
hancement) (23,24). Reports should emphasize tract stability,
progression, or regression to guide biologic therapy (3,7).

Serial MRI allows tracking of the response to therapy, be-
cause radiologic healing often lags behind clinical improve-
ment. In a meta-analysis (23), only 25% of patients with PFCD
achieved radiologic healing despite nearly one-half report-

ing  remission.  Persistent  fluid-filled  tracts  correlate  with  a
higher  relapse  rate,  while  fibrotic  tracts  indicate  sustained
remission (23,24). Radiologists should document whether a
tract is fully fibrotic, because this finding informs decisions
about  long-term  therapy.  Reflecting  this,  trials  (44,45)  in-
creasingly use MRI-based healing as a treatment endpoint,
with investigators recognizing that clinical remission alone
may be inadequate for long-term success.

The  Treatment  Optimization  and  Classification  of  Peri-
anal Crohn Disease (TOPCLASS) classification system allows
stratification of perianal Crohn disease according to severity,
stability, and surgical candidacy (46). The TOPCLASS classi-
fication system is clinically driven, incorporating symptom
burden, patient goals, and disease trajectory. Imaging find-
ings, although not core criteria, support decision-making by
showing when a patient who was not eligible for surgery (eg,
TOPCLASS 2b) transitions to being a surgical candidate (eg,
TOPCLASS 2a) due to therapy-induced fibrosis of a previous-
ly fluid-dominant fistula (47). Patients with irreversible per-
ineal destruction or severe symptoms despite undergoing a
fecal  diversion  procedure  may  meet  criteria  for  TOPCLASS
class  3,  for  which  proctectomy  is  required  to  enable  long-
term healing (Fig 8) (46).

Perianal  fistulas  may  be  incidental  findings  on  fluorine
18  (18F)  fluorodeoxyglucose  (FDG)  PET/CT  images,  with  fo-
cal uptake outlining the fistula tract (Fig 10). Authors of one
study (54) found distinct uptake in fistula tracts compared
with the adjacent anal mucosa, suggesting that PET/CT can
reveal unsuspected incidental perianal fistulas.

Endoanal US.—Endoanal US allows detection of fistulas intra-
operatively but has limited accuracy without the use of anes-
thesia. Given the short length of the anal canal (3–5 cm), endo-

anal US functions similarly to a low transrectal scan, making
transperineal US challenging without sedation (55). Overall,
US techniques are both time-consuming and uncomfortable
for awake patients, requiring direct probe manipulation in or
around the anal canal. MRI is the preferred option for patients
who can tolerate it.

Classification and Reporting of Fistulas

Course and Escape Patterns of Fistulas
Perianal fistulas follow defined anatomic escape patterns from
the intersphincteric space (Fig 3) (1,2,19). The intersphincteric

space  serves  as  the  origin  for  most  fistulas,  with  tracts  fol-
lowing one of four primary escape routes: (a) downward exit
through  the  anal  verge  (intersphincteric  fistula),  (b)  lateral
breach  of  the  external  sphincter  (transsphincteric  fistula),
(c)  upward  extension  into  the  supralevator  space  (supras-
phincteric  fistula),  or  (d)  aberrant  routes  bypassing  normal
sphincteric  anatomy  (extrasphincteric  fistula).  “Horseshoe”
abscesses represent a failure to exit, with the fistula accumu-
lating  fluid  in  the  intersphincteric  space,  without  external
drainage. Recognizing these patterns improves classification

Reporting Strategies
All  fistulas  should  be  documented  using  a  consistent  MRI
reporting framework, regardless of their cause. Essential re-
porting elements include the origin, course, and complexity
of the fistula, including branching and associated abscesses.
The most severe fistula should be classified using the Parks or
St James systems. Each tract should be evaluated separately if
multiple discrete fistulas are present (1,6). Structured report-
ing templates (Figs 19, 20, S1) promote concise standardized

communication of the anatomy, complexity, and evolution of
the  fistula.  Setons,  identified  as  hypointense  linear  intensi-
ty (signal voids), should be explicitly documented. For PFCD
fistulas,  additional  emphasis  is  placed  on  serial  change,  the
degree of fibrosis, and the presence of proctitis. Chronic PFCD
fistulas may remain stable in appearance for years; these of-
ten represent epithelialized seton tracts rather than resolving
fistula  tracts  (25,26).  Radiologists  should  compare  current
with prior studies whenever available to assess tract progres-
sion, healing, or branching complexity (3,5,7,56).

restriction,  arterial  phase  enhancement,  and  rim  enhance-
ment if they are sufficiently wide (Fig 13) (58). Chronic or par-
tially  healed  fistulas  demonstrate  mixed  signal  intensity  on
T2-weighted  MR  images,  with  hypointense  fibrosis  in  treat-
ed  segments  and  hyperintense  fluid  in  active  or  patent-ap-
pearing areas. The degree of fibrosis, as defined by van Rijn
et al (24), refers to the estimated proportion of a fistula tract
composed of fibrosis relative to nonfibrotic components such
as granulation tissue or fluid. A higher degree of fibrosis cor-
relates with long-term clinical closure, while fluid-persistent
tracts  are  associated  with  relapse.  While  van  Rijn  et  al  (24)
proposed a 0%–100% fibrosis scale for research purposes, this
model is not routinely applied in clinical reporting. Instead,
qualitative descriptors such as fluid-persistent, mixed, or fi-

brosis-dominant provide a useful narrative of fistula healing
and treatment response (Figs 6, 21; Table 4) (23,24).

In patients with severe PFCD, chronic inflammation and fi-
brosis may lead to formation of atypical abscesses that do not
conform  to  conventional  intersphincteric,  transsphincteric,
or supralevator pathways. Submucosal abscesses can develop
in  the  anal  canal  without  intersphincteric  extension  due  to
scarring in the intersphincteric space (Fig S2) (12).

PFCD  fistulas  frequently  coincide  with  diffuse  proctitis,
anorectal  junction  proctitis,  or  rectal  wall  thickening.  Ra-
diologists should document proctitis status because it affects
surgical  options  and  healing  (Figs  8,  20)  (56).  Extrasphinc-
teric tracts extending from the rectum also deserve empha-
sis  because  of  their  high  origin  and  their  association  with
proctitis, which limit standard surgical approaches (Fig 20).
The  distinction  between  active  proctitis  and  chronic  rectal
thickening is poorly defined. The modified Van Assche index
(63) allows classification of rectal involvement as normal (no
thickening), thickened (>3 mm), or showing T2 hyperintensi-
ty (ie, edema or stratification). However, these definitions do
not fully account for mild chronic thickening or the variable
appearance of an underdistended rectum, requiring radiolo-
gists to develop a practical internal threshold for distinguish-
ing “within normal limits” from mild proctitis on MR images.
MRI scoring systems (eg, the Van Assche Index, modified
Van  Assche  index,  the  magnetic  resonance  novel  index  for
fistula imaging in Crohn disease [MAGNIFI-CD], the pediat-

assessment of PFCD severity, treatment response, and prog-
nosis in clinical trials (63–67) (Table 5). The presence and size
of an abscess are predictors of poor outcomes, while healing
correlates with reduced hyperintensity and increasing fibro-
sis  at  T2-weighted  MRI.  An  international  Delphi  panel  con-

Surgical Management of Perianal Fistulas
The surgical approach depends on the complexity of the fistu-
la  and  the  involvement  of  the  sphincter.  Low  intersphincter-
ic  or  transsphincteric  tracts,  originating  below  the  anorectal
junction, are typically amenable to fistulotomy (>90% success,
minimal  incontinence)  (43),  while  high  or  complex  fistulas
that arise at or above the puborectalis muscle typically require
sphincter-preserving techniques or staged surgical repair (eg,

advancement flap repair, ligation of the intersphincteric fistu-
la tract) or a fecal diversion procedure (48,49). Laser closure of
the fistula (laser ablation of the tract) has been reported to have
healing rates of 44%–80% in patients with cryptoglandular fis-
tulas, with low morbidity (68). Evidence in patients with Crohn
disease remains limited, to our knowledge, so laser techniques
are reserved for select patients with PFCD.

In  PFCD,  setons  are  routinely  placed  to  control  sepsis,
maintain  tract  patency,  and  facilitate  medical  therapy  (50).
However, repeated insertion of setons often fails to fully heal
the  tract;  instead,  the  fistula  epithelializes  and  persists,  ap-
pearing as a chronic sinus on MR images (Fig 7) (17,26).

Preoperative  MRI  allows  evaluation  of  high  tracts  ex-
tending  above  the  anorectal  junction,  where  intersphinc-
teric fibrosis and altered pressure dynamics influence the
surgical approach (41). Imaging shows the involvement of
the  sphincter,  secondary  extensions,  and  abscesses,  di-
rectly  influencing  whether  fistulotomy,  seton  placement,
staged repair, or diversion is appropriate.

Surgery  in  patients  with  PFCD  is  complicated  by  chronic
inflammation,  recurrence  of  fistulas,  and  proctitis.  Active
proctitis  and  anal  canal  strictures  substantially  reduce  the
likelihood of surgical success. High-origin fistulas at or above
the anorectal junction frequently require fecal diversion pro-
cedures  instead  of  curative  repair  (Fig  8)  (33,50).  Persistent

Pitfalls and Special Considerations

Suboptimal  Imaging  Planes.—Accurate  assessment  of  peri-
anal fistulas depends on using proper imaging planes. Small

field-of-view  oblique  axial  turbo  spin-echo  MRI  best  defines
fistulas. MRI acquisition should be orthogonal to the anal canal
or  low  rectum  to  optimize  visualization.  However,  misalign-
ment  due  to  inexperienced  MRI  technologists  or  reliance  on
rectal cancer protocols that prioritize mid rectum imaging can
distort anal canal anatomy, obscuring fistula pathways (Fig 11)
(56). Recognizing misalignment prevents mischaracterization
of the extent of the fistula or the involvement of the sphincter.

Identifying Setons.—Setons in cryptoglandular and PFCD fis-
tulas maintain drainage, prevent abscesses, and promote scar-
ring. On CT images, setons appear as linear hyperattenuating
structures. On MR images, setons are thin hypointense signal
voids (Figs 22, 23) that can be subtle due to material composi-
tion (eg, silicone, nylon, rubber) (Fig S3) (26,33). Identification
of setons is improved with postcontrast 3D T1-weighted fat-sup-
pressed  MRI  sequences.  Seton-associated  tracts,  unlike  pri-
mary fistula tracts, are expected findings. Radiologists should
distinguish  between  a  fistula  actively  drained  by  a  seton  and

Postproctectomy and Ileoanal Fistulas
Persistent sinus tracts are common after proctectomy in pa-
tients with PFCD. Similarly, fistulas after total colectomy with
ileoanal  anastomosis  pose  diagnostic  challenges,  especially
in patients initially misdiagnosed with ulcerative colitis who
are later reclassified as having Crohn disease (46). This mis-
classification,  frequently  identified  in  patients  who  under-
went colectomy 15–25 years ago, complicates long-term treat-
ment due to recurrent fistulas, strictures, and pouch-related
complications (Fig 25) (72). In patients who have undergone
proctectomy,  MRI  allows  evaluation  of  inflammation  in  the
proctectomy space, nonhealing perineal wounds, and occult
sinus tracts. Persistent dead space impairs healing, leading to
chronic  fluid  collections  or  epithelialized  tracts  that  contin-

Secondary  lesions  and  mimics.—  Inflammatory  and  infec-
tious  processes  in  the  ischioanal  fossae  and  the  perineum
can  mimic  perianal  fistulas.  Infected  pilonidal  cysts  can  re-
semble  perianal  fistulas,  causing  inflammatory  stranding
and possible perineal abscesses. A lack of connection to the
rectum  or  anus  observed  on  images  and  at  examination  al-
lows them to be differentiated from perianal fistulas (73). In-
fected Bartholin gland cysts can mimic perianal fistulas, but,
similar to pilonidal cysts, they typically lack a direct connec-
tion to the anus. A perianal fistula extending to the labia may
secondarily  infect  the  Bartholin  gland,  creating  a  mixed  in-
tersphincteric fistula and a secondary Bartholin abscess (Fig
S7). MRI should allow determination of whether the primary
abnormality originates from the anal canal (ie, true fistula) or
the Bartholin gland (ie, cystic infection). Other causes of ab-
scesses in the ischioanal fossae include those from decubitus
ulcers,  which  are  readily  identifiable  by  their  overlying  skin
defect. Hidradenitis suppurativa can extend to the anus but
differs from perianal fistulas because of its characteristic skin
thickening (74).

Hemorrhoids and other intrinsic anal processes can mim-
ic fistulas. Prolapsed internal hemorrhoids typically follow a
vertical course resembling intersphincteric fistulas protrud-
ing from the anal verge (Fig 26). However, their serpiginous
shape,  intermediate  signal  intensity  on  non–fat-saturated
T2-weighted MR images, and frequent postcontrast enhance-
ment  help  distinguish  them  from  fluid-rich  tracts.  Hemor-
rhoids often enhance briskly, although enhancement may be
reduced or absent when they are thrombosed (74).

Cancers Associated with Perianal Fistulas
Although  they  are  usually  benign,  chronic  perianal  fistulas
increase  the  risk  of  mucinous  adenocarcinoma,  squamous
cell  carcinoma,  and  low  rectal  adenocarcinoma  extending
along  fistula  tracts,  with  PFCD  substantially  raising  the  risk

of anorectal cancer (34). Anal malignancies linked to perianal
fistulas may arise in the anal canal, extend through a fistula,
or originate from its epithelial lining. Malignancy in chronic
fistulas is rare (<1%), but the risk increases in patients with
PFCD  lasting  more  than  10  years,  particularly  in  those  with
persistent tracts (34).

Mucinous adenocarcinoma is the most common malignancy
associated with perianal fistulas and disproportionately affects
patients  with  PFCD  (34).  AT  MRI,  mucinous  adenocarcinoma
appears as T2-hyperintense microcystic tissue, with minimal
diffusion  restriction,  mimicking  a  fluid-filled  tract  and  war-
ranting biopsy when fistulas are chronic. These tumors often
arise in fistula tracts or prior abscess cavities and may resemble
a persistent tract due to their signal intensity characteristics.
Recognizing  microcystic  architecture  on  serial  MR  images  is
important because radiologists may detect malignancy before
clinical suspicion arises (75) (Figs 27, 28). In comparison, T2-in-
termediate signal intensity suggests squamous cell carcinoma,
especially in patients with risk factors such as HIV or human
papillomavirus infection. Tumors in these settings may distort
the  anatomy  of  the  fistula,  invade  the  intersphincteric  space,
and follow fistula pathways (Fig 29).

Innovative Techniques
Surgeons use 3D visualization for complex fistula anatomy on
MR images, especially in patients who previously underwent an
examination under anesthesia including probing of the fistula
tract  or  had  suspected  complexity.  Advanced  3D  visualization
allows mapping of a perianal fistula to the sphincter, including
3D-printed  models,  two-dimensional  renderings,  and  virtual
reality. All advanced 3D visualization options involve segment-
ing the fistula and sphincter complexes on small field-of-view
MR images (Figs 17, S8). Some surgeons report using 3D-printed
anatomic models and two-dimensional visualization of 3D seg-
mentation images in patients with perianal fistulas, with appli-
cations ranging from research to clinical care (76,77). Advanced
3D visualization aids patient education and surgical planning by
clearly representing complex perianal fistulas. In a randomized
study (77), 96% of patients found 3D-printed fistula models use-
ful, reporting improved understanding of the surgical process.

Conclusion
MRI remains the reference standard modality for evaluation
of perianal fistulas, providing opportunities for classification,
treatment monitoring, and surgical planning. In addition to
detecting tracts and abscesses, radiologists assess the course
of the fistula, internal and external openings, and longitudi-
nal  changes,  particularly  in  patients  with  PFCD.  Serial  MRI
allows  differentiation  of  fluid-persistent  tracts  from  fibro-
sis-predominant ones, refining the assessment of treatment
response  and  informing  therapeutic  decisions.  In  patients

with  PFCD,  documenting  the  severity  of  proctitis  and  track-
ing the composition of a fistula over time, whether it is stable,
progressing, or regressing, guides treatment adjustments and
helps to avoid unnecessary intervention.

Effective  reporting  of  MRI  examinations  helps  to  define
the origin of the tract, secondary extensions, the placement
of  setons,  and  supralevator  involvement.  Recognizing  the
spectrum of fistula evolution, from fluid-persistent to fibro-
sis-predominant,  helps  prevent  overtreatment  and  missed
progression of disease. MRI interpretation incorporating seri-
al assessment, classification of escape pathways, and evalua-
tion of fibrosis progression improves diagnostic accuracy and
reporting for cryptoglandular and PFCD-associated fistulas.
