Mucin-producing neoplasms in the abdomen and pelvis are a dis-
tinct entity, separate from simple fluid-containing neoplasms and
loculated fluid collections. Mucin is a thick gelatinous substance
and—owing to its high water content—has imaging features that
can be mistaken for those of simple fluid-containing neoplasms
with multiple imaging modalities. However, mucin-producing neo-
plasms arise from specific organs in the abdomen and pelvis, with
unique imaging appearances, knowledge of which is important to
guide accurate diagnosis and management. With its large field of
view and high soft-tissue resolution, MRI has advantages over other
imaging modalities in characterizing these neoplasms. The authors
focus on the spectrum of MRI features of such mucin-producing
neoplasms and illustrate how—despite a varied organ origin—some
of these neoplasms share similar MRI and histopathologic features,
thereby helping narrow the differential diagnosis. One common
finding in these tumors is that the presence of internal complexity
and solid enhancing components increases as the degree of malig-
nant transformation increases. Lack of internal complexity gener-
ally indicates benignity. These tumors have a varied range of prog-
nosis; for example, a low-grade appendiceal mucinous neoplasm is
indicative of a good prognosis, while a mucinous tumor of the rec-
tum is known to manifest at an early age with aggressive behavior
and poorer prognosis compared with its nonmucinous counterpart.

Introduction

Mucin-producing tumors are entities that abdominal radiologists
encounter commonly in clinical practice. Typically, mucinous tumors
are mass-forming neoplasms with abundant extracellular mucin
production by neoplastic epithelial cells, resulting in mucin-filled
cysts, cystic dilatation of the involved organ or ducts, or mucin pools
invading the walls of organs with embedded clusters or aggregates of
tumor cells floating within them. The resulting gross appearance im-
parts characteristic imaging findings. Despite diverse sites of origin
in the abdomen and pelvis, shared pathologic features translate into
substantial overlap in imaging features at cross-sectional imaging.
Owing to its markedly superior soft-tissue resolution and large

field of view, MRI enables comprehensive evaluation of these tu-
mors, which adds value in determining multicompartmental distri-
bution. In this article, we provide a comprehensive review of MRI
characteristics of mucin-producing tumors arising from various
organs in the abdomen and pelvis along with pathologic features,
which would enable radiologists to arrive at an accurate diagnosis.

Mucin: Biochemical Structure,
Function, and MRI Appearance
Mucins are complex high-molecular-weight
glycoproteins that contain a long peptide back-
bone with structurally variable glycosylation or
oligosaccharide bonds repeated throughout the
peptide chain. The different glycosylation bonds
determine the functional and chemical properties
of the mucin. At the two ends of the mucin chain
are chemical groups that further differentiate
mucins into those that are secreted or anchored
onto the cell membrane (1).

Mucin is extensively secreted in the hu-
man body. Epithelial cells lining hollow organs
including the gastrointestinal tract, gallblad-
der, respiratory tract, and genitourinary tract,
as well as solid visceral organs including the
surface of the liver, pancreas, eyes, salivary
glands, and lacrimal glands, all secrete mucin.
It is involved with multiple biochemical func-
tions for cells beyond protection and lubrica-
tion, including cell signaling, regeneration, and
apoptosis (2,3).

Abnormal production of mucin or alteration

of its chemical structure can lead to numer-
ous diseases in the human body, ranging from
suboptimal organ function to neoplasms (4,5).
Twenty-two genes encoding mucin in the human
body—MUC1 to MUC22—have been discovered.
Owing to its high water content, mucin has
a characteristic appearance at MRI, commonly

appearing hypointense on T1-weighted im-
ages and hyperintense on T2-weighted im-
ages. However, the oligosaccharides attached
to the peptide chain within thick mucin cause
T1 shortening, which may increase T1 signal
intensity within these tumors. The presence of
internal complexity and enhancing solid compo-
nents increases as the degree of malignant trans-
formation increases. Hence, administration of
intravenous contrast material at MRI is essential
to identify enhancing soft-tissue components,
suggesting malignancy over benignity. Increased
tissue cellularity within solid components causes
restricted diffusion, a feature that can also be
used for early detection of malignancy within
mucinous tumors (6).

Common Cystic Mucinous Neoplasms

Pancreas

Intraductal Papillary Mucinous Neoplasm of
the Pancreas.—Intraductal papillary mucinous
neoplasm (IPMN) of the pancreas is a common,
incidentally found, cystic exocrine neoplasm of
the pancreas with prevalence increasing with
age, peaking in the 5th to 6th decades of life.
IPMN is more common in men than in women,
although the difference is less dramatic in
the United States than in Japan or Korea (7).
Microscopically, IPMN consists of mucin-con-
taining dilated ducts lined by mucin-producing
epithelial cells. There can be associated low-
grade or high-grade dysplasia, based on the de-
gree of cellular atypia or invasive carcinoma. A
concomitant invasive mucinous adenocarcinoma
is known to arise at a location separate from the
IPMN in about 4.1% of cases (7).

Histologically, side-branch IPMNs are usually

lined by gastric-type epithelium, while main-
duct IPMNs show an intestinal-, pancreatobili-
ary-, or oncocytic-type lining epithelium, which
is associated with a higher risk of malignancy
(8). Macro scopically, IPMN is further classi-
fied into side-branch, main-duct, or combined
types on the basis of the anatomic location of
the involved pancreatic duct. Unlike other cystic
mucinous neoplasms of the pancreas, IPMN
communicates with the main pancreatic duct
and lacks ovarian-type stroma.

At MRI, side-branch IPMN manifests as a

variably sized focal cystic lesion that is hypointense
on T1-weighted images and hyperintense on T2-
weighted images (Fig 1) and is commonly multiple
at diagnosis with or without visible main-duct
communication. Main-duct IPMN can demon-
strate focal, segmental, or diffuse dilatation of the
main pancreatic duct (Fig 2). The focal form often
has associated upstream duct dilatation, albeit less
pronounced as compared with the diffuse form.
An enhancing mural nodule or a solid component
and diffusion-weighted imaging may aid in diag-
nosis of malignant IPMN (9,10).

Several guidelines have been proposed to strat-

ify management of IPMN, taking into account
the likelihood of associated invasive carcinoma.
The 2017 revision of the Fukuoka guidelines for
pancreatic IPMN management proposed criteria
for “worrisome features” and “high-risk stigmata”
(11). For IPMNs with worrisome features, endo-
scopic US was proposed for further evaluation.
At endoscopic US, IPMNs with a mural nodule
greater than or equal to 5 mm, a suspicious-
appearing main pancreatic duct, and abnormal
results of cytologic analysis of cyst fluid would
warrant surgical resection. In addition, surgical
resection is proposed for all IPMNs demonstrat-
ing high-risk stigmata at imaging.

Mucinous Cystic Neoplasm.—Mucinous cys-
tic neoplasm (MCN) of the pancreas is a rare
tumor occurring predominantly in middle-aged
women and rarely in men. The majority are
discovered incidentally at imaging, while large
tumors can cause abdominal pain or early satiety.
It manifests as a well-encapsulated cystic mass
lined by mucin-secreting cuboidal epithelium
with underlying ovarian-like stroma, commonly
located in the body or tail of the pancreas. The
cyst fluid contains a variable mixture of blood

products or necrotic debris in addition to mucin.
Lack of communication with the main pancreatic
duct allows differentiation from IPMN. Elevated
carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) level of the cyst
fluid and serum CA 19-9 level have been used to
predict risk of carcinoma in MCN (13).

At MRI, pancreatic MCN manifests as a uni-
or multilocular cystic mass with a well-defined
capsule and variable internal septa (Fig 3) with-
out obstruction of the main pancreatic duct. Al-
though commonly hyperintense on T2-weighted
images, MCN can exhibit decreased T2 signal
intensity with a high concentration of debris or
hemorrhagic content, which can also contribute
to increased T1 signal intensity. Peripheral calcifi-
cations are difficult to visualize at MRI, although
sometimes seen at CT. Enhancing nodular-
ity or papillary projections are associated with
increased risk of carcinoma, as are solid compo-
nents that demonstrate restricted diffusion.
Treatment is surgical resection owing to
the propensity for malignant degeneration. In
a multicenter study by Postlewait et al (14),
adeno carcinoma or high-grade dysplasia was
seen in 14.9% of resected pancreatic MCNs,
with pancreatic head or neck location, larger
size, solid component or mural nodule, and duct
dilatation as predicted risk factors.

Ovary

Mucinous Ovarian Tumors.—Mucinous tumors
are the second most common epithelial neo-
plasms of the ovary. Age at presentation varies
widely, but most are common during the 4th to
5th decades of life, frequently incidentally found
in asymptomatic patients (15). Symptomatic
patients may demonstrate subacute to chronic
abdominal distention, bloating, vague abdomi-
nal pain, early satiety, or urinary urgency (16).
At gross examination, mucinous ovarian tu-
mors are well-encapsulated uni- or multilocular
cystic masses filled with mucin (Fig 4A). Micro-
scopically, they are lined by mucin-secreting
epithelium, most often of the gastrointestinal
type, infrequently with an endocervical-type lin-
ing. On the basis of architectural complexity and
cellular atypia, these are classified into muci-
nous cystadenoma, mucinous borderline tumor,
or mucinous carcinoma (17) (Fig 4D–4F).
Although the vast majority (80%) are benign,
imaging does not allow reliable distinction of
carcinoma from cystadenoma. Additionally,
mucinous and serous ovarian neoplasms can
demonstrate overlapping imaging appearances
(18). Therefore, surgical resection is required for
definitive diagnosis and treatment planning.

At MRI, mucinous ovarian tumors appear as

a well-encapsulated uni- or multilocular cystic
mass (Fig 4B, 4C), with the signal intensity of

the cyst fluid reflecting the mucinous and hem-
orrhagic content. Increased hemorrhage may
lower the signal intensity on T2-weighted images
and increase it on T1-weighted images. Internal
septa are typically smooth and demonstrate en-
hancement. While MRI is insensitive for detect-
ing calcifications, peripheral calcifications within
the mass may manifest as susceptibility artifacts.
Although imaging does not allow reliable dif-
ferentiation of subgroups of mucinous ovarian
neoplasms, MRI features favoring malignancy
include a thick wall or internal septa, enhancing
solid components with restricted diffusion, and
associated peritoneal carcinomatosis (17,18).
Compared with serous ovarian tumors, muci-
nous ovarian tumors are more commonly unilat-
eral and larger at presentation (18). Enhancing
solid components are associated with carcinoma
in both mucinous and serous tumors, but papil-
lary projections specifically are more common in
serous carcinoma (18). Diffusion-weighted im-
aging may be helpful for differentiating benign
and malignant ovarian epithelial neoplasms and
for monitoring treatment response (19,20).
Staging of mucinous ovarian tumors is

surgical using the Fédération Internationale de
Gynécologie et d’Obstétrique (FIGO) system
(21). Excision of adnexa is sufficient in treating
benign tumors, while mucinous carcinoma ne-
cessitates surgical resection of all gross disease
and adjuvant chemotherapy.

Appendix

Mucinous Epithelial Neoplasm.—Mucinous
neoplasm is the most common epithelial tumor
of the appendix (22). Most are seen in elderly
patients with equal distribution. Patients some-
times present with symptoms of appendicitis
owing to obstruction of the appendicular lu-
men. In advanced disease, patients may pre sent
with abdominal distention from pseudomyxoma
peritonei.

Microscopically, these tumors consist of mucin-
secreting epithelial cells replacing normal mucosa
lining a mucin-filled dilated appendix (Fig E1)
and are classified into low-grade appendiceal
mucinous neoplasm (LAMN), high-grade appen-
diceal mucinous neoplasm (HAMN), and adeno-
carcinoma (23) on the basis of cellular atypia. The
mucin in both LAMN and HAMN can dissect
into the appendiceal wall and perforate the visceral
peritoneum, posing a high risk for subsequent
development of pseudomyxoma peritonei.

Appendiceal mucocele is a term used to de-
scribe the specific imaging appearance of a
mucin-distended tubular appendix, which can
be caused by both nonneoplastic and neoplastic
processes. Nonneoplastic dilatation rarely dis-
tends the appendix to greater than 2 cm (24). At
MRI, appendiceal mucinous neoplasm manifests
as a tubular right lower quadrant T1-hypoin-
tense and T2-hyperintense structure attached to
the cecum (Fig 5). Mucin or hemorrhage can
increase the signal intensity on precontrast T1-
weighted images.

Enhancing mural nodularity or thick septa
and wall thickening should raise concern for
carcinoma. Wall calcifications and intraluminal
gas secondary to rupture can be seen in some
cases as susceptibility artifacts, although they
are more easily detected at CT. Although not
as well described at MRI, CT features raising
concern for coexisting appendiceal mucinous
neoplasm have been described in patients pre-
senting with acute appendicitis, demonstrating
additional CT features such as cystic dilatation
with luminal diameter greater than 1.3 cm with
mural calcifications (25).

Radiologists must have a high index of suspi-
cion for appendiceal mucinous neoplasm when
interpreting CT or MRI studies in patients
presenting with acute appendicitis, as treatment
of appendiceal mucinous neoplasm involves open
or laparoscopic appendectomy, cecum resection,
and right hemicolectomy, with attention paid to
avoid spillage at excision.

Pseudomyxoma Peritonei.—Pseudomyxoma
peritonei is characterized by mucinous deposits
within the peritoneal cavity secreted by a variety
of mucinous neoplasms, with appendiceal muci-

nous neoplasm being the most common. Pseu-
domyxoma peritonei is caused less frequently
by mucinous neoplasms arising from the colon,
rectum, stomach, pancreas, or urachus (26).
Abdominal distention by this mucinous jelly like
material is colloquially called “jelly belly.”

The secreted mucin simulates the normal
flow of peritoneal fluid in the peritoneal cavity
by the so-called redistribution phenomenon,
resulting in site-selective mucin accumulation
(27). The pelvis, paracolic gutters, omentum,
and liver capsule are the earliest sites of muci-
nous implants, with early sparing of the mesen-
tery due to its relatively mobile nature. How-
ever, fixed segments of small bowel such as at
the ligament of Treitz and sites of adhesion from
prior surgery may be involved earlier (26).

Mucinous implants may not contain neoplastic

cells, although their presence correlates with a
worse prognosis. There is a histologic grading of
pseudomyxoma peritonei, based on the degree
of cellular components in mucinous implants.
This includes acellular pseudomyxoma peritonei,
low-grade mucinous carcinoma peritonei/dis-
seminated peritoneal adenomucinosis (DPAM),
high-grade mucinous carcinoma peritonei/peri-
toneal mucinous carcinomatosis (PMCA), and
high-grade mucinous carcinoma with signet-ring
cells (PMCA-S) (23).

Current evidence suggests that pseudomyx-
oma peritonei in women is more commonly of
appendiceal origin as opposed to ovarian origin
(28). Primary ovarian adenocarcinoma can cause
peritoneal carcinomatosis but infrequently leads
to pseudomyxoma peritonei (29). Careful exami-
nation of the appendix is necessary when ovarian
mucinous tumor is identified with pseudomyx-
oma peritonei. Similarly, concurrent mucinous

does not cause solid organ surface scalloping.
Diffusion-weighted imaging may improve de-

Treatment includes a combination of laparos-

and hyperthermic intraperitoneal chemotherapy

pelvis (32)—that can aid in preoperative plan-

ning. Specifically, radiologists should describe
any mucinous implants within the porta hepatis
and lesser omentum and around the inferior vena

complete surgical excision of these intraoperative
blind spots.

Rare Cystic Mucinous Neoplasms

Hepatobiliary System

Mucinous Cystic Neoplasm.—MCN of the liver
and biliary system is a rare neoplasm commonly
found incidentally, seen in middle-aged women
with a mean age of 51 years at presentation, with
invasive MCNs seen in older patients (33).

MCN was previously known as biliary cyst-
adenoma or cystadenocarcinoma in the presence
of malignant components. In the past 2 decades,
the presence of ovarian stroma underlying the
epithelium has been established as an essential
component of diagnosis of MCN of the liver and
pancreas, resulting in the World Health Organiza-
tion (WHO) requirement for this criterion (34).
Although the precise etiology is unknown, implan-
tation of ectopic ovarian rests during embryogen-
esis is the most accepted theory, also explaining its
predilection for the left lobe of the liver (33).

Macroscopically, MCN demonstrates a well-

encapsulated multilocular and multiseptated
cystic mass containing variable amounts of mucin
and hemorrhage (Fig 7A). Microscopically, the
tumor is lined by mucin-secreting pancreato-
biliary-type epithelium in the majority of cases
with underlying ovarian-like stroma (Fig 7B).
MCN is predominantly benign, with carcinoma-
tous changes seen in 5.6% of reported cases (33).
Infrequently, extrahepatic location within the
gallbladder has also been reported (35).

At MRI, MCN of the liver and biliary system
reflects its macroscopic appearance, manifesting as
a solitary encapsulated multilocular and multisep-
tated cystic mass commonly in segment IV of the
liver, without communication with bile ducts. Oc-
casionally, direct mass effect from the MCN can
give rise to upstream biliary obstruction without
downstream ductal dilatation. The cyst fluid dem-
onstrates the same MRI features as MCN of the
pancreas described earlier (Fig 7C, 7D). Standard
treatment is resection with wide margins (2 cm)
(36) to prevent local recurrence, which can be
seen in 80% of cases after partial resection and in
10%–20% after wide resection (37).

Intraductal Papillary Neoplasm of the Bile
Duct.—Intraductal papillary neoplasm of the
bile duct (IPNB) is a premalignant entity arising
from the bile ducts and is considered a counter-
part of pancreatic IPMN. It is more common in
men during the 5th and 6th decades of life (38).
In East Asian countries, IPNB is more common
than MCN, with liver flukes and hepatolithiasis
as the main contributing risk factors. In the West-
ern world, primary sclerosing cholangitis and
choledochal cyst are contributing risk factors.
Symptomatic patients present with obstruc-
tive-type symptoms including jaundice, elevated
bilirubin level, and right upper quadrant pain.
Biliary obstruction could be secondary to an
obstructing intraductal mass, hypersecretion of
mucin affecting normal bile flow, or a combina-
tion of both processes (38). Although IPNB is
a precancerous lesion, associated invasive carci-
noma is seen in only one-half of cases (38).

Communication with the biliary tree is a key
distinguishing feature. Approximately one-third
of IPNBs demonstrate hypersecretion of mucin,
resulting in focal dilatation of the bile duct at
gross examination. There are two main types of

Retroperitoneum

Urachus

Mucinous Cystic Neoplasm.—Primary retro-
peritoneal cystic neoplasms are rare and can be
further categorized into cystadenoma, border-
line tumor, and cystadenocarcinoma. Mucinous
metastases to the retroperitoneum are more
common than primary tumors (Fig E3). A meta-
analysis suggests a significant predilection for
middle-aged women, with fewer cases reported in
male patients (50).

Microscopic examination demonstrates mucin-

secreting epithelium lined by ovarian stroma.
Since there are no epithelial cells in the retro-
peritoneum, they are postulated to arise from
either ectopic ovarian tissue, dominant mucinous
epithelium from teratomatous tissue, residual uro-
genital apparatus from embryologic development,
or mucinous metaplasia of mesothelial cells (51).
At MRI, these are well-circumscribed cystic
masses, with cyst fluid signal intensity dictated
by the degree of hemorrhagic or proteinaceous
content and with solid nodular enhancing com-
ponents associated with carcinomatous change.

Mucinous Carcinoma of the Urachus.—The
urachus is the embryologic remnant resulting
from persistence of the allantois and cloaca,
connecting the urinary bladder dome to the
umbilicus (52). Both benign and malignant neo-
plasms arising from the urachus are rare. The
majority of malignant neoplasms arising in the
urachus are adenocarcinomas, with mucinous
being the predominant subtype; these are associ-
ated with a better survival rate than the anatom-
ically closely located urothelial carcinoma at the
same stage (53).

At MRI, urachal adenocarcinoma manifests

as a midline mass in the pelvis between the
umbilicus and bladder dome, most commonly
near the bladder dome. The mass commonly ap-
pears hyperintense on T2-weighted images with
variable T1 signal intensity, reflecting mucinous
contents (Fig E4). If present, solid components
demonstrate enhancement on postcontrast T1-
weighted images. Surgical excision is the defini-
tive treatment.

Noncystic Mucinous Neoplasms

Colon and Rectum
Mucinous adenocarcinoma is a subtype of
colorectal carcinoma (CRC), accounting for
approximately 10% of all CRCs (54,55). Mu-
cinous CRC occurs more commonly in women
and younger patients and is more common in the
proximal colon (56).

Colorectal mucinous adenocarcinoma is com-
posed of greater than 50% extracellular mucin at
histopathologic examination. Overproduction of
the MUC2 gene has been observed in mucinous
CRC; this gene produces mucin-2, a protein se-
creted by goblet cells in the colon (57). Differen-
tiating mucinous CRC from nonmucinous CRC
is vital, since mucinous CRC has poor response
to treatment and worse prognosis compared with
nonmucinous CRC when standard treatment is
applied (54).

Chronic inflammation caused by inflammatory

bowel disease can predispose patients to muci-
nous colorectal cancer (58,59). In this popula-
tion, malignancy should be suspected in patients
presenting with a mass at a bowel resection site
or within perianal fistulizing disease (Fig E5).

Mucinous neoplasms manifest as a typical T2-
hyperintense lobulated mass at MRI with variable
degrees of solid enhancing components (Fig 11).

Rectal Mucinous Adenocarcinoma.—Rectal
adenocarcinoma containing mucin is a distinct
type of rectal cancer, with mucinous adenocar-
cinoma and signet-ring cell carcinoma as the
two histologic subtypes. Mucin can also be seen
within rectal cancers as a response to neoadju-
vant therapy (60). Mucin that contains viable
tumor cells and acellular mucin both have similar
imaging appearances. This fact compounded by
the poor prognosis, early age of presentation,
and aggressive behavior of these tumors poses a
unique challenge in their management.

The signet-ring cell carcinoma subtype is

composed of more than 50% cells contain-
ing intra cellular mucin. Extensive submuco-
sal growth is typical, which gives rise to the
linitis plastica appearance, as with signet-ring
cell tumors of the stomach or colon. Owing to
the submucosal growth pattern with normal
overlying mucosa, imaging plays a major role
in identification of this tumor. At MRI, linitis
plastica manifests as long-segment thickening

Locally advanced rectal cancer is typically
treated with neoadjuvant chemoradiation therapy
followed by total mesorectal excision. Both non-
mucinous and mucinous rectal cancer can un-
dergo mucinous degeneration after neoadjuvant
therapy. The treatment-induced mucin is typically
acellular and is considered a treatment response,
as opposed to cellular mucin in mucinous adeno-
carcinoma. However, both have a similar appear-
ance at MRI, making evaluation of the treatment
response of mucinous tumors challenging.

Increased signal intensity heterogeneity of

cellular mucin on T2-weighted images may
provide a potential clue for distinguishing treat-
ment response from persistent mucinous tumor;
however, there is no robust consensus in the
literature. Close imaging follow-up and detailed
anatomic description of the presence and location
of mucin pools is currently the best option for
allowing complete surgical resection.

Adenocarcinoma with Intracellular
Mucin
By strict definition, adenocarcinomas with intra-
cellular mucin are different in gross morphology
and imaging appearance from the typical mucin-
producing tumors in the abdomen and pelvis;
these tumors are included here to illustrate dif-
ferences in their imaging appearance so as not to

confuse them with the classic mucin-producing
neoplasms described earlier. These are gland-
forming adenocarcinomas and contain a vari-
able amount of intracellular mucin, of which the
signet-ring cell type is an example.

Signet-ring mucinous tumors can affect any
segment of the gastrointestinal tract, from the
esophagus to the rectum. The most common
include rectal signet-ring adenocarcinoma—as
highlighted in a prior section—and gastric adeno-
carcinoma. Additionally, signet-ring cell carcinoma
arising from visceral organs such as the gallbladder
and pancreas has also been reported (45).

with worse prognosis than other types of gastric
carcinoma (69). Microscopically, signet-ring cell
carcinoma consists of neoplastic cells character-
ized by large intracellular cytoplasmic mucin and
peripherally displaced nuclei.

Cervix

Mucinous Adenocarcinoma of the Cervix.—Ad-
enoma malignum, also called minimal deviation
adenocarcinoma, is a small subset of cervical
adenocarcinoma. At histopathologic analysis, this
tumor demonstrates gastric foveolar or pyloric-
type epithelium, which invades the underlying
stroma as deeply invasive branching glands with
areas of solid papillae. The tumor cells contain
abundant clear or pale eosinophilic cytoplasm
with distinct borders, giving rise to the typical
imaging appearance (70).

Unlike the majority of cervical cancers, cervi-
cal mucinous adenocarcinoma of the gastric type
is not associated with human papillomavirus
(HPV) but instead is associated with Peutz-Jegh-
ers syndrome, warranting genetic testing in these
patients (71). Patients present with the classic
symptom of excessive watery vaginal discharge;
the tumor tends to manifest at an advanced stage
with a poorer 5-year survival rate (72).

At MRI, adenoma malignum appears as an
expansile cervical mass extending into the cervi-
cal stroma, frequently associated with accumu-
lation of endometrial fluid from obstruction of
the endocervical lumen. The mass may contain
multiple microcysts with enhancing components.
These microcysts are markedly hyperintense
on T2-weighted images and hypointense on
T1-weighted images and resemble a cluster of
nabothian cysts (73,74) (Fig E7), with enhancing
solid components that can demonstrate restricted
diffusion (75).

Less commonly, adenoma malignum may
manifest as a purely solid enhancing mass, with
more irregular margins than the multicystic form
(73). Careful examination for pelvic lymph-
adenopathy as well as tumor invasion into the
parametrium, vagina, or adjacent organs should
be performed, as these are commonly seen with
adenoma malignum at the time of diagnosis.

Mimics of adenoma malignum at MRI include

cervical hyperplasia, deep nabothian cysts, and
uterine cervicitis. Diagnostic confidence for
adenoma malignum may increase over that for
other benign causes if deep cervical stromal
involvement, enhancing solid components, and
ill-defined margins are observed within the mass
(74). However, tissue sampling is required for
definitive diagnosis.

Conclusion

Mucin-producing tumors of the abdomen and
pelvis are a distinct entity, not to be confused
with other serous fluid–containing neoplasms or
abdominal fluid collections. Although they arise
from multiple organ systems in the abdomen and
pelvis, the presence of thick gelatinous mucin with
a high water content imparts characteristic signal
intensity features at MRI, making MRI a powerful
tool for diagnosing these lesions. Moreover, many
of these tumors share common gross morpho-
logic and histopathologic features, allowing us to
subgroup them by organ system, as shown in the
Table. This alternative simplified approach to diag-
nosis of mucin-producing neoplasms in the abdo-
men and pelvis will hopefully provide the radiolo-
gist with an easier way to remember them, to guide
the appropriate next step in patient management.
