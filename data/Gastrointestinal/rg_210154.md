Sickle cell disorder (SCD) refers to a spectrum of hematologic
disorders that cause a characteristic clinical syndrome affecting the
entire body. It is the most prevalent monogenetic hemoglobinopa-
thy worldwide, with a wide range of focal and systemic expressions.
Hemoglobin gene mutation leads to the formation of abnormal
sickle-shaped red blood cells, which cause vascular occlusion and
result in tissue and organ ischemia and infarction. Recurrent epi-
sodes of acute illness lead to progressive multisystem organ damage
and dysfunction. Vaso-occlusion, hemolysis, and infection as a result
of functional asplenia are at the core of the disease manifestations.
Imaging plays an essential role in the diagnosis and management of
SCD-related complications in the abdomen and pelvis. A thorough
understanding of the key imaging findings of SCD complications
involving hepatobiliary, gastrointestinal, genitourinary, and mus-
culoskeletal systems is crucial to timely recognition and accurate
diagnosis. The authors aim to familiarize the radiologist with the
SCD spectrum, focusing on the detection and evaluation of mani-
festations that may appear at imaging of the abdomen and pelvis.
The topics the authors address include (a) the pathophysiology of
the disease, (b) the placement of SCD among hemoglobinopathies,
(c) the clinical presentation of SCD, (d) the role of imaging in the
evaluation and diagnosis of patients with SCD who present with
abdominal and pelvic manifestations in addition to extraperitoneal
manifestations detectable at abdominal or pelvic imaging, (e) imag-
ing features associated with common and uncommon sequelae of
SCD in abdominal and pelvic imaging studies, and (f) a brief over-
view of management and treatment of patients with SCD.

Pathophysiology and Clinical
Presentation

An HbS mutation affects molecular stability
and RBC function (1,3,6). Deoxygenated HbS
polymerizes, resulting in Hb aggregation and
clumping within the cell. This forms a rigid elon-
gated sickle-shaped RBC, further reducing HbS’s
affinity for oxygen (2,6). While polymerization
can be a reversible process with resolution in the
oxygenated state, it can also lead to RBC dehy-

dration by activation of several cell membrane
transport systems resulting in “dense” RBCs (an
irreversible process) (6).

The mechanisms behind SCD manifestations
are complex and multifactorial (Fig 3) (7). Ulti-
mately, microvascular obstruction by abnormally
shaped erythrocytes results in painful tissue isch-
emia, with subsequent restoration of blood flow
causing reperfusion injury (1,2). This recurring
series of events, leading to progressive ischemic
organ damage and chronic complications, charac-
terizes SCD (3).

By age 1 year, children may develop anemia,
pain crises, stroke, and widespread organ dam-
age, with the highest mortality rates occurring
between 6 months and 3 years of age (5,8). The
earliest signs and symptoms of disease frequently
include jaundice (icterus), fatigue, fussiness, and
dactylitis (painful swelling in the hands and feet)
(9). Stunted growth and delayed puberty are
common (9). Splenic dysfunction increases the
risk of infection, with pneumococcal sepsis being
the leading cause of death among children (5,8).
In this article, we focus specifically on the
abdominal and pelvic manifestations of SCD as
seen at various imaging modalities (Fig 4).

Vascular Manifestations
Vascular manifestations of SCD include vascu-
lopathy, VOC, and acute pain crises (2). Vascu-
lopathies are attributable to increased viscosity
and decreased deformability of abnormal RBCs
(Fig 3). Additionally, increased affinity of RBCs
to vascular endothelium may worsen VOC. Risk
factors include hypoxia, dehydration, infec-
tions, fever, high altitude, prolonged immobility,
extreme temperatures, physical or emotional
stress, trauma, steroid therapy, and anesthet-
ics (2). While small-vessel occlusion is typically
below the resolution of cross-sectional imaging,
evidence of infarction may be seen (7). Large-
vessel vasculitis can cause vessel narrowing with
or without inflammatory stranding (7). Treat-
ment includes hydration, pain control, proton
pump inhibitors, and, in the absence of neuro-
logic symptoms, anticoagulation therapy (2).

Splenic Manifestations

Splenomegaly and Splenic Sequestration
Splenomegaly occurs in approximately 10% of
patients older than 10 years and results from
vascular congestion (including sequestration),

infarction, or abscess (7,11). At CT, splenomeg-
aly is characterized by a craniocaudal diameter
greater than about 12 cm (Fig 7) (7).

Sequestration is characterized by rapid
pooling of blood in a solid organ, most com-
monly the spleen, followed by the liver. In acute
sequestration syndrome, splenic trapping of
sickled RBCs leads to acute vascular conges-
tion and anemia (2,7,11). Occurring mainly
in children with homozygous sickle cell Hb
(HbSS) genotype, sequestration may be trig-
gered by pain crisis, infection, or acute chest
syndrome (ACS) and complicated by cardiovas-
cular collapse, splenic rupture and/or hemor-
rhage, and potentially death (2,11,18). Seques-
tration occurs in 10%–30% of children ages 6
months to 3 years and in approximately 13% of
patients overall (although rare in patients older
than 8 years). Approximately 50% of children
experience recurrent episodes (7,11). The clini-
cal presentation includes sudden weakness or
lethargy, tachycardia, abdominal pain, and pallor
(7,11). Images show acute splenic enlargement
or larger-than-expected size for an autoinfarcted
spleen with or without intraparenchymal or

In SCD, recurrent VOC and subsequent splenic
infarcts lead to functional asplenia and auto-
splenectomy, usually by age 5 years and often
within the first 18–36 months of life (2,6,7,11).

replacement of healthy tissue by fibrosis, calcium,
and hemosiderin deposition (Fig 9) (11). At
contrast-enhanced CT, splenic infarcts appear as

ment and may be associated with splenomegaly in

US images, infarcted tissue is hypoechoic with no

decreased signal intensity on T1- and T2-weighted
images and fail to enhance with contrast material
administration (13). Autosplenectomy appears as

Splenic Abscess
Infarction and superinfection can lead to splenic
abscess formation, and patients present with fever

and left upper quadrant pain (7). US images
demonstrate a complex hypoechoic unilocular or
multiseptated lobulated mass. Depending on the
causative organism, air may be seen within the
abscess, appearing as multiple echogenic foci with
dirty posterior shadowing (7). Peripheral hyper-
emia may be present.

Renal Manifestations

Sickle Cell Nephropathy and End-Stage
Renal Disease
Generally, patients with SCD have increased
renal plasma flow and glomerular filtration
rate from prostaglandin hypersecretion causing
vasodilation (19). However, despite increased
renal perfusion, medullary perfusion is substan-
tially decreased, leading to VOC in the vasa recta
(21). The kidneys are particularly sensitive to
microvascular obstruction and ischemia owing
to their heavy vascularity and high rate of oxy-
gen consumption (11,22). While the renal cortex
is relatively spared from iron deposition in the
absence of transfusion therapy, repeat episodes of
intravascular hemolysis result in hemosiderin and
ferritin accumulation mainly in the proximal con-
voluted tubules (23,24). In SCD, increased po-
lymerization of HbS in the renal medulla leads to
VOC of the vasa recta and subsequent ischemia,
infarction, papillary necrosis, and scarring, any of
which can progress to acute failure (2,5,11). Not
surprisingly, approximately 60% of patients with
HbSS have renal involvement (22). Renal disease

Renal Cortical Infarcts
In SCD, renal cortical infarcts are character-
ized by a multifocal bilateral distribution,
appearing on contrast-enhanced CT images as
wedge-shaped or geographic regions of hypoen-
hancement with sharp margins (11). Similar to
pyelonephritis, multifocal infarction may appear
with a striated nephrogram at CT and MRI.
Unlike pyelonephritis, cortical rim enhancement
is typically preserved owing to a separate arterial
supply. This, along with clinical symptoms and
laboratory findings, helps to differentiate these
two entities (Fig 13) (11). If infarcts are compli-
cated by hemorrhage, perirenal hematoma may
be seen (11). Renal cortical necrosis may coexist
with renal infarcts and appear as a reversal of
the usual corticomedullary pattern, with the
cortex being hypointense at MRI relative to the
medulla, mimicking hemosiderin deposition
(Figs 5, 14).

Renal Papillary Necrosis
In approximately 2.3% of patients with SCD,
recurrent VOC leads to ischemia and necrosis of
the medulla and renal papillae (2,7). The mani-
festation of renal papillary necrosis ranges from
painless microscopic hematuria to frank anemia,

of the hepatic sinusoids leads to vascular conges-

Imaging findings include acute hepatomegaly and
differential perfusion (7). Treatment is supportive
and can include transfusion (2,7).

Hepatic Infarction and Abscess
Hepatic infarction, occurring in approximately
one-third of patients with SCD, manifests with
acute right upper quadrant pain and hepatomeg-
aly (11). Over time, fibrosis, capsular retraction,
and calcifications may be seen (11). Infarcts
appear as rounded or wedge-shaped peripheral
areas that are low attenuation on CT images,
hypointense on T1-weighted images, and hyper-
intense on T2-weighted images and demonstrate
patchy heterogeneous nonenhancement on CT
and MR images (Fig 17) (11). Concern for
superinfection and abscess formation should be
raised when lobular peripherally enhancing mul-
tiseptated collections with adjacent parenchymal
edema are detected (7,11).

Cirrhosis and Hepatocellular Carcinoma
Cirrhosis can result from iron deposition from
multiple transfusions, hepatitis infection, or re-
peated infarction (40). Imaging findings include a
heterogeneous nodular liver that may be larger than

in those without SCD owing to iron overload (Fig
E2) (40). At MRI, associated fibrosis is hypoin-
tense at T1-weighted imaging and hyperintense
at T2-weighted imaging (40). Decompensated
liver cirrhosis can manifest with ascites, varices,
splenomegaly, and other signs of portal hyperten-
sion (40). At Doppler US, portal hypertension
is characterized by sluggish flow with increased
respiratory pulsatility (41,42). In advanced disease,
dilatation of the portal vein, reversal of flow (hepa-
tofugal flow) in the main vein and its branches, and
portal vein thrombosis may occur (41–43).

As in patients without SCD, cirrhosis increases

the risk of hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) in
patients with SCD (44). On noncontrast CT im-
ages, HCC lesions are typically hypoattenuating
and heterogeneous, whereas at contrast-enhanced
CT and MRI, HCC demonstrates heterogeneous
arterial enhancement with rapid central washout
and residual capsular enhancement, although
iron overload can confound the appearance of
benign and malignant liver lesions (Fig 18) (44).
Unlike hypointense regenerating nodules, HCC
tends to be hyperintense relative to the liver at
T2-weighted imaging (Fig 18) (44). While re-
stricted diffusion is indicative of malignancy, it is
a nonspecific imaging feature (45,46).

Hepatopathy and Cholestasis
Hepatopathy refers to hepatic dysfunction often
associated with cirrhosis and elevated bilirubin
levels not explained by acute symptoms and can
coincide with cholestasis or sequestration (7,51).
Cholestasis and cholangiopathy occur when

recurrent end-arterial and hepatic sinusoidal
obstruction results in ischemic damage to the
biliary tree and hepatotoxicity from disruption
of biliary flow (7,11,51). While rare, acute pre-
sentations—typically including fever, abdominal
pain, and increasing bilirubin levels—are clinical
emergencies (7). Initially, images demonstrate
dilated biliary ducts (Fig E5), with recurrent
insult leading to intra- and extrahepatic bili-
ary duct strictures or biloma formation (Fig
20, Movie) (7,11,51,57). Although treatment is
mainly supportive, liver failure may occur and
require transplantation (7).

Pancreatitis
Limited data on pancreatitis in patients with SCD
suggest a prevalence similar to that of the general
population (7,58). A few case studies of SCD-
associated pancreatitis feature patients of all ages
and those with coinheritance of β-thalassemia
(59–63). Pancreatitis can occur when VOC results
in ischemia or secondary to obstruction by bili-
ary stones (7,60). Clinical presentation includes
fever, nausea and/or vomiting, and epigastric pain
radiating to the back (7). At contrast-enhanced
CT and MRI, uncomplicated pancreatitis appears
with mild pancreatic enlargement and edema with
or without peripancreatic inflammatory changes,
and decreased T1 signal intensity and parenchy-
mal enhancement (Fig 21) (7,11). In necrotizing
pancreatitis, nonenhancing pancreatic parenchyma
can be seen (11).

Potential complications include peripancre-
atic walled-off necrosis, pseudocyst formation,
pseudoaneurysm, or venous thrombosis (11,62).
Treatment depends on the cause but mainly
includes fluid resuscitation, early enteral feeding,
and pain control. In gallstone pancreatitis, chole-
cystectomy may be required (7,11).

Gastrointestinal Manifestations:
Bowel Ischemia and Sequelae
VOC of the small bowel and small bowel mesen-
tery often develops in SCD and can result in ileus,
ischemic enteritis and colitis, bowel infarction,
necrosis, and even perforation (7,11). CT im-
ages may demonstrate segmental or diffuse bowel
wall thickening (7,11). The “target” sign, while
a nonspecific feature, may be seen on contrast-
enhanced CT images owing to alternating contrast
enhancement of hyperemic mucosa, hypoattenu-
ation of edematous submucosa, and enhancing
hyperemic serosa (11,64,65). On noncontrast CT
images, hypoattenuating submucosal edema versus
underlying mucosal hemorrhage may appear with
a pseudo–target sign (11). Hypoenhancement of
mucosa or air within the bowel wall (pneumatosis
intestinalis) or mesenteric/portal venous gas is
suggestive of necrosis (Fig 22) (7,11). Superinfec-
tion and perforation of ischemic bowel can lead to
abscess formation, while chronic ischemia can lead
to gastric ulceration and peptic ulcer disease (11).
Treatment of ischemic bowel frequently requires
surgical resection (7).

Pregnancy Complications
Pregnancy-associated mortality rates in women
with SCD range from six to 22 times higher than
those in women without SCD (2,8). Patients are
at increased risk for hypertension, preeclampsia,
miscarriage, maternal death, preterm delivery, fe-
tal intrauterine growth retardation, urinary tract
infections, pyelonephritis, thromboembolism,
VOCs, ACS, and hemolytic crises (2). Manage-
ment is multidisciplinary and dependent on
close observation throughout pregnancy and the
postpartum period (2).

Other Manifestations of SCD at
Abdominal and Pelvic Imaging

Although musculoskeletal and pulmonary sys-
tems are only partially included in abdominal
and pelvic imaging, knowledge of the manifesta-
tions of SCD in these systems is essential to avoid
missed diagnoses. In this section, we provide a
brief review of the most commonly seen muscu-
loskeletal and pulmonary complications of SCD
that can be encountered at abdominal and pelvic
cross-sectional imaging.

bony changes result in biconcave deformity of
vertebral bodies from compression by interver-

hematopoiesis is iso- to hypointense to the disk

signal intensity at T2-weighted imaging; has simi-

relative muscle on the short τ inversion-recovery
sequence; and is mildly enhancing with gadolin-

Tissue and bone infarction, splenic dysfunc-

increase the risk of osteomyelitis, which affects
18% of patients (5,7,17). While long bone di-
aphyseal infections are most frequent, any bone
may be affected and hematogenous spread may
lead to multifocal infection (2,5,17). The patient
presentation includes pain, fever, and tumes-

elitis at CT and US are often nonspecific, and
MRI is the modality of choice.

infection extension, joint effusion, sinus tract,
and abscess formation can also be seen (2,68).

hypointense fluid, although the presence of he-

adjacent soft-tissue fluid collections; and abnor-

common to both osteomyelitis and infarction, a
combination of fat-saturated T2-weighted imag-

weighted imaging, as well as imaging features
including the location of the abnormality or the
presence of serpiginous borders or a double-line
sign, can help differentiate infarction from os-
teomyelitis (17). Nuclear imaging examinations
are helpful in differentiation of osteomyelitis
from other pathologic conditions.

Approximately 7% of patients develop septic

arthritis, typically co-occurring with VOC and
bone infarction (17). If acute, large joint effusion
may be depicted at radiography, CT, and US,
with MR images showing perisynovial edema
and synovial enhancement after intravenous
gadolinium administration (Figs E10, E11) (17).
Antibiotic therapy may be combined with surgi-

Pulmonary Manifestations
Pulmonary manifestations can be detected at
abdominal CT owing to inclusion of the lower
portion of the chest (Table 3) (2,3,5–8,69,70).
Infection is one of the most common triggers
of ACS, a leading cause of hospitalization and
death in patients of all ages (Figs 25, E12)
(2,3,5,7,69,71). ACS can also be triggered by
pulmonary embolism and infarction, with a hy-
percoagulable state increasing the risk of venous
thromboembolism by 3.5-fold (7,70). In severe
cases, ACS can progress to acute respiratory
distress syndrome and chronic lung disease (Fig
E13) (7,69).

Mainstays of Management and
Treatment of SCD

In patients with SCD, blood transfusions are
the mainstay of treatment of acute anemia by
both increasing the oxygen-carrying capacity
of blood and reducing complications of VOC
by decreasing the amount of circulating HbSS
(72). Newborn screening, immunizations,
improved detection and treatment of infection
with prophylactic and therapeutic antibiotic
therapy, and disease-modifying medications
have drastically improved patient survival (6).

Conclusion

Abdominal and pelvic manifestations of SCD
are primarily due to VOC, hemolysis, or infec-
tion. Cross-sectional imaging with US, CT, and
MRI plays an essential role in the identification
of complications of SCD. The affected organs are
variably involved. Prompt diagnosis and manage-
ment depend on recognition of early complica-
tions. Advancements in technology and new
treatment development will continue to improve
understanding and management of this complex
disease.
