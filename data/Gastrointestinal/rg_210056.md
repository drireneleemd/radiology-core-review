The widespread use of high–spatial-resolution cross-sectional imag-
ing has led to an increase in detection of incidental pancreatic cystic
lesions. These lesions are a diverse group, ranging from indolent
and premalignant lesions to invasive cancers. The diagnosis of sev-
eral of these lesions can be suggested on the basis of their imaging
appearance, while many other lesions require follow-up imaging
and/or aspiration. The smaller cystic lesions, often branch-duct in-
traductal papillary mucinous neoplasms, have overlapping imaging
characteristics that make diagnostic assessment of the natural his-
tory and malignancy risk confusing. Expert panels have developed
societal guidelines, based on a consensus, for surveillance of these
lesions. However, these guidelines are often inconsistent and are
constantly evolving as additional scientific data are accumulated.
Identification of features associated with increased risk of malig-
nancy is important for proper management. The concept of field
defect, whereby pancreatic adenocarcinoma develops at a site dif-
ferent from the site of the pancreatic cyst, adds to the complexity of
screening guidelines. As a result of the differences in guidelines, key
stakeholders (eg, radiologists, gastroenterologists, and surgeons)
must review and come to a consensus regarding which guideline, or
combination of guidelines, to follow at their individual institutions.
Standardized reporting and macros are helpful for ensuring the
uniformity of interpretations. Radiologists play a critical role in the
detection and characterization of pancreatic cystic lesions, in the
follow-up recommendations for these lesions, and in the detection
of associated cancer.

Introduction
The widespread use of high–spatial-resolution cross-sectional imag-
ing has led to increased rates of detecting pancreatic cystic lesions.
The spectrum of these lesions ranges from benign entities (pseudo-
cyst, inflammatory cyst) to potentially malignant neoplasms (muci-
nous neoplasm [MCN], intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasm
[IPMN]) and frankly malignant tumors (solid pseudopapillary neo-
plasm [SPN], pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor [PNET], and ductal
adenocarcinoma) (1). Most cystic lesions of the pancreas are benign
and incidentally detected with imaging in approximately 10% of the
population and in up to 30% of individuals older than 70 years (2).

TEACHING POINTS
	 The imaging features of IPMNs that were reported as “wor-
risome” for malignancy in the 2017 international consensus
Fukuoka  guidelines  and  suggest  the  need  for  further  inves-
tigation with endoscopic US include a cyst diameter greater
than 3 cm, an enhancing mural nodule smaller than 5 mm,
a thickened enhancing cyst wall, MPD dilatation of 5–9 mm,
an abrupt change in MPD diameter with upstream pancreatic
atrophy, lymphadenopathy, and a cyst growth rate of greater
than or equal to 5 mm per 2 years.

	 High-risk stigmata considered by the International Association
of Pancreatology to be indications for resection include ob-
structive jaundice, a 5-mm or larger enhancing mural nodule,
and MPD dilatation of 1 cm or greater without another cause
of obstruction.

	 Assessing the risk of a single pancreatic cyst developing into
invasive cancer can be challenging, and the presence of an
IPMN  may  portend  an  increased  risk  of  invasive  pancreatic
cancer at disparate sites within the pancreas.

	 Multiple societies have created guidelines for the manage-
ment of pancreatic cysts. Five major guidelines are the in-
ternational  consensus  Fukuoka  2017,  American  College  of
Gastroenterology  2018,  American  College  of  Radiology
2017,  European  Study  Group  2018,  and  American
Gastroenterology  Association  2015  guidelines.  However,
as  shown  in  Table  E1,  there  is  variability  in  the  guidelines
among the different societies.

	 As a result of the differences in guidelines, key stakehold-
ers  (eg,  radiologists,  gastroenterologists,  and  surgeons)
must  review  and  come  to  a  consensus  regarding  which
guideline (or combination of guidelines) to follow at their
individual institutions.

The assessment of pancreatic cystic lesions
should include careful assessment of imaging
features (cyst morphology, presence of duct
communication) and clinical findings including
cystic fluid analysis, when available, to improve
diagnostic accuracy. Identification of certain key
imaging findings that confidently suggest one
lesion rather than the other, and more important,
of features that are more often associated with
malignancy, is crucial, as these features are used
to determine proper management. The biologic
behavior and risk of malignant transformation
in select pancreatic cysts vary according to the
histologic subtype. A relevant possibility associ-
ated with pancreatic cystic lesions, specifically
IPMNs, is the risk of a field defect—that is, the
development of a pancreatic adenocarcinoma at a
location within the pancreas that is different from
the site of the cystic lesion. The adenocarcinoma
can occur concomitantly with the identifica-
tion of the cystic lesion (synchronously) or later
(metachronously). Many societies have developed
expert but nonuniform consensus clinical guide-
lines, which are continuously evolving according
to accumulating medical evidence, to facilitate
decisions regarding management and surveillance
of these lesions.

In this article, we describe an approach to
assessment of some of the predominant pancre-
atic cystic lesions, with an emphasis on features
suggestive of malignancy. Benign inflammatory
cysts such as pseudocysts and lymphatic cysts are
not discussed, as they are beyond the scope of
this article. However, in the setting of pancreati-
tis, a pseudocyst or walled-off necrosis should be
considered a cause for cystic masses.

We also review the current guidelines for sur-
veillance of pancreatic cysts, highlighting the com-
plexity of and controversies regarding managing
and following up these lesions. Finally, we discuss
the importance of the field defect related to devel-
opment of invasive pancreatic adenocarcinomas.

Background and Epidemiologic
Factors
Pancreatic cystic lesions are commonly detected
incidentally because of the increased use of and
improvements in cross-sectional imaging, as well
as an aging population. They are reported on
20%–44% of MRI images of the abdomen (3,4)
and on up to 3% of CT scans (5). The higher rate
of detection with MRI is due to the high contrast
resolution of T2-weighted MR images, which bet-
ter delineate even very small cystic lesions.
In a meta-analysis of 48 860 asymptom-
atic patients undergoing abdominal imaging,
a prevalence of 8% of patients were noted to
have pancreatic cysts (6). A large population-
based study from Northern Germany showed
a weighted prevalence of pancreatic cysts of
49.1% at whole-body MRI (7). In high-risk pop-
ulations, including patients with a family history
of pancreatic cancer, pancreatic cysts have been
reported in up to one-third of individuals (8).
It has been reported that individuals with one,
two, or three or more first-degree relatives with
pancreatic cancer have a 4.6, 6.4, and 32.0-fold
increased risk of pancreatic cancer, respectively.
As such, familial pancreatic cancer has been de-
fined as pancreatic cancer affecting at least two
first-degree relatives (9).

Imaging Features and Practical
Approach
The frequent detection of incidental pancreatic
cysts necessitates an organized systematic ap-
proach to evaluating these lesions with imaging.
The key features of pancreatic cysts that should
be evaluated include size, number of cysts, pres-
ence or absence of septa, enhancing cyst walls
and nodules, and relationship to the main pan-
creatic duct (MPD). These findings may guide
management, including follow-up imaging, refer-
ral to a specialist for endoscopic US and aspira-
tion, and/or surgical removal.

Communication with the MPD is a critical
distinguishing feature of pancreatic cysts (10).
Similarly, the presence of a ductal communica-
tion may suggest a pseudocyst or walled-off
necrosis in the setting of pancreatitis rather than
other lesions such as MCN. Many small cystic
lesions do not show a communication and are
usually presumed to be branch-duct IPMNs
and undergo a follow-up similar to that for these
mucinous neoplasms.

It is important to evaluate the associated
features of the cyst. An internal enhancing solid
component increases the suspicion for malig-
nancy. A thick enhancing cyst wall is another
feature that can suggest a malignant or poten-
tially malignant lesion such as PNET or adeno-
carcinoma (which can rarely appear cystic) or
MCN, instead of the typical thin-walled cyst in
branch-duct IPMN. MRI is often useful in eval-
uating internal complexity, as it enables better
characterization of the cyst content, including
the presence of hemorrhage, enhancing nodules,
and septa. Usually, diffusion-weighted imaging
is not helpful for differentiating neoplastic from
nonneoplastic cystic lesions, and its utility in
determining potentially malignant lesions has
not been established (11).

Demographic data can be useful, but they
should not be the predominant information
used to characterize pancreatic cystic lesions
(Table) (8,12–15). A common mnemonic based
on patient age that can help guide the differ-
ential diagnosis is that SPN is a “daughter”
lesion; MCN, a “mother” lesion; and serous
cystadenoma (SCA), a “grandmother” lesion
(12). Although this mnemonic serves as a broad
guideline, it is certainly not definitive, given the
significant overlap in features. Demographic
data are most specific in the setting of MCNs, as
these lesions are rare in males. Gender is usually
not associated with an increase in the malignant
potential of pancreatic cystic lesions, with the
apparent exception of MCNs, whereas advanced
age has been correlated with increased malig-
nant risk (16,17).

Pancreatic Cystic Lesions
The typical clinical and imaging characteristics
of the described pancreatic cystic lesions, as well
as the classic biochemical and molecular find-
ings at cyst aspirate fluid analysis, are summa-
rized in the Table (8,12–15). The main pancre-
atic cystic lesions discussed in this article are
depicted in Figure 1.

Serous Cystadenoma
SCAs are benign lesions that can mimic other
benign and malignant lesions. However, they

potentially can be diagnosed noninvasively when
they demonstrate a characteristic imaging ap-
pearance, especially on MR images. While SCAs
typically occur sporadically, there is an increased
incidence in patients with von Hippel–Lindau
syndrome. Most affected patients (up to 80%)
are asymptomatic at the time of diagnosis, with
lesions found incidentally (18).

Larger SCAs can cause symptoms (18). The

microcystic form has classic imaging features,
with multiple (>6) small (<2 cm) cysts that
result in a multilobulated external contour that
is not generally seen in other cystic lesions (Fig
2). The honeycomb pattern with numerous tiny
cysts may appear solid on contrast-enhanced
CT or MR images owing to enhancement of the
closely apposed small cyst walls and can mimic
a hypervascular neuroendocrine tumor. Simi-
larly, at US, these tiny cysts may have a solid ap-
pearance as a result of the numerous interfaces
from the small cyst walls. SCA and cystic PNET
are among the few hypervascular cystic pancre-
atic neoplasms (19).

T2-weighted MRI can be helpful in showing
the cystic components of cystic pancreatic neo-
plasms, especially the microcystic form. It is not
uncommon for large SCA lesions (15%–20%) to
consist of a combination of small and large cysts
(20). An enhancing scar with stellate calcifica-
tions is seen at CT in up to 30% of lesions and
is considered pathognomonic for SCA (12).
SCAs can be diagnosed with high specific-
ity. Challenges to diagnosis occur when an SCA
appears to be solid or has calcifications, mimick-
ing PNET; when oligocystic SCAs (lesions with
fewer cysts) (Fig 3) mimic the more malignant
MCN; or rarely, when SCA with intratumoral
hemorrhage mimics PNET or SPN. SCAs may
have a morphologic appearance that is similar
to that of branch-duct IPMNs, but they do not
communicate with the pancreatic duct. This dis-
tinction is significant, considering the association
of branch-duct IPMNs with cancer. However,
not all branch-duct IPMNs show a communica-
tion with the pancreatic duct. An additional chal-
lenge is that although SCAs are benign, they may
cause mass effect and upstream pancreatic duct
dilatation. Generally, asymptomatic SCAs do not
require follow-up imaging unless the diagnosis is
uncertain. Surgery is considered when patients
experience symptoms related to the compression
of other organs (13).

Mucinous Cystic Neoplasms
MCNs are seen nearly exclusively in women and
should be suspected especially in middle-aged
women who have typical imaging features, espe-
cially in the absence of a history of pancreatitis.

MCNs are usually (95% of cases) located in the
body or tail of the pancreas (21). The histologic
diagnosis is based on the presence of ovarian
stromata (22). These lesions are extremely rare
in male patients, with approximately 12 cases of
MCNs with ovarian-like stromata reported (10).
It is important to distinguish MCNs from
other lesions, including pseudocysts, as they are
considered to require surgery on the basis of their
malignant potential. Unlike SCAs, MCNs tend
to have fewer (<6) and larger (>2 cm) cysts (Fig

4), and they lack external lobulations. These neo-
plasms may have a “cysts-within-a-cyst” appear-
ance. Like IPMNs, MCNs are found to contain
mucin at aspiration, but unlike IPMNs, they do
not communicate with the MPD. They often have
septa or nodules on imaging studies and may
have capsular calcifications that are best appreci-
ated at CT.

Solid Pseudopapillary Neoplasm
SPN, also referred to as solid pseudopapillary
epithelial neoplasm, is a rare pancreatic tumor
that occurs predominantly in young women and
accounts for 1%–2% of all pancreatic exocrine
tumors. They are considered low-grade malignant
neoplasms and are removed surgically because of
the risk of malignancy. SPNs are associated with
a good prognosis, with a 5-year survival rate of
71.1%–97.0% (27).

The complex cystic components of SPNs
comprise hemorrhagic and necrotic material,
and their imaging features vary on the basis of
these components (Fig 7). SPNs tend to be large
(2.5–17.0 cm; mean size, 9 cm) at presentation

cent structures, nodal and distant metastases,
larger lesion size (cyst ≥4 cm), thick irregular
walls, enhancing soft-tissue components or mural
nodules, peripheral calcifications, and pancreatic
duct dilatation (Fig 5) (24–26). An MCN in a
male should be more concerning for malignancy
(17). Metastases, when present, occur mainly
in the liver and are typically cystic (Fig 6) (24).
Among MCNs smaller than 4 cm and without
worrisome features, malignant transformation has
occurred in 0.03% of cases (24).

that typically occur sporadically but also can
be associated with multiple endocrine neo-

pel–Lindau syndrome. PNETs are divided into
two groups: (a) syndromic or hyperfunctioning/
functional; and (b) nonsyndromic or nonfunc-
tional (31). Functional PNETs are usually
subcentimeter lesions and are brought to clinical
attention by symptoms caused by the hormones
produced by these tumors. On the other hand,

With MRI, the internal cystic component

demonstrates low signal intensity on T1-weighted
images and high signal intensity on T2-weighted
images. Somatostatin-receptor PET/CT has
been shown to be an excellent imaging modality
to locate the tumor, assess for metastases, and
affect management by identifying additional sites
of disease when surgery is contemplated (35).
Gallium 68–tetraazacyclododecane tetraacetic
acid-octreotate PET/CT can help detect PNET
(with 93% sensitivity and 91% specificity), stage
PNET, detect recurrences, and assess tumor het-
erogeneity, especially that of grade 2 and grade 3
tumors (36). Cystic PNETs have been reported
to demonstrate less aggressive behavior compared
with solid PNETs. They have also been shown

to be less likely to involve regional lymph node
metastases, vascular invasion (34,37), perineu-

Intraductal Papillary Mucinous Neoplasm
IPMNs are characterized by a dilated MPD
and/or dilated branch ducts with papillary projec-
tions composed of dysplastic mucinous epithelium
(39). Many cases of IPMN are detected inciden-
tally and are asymptomatic, with prevalences rang-
ing from 0.0008% to 10.0% in patients older than
70 years (40). According to the latest World Health
Organization recommendations, dysplastic grades
of IPMNs should be identified as low-, intermedi-
ate-, or high-grade dysplasia (replacing the term
carcinoma in situ) (41), and invasive carcinoma
(42). Histologically, IPMNs can be further clas-
sified as intestinal, pancreatobiliary, gastric, and
oncocytic subtypes (43). On the basis of the type
of ductal involvement, IPMNs can be categorized
as branch-duct, main-duct, or mixed-type IPMNs.

Branch-Duct IPMN.—Branch-duct IPMNs ac-
count for 41%–64% of IPMNs (44) and appear
as a cluster of small cysts with lobulated margins
and septa (grapelike lobulated appearance), or
unilocular cysts on imaging studies. On the basis
of its frequent location in the uncinate process,
IPMN should be suspected when a cystic lesion
in the uncinate process is encountered. Unlike
SCA, branch-duct IPMN communicates with the
pancreatic duct (Fig 10). This communication

lower, as the majority of small incidental branch-
duct IPMNs are not resected.

Main-Duct and Mixed-Type IPMNs.—Main-duct
IPMNs constitute up to 15%–21% of IPMNs
(44) and are characterized by segmental or dif-
fuse dilatation of the MPD caused by the tumor’s
mucin production. Main-duct IPMNs may ap-
pear to be cystic if only a short segment of the
MPD is involved. A soft-tissue nodule represent-
ing the mucin-producing tumor may be difficult
to identify because of its small size. Guidelines
differ regarding the size of the MPD that suggests
main-duct IPMN when other causes of dilata-

MPD is associated with an increased risk of

“Worrisome” Features and High-Risk
Stigmata

The imaging features of IPMNs that were
reported as “worrisome” for malignancy in the
2017 international consensus Fukuoka guide-
lines and suggest the need for further investiga-
tion with endoscopic US include a cyst diameter
greater than 3 cm, an enhancing mural nodule
smaller than 5 mm, a thickened enhancing cyst
wall, MPD dilatation of 5–9 mm, an abrupt
change in MPD diameter with upstream pan-
creatic atrophy, lymphadenopathy, and a cyst
growth rate of greater than or equal to 5 mm per
2 years (42). The American College of Radiol-
ogy guidelines suggest using an MPD diameter
threshold of 7 mm (23).

Clinically, an elevated carbohydrate anti-
gen 19-9 level also suggests malignancy (42).
The presence of a solid component or a mural
nodule in a cystic lesion is highly associated with
malignancy in IPMNs and MCNs, irrespective
of the subtype or cyst size (8). It is critical to
have high suspicion for malignancy in cases of
MCNs or IPMNs with a solid component, even
in the setting of pancreatitis, in which a fluid
collection might be suspected to be a pseudo-
cyst or walled-off necrosis (Fig 13). High-risk
stigmata considered by the International As-
sociation of Pancreatology to be indications for
resection include obstructive jaundice, a 5-mm
or larger enhancing mural nodule, and MPD
dilatation of 1 cm or greater without another
cause of obstruction (Fig 14) (42). In addition,
recent studies have shown that dilatation of the
dominant uncinate duct may be an independent
risk factor for high-grade dysplasia or invasive
carcinoma in patients with IPMN (51,52).
AlMasri et al (52) showed that the risk of high-
grade dysplasia or invasive carcinoma associated
with dilatation of the uncinate duct persisted
even with distant IPMNs in the dorsal pancreas.
As noted earlier, the size of cystic lesions is
one factor that has been used as a discriminatory
feature, with larger lesions (typically >3 cm) con-
sidered to be more worrisome, having greater ma-
lignant potential and requiring more aggressive
management according to the guidelines. This
was confirmed in a subsequent meta-analysis of

Field Defect

Assessing the risk of a single pancreatic cyst
developing into invasive cancer can be challeng-
ing, and the presence of an IPMN may portend
an increased risk of invasive pancreatic cancer at
disparate sites within the pancreas (Fig 15). This

field defect suggests that an IPMN may be an
indicator of background parenchymal abnormal-
ity, akin to cirrhosis leading to the development
of hepatocellular carcinoma, esophageal dyspla-
sia or Barrett esophagus leading to esophageal
carcinoma, or in the breast, lobular carcinoma in
situ being a marker of future increased risk for
cancer. Multiple studies (57–60) have shown the
increased prevalence of synchronous and meta-
chronous invasive adenocarcinoma during the
follow-up of IPMNs (Figs 16–18). In the Ing-
kakul et al (58) study, synchronous or metachro-
nous concomitant invasive pancreatic carcinoma
was detected in 9.3% of patients. Consequently,
in addition to closely assessing the known pan-
creatic cysts, it is important to fully examine the
remaining pancreatic tissue.

Study investigators have also looked at risk
factors associated with IPMN concomitant with
invasive carcinoma. Age older than 70 years
(57), new-onset or worsening diabetes mellitus,
and abnormal serum carbohydrate antigen 19–9
levels (58) have been shown to be associated with
invasive carcinoma. Ikegawa et al (61) showed

Follow-up Guidelines
Given the high prevalence of pancreatic cystic
lesions, which are increasingly being detected
with high–spatial-resolution cross-sectional
imaging, appropriate and systematic follow-
up guidelines are necessary. Multiple societies
have created guidelines for the management of
pancreatic cysts. Five major guidelines are the
international consensus Fukuoka 2017, Ameri-
can College of Gastroenterology 2018, Ameri-
can College of Radiology 2017, European Study
Group 2018, and American Gastroenterology
Association 2015 guidelines. However, as shown
in Table E1 (13,23,42,65,66), there is variability
in the guidelines among the different societies.
From a clinical perspective, the guidelines
vary with respect to the target patient population,
incorporation of clinical symptoms, and timing
and duration of the initial and long-term imaging

surveillance (45,67). Also, from an imaging per-
spective, these guidelines highlight slightly differ-
ent features associated with malignancy (45,67).
The differing approach to long-term surveillance
is especially notable. In both the 2015 American
Gastroenterology Association guidelines and
2017 American College of Radiology guidelines,
there is a set end point for surveillance for cases
of cyst stability, at 5 and 10 years, respectively.
The other guidelines do not recommend a set
end point for surveillance.

However, multiple studies (46,68–70) have
shown that patients with IPMNs have a long-
term risk of developing invasive cancer, extend-
ing beyond the 5-year timeline suggested by the
American Gastroenterology Association. Consid-
ering this long-term risk, the optimal duration of
follow-up is unclear. Multiple authors, including
Oyama et al (46), who suggest that these patients
should be followed for 20 years or longer, now
suggest that follow-up should exceed 5 years. Be-
cause of the large number of people with inciden-
tal pancreatic cysts, many of which are indolent,
the associated potential costs of surveillance will
be considerable.

stakeholders (eg, radiologists, gastroenterolo-
gists, and surgeons) must review and come to a
consensus regarding which guideline (or combi-
nation of guidelines) to follow at their individual
institutions. We developed our institutional pan-
creatic cyst guidelines after reviewing the avail-
able medical literature by using modified Delphi
methodology and reaching a consensus on the
proposed recommendations. The guidelines of
each society were provided in a table based on
cyst size, and our panel members were asked to
“agree” or “disagree” with each statement and
provide comments. This method was used until
an agreement was reached, with the final step
consisting of a face-to-face meeting with the
main contributors.

Subsequently, the proposed guidelines were

sent to all gastroenterology, surgery, and radi-
ology clinicians with an interest in pancreatic
imaging for comments, with subsequent revi-
sions performed until agreement was reached.
Given the differences in the guidelines reported
in the literature, our final institutional guidelines
represent a synthesis of the available literature
and guidelines, with recommended follow-up
intervals and durations. On the basis of these

institutional consensus guidelines, we created a
“macro” to standardize our follow-up recom-
mendations according to cyst size, similar to the
approach used to develop the societal guidelines.
We used this macro standardization to minimize
reporting inconsistencies among radiologists,
which are an issue that has been reported to be
problematic (71,72).

Abbreviated MRI Protocols

With increasing detection of small pancreatic
cystic lesions and potential lifelong follow-up for
patients with IPMNs, a short limited protocol and
noncontrast MRI are enticing. Studies have shown
no benefit to performing cyst assessment with the
addition of gadolinium-based contrast material
during surveillance imaging (73–75), and noncon-
trast MRI may be preferable to patients, consider-
ing increasing concerns about long-term gado-
linium retention. Pozzi-Mucelli et al (75) found
that short-protocol MRI with use of only axial and
coronal T2-weighted half-Fourier single-shot turbo
spin-echo sequences, as well as an axial noncon-
trast T1-weighted three-dimensional gradient-echo
fat-suppressed sequence, demonstrated perfor-
mances equivalent to that of contrast-enhanced
MRI in cyst follow-up.

The decreased cost and time achieved with

such a short-protocol MRI examination, as
compared with standard-protocol MRI, is also
advantageous owing to the increased detection
and reporting of pancreatic cysts and the sub-
stantial number of follow-up MRI examinations
required. However, while gadolinium-enhanced
MRI examinations, as compared with short-pro-
tocol MRI studies, have been evaluated for the
follow-up of known pancreatic cystic lesions, it
remains unclear whether these short noncontrast
protocols affect the identification of synchronous
or metachronous invasive carcinoma, which may
be subtle without gadolinium-based contrast me-
dium administration and is of concern given the
potential for field defect.

The limitations of using a short MRI protocol

include decreased sensitivity in the detection of
solid lesions in patients whose MR images show
a low–T1-signal-intensity pancreas associated
with atrophy and chronic pancreatitis. Given
the increasing understanding of the field defect
and the risk of carcinoma in the background
pancreas, further studies are needed. Until there
is more evidence or guidance, our institution
continues to perform contrast-enhanced MRI
examinations.

Conclusion

Incidental pancreatic cystic lesions are being
detected more frequently on imaging studies. An
organized approach to interpreting imaging stud-
ies to determine the diagnosis is critical. Those
cysts that cannot be diagnosed may require
follow-up imaging or aspiration. Expert consen-
sus guidelines from different societies have been
developed to promote standardized management
and follow-up. However, these guidelines are
inconsistent and evolving according to addition-
ally published scientific literature. Institutional
standardized reporting and macros are helpful for
ensuring the uniformity of interpretations.

The identification of worrisome features
and/or high-risk stigmata is important, as these
findings may convey the need for aspiration or
surgery. Newer concepts such as field defect also
are important considerations that may affect
management. Radiologists play a critical role in
the detection and characterization of pancreatic
cystic lesions, in the follow-up recommendations
for these lesions, and in the detection of cancer.
