Eosinophilic gastrointestinal disorders (EGIDs) are inflammatory
conditions of the gastrointestinal tract that are characterized by tis-
sue eosinophilia and end-organ dysfunction or damage. Primary
EGIDs are associated with atopy and other allergic conditions,
whereas secondary EGIDs are associated with underlying systemic
diseases or hypereosinophilic syndrome. Within the spectrum of
EGIDs, eosinophilic esophagitis is the most prevalent. Eosinophilic
gastroenteritis and eosinophilic colitis are relatively uncommon.
Eosinophilic infiltration of the liver, biliary tree, and/or pancreas
also can occur and mimic other inflammatory and malignant con-
ditions. Although endoscopic evaluation is the method of choice
for eosinophilic esophagitis, radiologic evaluation of the esophagus
plays an important role in the assessment of disease severity. CT
and MR enterography are the modalities of choice for demonstrat-
ing specific forms of eosinophilic gastroenteritis. CT and MRI are
important in the detection of abdominal visceral involvement in
EGIDs. Diagnosis is often challenging and relies on symptoms,
imaging findings, histologic confirmation of tissue eosinophilia,
and correlation with peripheral eosinophilia. Imaging is crucial for
identifying characteristic organ-specific findings, although imaging
findings are not specific. When promptly treated, EGIDs usually
have a benign clinical course. However, a delayed diagnosis and
associated surgical interventions have been associated with morbid-
ity. Therefore, a radiologist’s knowledge of the imaging findings of
EGIDs in the appropriate clinical settings may aid in early diag-
nosis and thereby improve patient care. An overview of the clinical
features and imaging findings of EGIDs and the eosinophilic disor-
ders of associated abdominal viscera is provided.

Introduction

Eosinophils are white blood cells of granulocyte lineage that are
produced in the bone marrow. They mature over a few days and
then get released into the bloodstream, where they stay in circula-
tion for about 8–12 hours before localizing in specific areas and
organs such as the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, thymus, uterus, and
lymph nodes. Eosinophils normally reside in the GI tract for up to 1
week (1). Because of the tissue distribution of eosinophils and their
ability to mount a significant immune response, the role of these
cells in humans includes protection against parasitic infections. The
cytoplasm of eosinophils contains eosin-staining granules that have
a high concentration of hydrolases and cationic and basic proteins.

Eosinophilic disorders include a spectrum of
pathologic conditions characterized by various
degrees of persistent blood and/or tissue hyper-
eosinophilia, with the potential for end-organ
dysfunction (5). Peripheral blood eosinophilia is
defined as an absolute eosinophil count higher
than 300–500 cells/mm3, which is the upper
limit of the normal range (1). Eosinophilic
disorders can involve single-organ systems or
multiple organs and be associated with hype-
reosinophilic syndrome (HES) (6). Common
organs and organ systems that are affected by
eosinophilia and dysfunction are the broncho-
pulmonary system, skin, and GI tract. While
eosinophils are normally present in various parts
of the GI tract, they are characteristically absent
or rare in the esophagus. The definitive number
of eosinophils required for a diagnosis of eosino-
philic disorders in different parts of the GI tract
is not universally accepted.

Eosinophilic GI disorders (EGIDs) are in-
creasingly being recognized owing to improved
awareness, increases in available treatment op-
tions, and the probable increasing prevalence of
these disorders (7). The manifestations of EGIDs
are protean, and radiologic evaluation plays a key
role in establishing the diagnosis and ruling out
other possible GI disorders that may cause simi-
lar clinical presentations. Clinical suspicion often
leads to a series of examinations in predisposed

individuals, including radiologic evaluation.
When diagnosed early, EGIDs tend to follow a
benign course with few complications. Therefore,
radiologists need to be aware of the imaging
appearances of EGIDs for early recognition that
can aid in the management of these disorders. In
this review, we describe and illustrate the clini-

Eosinophilic GI Disorders

EGIDs are diseases that are characterized by
symptoms related to GI dysfunction and eosin-
ophil-rich inflammation of the gut wall (7,8).
Eosinophils are normally present in the lamina
propria of the mucosa throughout the GI tract.
Although the upper limit of normal for the num-
ber of eosinophils in GI tissues is not well estab-
lished, the highest density of eosinophils is found
at or around the terminal ileum and cecum, with
decreasing numbers of eosinophils in the gut wall
along both directions of the GI tract (9).

EGIDs can be broadly classified as primary

or secondary EGIDs (Tables 1, 2) (7,8). Pri-
mary EGIDs primarily affect the GI tract, with
eosinophil-rich inflammation in the absence of
known causes of eosinophilia such as parasitic
infections, malignancy, and drug reactions. These
EGIDs are associated with atopy and allergies
and can be localized to one or more organs in the
GI tract (7). Primary EGIDs include eosinophilic
esophagitis (EoE), eosinophilic gastritis (EoG),
eosinophilic duodenitis, eosinophilic enteritis,
eosinophilic colitis (EoC), and eosinophilic gas-
troenteritis (EGE). EGE refers to disorders of the
stomach and/or small intestine that are character-
ized by pathologic eosinophilic infiltration (10).
EoE is the most common type of EGID, account-
ing for greater than 90%–95% of these disorders
(11,12). With secondary EGIDs, eosinophilic
infiltration of the GI tract occurs in association
with a separate known cause or identifiable sys-
temic disease.

Peripheral eosinophilia is absent in up to 25%

of patients with EGIDs and therefore is not a
prerequisite for diagnosis (13). The diagnosis of
EGID is based on clinical suspicion, supportive
imaging findings, and histologic confirmation
of eosinophil-rich inflammation in the tissue or
organ. Because the clinical symptoms of EGIDs
are variable, a diagnostic evaluation for EGID
is recommended for patients with refractory
GI symptoms, especially those with a history
of allergy-related diseases or a family history of
EGIDs (8). EGIDs tend to be chronic and re-
current, but they usually follow a benign course
when they are promptly identified and treated.

Eosinophilic Esophagitis
EoE is a chronic immune system–related or
antigen-mediated disorder of the esophagus that
is characterized clinically by symptoms related
to esophageal dysfunction and characterized
histologically by eosinophil-predominant inflam-
mation involving 15 or more eosinophils per
high-power field (15). EoE is the most common
EGID, and the incidence of this disorder has in-
creased in the past few decades, as evidenced by
a prevalence of 34 of 100 000 children and 42 of
100 000 adults (16–18). EoE has a bimodal age
distribution, most commonly affecting children
and young adults in their 3rd–4th decade of life,
and is more common in males (male-to-female
ratio, 3:1) (19).

Pathologic Features of EoE.—EoE is a result of a
complex interaction between immunogenetic fac-
tors and environmental exposure in predisposed
individuals with atopy (Fig 2). The environmen-
tal factors that are hypothesized to contribute to
the rising incidence of EoE are consumption of
immunogenic foods, changes in airborne pollen
concentration, decreased frequency of Helicobacter

Immunologic factors evolve around the in-
creased permeability of the esophageal mucosal
barrier against environmental antigens (Fig 2)
(7). The associated chronic eosinophilic inflam-
mation leads to fibrosis. Tissue fibrous remodel-
ing, which typically occurs at the mucosal and
submucosal levels, becomes evident at imaging
and endoscopy as rings, segmental narrowing,
and strictures.

The natural course of untreated EoE is chronic

and progressive, with recurrent episodes of food
obstruction. The clinical presentations of esopha-
geal dysfunction vary according to patient age. In
infants and older children, common symptoms
are vomiting, food aversion, and rarely, failure to
thrive. In young adults, common symptoms are
dysphagia, especially with solid foods, and food
impaction. EoE is a leading cause of emergency
department visits for esophageal food impaction
among young adults (23). Esophageal perfora-
tion is a rare but serious complication of EoE
that occurs in approximately 2% of cases; most
commonly, it is due to a food bolus impaction or
occurs after mechanical dilation of esophageal
strictures (24). Inflamed and fragile mucosal and
fibrous remodeling of the esophagus from EoE is
believed to be a predisposing factor (24).

At endoscopy, the esophageal disease tends

to be patchy, necessitating multiple biopsies.

sites, with at least two specimens taken from the

EoE is a chronic disease that often requires in-
definite long-term treatment. Practice parameter
guidelines of the American Gastroenterological
Institute and the Joint Task Force on Allergy-
Immunology (AGA/JTF) recommend the use
of topical glucocorticosteroids in patients with
symptomatic EoE, with continuation of this ther-
apy in remission, and endoscopic dilation in adult
patients with dysphagia from EoE stricture (38).
The AGA/JTF guidelines also suggest the use of
proton pump inhibitors, an elemental diet, an
empiric six-food elimination diet, and an allergy
testing–based diet, rather than no treatment.

Eosinophilic Colitis
Primary EoC has a bimodal age distribution, af-
fecting infants more frequently than adolescents
and adults (7). Symptoms are nonspecific and
vary according to the colonic segment involved. In
infants, EoC is also known as allergic proctocolitis

of infancy or milk-protein proctocolitis. Gradual
onset of rectal bleeding and diarrhea in a well-ap-
pearing infant is the characteristic presentation for
EoC, and the diagnosis is clinically based on the
resolution of the disorder with initiation of protein
hydrolysate formula (22). In adults, the most com-
mon presenting symptom is diarrhea, which can
be bloody occasionally. Symptoms of large-bowel
obstruction also are more common in this age
group. The cause of EoC is not completely un-
derstood but is believed to be an allergic reaction.
The diagnosis relies on GI symptoms, endoscopic
biopsy results, and exclusion of secondary causes.
In up to 70% of cases, no mucosal abnormal-

ity is observed at endoscopy (54). The imag-
ing appearance of EoC is that of inflammatory
change (Fig 13). Primary and secondary involve-
ment cannot be reliably distinguished and may
mimic infectious colitis and inflammatory bowel
disease (Fig 14). With the mucosal form of EoC,
which is the type most frequently observed, the
appearance may be that of the araneid limblike
sign or the more familiar “accordion” sign seen
with pseudomembranous colitis (55). Histologic
analysis reveals increased eosinophils in the co-
lonic mucosa, and the architecture of the mucosa
is generally preserved, helping to distinguish EoC
from other colitides (7).

Eosinophilic Liver Disease
The liver is an uncommon site for primary eo-
sinophilic infiltration, and up to 30% of primary
EGIDs can occur in the liver (56). However, sec-
ondary hepatic involvement is very well recognized
and is associated with parasitic infections, HES,
drug reactions, malignancies, and autoimmune
conditions (57,58). Eosinophilic hepatic involve-
ment can range from simple eosinophilic infiltra-
tion to eosinophilic granuloma and eosinophilic ab-
scesses (Fig 15), with predominantly eosinophilic
inflammation seen at histopathologic analysis.

Although eosinophilic infiltration of the liver is
commonly accompanied by symptoms, it can be
incidentally detected at imaging (59). With con-
trast-enhanced CT, eosinophilic lesions of the liver
are best visualized during the portal venous phase

as multiple hypoattenuating nodular or rounded
lesions (60). Most of the ill-defined eosinophilic
infiltrations show gradual enhancement, with por-
tal vein branches traversing the lesions (56). Gran-
ulomas and abscesses have a nodular appearance
with or without rim enhancement. When detected
incidentally, these lesions should be differentiated
from malignant lesions in the liver. The features
that are the most helpful in distinguishing eosino-
philic lesions from malignancy are lack of displace-
ment of vessels and ducts, ill-defined margins, and
gradual enhancement; these findings generally are
better demonstrated with use of MRI (56).

for peripheral eosinophilia may be valuable in
determining the diagnosis. However, peripheral
eosinophilia is absent in more than 50% of pa-

Eosinophilic Cholangiopathy
Eosinophilic cholangiopathy is a rare condition
that is characterized by eosinophilic infiltration of
the biliary tract (63). The term eosinophilic chol-
angiopathy is used to describe both eosinophilic
cholangitis and eosinophilic cholecystitis when
the eosinophilic infiltration is focused in the bile
duct and gallbladder, respectively. It is an exceed-
ingly rare cause of recurrent benign cholangitis
and biliary stricturing (64). The exact cause is
not known, but eosinophilic cholangiopathy has
been associated with cholelithiasis, HES, allergy,
and parasitic infections.

3 109/L in peripheral blood, demonstrated on
two occasions at least 1 month apart. End-organ
dysfunction is defined by fibrosis, thrombosis, and/
or organ-specific manifestations (6). The organ
systems commonly involved are the skin, lungs,
heart, GI tract, and nervous system (Figs 19,
20). HES is further classified as idiopathic HES
when the underlying cause is not identified and as
secondary HES when the underlying cause of the
polyclonal hypereosinophilia is known. Common
causes of secondary HES are reactive diseases
(drug hypersensitivity, infections, and allergic dis-
ease), systemic autoimmune disorders, vasculitides
(inflammatory bowel disease, eosinophilic granulo-
matosis with polyangiitis, Wegener granulomatosis,
polyarteritis nodosa), and malignancies. Eosino-
philic infiltration of the esophagus, stomach, and
bowel can occur with HES and can frequently be
mistaken for primary EGIDs. However, EGIDs
are allergy related, and rarely patients have an
overlap between HES and primary EGID (10).

Eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis

can manifest with dysphagia, abdominal pain,
vomiting, and bloody diarrhea. However, patients
with this disease tend to have asthma, pulmonary
symptoms or nasal polyps, and migratory infil-
trates at chest radiography. Biopsy of the lung
lesions, which reveals eosinophil-rich necrotizing
granulomatous vasculitis, is helpful for determin-
ing the diagnosis. HES initially appearing as an
EGID has been reported and can be indistin-
guishable from HES but needs to be confirmed in
larger prospective studies (71). Systemic steroids
are the initial therapy for symptomatic patients
with HES.

Conclusion

The reference standard finding of EGID is
hypereosinophilia in the tissues and eosinophil-
mediated tissue damage. After the exclusion of
secondary causes of eosinophilia, the diagnosis of
primary EGID is based on GI symptoms, imaging
findings of GI involvement, peripheral eosino-
philia, and biopsy-proven eosinophilic infiltrates.
EGIDs are characteristically recurrent, with vari-
able periods of remission. When promptly treated,
EGID usually follows a benign course. However,
morbidity is related to delayed diagnosis and surgi-
cal interventions. Hepatic, biliary, and pancreatic
involvement is uncommon, and a high degree of
clinical suspicion is required to make the diagno-
sis. Multisystem manifestations occur in associa-
tion with hypereosinophilia syndrome. Knowledge
of the radiologic features of EGIDs will assist in
diagnosing these disorders and aid in early initia-
tion of proper patient care.
