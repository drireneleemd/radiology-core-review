Pancreatic surgery is considered one of the most technically challenging surgical
procedures, despite the evolution of modern techniques. Neoplasms remain the
most common indication for pancreatic surgery, although inflammatory conditions
may also prompt surgical evaluation. The choice of surgical procedure depends on
the type and location of the pathologic finding because different parts of the pancre-
as have separate vascular supplies that may be shared by adjacent organs. The surgi-
cal approach could be conventional or minimally invasive (laparoscopic, endoscopic,
or robotic assisted). Because of the anatomic complexity of the pancreatic bed,
perioperative complications may be frequently encountered and commonly involve
the pancreatic-biliary, vascular, lymphatic, or bowel systems, irrespective of the sur-
gical technique used. Imaging plays an important role in the assessment of suspect-
ed postoperative complications, with CT considered the primary imaging modality,
while MRI, digital subtraction angiography, and molecular imaging are considered
ancillary diagnostic tools. Accurate diagnosis of postoperative complications re-
quires a solid understanding of pancreatic anatomy, surgical indications, normal
postoperative appearance, and expected postsurgical changes. The practicing radiol-
ogist should be familiar with the most common perioperative complications, such
as anastomotic leak, abscess, and hemorrhage, and be able to differentiate these
entities from normal anticipated postoperative changes such as seroma, edema and
fat stranding at the surgical site, and perivascular soft-tissue thickening. In addition
to evaluation of the primary operative fossa, imaging plays a fundamental role in
assessment of the adjacent organ systems secondarily affected after pancreatic sur-
gery, such as vascular, biliary, and enteric complications.

Introduction
Pancreatic surgical procedures are performed for a wide range of indications. The most
common  indication  is  malignancy,  primarily  pancreatic  ductal  adenocarcinoma  (1).
Other  indications  include  pancreatic  neuroendocrine  tumors,  cystic  pancreatic  neo-
plasms,  pancreatic  metastases,  chronic  pancreatitis  not  responding  to  medical  man-
agement, and complications of acute pancreatitis (2–4). The technique for pancreatic
resection varies depending on the location of the pancreatic pathologic entity, as differ-
ent parts of the pancreas have separate vascular supply, which is also shared by adjacent
structures. Pancreatic surgeries can be performed with an open approach or minimally
invasive  (laparoscopic  or  robotic-assisted)  techniques  (5).  Endoscopic  surgical  proce-
dures may be used to manage complications associated with acute pancreatitis (4,6).

Understanding the anatomy of the pancreas, recognizing indications for pancreatic
surgery, being familiar with postoperative changes in anatomy, and identifying expected
postsurgical changes are crucial for detecting postsurgical complications. Despite vari-
ations in pancreatic surgical techniques, these procedures commonly manifest similar
complications  such  as  pancreaticobiliary,  bowel,  and  vascular  complications,  among
others. Proper utilization of suitable imaging modalities is vital in the diagnosis of post-
operative complications and in directing their treatment.

TEACHING POINTS
	 Complications  often  occur  at  the  sites  of  surgical  anastomoses,  and
knowledge of the surgical approach is critical for identifying the loca-
tion and type of complication with imaging.

	 Leakage  of  pancreatic  juice  can  trigger  the  autodigestion  of  nearby
structures, resulting in complications such as vascular erosion, hemor-
rhage, pseudoaneurysms, and fistulous communications with adjacent
structures. Additionally, it is known to be associated with delayed gas-
tric emptying, ileus, and infections, including abdominal abscesses and
wound infections.

	 Unexplained  abdominal  pain,  features  of  pancreatic  exocrine  insuffi-
ciency, and attacks of acute pancreatitis in the late postoperative pe-
riod associated with imaging findings of progressive pancreatic ductal
dilatation  and  abrupt  narrowing  at  the  anastomotic  site  should  raise
concern for stricture formation.

	 Risk factors for anastomotic leak include preoperative radiation thera-
py, small size of the bile duct, and anastomosis of the CBD rather than
the common hepatic duct to the jejunal loop. Pancreatic leak can also
lead to bile leaks.

	 Early-onset hemorrhage is due to surgical factors including inadequate
hemostasis, slipped ligature, or fresh bleeding at a surgical site. It can oc-
cur due to arterial or venous injury or bleeding along the anastomotic site
or resected parenchymal surface. Late-onset hemorrhage can occur up to
several days or weeks after surgery and generally has a pathologic cause.

This article reviews the normal anatomy and embryology
of the pancreas, indications for pancreatic surgeries, various
types of pancreatic surgeries and their normal postoperative
appearances,  expected  postsurgical  findings,  complications
of pancreatic surgical procedures, and the role of imaging in
their assessment.

Pancreatic Anatomy and Embryology
The  pancreas  is  a  foregut-derived  retroperitoneal  structure
situated in the anterior pararenal space. It develops in the 4th
week of embryogenesis from separate dorsal and ventral pan-
creatic buds (7,8) (Fig S1). Anatomically, the pancreas is divided
into the head, neck, body, and tail. The C-loop of the duodenum
surrounds the head of the pancreas. The neck lies anterior to
the portal venous confluence and the proximal superior mes-
enteric  vein  (SMV).  The  splenic  vein  courses  posterior  to  the
body and tail of the pancreas. Anteriorly, the pancreas is bor-
dered by the stomach and the lesser sac (9).

trunk and in part from the superior mesenteric artery (SMA).

Vascular Reconstruction during Pancreatic Surgical
Procedures
Assessment  of  resectability  is  most  commonly  based  on  the
National  Comprehensive  Cancer  Network  (NCCN)  guide-
lines  (23–25).  Vascular  reconstruction  may  be  performed
for  borderline  resectable  pancreatic  tumors.  The  objective  is
to  achieve  a  microscopically  negative  resection  margin  (R0)
with  no  residual  pancreatic  tumor,  an  important  indepen-
dent factor impacting survival (26). Venous reconstruction of
the involved portions of the portal vein and SMV is commonly
performed (27). The reconstruction options for venous grafts
include autologous veins, falciform ligament, parietal perito-
neum, cryopreserved allografts, or prosthetic grafts (25).

Arterial resections are not routinely performed, as studies
have  shown  that  arterial  reconstructions  during  pancreatic
surgery have significantly higher morbidity and lower short-
term and long-term survival (24,25,27). Resection of the SMA,
common  hepatic  artery,  or  celiac  axis  is  the  main  arterial
resection  performed  in  pancreatic  malignancies  (28).  When

arterial  reconstructions  are  performed,  they  can  involve  a
direct anastomosis, arterial transposition, or arterial bypass
graft (29).

Surgeries for Chronic Pancreatitis Not Responding to
Medical Management
Chronic pancreatitis has various clinical and imaging presen-
tations. It may involve a long segment of the main pancreatic
duct (MPD) with formation of strictures and/or stones. Focal
chronic pancreatitis in the head of the pancreas may result in
an inflammatory mass that can be mistaken for malignancy.
In some cases, there may be isolated involvement of the pan-
creatic body or tail. The surgical approach selected for treat-
ment is dependent on the type of pancreatic involvement and
can be classified as a drainage, resection, or combined proce-
dure (Tables 1, 3; Fig 2) (2,11,30).

Imaging Evaluation of
Postsurgical Complications
Various  imaging  modalities  can  be  used  to  assess  postop-
erative  complications  of  pancreatectomy  and  pancreatic
interventions. CT is the primary imaging modality used for
postoperative pancreatic imaging due to its excellent spatial
resolution, short imaging time, and accurate depiction of air
and calcifications. Additionally, CT angiography aids in de-
tecting vascular complications (31). The CT protocol for post-
operative  surveillance  should  be  multiphase.  It  should  in-
clude contrast-enhanced imaging during the arterial phase
(pancreatic parenchymal phase) at 40–50 seconds and por-
tal venous phase at 65–70 seconds, which is also in line with
the recommended protocol for preoperative evaluation (32).

MRI, on the other hand, has excellent soft-tissue resolu-
tion and helps evaluate the pancreaticobiliary ductal anat-
omy,  tumor  recurrence,  and  indeterminate  hepatic  lesions
seen  at  CT.  MRI  with  hepatobiliary  contrast  material  may
help evaluate suspected biliary leak. MRI is limited by lon-
ger acquisition times, reliance on breath hold, lower spatial
resolution,  and  suboptimal  assessment  of  air  and  calcifi-
cations  (32).  Secretin-enhanced  MR  cholangiopancreatog-
raphy  (MRCP)  can  be  performed  to  evaluate  postoperative
anatomy and possible stenosis of the MPD. It has good spec-
ificity in cases of leak. However, it should not be used to ex-
clude leaks, due to the hazardous effect of the leaking pan-
creatic juice (33).

Digital subtraction angiography is often necessary for eval-
uation  and  management  of  vascular  complications.  Fluoro-
scopic evaluation may also be used to assess bowel motility,
differentiate  ileus  from  bowel  obstruction,  and  evaluate  the
integrity of bowel anastomosis. US is a valuable tool in guid-
ing drainage procedures, while a hepatobiliary iminodiacetic
acid  (HIDA)  scan  can  help  confirm  the  presence  of  biliary
leak. PET/CT may be considered in cases of suspected recur-
rence with negative CT results (11,15,31,32,34).

Expected Findings in the Late Postoperative Period

Pancreatic  Ductal  Dilatation  and  Parenchymal  Atrophy.—
Pancreatic  ductal  dilatation  can  persist  after  surgery.  How-
ever, progressively increasing dilatation should raise concern
for local recurrence. Atrophy of the residual parenchyma af-
ter surgery is not uncommon, which may or may not be asso-
ciated with dilatation of the MPD (15).

Postsurgical  Scarring.—Soft-tissue  thickening  surrounding
the mesenteric vasculature may persist into the late postop-
erative period. It can mimic tumor recurrence, and, therefore,
comparison with prior examinations to assess subtle changes
is prudent (Fig 5) (36). Radiation therapy, including adjuvant
therapy,  can  also  produce  ill-defined  soft-tissue  thickening
and stranding in the postoperative bed that can persist long
after surgery and mimic tumor recurrence.

Prominent  Regional  Lymph  Nodes.—Prominent  regional
lymph nodes can also manifest in the late postoperative pe-
riod (Fig 4). However, an interval increased size of the lymph
nodes  compared  with  prior  imaging  examinations  should
raise suspicion for nodal metastases (15).

Postoperative Complications of Pancreatic
Surgeries
Pancreatic operative procedures are among the most challeng-
ing gastrointestinal surgeries and have a high rate of postoper-
ative complications (37). Often patients have other comorbidi-
ties that directly correlate with an increased risk of developing
complications  (38,39).  There  are  several  parameters  used  to
classify surgical complications, including timing (early vs late)
and severity (major vs minor) or based on the specific struc-
tures  or  organs  affected.  Morbidity  and  mortality  rates  can
vary  significantly  among  institutions,  with  high-volume  cen-
ters generally experiencing lower rates (40,41).

Beugniez et al (42) in a root-cause analysis of 3195 patients
reported a 90-day mortality rate of 4.4% (42). Harnoss et al
(43) in a systematic review stated that the most common com-
plications were delayed gastric emptying, pancreatic fistula,
and postoperative hemorrhage, with a reported incidence of
22.8%, 21.9%, and 5.9%, respectively (43). Postoperative com-
plications are classified according to the affected organs into
pancreaticobiliary, vascular, bowel, and other complications
in  Table  4.  It  is  not  uncommon  to  find  multiple  complica-
tions  in  the  same  patient.  Complications  often  occur  at  the
sites of surgical anastomoses, and knowledge of the surgical
approach  is  critical  for  identifying  the  location  and  type  of
complication with imaging.

Postoperative Collections.—Small-volume transient uncom-

in size at serial imaging (15,34).

Surgical Site Edema and Fat Stranding.—Edema is common
at  the  gastrojejunostomy,  hepaticojejunostomy,  and  pancre-
aticojejunostomy anastomotic sites. Edema at the respective
anastomotic sites may cause dilatation of the pancreatic duct
or  biliary  system.  Surgical  anastomotic  site  edema  and  col-
lapsed or crowded bowel loops can mimic local tumor recur-
rence (11,17,34).

Soft-Tissue  Thickening  Surrounding  the  Vasculature.—Peri-
vascular  cuffing  (Fig  3)  can  manifest  as  thickening  of  soft
tissue  around  the  celiac  trunk  and/or  superior  mesenteric
vasculature and may resemble a mass. A negative surgical re-
section margin makes the possibility of residual tumors less
likely  (15).  Comparison  with  preoperative  imaging  findings
can be useful, as residual disease following surgery is likely to
be contiguous with the site of primary malignancy (11,35,36).

Prominent  Regional  Lymph  Nodes.—Enlarged  lymph  nodes
can manifest in the surgical bed in the postoperative period
and can measure greater than 1 cm in diameter. These lymph
nodes are rarely metastatic. They can be followed up at sub-
sequent imaging to ensure stability or resolution (Fig 4) (31).

ing abdominal abscesses (Fig 8) and wound infections (Fig 9).
In some cases, sepsis and multiorgan failure can also result
from pancreatic leak (39,46,47).

The  primary  reason  for  development  of  the  leak  is  the
damaging effect of pancreatic enzymes on the anastomotic
site  (48).  There  are  several  predisposing  factors  that  in-
crease the risk of pancreatic leak, which can be categorized
into  pancreas-related,  patient-related,  and  procedure-re-
lated  factors.  With  respect  to  pancreas-related  factors,  a
soft texture of the residual parenchyma and high pancreatic
juice output are markers for good pancreatic function. How-

ever,  such  pancreatic  tissue  is  more  difficult  to  suture  and
more  likely  to  leak  compared  with  fibrosed  pancreas.  The
volume of the residual pancreatic parenchyma and the size
of the MPD (diameter <3 mm) are other contributing factors.
Procedure-related factors also play a significant role and in-
clude  type  of  resection,  technique  of  anastomosis,  and  in-
traoperative  blood  loss.  Patient-related  factors  include  ad-
vanced  age,  male  patients,  malnourishment,  jaundice,  and
higher body mass index (39,47).

Pancreatic leak is the most common cause of pathologic
fluid collection after surgery (39). The rate of clinically signif-
icant leak after distal pancreatectomy ranges from 5% to 40%
in various studies. It is generally higher than the incidence
observed after pancreaticoduodenectomy, which is reported
to be between 6% and 14%. However, the leak following dis-
tal pancreatectomy tends to be less severe in terms of clin-
ical impact than those following pancreaticoduodenectomy
(15,47). The diagnosis of leak is essentially based on clinical
and  laboratory  parameters  and  covers  a  spectrum  ranging

detection and diagnosis of dehiscence, and findings include
a  visible  separation  between  the  pancreatic  remnant  and
jejunum at CT, the presence of fluid collection at the suture
line or in a peripancreatic location, and free air at the site of
the anastomosis or within the abdominal cavity (Figs 10, 11)

should remain vigilant in examining the cut margin to rule
out the possibility of dehiscence (Fig 12A) (51).

pancreaticojejunal stent placement as needed (52). In severe
cases, surgical exploration and repair of the dehiscence can
be  performed.  Rarely,  completion  pancreatectomy  may  be
executed when other options are exhausted (53).

Postoperative  Pancreatitis.—Pancreatitis  can  occur  in  the
early or late postoperative period (Fig 14). During the early
postoperative period, it is difficult to differentiate mild acute
pancreatitis  from  postsurgical  changes.  Elevated  amylase
and lipase levels are also unreliable to diagnose acute pan-
creatitis  during  the  early  postoperative  period  (54).  Severe
acute  pancreatitis  will  appear  at  CT  or  MRI  with  extensive
peripancreatic  inflammatory  changes  and  fluid  collections
(Fig 14) (15,31). Postsurgical changes are commonly general-
ized rather than focal, while localized inflammatory changes
around  the  pancreatic  remnant  should  raise  suspicion  for
acute pancreatitis (15). Patients with postoperative pancre-
atitis  can  develop  a  spectrum  of  complications  similar  to
those of primary acute pancreatitis.

Pancreaticojejunostomy  Stricture.—Pancreaticojejunos-
tomy stricture is a long-term surgical complication seen in
up  to  one-third  of  patients.  The  reported  median  time  for
development  of  a  stricture  after  pancreaticoduodenectomy
is  46  months  (55).  Development  of  pancreatic  atrophy  and
pancreatic  ductal  dilatation,  which  occur  commonly  after
pancreatic  surgery,  makes  the  diagnosis  of  a  stricture  dif-
ficult.  Unexplained  abdominal  pain,  features  of  pancreatic
exocrine  insufficiency,  and  attacks  of  acute  pancreatitis  in
the late postoperative period associated with imaging find-
ings of progressive pancreatic ductal dilatation and abrupt
narrowing at the anastomotic site should raise concern for
stricture  formation  (Fig  15)  (31,34,55).  A  stricture  needs  to
be differentiated from tumor recurrence at the anastomotic
site, which can also cause obstructive pancreatic ductal dil-
atation (Fig 15) (31).

Serial  follow-up  cross-sectional  imaging  can  help  in
making the diagnosis. T2- and diffusion-weighted MRI se-
quences  may  help  assess  recurrence  at  the  anastomotic  site,
while MRCP helps in the assessment of ductal dilatation (Fig
15).  Endoscopic  retrograde  cholangiopancreatography  (ERCP)
can help in diagnosis and management (Fig 15). Most cases are
managed by balloon dilatation and stent placement in the pan-
creatic duct (Fig 15) (15).

Bile Leak.—Biliary fistula or bile leak can occur as a complica-
tion of pancreatic surgeries involving the head region and can
be caused by anastomotic leakage or iatrogenic biliary injury,
with  a  reported  incidence  of  8%  after  pancreaticoduodenec-

serum bilirubin, from the 3rd postoperative day (57). There is

increased risk for postoperative morbidity with extended hos-
pitalization,  biliary  peritonitis,  abscess  formation,  and  other
infections in these patients. Risk factors for anastomotic leak
include  preoperative  radiation  therapy,  small  size  of  the  bile
duct, and anastomosis of the CBD rather than the common he-
patic duct to the jejunal loop (36,56). Pancreatic leak can also
lead to bile leaks (56). The diagnosis may be suspected clini-
cally by raised bilirubin levels in the drain fluid.

Gastrojejunostomy Stricture and Marginal Ulcer.—Gastroje-
junostomy  stricture  and  marginal  ulcer  are  similar  to  those
complications  that  occur  after  other  gastrojejunal  surgical
anastomoses. Gastrojejunostomy site narrowing in the early
postoperative  period  may  be  related  to  surgical  site  edema.
Stricture  formation  in  the  late  postoperative  period  may  be
related to ischemia or scarring. Symptoms include postpran-
dial abdominal pain, bloating, and vomiting. Fluoroscopy is
helpful for confirming the diagnosis of stricture (69,70). Mar-
ginal ulcers are formed in the jejunum at or just distal to the
anastomotic site due to exposure to acidic gastric contents. Al-
though the diagnosis is made at endoscopy, CT performed for
abdominal pain may demonstrate edema and fat stranding at
the gastrojejunostomy site or complications like perforation
or bleeding (71). In rare cases, a secondary gastric adenocarci-
noma may develop at the gastrojejunal anastomosis (72).

Afferent Limb Syndrome.—The afferent limb is the section of
the small bowel located before the gastrojejunal anastomosis
where biliopancreatic secretions are drained. When this loop
becomes obstructed, it leads to a condition known as afferent
limb syndrome (Fig 21), a type of closed-loop obstruction usu-
ally requiring surgery to prevent further complications. The
causes of afferent limb syndrome may include tumor recur-
rence, postoperative edema, adhesions, and stricture forma-
tion (34). CT images can show dilated small bowel proximal to
the gastrojejunostomy site, while the bowel loops distal to the
anastomotic site will be collapsed (Fig 21).

Other Complications

Abscess.—Intra-abdominal collections that develop after pan-
creatic  surgery  can  get  infected  and  form  abscesses.  These
can  occur  in  and/or  away  from  the  operative  bed  (Fig  8).

venous occlusion. In addition to the direct sign of vascular oc-
clusion, other possible findings include bowel wall thickening,
hemorrhage as evidenced by hyperattenuating bowel wall on
unenhanced  CT  images,  nonenhancement  of  the  bowel  wall,
pneumatosis intestinalis, and portal venous gas (67). Ischemic
gastropathy is a complication of an Appleby procedure and can
manifest with refractory gastric ulcers, which may be compli-
cated  by  bleeding  or  perforation  (17).  Beger  and  Frey  proce-
dures involve resection of the pancreatic head and may lead to
ischemia of the CBD or duodenum owing to the possible dis-
ruption of their blood supply (11).

Bowel Complications
Postpancreatic  surgery  bowel  complications  include  delayed
gastric emptying, gastrojejunostomy leak or stricture, ischemia
or perforation (Figs 20, S6), ileus, bowel adhesions or obstruc-
tion (Fig S7), and marginal ulcers. Some of these bowel compli-
cations are unique to pancreatic surgical procedures involving
creation  of  gastrojejunostomy,  such  as  pancreaticoduodenec-
tomy and total pancreatectomy.

A  low-attenuation  collection  with  rim  enhancement  is  the
most  common  appearance  at  cross-sectional  imaging.  Liver
abscesses  tend  to  rapidly  increase  in  size  and  can  display  a
double  target  sign,  seen  in  about  30%  of  cases  (15,31).  How-
ever, the CT signs may not be obvious during the early stage
of infection. Clinical and laboratory parameters can suggest
the diagnosis of an abscess. Analysis of the aspirate confirms
the diagnosis of an abscess. Abscess formation can also lead
to complications at anastomotic sites, including vascular ero-
sions and hemorrhage.

Tumor  Recurrence.—Tumor  recurrence  can  manifest  locally
at  the  surgical  site  or  as  metastatic  disease,  with  the  latter
more  common  than  the  former  (34).  Local  recurrence  can
manifest  as  soft-tissue  thickening  at  the  surgical  site.  It  can
occur  surrounding  the  mesenteric  vasculature  or  around
the  celiac  axis/hepatic  vasculature.  It  can  also  manifest  as
regional  lymphadenopathy,  albeit  less  common  (31).  A  posi-
tive surgical margin is the most important predictor for local
recurrence (15). Metastases in the abdomen commonly occur
in the liver and peritoneum. Peritoneal metastases can pres-
ent as peritoneal carcinomatosis and may be associated with
ascites (31). Both CT and MRI can help in evaluation of recur-
rence.  CT  has  the  advantage  of  excellent  spatial  resolution,
while MRI is superior in evaluation for metastatic disease in
the liver. Serial follow-up imaging is essential in differentiat-
ing local recurrence from normal postoperative findings.

Seeding  metastases,  from  pancreatic  cancer  following
pancreatectomy or interventional procedures, is a rare com-
plication with a few cases reported in the literature (73,74). In
most cases, tumor recurrence occurred within the body wall
(Fig 22) at the laparotomy site, along the course of the post-
surgical drain (Fig 22), or along the tract of a needle biopsy or
percutaneous transhepatic biliary drainage catheter. Seeding
metastasis within the posterior wall of the stomach has also
been  documented  following  an  endoscopic  US-guided  fine
needle  aspiration  of  pancreatic  cancer  (73,74).  The  imaging
features of the metastatic deposits can be similar to those of
the primary tumor (Fig 22).

Understanding  the  spectrum  of  pancreatic  surgeries,  indica-
tions, and commonly encountered complications is important
for radiologists since imaging has become the primary method
for  investigating  perioperative  problems.  There  are  multiple
unique complications given the inherent technical challenges
of operating on the pancreas. As described in this article, a sys-
tems-based  approach  for  addressing  potential  complications
provides a strong interpretive framework for reviewing postop-
erative CT or MRI examinations.
