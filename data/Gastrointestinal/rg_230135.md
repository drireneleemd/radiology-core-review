Since  its  inception  in  the  early  1900s,  the  small  bowel  fol-
low-through (SBFT) study was the only noninvasive diagnos-
tic imaging tool for the small bowel for nearly half a century.
Although CT enterography, MR enterography, and endoscopy
have helped to make substantial contributions to the field of
gastrointestinal medicine, SBFT remains useful in certain di-
agnostic scenarios. In particular, it provides real-time assess-
ments  of  both  function  and  anatomy,  making  it  valuable  in
evaluating  and  managing  adhesive  small  bowel  obstruction
and postoperative anatomy.

SBFT is performed using fluoroscopy in a stepwise fashion
after the administration of oral contrast media. Barium is the
preferred contrast media as it allows a more detailed evalua-
tion. However, water-soluble contrast media should be used in
place of barium when evaluating for leak or transit time in the
setting of adhesive small bowel obstruction. Unlike cross-sec-
tional  imaging  modalities,  fluoroscopic  SBFT  is  a  dynamic
examination  that  requires  the  input  and  active  attention  of
a radiologist with technical expertise. The radiologist may be
required to tailor the examination to answer specific clinical
questions as well as make modifications in real time during
the study based on any abnormalities that are observed.

Using  a  pattern-based  approach,  fluoroscopic  SBFT  can
help  diagnose  a  variety  of  conditions  affecting  the  small
bowel,  including  inflammatory,  neoplastic,  obstructive,  and
malabsorptive  conditions.  Small  bowel  morphologic  struc-
tures can typically be characterized as one of the following di-
agnostic patterns: thickened folds (Fig 1), irregular or nodular
folds, featureless or mucosal effacement (Fig 2), and dilated
bowel with smooth regular folds. Paired with the clinical his-
tory and laboratory findings, SBFT can help to further narrow
the differential diagnosis.

The water-soluble contrast challenge is a modified version
of traditional fluoroscopic SBFT used to evaluate small bowel
transit in special clinical situations such as small bowel ob-
struction due to adhesive disease or in critically ill patients.
This  procedure  involves  administering  a  water-soluble  oral
contrast agent and obtaining a series of portable abdominal
radiographs to track the progress (or lack thereof ) of the con-
trast media through the small bowel. The water-soluble con-
trast  challenge  offers  high  sensitivity  and  specificity  when
used  as  a  triage  tool  to  distinguish  patients  with  adhesive

Knowledge  of  the  indications,  contraindications,  tech-
niques, and pitfalls of SBFT is essential for providing appro-
priate patient care. The slide presentation provides an over-
view  of  the  important  elements  of  fluoroscopic  SBFT  and
includes both radiologic and illustrative examples of normal
anatomy  and  pathologic  conditions  for  which  the  examina-
tion can provide crucial diagnostic information.

The presentation allows the viewer to navigate through a
series  of  classic  cases  using  a  pattern-based  approach,  with
the goal of reaffirming the value and utility of SBFT in an age
of ubiquitous cross-sectional imaging.
