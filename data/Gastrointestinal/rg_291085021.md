**Figure 3.**  Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatogram shows that the cystic duct is completely occluded, and there is no contrast material within the gallbladder lumen. Filling defects in the distal common bile duct were thought to represent small calculi, which were subsequently removed at sphincterotomy.

**Figure 4.**  Photograph of the mucosal surface of the excised gallbladder shows numerous dark-brown polypoid lesions. (Scale is in centimeters.)

* [Download as PowerPoint](/action/downloadFigures?doi=10.1148%2Frg.291085021&id=F4)

![](/cms/10.1148/rg.291085021/asset/images/g09ja20c05x.jpeg)

**Figure 5.**  Low-power photomicrograph (original magnification, ×8; hematoxylin-eosin stain) of a specimen taken from the excised gallbladder shows polypoid malignant melanoma. Beneath intact epithelium, massive infiltration of the lamina propria by malignant melanocytes is seen. There is no involvement of the other layers of the gallbladder wall.

* [Download as PowerPoint](/action/downloadFigures?doi=10.1148%2Frg.291085021&id=F5)

![](/cms/10.1148/rg.291085021/asset/images/g09ja20c06a.jpeg)

**Figure 6a.**  Malignant melanoma of the gallbladder. **(a)** High-power photomicrograph (original magnification, ×200; hematoxylin-eosin stain) shows sheets of large epithelioid cells with malignant features, such as markedly pleomorphic nuclei and prominent nucleoli, and abundant cytoplasm that contains a variable amount of melanin pigment (arrows). Numerous melanophages also are seen (arrowheads). **(b)** High-power photomicrograph (original magnification, ×100; immunoperoxidase stain) shows diffuse strong immunoreactivity for melan-A antigen (arrows). These findings helped confirm the presence of a melanocytic neoplasm.
