A wide range of imaging manifestations of liver metastases can be
encountered, as various primary cancers preferably metastasize to
the liver (organ-specific metastases), with the imaging character-
istics largely depending on various primary tumor–specific factors
such as histopathologic category, degree of tumor differentiation,
histologic behavior, and intratumor alterations. Characteristic imag-
ing features potentially can help provide a more precise diagnosis in
some clinical settings. These settings include those of (a) primary
cancers of hollow organs such as gastrointestinal organs, the lungs,
and the bladder, owing to the appearance of metastases that cannot
be applied to the liver, which is a parenchymal organ; (b) unknown
primary tumors; (c) more than one primary tumor; (d) another
emergent malignancy; and (e) transformation to a different histo-
pathologic tumor subtype. The characteristic features include the
target sign on T2-weighted MR images or during the hepatobili-
ary phase of hypovascular metastasis, the peripheral rim washout
sign on delayed phase images, peritumor hyperintensity during the
hepatobiliary phase, hypervascular metastasis, a cystic appearance
with marked hyperintensity on T2-weighted images, marked hyper-
intensity on T1-weighted images, calcification, capsular retraction,
absence of the vessel-penetrating sign, distribution of liver metasta-
ses, and rare intraductal forms of metastases. In addition to various
factors associated with the primary cancer, desmoplastic reactions
around the tumor—which can be observed in adenocarcinomas
with peripheral and peritumor enhancement, distinct arteriopor-
tal shunts with metastases from pancreatic ductal carcinoma, and
pseudocirrhosis—also can affect these findings. The authors review
the characteristic imaging findings of liver metastases from vari-
ous primary cancers, with a focus on the mechanisms that underlie
organ-specific liver metastases.

Introduction
The liver is one of the most common sites of metastases, and liver me-
tastases are more common than primary liver cancers (1–3). A large
number of malignancies—including cancers of the gastrointestinal sys-
tem (eg, colorectum, stomach, pancreas, biliary), genitourinary system
(eg, kidney, prostate), gynecologic system (eg, ovary, endometrium,
cervix), neuroendocrine system, and breast; soft-tissue sarcomas;
melanomas; and lymphoma (4)—tend to metastasize to the liver.

As patients with liver metastases have a worse prognosis than
patients who do not have them (1), accurate diagnosis is crucial
for appropriate management of these secondary cancers. Imaging
findings have an important role in facilitating accurate evaluations
to detect and characterize metastases. Thus, the number, location,

Frequency of Liver Metastases
Liver metastases have been frequently identified
in patients who have secondary tumors from
various primary cancers, mainly those from gas-
trointestinal organs, according to data from the
SEER database (1) and knowledge of different
histologic subtypes (Table 1). Data from an-
other database limited to adenocarcinoma cases
(5) have shown the liver to be one of the or-
gans most affected by metastatic disease, which
arises most frequently from primary sites in
the colorectum, pancreas, stomach, breast, and
lung (Table 2). More than 50%–90% of colo-
rectal, breast, gastric, lung, and prostate cancers
are adenocarcinomas (6). The second most
frequently observed subtype is squamous cell
carcinoma, which is found with several cancers
(6). A large number of other malignant tumors

from almost any site—including neuroendo-
crine tumor (NET), gastrointestinal stromal
tumor (GIST), malignant melanoma, soft-tissue
sarcoma, and lymphoma—can metastasize to the
liver (4).

Thus, a better understanding of the frequent

sites of primary cancers could be useful in
clinical cases involving more than one primary
tumor. For example, in a patient with simultane-
ous colorectal and prostate carcinomas, multiple
liver masses may likely be due to colorectal liver
metastases, considering the frequency of these
cancers (Table 1).

Why the Liver Is a Dominant Site of
Metastases
Tumor cells are known to colonize in specific or-
gans (organ-specific metastases) other than the
liver. For example, 91% of ovarian cancers me-
tastasize to the peritoneum (Table 2) (5), 90%
of prostate cancers metastasize to bone, and
85% of renal cell carcinomas metastasize to the
lung. Even so, various primary cancers prefer-
ably metastasize to the liver, and several factors
are speculated to be related to organ-specific
metastases of the liver (7).

Blood Flow Factors (Portal Circulation)
The liver’s vast blood supply, which originates
from the portal and systemic systems, may pro-
vide a fertile environment for metastases. On the
basis of pathologic examination of the anatomic
structure of the vascular system and the accom-
panying hemodynamic flow, Ewing (8) pointed
out the importance of the associated metastatic
dissemination and geometric factors. The high
frequency of liver metastases that results from
pancreatic adenocarcinoma can be explained by
Ewing’s mechanical theory.

Factors That Create a Fertile
Environment in the Liver Parenchyma
The fenestra of the hepatic sinusoidal endothe-
lium may facilitate formation of a preferable
environment for metastases and penetration of
malignant cells into the hepatic parenchyma
(microenvironment) (9). From pathologic and
clinical perspectives, Paget (10) postulated that
metastases are not events due to chance; rather,
they occur because some tumor cells (“seeds”)
grow preferentially in the microenvironment of
select organs (“soil”), and metastases occur only
when the appropriate seed is implanted in suit-
able soil. The fact that uveal melanoma specifi-
cally has liver metastases, even when the pri-
mary site is far from the liver (11), may support
the potential role of Paget’s seed-soil theory, but
it cannot be explained by Ewing’s theory.

Tumor-related Factors
The theories of both Paget (10) and Ewing (8)
could be partially correct, but neither theory alone
can explain all of the phenomena associated with
organ-specific metastases. Studies have shown

that the primary tumor antecedently consolidates
the microenvironment of the metastatic site (soil),
for ease in colonization of tumor cells (seeds),
by effusing organ-specific exosomes; this process
constitutes formation of a premetastatic niche (12).

Exosomes, which range from 40 to 160 nm in di-
ameter (average, 100 nm), are a subset of extracel-
lular vesicles that are surrounded by a lipid bilayer
membrane and secreted by most eukaryotic cells
(13). The exosomes play a crucial role in regulating
tumor growth, metastasis, and angiogenesis during
the process of cancer development (14). It has
been shown that different types of proteins (inte-
grins) are found on the surface of exosomes that
are derived from different types of malignant cells
(15). For example, in the liver, exosomes expressed
by the specific integrins αv and β5 preferentially
fuse with Kupffer cells (15). Thus, tumor exosomes
derive organ-specific metastasis, during which
there is integrin-mediated fusion with resident cells
(16). As cellular changes to the target organ are
induced after uptake of the tumor exosomes, the
changes can promote proliferation and colonization
of cancer cells at the metastatic sites (Fig 1).

Metastasis Process
Although the mechanisms that drive each step of
the metastasis process remain relatively unclear
owing to their complexity, recently introduced

engineered models have elucidated key features
of the metastatic cascade (17). There are six
major steps involved in metastatic progression of
tumors: (a) invasion of the basement membrane
and cell migration, (b) intravasation within the
surrounding vasculature or lymphatic system,
(c) survival within the circulation, (d) extrava-
sation from the vasculature to the secondary
tissues, (e) subsequent proliferation at distant
locations, and finally (f) colonization of second-
ary tumor sites (Fig 1) (18).

One important cell remodeling process that
occurs during the initial local invasion is epithe-
lial-to-mesenchymal transition (19). During this
transition, there is fragmentation of the base-
ment membrane along with loss of polarized
organization of the epithelial cells, after which
the cells become migratory, develop invasive
capabilities, and then transform into highly mi-
gratory mesenchymal cells. During the reverse
process—that is, mesenchymal-to-epithelial
transition—this action appears to support a
metastatic outgrowth for proliferation at distant
sites (Fig 1) (19).

Imaging Techniques
Accurate detection and characterization of liver
metastases depends on optimization of imaging
modalities. Multiple imaging techniques includ-
ing CT, MRI, and fluorine 18–fluorodeoxyglu-
cose PET/CT are widely used. In a meta-analysis
(20), MRI, CT, and PET/CT had sensitivity of
93.1% (95% CI: 88.4%, 96.0%), 82.1% (95%
CI: 74.0%, 88.1%), and 74.1% (95% CI: 62.1%,
83.3%), respectively. It is important that the
majority of the MRI procedures included in the
analysis were carried out by using gadoxetic acid
(Primovist; Bayer Schering) and included diffu-
sion-weighted imaging.

The hepatobiliary contrast agent gadoxetic
acid, which has replaced previously used hepato-
specific contrast agents such as mangafodipir
trisodium and superparamagnetic iron oxide, has
high sensitivity (90.3%) for detecting 1-cm or
smaller liver metastases on hepatobiliary phase
MR images (21). The diagnostic performance
of diffusion-weighted imaging and hepatobiliary
phase MRI and the efficacy of combining these
sequences for evaluation of liver metastases have
been demonstrated in previous studies (22,23).
For example, the per-lesion sensitivity of
gadoxetic acid–enhanced MRI combined with
diffusion-weighted imaging (90%) was found to
exceed that of other imaging modalities, such as
CT and PET/CT (sensitivity of 68% and 61%,
respectively), especially in detection of lesions
smaller than 10 mm (sensitivity of 74% for MRI
combined with diffusion-weighted imaging vs
16% and 9% for CT and PET/CT, respectively)
(23). In the same report, it was shown that the
diagnostic performance of PET/CT was relatively
low for detection of small (<10 mm) lesions.

Furthermore, diffusion can be quantified by
using the apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC),
with low ADC corresponding to restricted dif-
fusion. The mean ADC of malignant lesions has
been reported to be 0.980 3 10−3 mm2/sec, and
applying an ADC cutoff of 1.066 3 10−3 mm2/
sec has resulted in specificity of 86.6% and
sensitivity of 73.6% for detection of malignancy
(24). Although there may be an overlap in the
ADC for liver metastases and other benign or
malignant focal lesions (24,25), some metasta-
ses, such as those of NET and lymphoma, can
have characteristically low ADCs (26,27), while
others such as mucinous metastases can have
higher ADCs (28).

Other MRI sequences, including T1- and T2-
weighted imaging with or without fat suppression,
as well as CT, also are necessary to characterize
focal liver lesions. Thus, CT and MRI findings are
reviewed in this article. Gallium 68–tetraazacy-
clododecane tetraacetic acid-octreotate PET/CT is

increasingly becoming an option for patients with
known NET. PET appears to have good sensitivity
and specificity (92.9% and 75%, respectively) for
detecting liver metastases (29), although further
studies are needed to determine whether it should
be performed routinely.

Vascular Supply of Liver Metastasis
and Dynamic Contrast-enhanced
Study

For characterization, it is crucial to combine
pre- and postcontrast CT and MR images for a
dynamic contrast-enhanced imaging study. Solid
liver metastases are typically supplied by arterial
blood flow (30); hence, they can be classified as
hypovascular or hypervascular depending on the
degree of the underlying hepatic arterial supply
(31). Hypovascular metastases show decreased
enhancement relative to the background liver
parenchyma and are most conspicuous on portal
venous phase images, which are acquired ap-
proximately 60–80 seconds after initial injection
of contrast agent (32).

Hypervascular metastases enhance earlier than

the background liver on arterial phase images,
which are acquired approximately 30–40 seconds
after the initial injection (32). The lesions show
washout on portal phase images and similar or
decreased enhancement relative to the back-
ground liver parenchyma on delayed phase im-
ages, which are acquired approximately 150–180
seconds after the initial injection (32). Thus,
dynamic contrast-enhanced images can depict
both hypovascular and hypervascular metasta-
ses, although the optimal number and choice of
acquisition phases for CT remain contentious,
given the potential risks associated with higher
radiation doses.

Importance of Noting Specific
Imaging Features That Suggest a
Specific Primary Cancer
The imaging findings of liver metastases largely
depend on various primary tumor–specific
factors, such as histopathologic category (eg,
adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma,
small cell carcinoma, transitional cell carci-
noma, NET, sarcoma, melanoma, lymphoma),
tumor differentiation (eg, well, moderate, poor),
histologic behavior (eg, solid, cystic, mucinous),
and intratumor alterations (eg, necrosis, degen-
eration, hemorrhage). However, the imaging
features of several primary cancers sometimes
overlap. For example, liver metastases from
colorectal and gastric adenocarcinoma can have
similar appearances, manifesting as multifocal
hypovascular nodular lesions with hypoattenu-
ation on noncontrast CT images, hypointensity

Peripheral and Peritumor Enhancement
Desmoplastic reactions around colorectal liver
metastases are closely related to the peripheral
and peritumor enhancement seen on arterial
phase and portal venous phase images (Fig 3).
Other histopathologic changes in the surround-
ing hepatic parenchyma, such as lymphocytic
infiltration, compressed hepatic parenchyma,
and sinusoidal congestion, also can be related to
this enhancement (36).

Histopathologically, colorectal liver metastases

are classified into three distinct histopathologic
growth patterns—desmoplastic, pushing, and
replacement—which are based on the interface
between the cancer cells of the metastasis and the
cancer cells of the surrounding normal liver and
different mechanisms to obtain a vascular supply
(37). It is important that responses to chemo-
therapy are different, depending on the histo-
pathologic growth patterns (38). The desmoplastic
pattern corresponds to metastases with a promi-
nent desmoplastic reaction, showing peripheral
and peritumor enhancement. Thus, the imaging
features might enable prediction of the response to
chemotherapy, although there have been no stud-
ies linking imaging features to histologic subtypes.

Distinct Arterioportal Shunts with
Pancreatic Metastases
Pancreatic ductal adenocarcinoma is the most
common form of pancreatic cancer and is char-
acterized by abundant fibrosis, which is caused
by desmoplastic reaction around the tumor.
Extremely dense desmoplastic reactions are
equally observed at the metastatic site, with the
fibrous stroma more frequently and excessively
broad beyond the cancer cells (34). Extremely
dense desmoplastic reactions tend to compress
the peripheral vessels and reduce blood flow,
and more hypotensive portal or hepatic veins are
more easily affected. This phenomenon can be
depicted as arterioportal shunts accompanied

on T1-weighted images, and moderate hyperin-
tensity on T2-weighted images.

Even with overlapping findings, it is not dif-
ficult to diagnose liver metastasis in a patient with
a known sole primary cancer. However, a better
understanding of the typical and characteristic im-
aging findings could aid in making a more precise
diagnosis in the following situations: (a) Primary
cancers of hollow organs such as gastro intestinal
organs, the lungs, and the bladder, owing to the
appearance of metastases that cannot be applied to
the liver, which is a parenchymal organ. For such
cases, it is important to know the typical findings
of each primary cancer. (b) Unknown primary
tumors. For example, hypovascular and hypervas-
cular liver masses each would indicate a different
primary cancer. (c) More than one primary tumor.
For example, a hypovascular mass with calcifica-
tion would indicate primary colo rectal adenocar-
cinoma rather than primary prostatic adenocar-
cinoma in a patient with both of these primary
cancers. (d) Another emergent malignancy. For
example, in a patient with colorectal adenocar-
cinoma, multiple hypervascular masses might
indicate the potential for another primary cancer
such as a new NET. (e) Transformation to a dif-
ferent histopathologic subtype. In addition, some
characteristic imaging findings represent biomark-
ers of prognosis or treatment response.

(34). Desmoplastic reactions around the tu-
mor, which consist of fibrosis growth caused by
cancer-associated fibroblasts around tumor cells

Pseudocirrhosis
After chemotherapy, pseudocirrhosis, a rare
condition, can lead to morphologic changes such
as multiple liver metastases. This is seen particu-
larly with liver metastases from breast cancer;
however, it is rarely observed with liver metasta-
ses from pancreatic, colon, medullary thyroid, or
esophageal cancers (39). It is difficult to predict
the occurrence of pseudo cirrhosis because it is
not related to the number of metastases, the his-
topatholic features, or the chemotherapeutic regi-
men (40). Although the pathophysiologic mecha-
nism of pseudocirrhosis has yet to be elucidated,
it might be deeply involved with desmoplastic

reaction. Therefore, abundant fibrosis caused by
desmoplastic reaction around the tumor, fibrotic
changes caused by chemotherapy, repeated tumor
shrinkage and enlargement, and nodular regen-
erative hyperplasia in response to chemotherapy
might invoke the irregular atrophy-hypertrophy
complex of hepatic parenchyma (41).

In addition, a relatively good response to neo-
adjuvant chemotherapy and a relatively long sur-
vival period could lead to morphometric changes
in the liver (Fig 5). Although CT images depict
hepatic morphometric changes that mimic cir-
rhosis, residual metastases cannot be separately
represented by fibrous changes (41). While ADC-
based quantification might facilitate accurate
assessment of the hepatic condition by means of
discrimination between residual metastases and
fibrous change (42), there have been few descrip-
tions of MRI findings.

In diagnosis of liver metastases, several charac-
teristic imaging findings need to be taken into
consideration. These findings include tumor
vascularity, the characteristic atttenuation values
and signal intensities of lesions due to specific

primary cancers, specific signs due to character-
istic pathologic entities, the pathognomonic dis-
tribution, and rare intraductal metastatic forms
(Tables 3, 4). Along with accurate detection of
the lesion, consideration of the following issues
is necessary: whether the imaging findings are

Target Sign on T2-weighted Images of
Hypovascular Metastases
Metastatic adenocarcinomas are the most common
type of carcinoma (6) and are often accompanied
by various degrees of fibrosis, liquefactive necro-
sis, and degeneration in predominantly central
areas (43). At CT, metastatic adenocarcinomas
appear as nonspecific hypovascular lesions with
peripheral and peritumor enhancement on arte-
rial or portal venous phase images (Fig 3) (36).
In contrast, at MRI, in predominantly central
areas, fibrous change or old hemorrhage is de-
picted as hypointensity on T1- and T2-weighted
images, with liquefactive necrosis or degenera-
tion depicted as clear areas of hyperintensity on
T2-weighted images and slight hyperintensity on
T1-weighted images (44,45).

However, peripherally dominant viable tumor

cells are depicted as moderate-hyperintensity
lesions on T2-weighted images and hypointense
lesions on T1-weighted images (Figs 6, 7, E2A).
The histopathologic contrast between central
and peripheral areas is seen as the target sign on
T2-weighted images (Figs 6B, 7A, E2A) (44).
This finding has been observed in 25% of me-
tastases, those from colorectal adenocarcinoma
in particular (46).

Peripheral Rim Washout Sign
The peripheral washout sign is defined as an
enhancing liver lesion with a peripheral rim of
decreased enhancement relative to its center and
the surrounding liver parenchyma on delayed
phase MR images obtained by using extracellular
contrast agents (Fig E3) (47). The washout sign
is observable in 25% of patients with liver me-
tastases from NET or from colorectal, breast, or
gastric carcinomas (47). Peripheral washout may
reflect a transitory perfusion within the well-vas-
cularized peripheral portion of the lesion, which
has both good arterial supply and good venous
withdrawal of contrast material (47).

Hypervascular Metastases
Hypervascular metastases typically arise from
hypervascular primary tumors, including NET,
renal cell carcinoma, thyroid carcinoma, cho-
riocarcinoma, pheochromocytoma, malignant
melanoma, and sarcomas (54), which show
hypoattenuation on nonenhanced CT images,
moderate hypointensity on T1-weighted images,
and moderate hyperintensity on T2-weighted
images. Dynamic contrast-enhanced CT and
MRI depict early enhancement on arterial phase
images and washout on portal venous phase
images (Fig 8) (32), which are indistinguishable
from the enhancement findings of hepatocellular
cancer (Fig E5).

However, multifocal hypervascular lesions in
patients without cirrhosis might be more likely
to be liver metastases (55). In addition, liver me-
tastases from gastrointestinal or breast adeno-
carcinoma potentially can have an appearance

similar to that of a hypervascular lesion owing to
marked peripheral and peritumor enhancement
(Fig E6) (36,54).

The washout in small (≤5 mm) metastases
from NET on portal venous phase images is
sometimes unclear, although arterial phase
hyperenhancement can be observed (56). In
addition, hypervascular metastases frequently
accompany various degrees of degeneration
(Fig 9) (54,57).

Intratumor Hemorrhage (Fluid-Fluid Level)
Intratumor hemorrhage (fluid-fluid level) can be
caused by the hemorrhagic content, which also is
closely related to necrosis or degeneration associ-
ated with metastases. This is encountered espe-
cially in metastases from NET and is also found in
tumors such as melanoma, angiosarcoma, ovarian
carcinoma, leiomyosarcoma, and squamous cell
carcinoma (60,61). Intratumor hemorrhage can
exhibit slight hyperintensity on T1-weighted im-
ages (45) and characteristic fluid-fluid levels on
T2-weighted images (Fig 13) (60). The attenu-
ation and signal intensity at imaging depend on
the degree and duration of the intratumor hemor-
rhage. Intratumor hemorrhage infrequently in-
duces hepatic rupture and hemoperitoneum (61).

Calcification
Calcification related to liver metastases is most
commonly observed in primary tumors of mu-
cinous adenocarcinoma of the colorectum and
stomach, breast, and high-grade serous carci-

noma, although it is rarely observed in other
tumors such as thyroid carcinoma and chondro-
sarcoma (63). Intratumor calcification is clearly
depicted on noncontrast CT images (Fig 15),
with patterns and distributions (central and pe-
ripheral) that have been found to vary (64).

In addition, the calcification is often accom-

panied by capsular retraction adjacent to the
subcapsular area (Fig 15C). In small metasta-
ses, it is possible that only the calcification will
be depicted at CT (Fig 15A); therefore, care
should be taken not to miss the small area of high
attenuation.

Although the development of calcifications is
considered to be a marker of treatment response,
especially in KRAS wild-type colorectal adeno-
carcinoma treated with antiepidermal growth
factor receptor drugs, the correlation between
calcification of pretreatment metastases and
effectiveness of chemotherapies or prognosis
remains controversial (65).

Hepatic Capsular Retraction
When the convex hepatic contour exhibits fo-
cal flattening or concavity, this is defined as
hepatic capsular retraction (66). A variety of
hepatic neoplasms and nonneoplastic lesions,
including liver metastases, have been reported
to be associated with hepatic capsular retrac-
tion. Capsular retraction usually occurs adja-
cent to subcapsular metastases (Figs 6, 15, E2)
from primary tumors such as lung, breast, and
colorectal adenocarcinomas (66). These tumors
tend to appear to accompany substantial fibro-
sis caused by desmoplastic reactions around the
metastases (34).

Vessel-penetrating Sign
The vessel-penetrating sign is an imaging find-
ing in which intrahepatic vessels course through
the tumor without disruption or constriction,
and it is often observed in primary and second-
ary lymphomas (Fig E9) (67). Primary hepatic
lymphoma is extremely rare, whereas secondary
liver involvement occurs in 50% of patients with

non-Hodgkin lymphoma (67,68). In addition to
the vessel-penetrating sign, other typical imag-
ing features—such as homogeneous attenuation
or signal intensity at CT or MRI, respectively;
homogeneous poor enhancement relative to the
background liver parenchyma on contrast-en-
hanced images; and relatively low ADC—could
be useful clues for diagnosing lymphoma (27).
When a multifocal liver mass is encountered
without the vessel-penetrating sign at follow-up of
a patient with lymphoma, it could suggest another
newly appearing malignant tumor with liver metas-
tases (Fig 16). However, this is a rare occurrence.

solitary mass or confluent masses (Fig E10). In
addition, the following two characteristic distri-

metastases and metastases prominently located
in the gallbladder fossa of the liver.

Diffuse Small Nodular Metastases (Miliary
Liver Metastases)
On rare occasions, tumors can infiltrate diffusely
and spread as tiny nodules in the whole liver. This
phenomenon is referred to as miliary liver metas-
tases and has been reported with lung and breast
carcinomas, uveal melanoma, and NET (69). This
manifestation can sometimes be accompanied
by diffuse intrasinusoidal metastases, which are
clearly depicted at MRI as numerous tiny nodular
metastases on T2- or diffusion-weighted images
(Fig 17). On the other hand, CT images do not
depict these tiny metastatic nodules and show only
enlargement of the entire liver (Fig 17B).

Metastases Prominently Located in the
Gallbladder Fossa
Gallbladder tumors often metastasize promi-
nently to the gallbladder fossa of the liver as
multiple or solitary lesions (Fig 18). This occurs

because the drainage veins of the gallbladder
(cystic veins) flow into the intrahepatic portal
branches or sinusoids around the gallbladder
fossa (70).

Rare Intraductal Forms of Metastases

Intrasinusoidal Metastases
Most metastases form nodular lesions in the liver
parenchyma and rarely appear after ductal metas-
tases. Rarely, tumors infiltrate and spread along
the hepatic sinusoids instead of forming discrete
masses, making the disease radiologically occult.
These rare metastases can be associated with solid
tumors, including breast cancer, small cell lung
cancer, gastric cancer, and melanoma, and may
cause acute liver failure at a high rate (71).

Despite these life-threatening conditions,

noncontrast and contrast-enhanced CT and MRI
sometimes depict hepatomegaly only (Fig 19).

Portal Vein Tumor Thrombosis
In contrast to the thrombus seen with hepatocel-
lular carcinoma, intrahepatic tumor thrombus
occurs infrequently with liver metastases. Involve-
ment of the intrahepatic portal vein has been
reported with several tumors, such as gastric
and colorectal adenocarcinoma and gastric NET
(73). In general, intrahepatic tumor thrombus is
usually associated with and shows continuity with
nodular metastases (Fig 20).

Biliary Metastasis
There are two types of biliary metastases: biliary
epithelium metastases and intraluminal metas-
tases of the bile duct, and both types can occur

concurrently. Intra- and extrabiliary epithelium
metastases rarely occur with colorectal, gastric,
breast, uterine, or ovarian carcinomas (74) and
involve homo geneous thickening and enhance-
ment of the bile duct, with smooth tapered dilata-
tion of this duct.

Extrabiliary epithelium metastases are typically

accompanied by other metastases such as those
involving a hepatic parenchymal mass or multiple
lymph node metastases (Fig 21). When only the
extrahepatic bile duct is involved, it is difficult to
differentiate primary bile duct cancer. Further-
more, dilatation of the bile duct can sometimes
be indistinguishable from compression of the bile
duct by lymph node metastases within the hepa-
toduodenal ligament.

Intraluminal metastases of the bile duct (ie,
intrabiliary metastases) form a polypoid mass
within the lumen of the bile duct. The polypoid
mass can invade the biliary epithelium and lead
to dilatation of the peripheral bile duct, which is
often accompanied by a solid metastatic mass in
the parenchyma (75). Such metastases have also

Lymphatic Invasion
Intrahepatic lymphatic invasion by tumor cells
is a manifestation of diffuse lymphatic spread
of malignant cells. It has been reported in 4%
of cases of liver metastases, especially those of
gastric cancer and lymphoma (78). Malignant
cells within lymphatic vessels form a periportal
mass and dilate the lymphatic vessels.

Imaging Biomarkers of Prognosis or
Treatment Response
Several imaging findings described in earlier sec-
tions of this review could be used for predicting
the prognosis or the effectiveness of chemothera-
pies, serving as imaging biomarkers. However, it
is necessary to understand that these results are
not absolute and could change in the future on
the basis of data from larger studies.

Conclusion

Although the imaging features of liver metasta-
ses from various primary malignancies overlap,
obtaining a better understanding of the mecha-
nisms that underlie organ-specific metastases and
determining the characteristic features of liver
metastases from specific primary cancers can
help provide a more precise diagnosis.
