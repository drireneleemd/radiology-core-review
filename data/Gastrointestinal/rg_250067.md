For management of hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC), locoregional therapies are commonly used as curative interventions,
bridging strategies before a liver transplant, or methods to reduce the tumor burden. After treatment, imaging surveillance is
essential to guiding subsequent patient treatment decisions by enabling timely detection of recurrence or residual tumor viabil-
ity. However, imaging interpretation can be challenging due to variability in expected findings, based on the treatment modal-
ity, response achieved, and time since therapy. The CT and MRI Liver Imaging Reporting and Data System (LI-RADS) treatment
response assessment algorithm was developed to standardize imaging interpretation and reporting after locoregional therapy
for primary liver malignancies. Integrating emerging evidence and feedback from clinical users, the updated 2024 treatment
response assessment algorithm establishes distinct criteria for nonradiation- and radiation-based therapies and includes ancil-
lary MRI features to improve detection of tumor viability. The authors review the updated LI-RADS treatment response assess-
ment algorithm, with particular attention to clinical applicability and implications for patient care.

Introduction
Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is the most common prima-
ry liver cancer and the sixth most frequently diagnosed can-
cer  worldwide.  Historically  common  in  regions  with  a  high
prevalence of viral hepatitis, HCC is now diagnosed with in-
creasing frequency in countries with a traditionally low risk
of hepatitis, such as the United States. This increase reflects
the effectiveness of antiviral treatments in reducing the num-
ber of cases of viral hepatitis along with an increase in alco-
hol-related  cirrhosis  and  metabolic  dysfunction–associated
steatotic liver disease (1).

Established therapeutic strategies for patients with HCC in-
clude  surgical  resection,  a  liver  transplant,  locoregional  ther-
apy,  and  systemic  therapy  (2,3).  Locoregional  therapies  are
employed as curative interventions for early-stage disease, to
reduce the tumor burden, or as a bridge therapy for patients
waiting for a liver transplant. After locoregional therapy is per-
formed, patients typically undergo imaging surveillance every
3 months with multiphasic CT or MRI. Accurate posttreatment
imaging is essential for guiding patient treatment, enabling as-
sessment of residual tumor viability, while allowing detection
and staging of untreated lesions elsewhere in the liver.

the  LI-RADS  TR  assessment  criteria  in  2024.  This  update  introduced  a
dedicated  algorithm  for  radiation-treated  lesions,  simplified  the  defi-
nition of viable disease, and incorporated ancillary imaging features to
upgrade lesions to the viable category.

	 LR-TR viable criteria were simplified in the 2024 update to the LI-RADS
TR assessment algorithms, and they now include the presence of mass-
like enhancement (any degree, any phase) in the treated lesion, along
its  margin,  or  at  the  surgical  margin.  This  enhancement  may  appear
nodular, smooth, or irregular, distorting the surrounding parenchyma.
	 The detection of restricted diffusion (any degree) or mild-to-moderate
hyperintensity on T2-weighted MR images in areas of uncertain mass-
like enhancement can be used at the radiologist’s discretion to “upcat-
egorize” lesions from LR-TR equivocal to LR-TR viable.

	 Tumor  response  after radiation-based  treatment may not be immedi-
ate, evolving gradually through various mechanisms of cell death. Early
posttreatment imaging frequently reveals persistent masslike enhance-
ment  and  ancillary  features  suggestive  of  tumor  viability,  requiring
careful evaluation of changes over time.

	 After  radiation-based  treatment,  ancillary  features  include  new  or  in-
creased  restricted  diffusion  or  mild-to-moderate  hyperintensity  on
T2-weighted MR images, which must correspond to the area of stable or
decreased masslike enhancement.

Recognizing  the  complexity  of  assessing  treatment  re-
sponse  (TR),  the  American  College  of  Radiology  (ACR)  in-
troduced  the  TR  assessment  algorithm  as  part  of  the  Liver
Imaging Reporting and Data System (LI-RADS) in 2017. This
system standardizes the evaluation and reporting of individ-
ual lesions in patients treated with locoregional therapy who
are at high risk for HCC. The 2017 LI-RADS TR assessment al-
gorithm allows categorization of treated lesions as nonevalu-
able, nonviable, equivocal, or viable based on the probability
of residual viable tumor (4).

Studies  (5,6)  have  demonstrated  that  posttreatment  im-
aging  features  differ  substantially  based  on  therapy  type.
Nonradiation-based  therapies  such  as  ablation  and  embo-
lization  typically  produce  immediate  tumor  destruction.
In  comparison,  radiation-based  therapies  cause  delayed
tumor  cell  death,  resulting  in  persistent  imaging  findings
that evolve over time (7,8). These distinct response patterns
revealed limitations in applying a single TR assessment ap-
proach across all therapy types. Incorporating emerging ev-
idence and user feedback (9), the ACR updated the LI-RADS
TR  assessment  criteria  in  2024  (Fig  1).  This  update  intro-
duced  a  dedicated  algorithm  for  radiation-treated  lesions,
simplified the definition of viable disease, and incorporated
ancillary  imaging  features  to  upgrade  lesions  to  the  viable
category (10). Lyschik et al (11) described a complementary

LI-RADS TR assessment algorithm for evaluation of lesions
with contrast-enhanced US.

The  following  sections  of  this  review  outline  the  ACR’s
updated  2024  LI-RADS  TR  assessment  criteria,  highlighting
their  clinical  implications  and  providing  practical  guidance
for imaging interpretation after locoregional therapy and sur-
gical resection.

CT and MRI Techniques
Multiphasic contrast-enhanced CT or MRI is essential to eval-
uate TR after locoregional therapy. CT involves the use of io-
dine-based contrast material and should also include an un-
enhanced  sequence  to  distinguish  true  enhancement  from
posttreatment  changes.  MRI  involves  either  extracellular  or
hepatobiliary  contrast  agents,  such  as  gadobenate  dimeglu-
mine or gadoxetate disodium.

The minimum protocol for both CT and MRI includes im-
aging during the arterial and portal phases as well as during
either  the  delayed  phase  or  transitional  and  hepatobiliary
phases,  depending  on  the  contrast  agent  used.  Acquisition
during the late arterial phase is critical for detecting residual
tumor because this phase coincides with peak enhancement
and  optimal  lesion-to-background  contrast.  This  phase  can
be identified based on portal vein enhancement: The early ar-
terial phase shows no portal enhancement, while the late ar-
terial phase demonstrates at least some portal enhancement
relative to that of the liver parenchyma (10,12,13).

Additional  required  MRI  sequences  include  unenhanced
T1-weighted  (in-phase  and  out-of-phase)  and  T2-weight-
ed  imaging.  Diffusion-weighted  and  subtraction  MRI  are
recommended  to  enhance  diagnostic  accuracy  (10).  Diffu-
sion-weighted MRI sequences generate signal intensity con-
trast  through  detection  of  restriction  of  the  random  motion
of water molecules. After the patient undergoes locoregional
therapy, restricted diffusion reflects residual hypercellularity,
potentially improving detection of any residual tumor tissue.
Diffusion-weighted  MRI  protocols  should  include  imaging
with at least two b values (b = 0–50 sec/mm2 and 500–1000
sec/mm2), with corresponding apparent diffusion coefficient
maps to distinguish true diffusion restriction from T2-weight-
ed “shine-through.”

Subtraction MRI techniques digitally remove baseline sig-
nal intensity from postcontrast T1-weighted MR images and
selectively demonstrate perfusion-related signal intensity. To
obtain valid subtraction MR images, acquisition parameters
between sequences must be identical. Accurate coregistration
is essential because even minor positional differences gener-
ate misregistration artifacts that appear as high- or low-sig-
nal-intensity bands around structures (10,12) (Fig S1). A sum-
mary of the imaging protocol for MRI is provided in Table 1.

Ablative therapy can be performed with curative intent for

are less effective for lesions larger than 3 cm or those adjacent
to major blood vessels due to energy dissipation (2,3).

Transcatheter  therapy  delivers  therapeutic  agents  to  the
feeding arteries of the tumor to induce necrosis (Fig 3A). Be-
cause the background liver is predominantly supplied by the
portal vein, selective arterial catheterization enables targeted
therapy  while  minimizing  liver  injury.  Transcatheter  meth-
ods include transarterial embolization, transarterial chemo-
embolization  (TACE),  and  drug-eluting  bead  TACE  (Fig  3B).
Transarterial radioembolization with yttrium 90 (90Y) follows
a similar technique but primarily exerts its effect through ra-
diation.

TACE involves infusion of a chemotherapeutic agent, typi-
cally doxorubicin, which can be mixed with ethiodized oil (ie,
Lipiodol; Guerbet). The radiopaque oil enables assessment of
therapy dispersion on CT images, with the extent of the accu-
mulation of oil correlating with tumor response after TACE.
Diffuse oil distribution in the treated tumor is associated with
near-complete necrosis at pathologic examination, compared
with absent, patchy, or partial oil coverage (16).

TR Categories
Nonradiation-based  therapy  induces  immediate  cell  death
due to ischemia or direct cellular damage, which is evidenced
by  the  disappearance  of  tumoral  enhancement  at  posttreat-
ment  imaging  (5,6).  Therefore,  the  detection  of  residual  or
new masslike enhancement in the treated lesion or along its
margins indicates the presence of a viable tumor.

LI-RADS TR Nonevaluable.—The LI-RADS (LR)-TR category is
assigned  when  masslike  enhancement  cannot  be  evaluated
due to poor image quality or technical limitations (eg, missing
images from contrast-enhanced phases) (Fig 4). The underly-
ing issue should be reported to suggest an alternative modali-
ty, if needed. When technical limitations preclude evaluation,
the nonevaluable category communicates that assessment is
inconclusive and alternative imaging should be performed or
follow-up intervals should be shortened.

LR-TR Nonviable.—LR-TR nonviable is defined by the absence
of masslike enhancement (any degree, any phase) at the treat-
ed lesion, along its margin, or at the surgical margin. Exam-

ples  of  findings  observed  in  this  category  include  complete
disappearance  of  the  lesion,  no  intralesional  enhancement,
smooth perilesional enhancement, and parenchymal perfu-
sional changes (Fig 5).

LR-TR  Equivocal.—The  LR-TR  equivocal  category  is  assigned
when  there  is  uncertainty  regarding  the  presence  or  mor-
phology  of  masslike  enhancement  in  a  treated  lesion,  along
its margin, or at the surgical margin (Fig 7). LR-TR equivocal
observations  harbor  pathologic  viability  in  33%–83%  of  cas-
es (26–30). Despite a substantial probability of residual via-
ble  disease,  the  overall  survival  time  in  patients  with  LR-TR
equivocal observations is similar to that in those with LR-TR
nonviable  observations  and  is  significantly  better  than  in

those with LR-TR viable observations (31,32). As a result, close
imaging follow-up is typically performed because unwarrant-
ed retreatment for indeterminate findings may contribute to
hepatic decompensation and poor clinical outcomes (33,34).

LR-TR Viable.—LR-TR viable criteria were simplified in the 2024
update  to  the  LI-RADS  TR  assessment  algorithms,  and  they
now include the presence of masslike enhancement (any de-
gree,  any  phase)  in  the  treated  lesion,  along  its  margin,  or  at
the surgical margin. This enhancement may appear nodular,
smooth, or irregular, distorting the surrounding parenchyma
(Fig 8). The LI-RADS 2017 TR assessment criteria for LR-TR vi-
able included nodular, masslike, irregular, or irregularly thick
tissue,  with  arterial  phase  hyperenhancement,  washout,  or
enhancement  similar  to  that  before  the  patient  underwent
treatment; the latter two were specific features expanding on
the  modified  Response  Evaluation  Criteria  in  Solid  Tumors
(RECIST) for HCC (4).

Ancillary Features
The updated 2024 LI-RADS TR assessment criteria introduced
ancillary features intended to enhance the detection of resid-
ual tumors in cases of equivocal enhancement. The detection
of restricted diffusion (any degree) or mild-to-moderate hy-
perintensity on T2-weighted MR images in areas of uncertain
masslike enhancement can be used at the radiologist’s discre-
tion to “upcategorize” lesions from LR-TR equivocal to LR-TR
viable (Fig 9).

Validation studies of the 2017 TR assessment criteria have
shown that LR-TR nonviable and LR-TR viable criteria demon-
strate  high  specificity  for  detection  of  complete  and  incom-
plete pathologic necrosis, respectively (28,35). In comparison,
LR-TR equivocal criteria have shown lower accuracy in assess-
ing  the  extent  of  tumor  necrosis,  with  radiologic-pathologic
correlation  studies  indicating  residual  tumor  in  33%–83%  of
equivocal cases (26–30). Because features of viability are more
frequently observed in larger residual disease, equivocally vi-
able components tend to be smaller than viable components
(29,30).

Sensitivity for viable tumors might not be enough to guide
treatment because meta-analyses (35,36) have shown it to be
approximately  60%.  Incorporating  LR-TR  equivocal  and  LR-
TR  viable  criteria  improves  sensitivity  and  positive  predic-
tive value for incomplete necrosis, albeit with a reduction in
specificity  (29,37).  A  counterargument  to  combining  LR-TR
equivocal and LR-TR viable criteria is that the outcomes of the
former resemble those with LR-TR nonviable criteria (31,32).
However, identification of LR-TR equivocal cases where early
intervention may be beneficial is an important clinical dilem-
ma.  Evolving  research  explores  the  integration  of  ancillary
features  with  LR-TR  equivocal  criteria,  specifically  diffusion

Postsurgical Findings

Radiation Damage Mechanism
Radiation  therapy  aims  to  inhibit  cellular  replication  and
induce  cell  death  by  causing  substantial  DNA  damage.  This

through indirect mechanisms involving the production of re-

Radiation-induced  damage  activates  cellular  signaling
pathways that regulate the modality of cell death, including
(a) apoptosis, a gene-regulated active form of cell death with
rapid onset; (b) necrosis, an uncontrolled and inflammato-
ry  cell  death,  potentially  secondary  to  extensive  apoptosis
overwhelming the phagocytes; (c) senescence, in which cells
lose  their  proliferative  capacity  but  remain  metabolically
active; and (d) mitotic catastrophe, characterized by inade-
quate  progression  through  the  division  phase  with  under-
repaired DNA, resulting in cells with chromosomal aberra-
tions (45–48).

Which of these cell death pathways predominates depends
on the radiation dose and tumor characteristics, including the
cell cycle phase, microenvironment, and tissue of origin. Im-
mediate cell death through apoptosis or necrosis may occur
in  highly  radiosensitive  tumors  or  at  higher  ablative  doses.
In  solid  tumors,  mitotic  catastrophe  is  the  predominant  re-
sponse to clinical radiation therapy, often leading to delayed
cell death over weeks to months (48). During this period, af-
fected cells remain metabolically active while in cell cycle ar-
rest, contributing to persistent imaging findings.

Treatment Methods
Historically, the role of radiation therapy in the treatment of
HCC  was  limited  due  to  hepatic  radiosensitivity  and  the  as-
sociated risk of radiation-induced liver disease. The develop-
ment of more efficient delivery methods, such as stereotactic
body radiation therapy (SBRT), along with selective approach-
es for transarterial radioembolization in the form of radiation
segmentectomy, has enabled high tumoral doses while mini-
mizing radiation exposure in the surrounding tissues (49,50).
SBRT  uses  3D  radiation  planning  to  deliver  ablative  dos-
es  of  radiation  to  the  targeted  tumor  (Fig  10).  With  imaging
guidance, the clinical target volume is defined by extending
the gross tumor volume with a 5–10-mm margin to account
for  possible  subclinical  disease.  The  planned  target  volume
is then expanded to accommodate variations in size and po-
sition throughout different respiratory phases. Treatment is
delivered  through  multiple  precise  beams  from  diverse  an-
gles to create steep dose gradients in the target volume. De-
spite the rapid dose decrease, surrounding tissues receive in-
termediate to low doses of radiation, which limits its use for
tumors  near  gastrointestinal  structures  or  in  patients  with
liver dysfunction (3,49).

Transarterial radioembolization involves targeted delivery
of microspheres loaded with radioactive 90Y through selective
catheterization of tumor-feeding hepatic arteries (Fig 11). The
microspheres lodge in the distal circulation, providing local-
ized  radiation  within  a  1–10-mm  penetration  depth.  Treat-
ment planning involves angiography with cone-beam CT, and
often  technetium  99m  (99mTc)  SPECT/CT  to  assess  the  lung
shunt fraction and the regional dose distribution (51,52).

and  the  hepatic  reserve.  For  extensive  disease,  lobar  or  se-
quential bilobar approaches aim to balance effective tumor-

include new or increased restricted diffusion or mild-to-mod-

Radiation-induced Parenchymal Changes
Radiation-based therapies allow delivery of most of the radia-
tion dose to the targeted tumor, but some off-target exposure
to  the  surrounding  parenchyma  is  inevitable.  The  extent  of
the  parenchyma  affected  varies  according  to  the  treatment
modality,  with  SBRT  causing  peritumoral  changes  based  on
the target volume, while radiation segmentectomy produces a
wedge-shaped vascular pattern (67,68). Parenchymal chang-
es arise from radiation-induced apoptosis of endothelial cells,
leading to sinusoidal obstruction syndrome (69,70).

In the acute phase (1–3 months after treatment), accumu-
lated cellular debris, red blood cells, and fibrin obstruct sinu-
soidal  flow,  causing  congestion  and  central  venule  obstruc-
tion. Disrupted blood flow leads to ischemia and necrosis of
centrilobular hepatocytes.

These  pathologic  changes  manifest  at  imaging  as  geo-
graphic  areas  of  hypoattenuation  on  precontrast  CT  imag-
es and increased signal intensity on both T2-weighted and
diffusion-weighted  MR  images  (71,72).  The  increased  sig-
nal  intensity  on  diffusion-weighted  MR  images,  with  cor-
responding  apparent  diffusion  coefficient  hyperintensity,
indicates T2-weighted “shine-through” rather than true dif-

fusion restriction (Fig S4). Postcontrast MRI shows compen-
satory  arterial  phase  hyperenhancement  that  may  persist
or fade during the portal and delayed phases (67,69–71) (Fig
14A–14C). Relative portal hypoenhancement may occur due
to impaired flow of contrast material from increased sinu-
soidal pressure. In addition, hepatocyte damage from isch-
emia reduces the uptake of hepatobiliary contrast material,
manifesting  as  hypointensity  on  hepatobiliary  phase  MR
images (47,67,69) (Fig S5).

During  the  subacute  (3–6  months)  and  chronic  (>6
months)  phases,  reparative  processes  lead  to  progressive  fi-
brosis of the irradiated parenchyma. Collagen deposition by
activated myofibroblasts in the perisinusoidal and perivenu-
lar  spaces  leads  to  increasing  centrilobular  obstruction  and
eventual replacement by fibrous tissue.

These changes are reflected at imaging as a gradual reduc-
tion in arterial phase hyperenhancement, while delayed phase
enhancement progressively increases due to retention of con-
trast material in fibrotic tissue (68) (Fig 14D–14F). The affected
parenchyma  demonstrates  a  progressive  reduction  in  signal
intensity  on  both  T2-weighted  and  diffusion-weighted  MR
images, with T2-weighted images in some patients displaying

For treated tumors categorized as LR-TR viable, LR-TR equivo-
cal, or LR-TR nonprogressing, the lesion size should be docu-
mented in millimeters in the radiologic report to facilitate ap-
propriate clinical decision making. Size measurement should
represent  the  longest  dimension  of  masslike  enhancement
in or along the lesion margin, excluding intervening nonen-
hancing portions and benign findings such as rim enhance-
ment (10) (Fig 15).

Lesions  can  be  measured  in  any  standard  orthogonal
imaging  plane  and  during  any  dynamic  imaging  phase  that
clearly delineates the lesion. However, the axial plane is rec-
ommended  to  ensure  consistency  in  subsequent  follow-up
examinations.  In  challenging  cases  such  as  HCC  with  a  dif-
fuse growth pattern, the margins on postcontrast MR images
can be obscured by perfusional changes or poorly discerned
due to isoenhancement with the background liver. For these
lesions,  correlation  with  images  from  other  sequences  such

Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them
Evaluation  of  TR  can  be  challenging  as  tumoral  changes,
parenchymal  alterations,  and  technical  limitations  may
impede  identification  of  viable  disease.  Misinterpretation
of  imaging  findings  can  substantially  affect  patient  care
through  inappropriate  treatment  decisions  or  delayed  in-
terventions. Radiologists must recognize common imaging
pitfalls in posttreatment surveillance to ensure accurate as-
sessment of TR. Table 2 summarizes the key imaging pitfalls
of  the  LI-RADS  TR  assessment  algorithm,  why  they  occur,
and how to avoid them.

Detection of masslike enhancement on dynamic images is
the primary goal in TR assessment. Most HCCs develop high
arterial  flow  due  to  angiogenesis  and  demonstrate  peak  en-
hancement during the late arterial phase, with some lesions
displaying enhancement exclusively during this window (10).

Suboptimal acquisition or lack of acquisition during the early
arterial phase may diminish the conspicuity of arterial phase
hyperenhancement,  limiting  detection  of  residual  disease
and appropriate characterization of untreated HCC during TR
assessment (10,12,13) (Fig 19).

Treatment-induced  perfusional  alterations  can  simulate
enhancement  related  to  viable  tumor,  potentially  resulting
in  misdiagnosis  and  inappropriate  retreatment.  These  per-
fusional alterations appear as wedge-shaped and geographic
areas  of  arterial  phase  hyperenhancement  that  diminish  on
portal venous and delayed phase images (20–22,69,78). Addi-
tional features include isointensity or occasionally mild hyper-
intensity on T2-weighted MR images due to edema. Areas of
perfusional enhancement lack masslike features characteris-
tic of viable tumors, showing preserved background architec-
ture,  with  vessels  coursing  through  the  region  unperturbed.

maintain  these  characteristics  in  8%–10%  and  13%–43%  of
cases,  respectively  (29,38,39).  To  maintain  specificity  when
detecting  viable  tumor,  ancillary  features  must  be  precisely

colocalized to the area of equivocal enhancement after non-
radiation-based treatments and to stable or decreasing mass-
like enhancement after radiation-based treatments.

When evaluating diffusion restriction, radiologists should
recognize  benign  causes  of  this  finding  that  mimic  residual
disease.  Intralesional  hemorrhage  and  liquefactive  necrosis
in  the  treated  area  can  produce  diffusion  restriction  in  the
absence  of  viable  tumor  (Fig  S6D,  S6E).  T2-weighted  shine-
through represents another source of false-positive diffusion
restriction  because  edematous  tissue  appears  hyperintense
on diffusion-weighted MR images (13,80). This effect is more
pronounced with lower b values (<800 sec/mm2) and can be
differentiated from true restriction by visualizing maintained
hyperintensity  on  the  corresponding  apparent  diffusion  co-
efficient map.

In  radiation-based  treatments,  prolonged  intervals  ex-
ceeding 6–8 weeks between baseline imaging and treatment
can limit response assessment. Undocumented tumor growth
during this period can manifest as apparent enlargement on
early  posttreatment  images,  leading  to  incorrect  categoriza-
tion as LR-TR viable (40,81). In these cases,the LR-TR nonpro-
gressing category should be assigned to allow additional time
for a clearer evaluation of tumor response.

ated from a new tumor adjacent to a treated lesion?
A residual or recurrent tumor manifests as an area of mass-
like enhancement in the lesion or at its margin. Conversely, a
new tumor is seen separate from the treated lesion.

Does LR-TR nonviable exclude microscopic viability?
No, LR-TR nonviable indicates a low likelihood of clinically sig-
nificant viable tumor tissue, but imaging cannot exclude mi-
croscopic foci of viable cells. Current evidence demonstrates
that the LR-TR nonviable criteria accurately predict complete
tumor necrosis in pathologic studies, with negative predictive
values  of  81%–93%  (28,30,39).  However,  radiologic  detection
requires a minimal tumor volume for reliable assessment. A
study published in 2022 (29) demonstrated 35% sensitivity for
the  LR-TR  viable  or  equivocal  category  in  detecting  tumors
smaller than 10 mm, compared with 80% for tumors measur-
ing at least 10 mm.

Conclusion
Multiple  locoregional  therapies  are  available  for  HCC,  with
imaging findings specific to each modality. The updated 2024
CT and MRI LI-RADS TR assessment criteria provided distinct
algorithms for use after nonradiation or radiation-based ther-
apies, simplified the definition of viable disease, and incorpo-
rated  MRI  ancillary  features  to  enhance  detection  of  tumor
viability. These updated criteria can improve diagnostic accu-
racy, resulting in more effective clinical management of HCC.
