Low Rectal Cancers at Initial Staging MRI

Matthew H. Lee, MD • David H. Kim, MD

Low rectal cancers, which are associated with
increased risk of local recurrent disease and
poorer prognosis, have unique anatomic con-
siderations and issues for staging and treat-
ment that do not apply to mid and high rectal
cancers. Although tumor histology help drive
the staging and treatment of all rectal cancers,
it is of particular importance in low rectal can-
cers, which may involve the anal canal, to help
establish whether a low rectal mass should be
staged and treated as a rectal cancer (ie, ade-
nocarcinoma) or an anal cancer (ie, squamous
cell carcinoma). Optimal staging and treat-
ment of rectal cancer are contingent on tumor
location and local extent, which help guide
management decisions including neoadjuvant
therapy and curative surgical treatment strat-
egies. Tumor location in the low rectum and
local involvement of the anal canal, sphinc-
ter, and pelvic floor help determine whether
a patient can undergo sphincter-preserving
resection such as a low anterior resection
versus abdominoperineal resection to achieve
negative surgical margins. Issues exist related
to the anatomy and patterns of disease spread
that are unique to the low rectum and include
how to determine and stage anal sphincter in-
volvement, mesorectal fascia status at the pel-
vic floor, and nodal status of extramesorectal
nodes such as the external iliac and inguinal
lymph nodes. For these reasons, it is impera-
tive that radiologists who interpret rectal can-
cer staging MRI examinations feel comfortable
with the unique anatomy of the low rectum
and anal canal, nuances of low rectal cancer
local disease spread, and treatment paradigms
for low rectal cancer.

TEACHING POINTS
	 A foundational concept for rectal cancer is that tumor histology determines
whether a tumor is staged and treated as a rectal or an anal cancer, which is
of particular importance for low rectal cancers that can have anal involvement.
If the tumor is an adenocarcinoma, it is staged and treated as a rectal cancer,
whereas if the tumor is a squamous cell carcinoma, it is staged and treated
as an anal cancer.

	 For low rectal cancers, the essential surgical margins are the CRM or the radial
margin of the specimen, which is determined at MRI by the closest distance of
tumor to the MRF, and the DRM, which is determined at MRI by the anorectal
junction to tumor distance.

	 Currently, the Society of Abdominal Radiology Colorectal and Anal Cancer
Disease-Focused Panel recommends assigning a T stage on the basis of the
rectal component and reporting anal involvement with a specific description
of the extent of sphincter involvement (location and length), and specifically if
the tumor involves the IAS, ISS, or EAS. For low rectal cancers involving only
the sphincter without a distal rectal component, no formal T stage is assigned,
but rather a precise description of the components of the sphincter involved.
	 The status of MRF involvement and predicted CRM for low rectal cancers at
the pelvic floor is determined by whether the tumor is contained by the mus-
cularis propria (ie, T1 or T2 tumor) or extends beyond it (ie, T3 or T4 tumor).
	 The important caveat for nodal staging of low cancers that involve the anal
canal is that because of lymphatics below the dentate line that drain directly
to the external iliac and inguinal groups, lymph nodes in these distributions
should be considered N+ and not M+ disease.

Introduction
Colorectal  cancer  (CRC)  is  the  third  most  common  cancer
in men and women and the second most common cause of
death due to cancer in the United States, with an estimated
46 050  new  cases  of  rectal  cancer  in  2023  (1).  Decreases  in
the overall CRC incidence and mortality rates in recent de-
cades are attributable to a combination of improved cancer
prevention  efforts  and  screening,  early  diagnosis,  and  im-
proved  treatment  modalities  (2).  However,  CRC  incidence
and  mortality  rates  have  increased  in  younger  adults  with
an increased incidence of 1%–2% per year in patients under
50  years  of  age  since  the  mid  1990s  and  increased  mortal-
ity rates of 1.2% per year in patients under 50 years of age
from  2005  to  2020  (1).  Rectal  cancer  is  largely  driving  this
early-onset trend in CRC with a near doubling of newly di-
agnosed rectal cancer in patients under 55 years of age from
1995 to 2019 (15%–28%) and a 2% per year increase in rectal
cancer  incidence  in  patients  under  50  years  of  age  during
the  past  decade  (3).  In  2023,  rectal  cancer  accounts  for  an
estimated 37% of new CRC cases in patients under 50 years
of age (3).

Rectal  MRI  has  emerged  as  the  preferred  imaging  mo-
dality for locoregional clinical staging of rectal cancer (4,5).

Optimal treatment planning for low rectal cancer involves
a multidisciplinary team approach. The team includes mem-
bers who have expertise in surgery, medical oncology, radi-
ation oncology, radiology, and pathology (5). Staging MRI is
critical  in  determining  the  treatment  strategy  on  the  basis
of  prognostic  imaging  features  such  as  tumor  category  (ie,
T2 vs T3) and predicted CRM, which have implications for
local  recurrence,  disease-free  survival,  and  overall  survival
(6). The MERCURY II trial validated the use of MRI for as-
sessment of low rectal cancers and predicted CRM, allowing
for improved risk stratification and a more tailored approach
to treatment, including options for sphincter-preserving re-
section versus more extensive resection with permanent co-
lostomy (7).

We  address  issues  that  are  unique  to  low  rectal  cancers
that are not germane to mid or high tumors at initial rectal
cancer staging with rectal MRI. We present the key concepts
of rectal cancer classification, important anatomic consider-
ations for low rectal cancers that involve the pelvic floor and
anal sphincter, and fundamental principles of treatment and
staging for low cancers, with special attention to issues that
have ongoing debate and controversy.

Tumor Histopathologic Features
and Classification
A foundational concept for rectal cancer is that tumor his-
tology determines whether a tumor is staged and treated as

a rectal or an anal cancer, which is of particular importance
for low rectal cancers that can have anal involvement. If the
tumor  is  an  adenocarcinoma,  it  is  staged  and  treated  as  a
rectal  cancer  (Fig  2),  whereas  if  the  tumor  is  a  squamous
cell  carcinoma,  it  is  staged  and  treated  as  an  anal  cancer
(Fig 3). The determination is based solely on tumor histol-
ogy  irrespective  of  the  location  of  predominant  tumor  in-
volvement. No specific imaging features (eg, location, size,
or  signal  intensity)  can  reliably  help  distinguish  between
rectal adenocarcinoma and anal squamous cell carcinoma.
The  importance  of  confirming  tumor  histology  cannot  be
overstated, as rectal and anal cancer have different staging
and treatment strategies. For example, the T stage for rec-
tal cancer is determined by the depth of tumor penetration,
whereas  the  T  stage  for  anal  cancer  is  determined  by  the
tumor size. Additionally, curative surgical resection is a cor-
nerstone  for  the  treatment  of  patients  with  nonmetastatic
rectal cancer in combination with neoadjuvant or adjuvant
chemotherapy and radiation therapy, whereas most patients
with anal cancer are treated with chemotherapy and radia-
tion therapy without surgery (12).

Rectal  cancers  can  be  classified  into  “high,”  “mid,”  and
“low” cancers on the basis of the anal verge distance (Fig 4).
The anal verge is the terminus of the anal canal, which is a
band  of  nonkeratinized  squamous  epithelium  without  hair
follicles that extends from the intersphincteric groove to the
adjacent perianal skin (13). Clinically, the anal verge distance
is best measured with a rigid proctoscope that can be used to
help directly visualize the tumor (Fig 5) and determine the

Anatomic Considerations for
Low Rectal Cancer
Evaluation of low rectal cancers at staging MRI requires a con-
fident understanding of the anatomy of the rectum, pelvic floor,
and anal canal and sphincter complex. Knowledge of this anat-
omy is essential for MRI staging and can improve interpretive
precision because accurate delineation of low rectal cancers and
their relationship to anatomic structures, such as the rectal wall,
pelvic floor, and anal sphincter, has important implications for
treatment and prognosis.

Rectum
The rectum is variable in length but is commonly defined as the
15 cm of large bowel above the anal verge (5,16) and subdivided
into thirds (upper, mid, and lower) on the basis of the anal verge
distance. The National Comprehensive Cancer Network defines
the rectum at imaging as the distal large bowel below a virtual
line drawn from the sacral promontory to the upper margin of
the pubic symphysis on sagittal MR images (2). More recently,
the “sigmoid takeoff” has been identified by expert consensus
as a preferred anatomic landmark at imaging for identifying the
rectosigmoid  junction  and  distinguishing  rectal  from  sigmoid
cancers (17,18). At MRI, the sigmoid takeoff is identified as an

The mesorectum is the fat tissue “cushion” that surrounds
the  rectum  and  contains  perirectal  fat,  lymph  nodes,  vas-

T2-hypointense  line  surrounding  the  mesorectum  (Fig  7).
The loose areolar tissue plane between the facsia propria of
the rectum and the presacral fascia forms the “holy plane”
of  dissection  during  total  mesorectal  excision  (TME)  (20).

Anal Canal
The  anal  canal  is  formed  proximally  as  the  rectum  extends
through the pelvic hiatus at the level of the puborectalis muscle
(Fig 8) (23). The anorectal junction can be identified at MRI by
two  landmarks:  the  U-shaped  puborectalis  muscle  that  loops
around the posterior margin of the anorectal junction and in-
serts along the pubic symphysis and the thickened inner mus-
cular layer that corresponds to the IAS (Figs 4, 8, 9) (19). The
puborectalis creates an angle that helps to identify the anorec-
tal junction. Clinically, the puborectalis muscle is the anorectal
ring at digital rectal examination that demarcates the superior
margin of the anal sphincter. Along with the anorectal junc-
tion to tumor measurement at MRI (Fig 10), the anorectal ring
helps predict the DRM if the sphincter were spared.

In total, the puborectalis muscle, IAS, and external anal
sphincter  (EAS)  form  the  “surgical”  anal  canal,  which  ex-
tends  from  the  anorectal  ring  superiorly  to  the  anal  verge
inferiorly. The “anatomic” anal canal, on the other hand, ex-

tends  from  the  dentate  line,  which  is  the  division  between
the  embryonic  endoderm  and  ectoderm  superiorly,  to  the
anal  verge  inferiorly.  The  dentate  line  is  not  consistently
identified at MRI (16) but is near the junction of the upper
and middle third of the anal canal and can be estimated to be

 2.5 cm above the anal verge (24,25).

Anal Sphincter Complex
The muscular anal canal assumes a double-layered cylindri-
cal  tube-in-tube  configuration.  The  inner  layer  comprises
the IAS and conjoined longitudinal muscle (CLM), and the
external  layer  comprises  the  EAS  and  puborectalis  muscle
(21). The IAS is a downward continuation of the thickened
circular layer of the rectal muscularis propria. Longitudinal
fibers of the rectal muscularis propria extend inferiorly from
the  anorectal  ring  where  they  join  fibers  of  the  puborecta-
lis muscle to form the CLM (23,26). The CLM is interposed
between the IAS and EAS in the ISS. At MRI, the IAS tends
to have homogeneous intermediate gray signal intensity on
T2-weighted images, whereas the EAS is more hypointense
and striated in comparison. The CLM is intermittently seen
as  a  thin  band  closely  apposed  to  the  lateral  border  of  the
IAS (Figs 8, 9).

The EAS is composed of striated muscle that surrounds
the  IAS  and  CLM.  The  EAS  is  commonly  subdivided  into
a proximal deep portion, mid superficial portion, and distal
subcutaneous portion (Fig 9). The deep portion is in conti-
nuity with the puborectalis muscle, and the superficial por-
tion attaches posteriorly to the coccyx via the anococcygeal

Lymphatic System
Lymphatic drainage of the rectum and anal canal differs by
location, and the anterior peritoneal reflection is an import-
ant landmark, delineating the different patterns of lymphatic
drainage (27). The majority of lymphatic drainage above the

level of the anterior peritoneal reflection flows in a superior
direction along the mesorectal and superior rectal lymphatics
that parallel branches of the superior rectal artery and infe-
rior mesenteric artery extending to its origin from the aorta.
Lymphatics  from  the  upper  two-thirds  of  the  rectum  follow
this  dominant  drainage  pattern  into  the  inferior  mesenteric
lymph nodes (Fig 11).

Although  the  majority  of  lymphatic  flow  from  the  lower
third of the rectum below the anterior peritoneal reflection is
via the aforementioned mesorectal pathway, lymphatic drain-
age can also occur laterally along the middle rectal artery and

Treatment Paradigms for Low Rectal Cancers
Treatment  of  rectal  cancer  involves  a  multidisciplinary  ap-
proach, and accurate clinical staging with MRI is imperative
to help inform radiologists on treatment options, particularly
for locally advanced rectal cancer. Although the nuances of
neoadjuvant  and  adjuvant  treatment  strategies  are  beyond
the  scope  of  this  article,  it  is  important  to  understand  the
principal branch points in the decision tree for low rectal can-
cer treatment that can be determined at initial staging MRI.
Establishing  the  presence  of  locally  advanced  rectal  cancer
defined as stage II (ie, T3 or T4, node negative) or stage III (ie,
node-positive disease without distant metastases) nonmeta-
static  disease  is  imperative,  as  these  patients  will  likely  re-
quire a combination of neoadjuvant or adjuvant chemother-
apy, radiation therapy, and surgery (2). In the United States,
total  neoadjuvant  therapy,  which  includes  neoadjuvant
chemotherapy  and  chemoradiation  followed  by  resection,
is increasingly preferred for stage II and stage III cancers to

minimize  the  risk  of  locoregional  recurrence  and  theoreti-
cally eradicate micrometastatic disease at the patient’s initial
presentation.  Neoadjuvant  therapy  with  tumor  regression
has enabled sphincter-preserving surgical approaches as well
as watch-and-wait strategies, which avoid initial resection al-
together (28).

Surgical Strategies for Low Rectal Cancer
There  are  two  general  categories  for  surgical  treatment  of
low  rectal  cancer  depending  on  tumor  location,  histologic
characteristics,  and  extent  of  disease:  (a)  organ-preserving
local  excision  techniques  (eg,  transanal  excision,  transanal
endoscopic  microsurgery,  and  transanal  minimally  invasive
surgery) for early-stage T1 cancers and (b) radical resections
with TME for locally advanced tumors, namely low anterior
resection  (LAR),  ultra-LAR,  and  abdominoperineal  resec-
tion (APR). A summary of surgical treatment paradigms for
locally  advanced  low  rectal  cancers  is  shown  in  Figure  12.
Local excision for T1 cancers, which are typically evaluated
with transrectal US, are not addressed herein.

APR and Variants
Traditional  (extrasphincteric)  APR  is  performed  when  a
negative DRM with preservation of the anal sphincter is un-
likely. It involves removal of the rectum and distal colon, me-
sorectum, anal canal, and anus with permanent end colos-
tomy (Fig 12) (33). Extralevator abdominoperineal excision
is  a  variant  of  APR  that  involves  more  extensive  resection
that potentially decreases the risk of positive resection mar-
gins for locally advanced low cancers but at the expense of
increased morbidity (33–35). Although TME, smaller DRMs,
and  the  capability  to  perform  low  colorectal  and  coloanal
anastomoses have contributed to decreasing APR rates over
time (33), APR remains the procedure of choice for patients
with T3  or T4  tumors  that  involve  the  anal  canal,  external
sphincter, or levator ani musculature (31).

Sphincter-Preserving Treatments
An inherent challenge to low rectal cancer treatment is the
balance  between  patient  desire  for  sphincter-preserving
continent  reconstruction  and  optimal  oncologic  resection
(36).  In  appropriately  selected  patients,  adverse  oncologic
outcomes  are  not  observed  with  sphincter-preserving  tech-
niques, although functional outcomes (eg, fecal continence,
frequent defecation) vary (36,37). For this reason, sphincter
preservation  is  contingent  on  the  ability  to  achieve  an  R0
resection and adequate anal function (5,38).

LAR can be applied to low rectal tumors if there is enough
distance  between  the  inferior  edge  of  tumor  and  sphincter
to yield a negative DRM. The anal sphincter remains intact
with the creation of a low (colorectal) or ultra-low (coloanal
or  intersphincteric)  anastomosis  (Fig  12).  Intersphincteric
resection  is  a  sphincter-preserving  alternative  in  patients
with T1–T3 low rectal cancers (3.0–3.5 cm of the anal verge)
with or without IAS involvement (Fig 14) (39), which allows

specimen,  which  is  determined  at  MRI  by  the  closest  dis-
tance  of  tumor  to  the  MRF,  and  the  DRM,  which  is  deter-
mined  at  MRI  by  the  anorectal  junction  to  tumor  distance
(Figs 1, 12).

TME addresses radial tumor spread and is a guiding prin-
ciple for optimal oncologic resection for rectal cancer (30).
TME involves en bloc resection of rectal cancer as a “tumor
package” along with the mesorectum, draining lymphatics,
and tumor deposits along the holy plane between the visceral
MRF and parietal pelvic fascia, diminishing the potential for
local  recurrence  (30)  while  also  enabling  extension  of  the
dissection into the ISS and creation of very low anastomoses
(31). The DRM is typically not an issue for high and mid rec-
tal cancers in terms of obtaining negative margins above the
sphincters but is an area of concern for low cancers.

Important Staging Principles Specific to
Low Rectal Cancers
Rectal cancer is staged using the TNM staging system accord-
ing to the American Joint Committee on Cancer (AJCC) Can-
cer  Staging  Manual. We  present  unique  issues  and  nuances
for  low  rectal  cancer  related  to  staging  the  primary  tumor,
with an emphasis on T staging, resection margins (ie, CRM,
DRM), and nodal staging.

Tumor
For rectal cancer, T0 tumors are those with no visible tumor,
T1  tumors  involve  and  are  confined  to  the  submucosa,  T2
tumors extend through the submucosa and involve the mus-
cularis  propria,  T3  tumors  penetrate  through  the  muscu-
laris propria into the subserosa and perirectal tissue, and T4
tumors  penetrate  to  the  surface  of  the  visceral  peritoneum
(T4a) or invade or are adherent to other organs or structures
(T4b) (Fig 16). Distinguishing between T1 and T2 cancers at
MRI is difficult, and endoscopic rectal US should be consid-
ered  in  candidates  for  local  excision  when  this  determina-
tion is necessary (2,40). Recently, the submucosal enhancing
stripe, which is an uninterrupted enhancing band between
the  rectal  tumor  and  muscularis  propria  seen  on  contrast-
ed-enhanced  T1-weighted  MR  images,  has  been  identified
as  a  means  to  differentiate  T0  and  T1  tumors  from  T2  tu-
mors  (41).  However,  at  MRI  tumor  staging, T1  and T2  cat-
egories are often combined (eg, reported as T1/2 in the So-
ciety  of  Abdominal  Radiology  Colorectal  and  Anal  Cancer
Disease-Focused Panel reporting template) as the treatment
decision for neoadjuvant therapy or immediate resection is
contingent  on  whether  the  tumor  is  confined  to  the  bowel
wall (ie, contained by the muscularis propria and T1 or T2)
or extends beyond the muscularis propria (ie, T3 or T4) (14).
The T stage also holds prognostic value, as patients with stage
IIIa disease (ie, smaller tumors, positive regional lymph nodes;
T1 or T2, N1/N1c; T1, N2) have more favorable outcomes than
patients with stage II disease (ie, larger T3 or T4 tumors, no
regional lymphadenopathy) (42).

vic floor and anal sphincter. Low rectal cancers involving the
pelvic floor musculature constitute T4b tumors. Less certain,
however, is how to decide on the T stage for low rectal cancers
that involve the anal sphincter complex. The following ques-
tions should be considered in particular:

1. Should the anal sphincter be considered an adjacent “or-

gan” or “structure” and thus the tumor is T4b if involved?

2. Should the IAS be considered an inferior continuation of
the distal rectum (muscularis propria) and therefore the tumor
is T3 if involved?

3. Should the EAS be considered a continuation of the pel-
vic floor musculature or an adjacent structure and the tumor
is T4 if involved?

Indeed, there is uncertainty on how to treat low rectal can-
cers that involve the anal sphincter complex and the specific
definition of “organ” and “structure,” which confer T4b status,
as these terms are not specifically addressed in the AJCC stag-
ing manual.

A recent international multidisciplinary expert panel con-
sensus  recommended  categorizing  EAS  involvement  as  T4b
disease on the basis of skeletal muscle invasion and not taking
IAS and ISS involvement into account when classifying the T
stage (16,43), although there is no unanimity across societies.
Currently, the Society of Abdominal Radiology Colorectal and
Anal Cancer Disease-Focused Panel recommends assigning a
T stage on the basis of the rectal component and reporting anal
involvement with a specific description of the extent of sphinc-
ter  involvement  (location  and  length),  and  specifically  if  the
tumor involves the IAS, ISS (Fig 17), or EAS (Fig 18) (14). For
low rectal cancers involving only the sphincter without a dis-

tal rectal component, no formal T stage is assigned, but rather
a  precise  description  of  the  components  of  the  sphincter  in-
volved (14). Since the TNM system is driven by outcomes, it is
unclear whether the same anatomic thresholds are applicable
for the sphincter as seen for the rectal wall with mid and high
tumors.

The  European  Society  of  Gastrointestinal  and  Abdominal
Radiology  adopts  a  similar  descriptive  approach  and  recom-
mends  specifying  the  following:  (a)  tumor  involvement  of
the IAS, ISS, and EAS; (b) involvement of the proximal, mid,
or  lower  third  of  the  sphincter  complex;  and  (c)  pelvic  floor
involvement (44). The emphasis again is on the accurate de-
lineation  of  tumor  extent  and  specific  anal  involvement  to
inform  treatment  options.  It  is  of  utmost  importance  to  be
familiar with local institutional practice patterns and engage
with  members  of  the  multidisciplinary  team  to  ensure  clear
communication on a case-by-case basis.

MRF Status and Margins
At staging MRI, the MRF is the anatomic landmark for pre-
dicting  the  CRM  status  on  the  surgical  specimen  in  patients
undergoing TME. An MRF distance of 1 mm or less between
the  tumor  and  MRF  constitutes  an  involved  MRF,  which  in
turn predicts a positive CRM during surgery. An MRF distance
greater than 1 mm constitutes an uninvolved MRF. The MRF
measurement is made between the closest portion of primary
tumor, extramural vascular invasion, or tumor deposit to the
MRF. An enlarged mesorectal lymph node close to the MRF
is not included, as it typically does not lead to a positive CRM
and increased future local recurrence but should be described
for surgical planning (45). MRI is 77% sensitive and 94% spe-
cific for MRF involvement (46), and 5-year follow-up results
