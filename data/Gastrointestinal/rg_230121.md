Liver congestion is increasingly encountered in clinical practice and presents diagnostic pitfalls of which radiologists must
be aware. The complex altered hemodynamics associated with liver congestion leads to diffuse parenchymal changes and the
development of benign and malignant nodules. Distinguishing commonly encountered benign hypervascular lesions, such as
focal nodular hyperplasia (FNH)–like nodules, from hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) can be challenging due to overlapping
imaging features. FNH-like lesions enhance during the hepatic arterial phase and remain isoenhancing relative to the back-
ground liver parenchyma but infrequently appear to wash out at delayed phase imaging, similar to what might be seen with
HCC. Heterogeneity, presence of an enhancing capsule, washout during the portal venous phase, intermediate signal intensity
at T2-weighted imaging, restricted diffusion, and lack of uptake at hepatobiliary phase imaging point toward the diagnosis of
HCC, although these features are not sensitive individually. It is important to emphasize that the Liver Imaging Reporting and
Data System (LI-RADS) algorithm cannot be applied in congested livers since major LI-RADS features lack specificity in distin-
guishing HCC from benign hypervascular lesions in this population. Also, the morphologic changes and increased liver stiffness
caused by congestion make the imaging diagnosis of cirrhosis difficult. The authors discuss the complex liver macro- and mi-
crohemodynamics underlying liver congestion; propose a more inclusive approach to and conceptualization of liver congestion;
describe the pathophysiology of liver congestion, hepatocellular injury, and the development of benign and malignant nodules;
review the imaging findings and mimics of liver congestion and hypervascular lesions; and present a diagnostic algorithm for
approaching hypervascular liver lesions.

TEACHING POINTS
	 To distinguish the various causes of liver congestion, evaluation of the
hepatic veins is essential, and patency, size, and waveform morphology
should be carefully assessed.

	 The diagnosis of cirrhosis based on imaging alone in patients with con-
gestive hepatopathy should be made with caution to avoid the reper-
cussions of a false-positive diagnosis.

	 The delayed washout feature of some FNH-like nodules overlaps with
HCCs. As such, the LI-RADS algorithm should not be applied in this pop-
ulation. Distinguishing these lesions from HCC is challenging.

	 MRI  can  be  helpful  in  demonstrating  ancillary  features  such  as  inter-
mediate  signal  intensity  at  T2-weighted  imaging,  restricted  diffusion,
intralesional signal loss in fat-containing HCCs (steatohepatic and clear-
cell HCC subtypes), hypointensity in the HBP, and tumor in vein.

	 Due to the altered hemodynamics present in congestive hepatopathy,
distinguishing benign from malignant lesions at imaging remains chal-
lenging. The major features of HCC recognized by CT/MRI LI-RADS (arte-
rial phase hyperenhancement and washout) do not have high specificity
for diagnosis of HCC in patients with congestive hepatopathy. As such,
LI-RADS and Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network criteria
should not be applied in this population.

Introduction
Liver congestion is encountered with increasing frequency in
daily  radiology  practice  primarily  because  of  the  increasing
longevity of patients with heart disease due to improved surgi-
cal techniques and medical management (1–3). Altered hemo-
dynamics caused by impaired hepatic venous outflow in these
patients can lead to imaging pitfalls. Liver congestion and as-
sociated lesions mimic the imaging patterns standardly used
to diagnose fibrosis and hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). The
assessment  of  focal  lesions  in  patients  with  liver  congestion
can  be  particularly  challenging  given  that  the  Liver  Imaging
Reporting and Data System (LI-RADS) algorithm cannot be ap-
plied in these patients (4).

Congestive hepatopathy has been used in the literature to
refer  to  cardiac  causes  of  liver  congestion  (5).  However,  it  is
important to recognize that there are additional causes of liv-
er  congestion,  including  hepatic  venous  thrombosis  (Budd-
Chiari syndrome [BCS]) and sinusoidal obstruction syndrome
(SOS; previously called hepatic veno-occlusive disease) that re-
sult in impaired venous outflow from the liver. These disease
processes  have  a  similar  pattern  of  parenchymal  injury  and
histopathologic  sequelae  and  overlapping  imaging  features
(5). Thus, we propose a more comprehensive approach to con-
gestive  hepatopathy  rooted  in  pathophysiologic  findings  and
sequelae in the liver rather than in a single congestive cardiac
cause.  All  causes  of  impaired  hepatic  venous  outflow  lead  to
altered hepatic hemodynamics, myriad types of liver regenera-
tion, a shared pattern of congestive fibrosis, and HCC.

This article reviews the imaging findings and pitfalls of liv-
er  congestion,  the  underlying  pathophysiology  that  leads  to
these  findings,  and  the  radiologic-pathologic  correlation.  We
also present a diagnostic algorithm for radiologists to use when
approaching challenging arterially enhancing observations to
avoid the untoward consequences of diagnostic pitfalls.

Vascular Anatomy of the Liver

Inflow
The two sources of blood to the liver are the portal vein and
the hepatic artery, which supply a total blood volume of ap-
proximately  25%  of  the  cardiac  output  (Fig  1)  (6).  The  stan-
dard anatomy consists of the portal vein and  hepatic artery
entering the liver at the porta hepatis and dividing into right

which supply the hepatocytes. There are three potential com-

role in maintaining perfusional balance to the hepatocytes in
vascular disorders: trans-sinusoidal, transversal (via the vasa
vasorum of the portal vein), and transplexal (via the peribili-

Within  the  hepatic  lobule,  hepatocytes  are  arranged  in
cords along the sinusoids (12). The metabolic differentiation
and function of these hepatocytes vary according to their rel-
ative  proximity  to  the  hepatic  artery  and  portal  vein,  which
affects their exposure to oxygen and metabolites. Zone 1 he-
patocytes are nearest the portal vein, zone 3 hepatocytes are
nearest the central vein (ie, centrilobular), and zone 2 hepato-
cytes  are  in  between  zones  1  and  3  (11,13).  The  hepatocytes
in periarterial zone 1 perform oxygen-demanding metabolic
functions  (eg,  gluconeogenesis,  ureagenesis,  and  β-oxida-
tion), whereas those in perivenular zone 3 perform functions
that require less oxygen (eg, glycolysis, lipo- and ketogenesis,
triglyceride synthesis, and alcohol metabolism).

Outflow
From  the  sinusoids,  blood  flows  down  a  pressure  gradient
away from the liver via the central venules and hepatic veins
to the inferior vena cava (IVC) and finally to the right atrium
of the heart.

Pathophysiology and Causes

Pathophysiology
All  of  the  causes  of  liver  congestion  result  from  impaired
drainage of blood from the liver, both passive and obstructive
(Fig 4). The microhemodynamics at the level of the sinusoids
that result from alterations in the balance of inflow between
the hepatic artery and portal vein due to impaired blood out-
flow impact the microenvironment within the hepatic lobule
and the zonally distributed hepatocytes.

Sinusoidal Circulation in the Setting of Congestive
Hepatopathy
When  hepatic  venous  drainage  is  impaired,  pressure  builds
within  the  central  hepatic  veins  and  is  transmitted  back  to
the hepatic sinusoids. In contrast to capillaries, sinusoids lack
connective tissue. Thus, prolonged elevated venous and sinu-
soidal  pressures  cause  perisinusoidal  edema,  hemorrhage,
and  thrombosis.  Zone  3  around  the  central  vein  is  the  first
affected.  Subsequent  centrilobular  perisinusoidal  ischemia
and hepatocyte atrophy is followed by fibrosis (14). Ultimate-
ly, bridging fibrous septa develop between the central veins, a
pattern characteristic of congestive hepatopathy and distinct
from other noncongestive causes of cirrhosis (14) (Fig 5). The
fibrotic bands coalesce, dividing the parenchyma into round-
ed areas of relatively preserved zone 1 periportal hepatocytes
surrounded  by  atrophic  and  fibrotic  zone  3  perivenular  re-
gions. These periportal zone 1 nodular areas of parenchyma
are more commonly known as regenerative nodules (11).

The portal vein supplies approximately 75% of the efferent
blood flow in the form of partially deoxygenated nutrient-rich
blood  from  the  gastrointestinal  tract,  spleen,  and  pancreas.
The hepatic artery supplies the remainder of the blood inflow
in the form of oxygen-rich blood from the aorta—a key balanc-
ing feature for maintaining hepatic perfusion. In situations of
decreased portal venous inflow, the blood flow in the hepatic
artery increases via the hepatic arterial buffer response, which
can compensate for up to a 60% decrease in portal flow (6). In
contrast, the portal vein cannot significantly compensate for
alterations in the arterial flow (Fig 2).

A small amount of inflow to the liver occurs via small ab-
errant  veins  that  drain  directly  from  adjacent  organs  (eg,
gallbladder and stomach) into the liver, also known as third
inflow  (7).  These  veins  communicate  with  the  intrahepatic
portal venules and hepatic arterioles and lead to local alter-
ations in the microvascular environment (8), often appearing
on  images  as  variant  enhancement  or  focal  fatty  sparing  or
deposition.

Cause of Liver Congestion
The  cause  of  liver  congestion  can  be  divided  into  three
broad categories based on the level of primary vascular im-
pairment  relative  to  the  hepatic  veins:  supravenous,  intra-
venous,  or  infravenous  causes  of  impaired  hepatic  venous
outflow (Table).

Supravenous causes of liver congestion are primarily car-
diogenic,  leading  to  increased  right-sided  pressure  in  the
heart and passive impairment of venous drainage. These are
the  most  common  causes  of  congestive  hepatopathy,  tradi-
tionally  referred  to  by  the  term  congestive  hepatopathy.  They
include  intracardiac  myocardial  and  valvular  causes  (eg,
cardiomyopathies,  both  ischemic  and  nonischemic;  chronic

Intravenous  causes  of  liver  congestion  refer  to  those  that
cause obstruction of the hepatic veins, primarily BCS, which
is  characterized  by  any  complete  or  partial  hepatic  venous
outflow  obstruction.  The  most  common  causes  of  BCS  are
prothrombotic  conditions  (including  both  acquired  and  in-
herited,  such  as  myeloproliferative  neoplasms,  essential
thrombocythemia,  factor  V  Leiden  mutation,  paroxysmal
nocturnal  hemoglobinuria,  and  JAK2  V617F  mutation)  (18).
Secondary BCS is the term applied to extrinsic compression of
the hepatic veins or IVC by tumor or other space-occupying
conditions, which leads to the same pathophysiologic result.

Lastly, infravenous causes of liver congestion refer to those
that  cause  obstruction  below  the  level  of  the  hepatic  veins,

that  is,  at  the  level  of  the  sinusoids.  SOS  occurs  when  sinu-
soidal endothelial cells slough off, resulting in sinusoidal ob-
struction (19) (Figs 5, S1). Injury to the sinusoidal endothelial
cells  is  usually  the  result  of  toxic  effects,  such  as  from  che-
motherapy  agents  (frequently  high-dose  agents  required  in
patients with bone marrow transplant, alkylating agents such
as cyclophosphamide, and platinum complexes such as oxal-
iplatin) and environmental ingestion (eg, pyrrolizidine alka-
loids,  which  are  plant  toxins  found  in  tea,  herbal  infusions,
and food supplements) (20).

Imaging Workup, Findings, and
Radiologic-Pathologic Correlation

Diffuse Findings

Ultrasonography.—US  is  often  the  first  imaging  modality
performed  to  help  identify  liver  congestion.  Because  of  its
accessibility  and  lack  of  ionizing  radiation,  US  is  typically
the first step in the workup of abnormal liver function, right
upper  quadrant  pain,  or  a  palpable  abnormality,  as  well  as
screening for cirrhosis (via elastography) or HCC in high-risk
patients (21).

All  causes  of  liver  congestion  lead  to  elevated  central  ve-
nous pressure. At US this manifests as increased portal vein
phasicity (Fig S2A) and, with increasing severity, evidence of
volume overload such as perihepatic ascites, gallbladder wall
edema, periportal edema, and anasarca (Fig S2B) (22). To dis-
tinguish  the  various  causes  of  liver  congestion,  evaluation  of
the hepatic veins is essential, and patency, size, and waveform
morphology should be carefully assessed.

In  supravenous  causes  of  liver  congestion,  which  are
primarily  cardiogenic,  standard  gray-scale  US  evaluation
demonstrates dilated IVC and hepatic veins, with the litera-
ture reporting the normal caliber of the middle hepatic vein
to  be  approximately  6  mm  and  greater  than  8–10  mm  to  be
enlarged  (22,23).  Evaluation  of  spectral  Doppler  US  images
demonstrates  aberrations  in  the  expected  triphasic  hepatic
venous waveform due to altered transmitted pulsatility and
flow from the heart (24). These variations in phasicity will de-
pend on the underlying cardiac cause (eg, tricuspid regurgita-
tion versus pericarditis versus Fontan physiology) and should
be  evaluated  carefully.  For  example,  tricuspid  regurgitation
leads to altered relationships between the ventricular systolic
and diastolic segments of the hepatic venous waveform as the
severity of the regurgitant jet worsens (Fig 6).

In contrast, intra- and infrahepatic venous causes of liver
congestion have small to normal-sized hepatic veins on gray-
scale US images and dampened hepatic venous waveforms in
the affected veins on spectral Doppler US images (Fig 7). It is
important to note that other causes of liver disease can lead to
dampened hepatic venous waveforms. For example, well-es-
tablished  cirrhosis  as  well  as  iatrogenic  altered  circulation
(such as after a Fontan procedure) lead to monophasic hepat-
ic  venous  waveforms  due  to  elevated  parenchymal  stiffness
and surgical bypass of the heart (the hepatic veins and IVC are
surgically connected to the pulmonary arterial circuit) (Fig 8).

ly  peripheral  reticular  bandlike  areas  of  hypoenhancement
with central sparing, most notably during the earlier phases of
contrast enhancement (27,29,30). With time, contrast materi-
al has the opportunity to equilibrate and perfuse the hepatic
parenchyma, leading to more uniform enhancement (Fig 9B).
Hepatobiliary  MRI  contrast  material  is  often  used  to  eval-
uate focal liver lesions and, given its unique mechanism and
use  in  delayed  phase  imaging,  has  some  advantages  over  ex-
tracellular CT and MRI contrast material. Initially, the hepat-
ic arterial phase and portal venous phase (PVP) demonstrate
the mosaic mottled perfusion pattern similar to that seen with

extracellular  contrast  material  (Fig  10A)  (27).  However,  the
15–20-minute delayed T1-weighted hepatobiliary phase (HBP)
demonstrates the dilated sinusoids to better effect, which are
hypointense and sharply elucidated due to lack of uptake of the
hepatobiliary contrast material (Fig 10B) (31).

Studies  are  emerging  that  suggest  that  higher  apparent
diffusion coefficient values and intravoxel incoherent motion
may help detect liver congestion before fibrosis in congestive
hepatopathy (32). Additionally, congestion and fibrosis can re-
sult in altered T1 and T2 relaxation times (ie, decreased T1 and
increased T2 signal intensities) (33).

hepatic  arterial  and  portal  venous  inflow  (Fig  2),  as  well  as
the  complex  trans-  and  perisinusoidal  microhemodynamics

Benign Regenerative Nodules

Pathologic  Findings.—Regenerative  nodules  refer  to  hyper-
plastic growths of hepatocellular origin that occur in response
to cell injury or hypoperfusion (Fig S5). Multiacinar regener-
ative nodules are typically seen in the setting of cirrhosis or
advanced  hepatic  vascular  disorders  and  are  composed  of
benign  hepatocytes  and  portal  tracts  (artery,  vein,  and  bile
ductules)  with  occasional  scarring  (37).  Since  these  nodules
are not routinely biopsied and congestive hepatopathy is un-
common, the imaging features of these nodules are difficult
to study, and distinguishing them from other benign nodules
at imaging remains challenging.

Premalignant Adenomas

Pathologic  Findings.—Adenomas  have  been  reported  in  pa-
tients with congestive hepatopathy and can be multiple. They
are  solid  lesions  composed  of  hepatocytes  arranged  in  cell
plates one to two cell layers thick (Fig S7), often surrounded
by  a  pseudocapsule.  Microscopic  evaluation  demonstrates
unpaired arteries and absent interlobular bile ductules (54).
They can have variable histologic findings based on subtype,
with the inflammatory subtype being associated with marked
sinusoidal dilatation and inflammatory cell infiltrates (54).

Ultrasonography.—At  US,  adenomas  are  variable  in  ap-
pearance,  which  reflects  their  variable  internal  character-
istics  (42,55).  Small  adenomas  (<3–5  cm)  typically  appear
isoechoic to background liver, reflective of the fact that they
are composed of hepatocytes (42). In cases where the back-
ground liver parenchyma is altered (eg, steatosis, cirrhosis),
they may appear hypoechoic. Fat-containing subtypes of ad-
enomas (hepatocyte nuclear factor-1 [HNF1]–α mutated and
glycogen storage disease –related HCAs) are usually hypere-
choic.  Adenomas  that  undergo  degenerative  change  (hem-
orrhage, necrosis, calcification), more common in larger ad-
enomas (>5 cm), may appear heterogeneous at gray-scale US
(42).  Color  Doppler  US  is  not  sensitive  for  vasculature  and
typically  only  detects  large  intralesional  vessels  (42).  Con-
trast-enhanced  US  is  useful  for  differentiating  HCAs  from
FNH-like lesions but not HCAs from HCCs. Adenomas show
arterial  hyperenhancement  and  are  hypoenhancing  in  the

PVP and delayed phase, similar to HCCs, whereas FNH-like
lesions show hyperenhancement in both arterial and portal
venous phases (42).

CT  and  MRI.—At  cross-sectional  contrast-enhanced  imaging
using  extracellular  agents,  adenomas  are  usually  hyperen-
hancing during the arterial phase and typically do not wash
out,  although  washout  and  other  signal  characteristics  can
vary with subtype and degree of degeneration (Fig 13). HNF1-α
mutated adenomas are often seen in congestive hepatopathy
and frequently show intralesional fat at imaging. This mani-
fests as signal dropout on opposed-phase images. The inflam-
matory subtype is associated with increased signal intensity
on  T2-weighted  images,  particularly  in  the  periphery  (atoll
sign) (56,57). HCAs are hypointense at delayed HBP imaging
due  to  lack  of  uptake  of  contrast  material  (57),  with  the  ex-
ception of some of the inflammatory and β–catenin mutated
HCAs. This feature overlaps with that of HCC but distinguish-
es HCAs from FNH-like lesions. More data on the imaging fea-
tures and types of adenomas in liver congestion specifically are
needed.

Mimics, Pitfalls, and Diagnostic Algorithm

Imaging Mimics
Heterogeneous mosaic liver enhancement is a major imaging
finding of liver congestion and is seen with all causes of im-
paired venous outflow (30). In fact, it can be the only imaging
finding in SOS and certain forms of acute BCS. Yet, this find-
ing  is  not  specific  for  liver  congestion  and  can  be  seen  with
other disease processes that do not result in impaired hepatic
venous  drainage  (63).  Other  processes  that  lead  to  sinusoi-
dal  dilatation  and  nonobstructive  congestion  cause  a  simi-
lar delay in the distribution of contrast material through the
hepatic  microcirculation.  Some  examples  include  systemic
infectious and inflammatory conditions (eg, sepsis), hyperes-
trogenic  states,  drugs,  and  chronic  portal  venous  occlusion
(Fig 14) via an interleukin-6 (IL-6)– and vascular endothelial
growth  factor  (VEGF)–mediated  pathway  (63,64).  Similarly,
arterioportal  shunts,  other  high-volume  states,  and  chronic
portal vein occlusion via hepatocyte atrophy and “ex-vacuo”
dilatation of the sinusoids can result in mosaic enhancement
(63).  While  these  alternative  causes  can  lead  to  mottled  he-
patic perfusion at cross-sectional imaging, they will not show
hepatic vein occlusion, as seen at CT or MRI and US in BCS, or

Assessment of Fibrosis
Accurate  assessment  of  liver  fibrosis  impacts  the  manage-
ment of patients with long-standing liver congestion, partic-
ularly those who are being evaluated for heart transplant (14).
Yet,  laboratory  and  clinical  parameters—such  as  the  fibro-
sis-4 index (FIB-4) and the model for end-stage liver disease
excluding the international normalized ratio (MED-XI)—have
poor to moderate correlation with fibrosis in this population
(65,66). Imaging has not proved to be significantly better for
accurately  predicting  fibrosis.  For  example,  the  stigmata  of
portal  hypertension  so  commonly  associated  with  cirrhosis
does  not  equate  to  advanced  liver  fibrosis  in  these  patients
due to the early development of portal hypertension caused
by impaired venous outflow. Furthermore, morphologic signs
usually considered classic for cirrhosis have low specificity for
advanced fibrosis in the setting of liver congestion, as these
changes may result from processes other than fibrosis (such
as nodular regenerative hyperplasia, as discussed).

Although such findings do show a direct correlation with
time  since  the  Fontan  procedure,  they  have  low  specificity
for  the  detection  of  advanced  fibrosis  (Fig  15A).  Lemmer  et
al (14) found that 70% of patients who underwent the Fontan
procedure  with  imaging  criteria  suggestive  of  cirrhosis  did
not develop hepatic decompensation or other complications
after their heart transplant, which might have been expected
based on the degree of cirrhosis demonstrated at imaging.

Elastography
The  role  of  elastography  in  patients  with  liver  congestion  is
under  investigation.  Both  fibrosis  and  congestion  can  result
in increased liver stiffness. While low liver stiffness measures

can  effectively  rule  out  advanced  fibrosis,  elevated  stiffness
values  should  be  interpreted  with  caution  due  to  the  con-
founding  effect  of  liver  congestion  (Fig  15B).  Studies  using
transient  elastography,  acoustic  radiation  force  impulse,
shear-wave US elastography, and MR elastography (MRE) have
shown at least moderately elevated stiffness in patients with
liver congestion, with average values above the conventional-
ly used cutoffs for the diagnosis of significant fibrosis (67,68).
All of these studies show a statistically significant correlation
between liver stiffness and time since the Fontan procedure.
Some  studies  observed  a  correlation  between  MRE  stiffness
values and fibrosis scores but suggested using higher cutoffs
for  the  diagnosis  of  advanced  fibrosis  in  patients  who  have
undergone the Fontan procedure (69). There are also emerg-
ing data on the role of elastography as a longitudinal marker
for prognostication and prediction of circulatory failure in pa-
tients with Fontan physiology, suggesting that it may be use-
ful for monitoring cardiac dysfunction and not just the degree
of liver fibrosis (70–72).

complications.  Interpretation  of  biopsy  results  can  be  chal-

heterogeneous  peripherally  predominant  pattern  of  fibrosis
in  patients  with  liver  congestion  (2).  Given  the  unique  cen-

Assessment of Focal Hypervascular Observations
and an Algorithmic Approach
Benign hypervascular lesions are commonly encountered in
adolescent  and  adult  patients  with  long-standing  liver  con-
gestion, with a reported prevalence of up to 31% at cross-sec-
tional imaging (75). At the same time, these patients are also
at increased risk for developing hepatic adenomas and HCC
(25,  42),  with  a  reported  HCC  prevalence  of  1.3%  in  patients
who  underwent  the  Fontan  procedure  in  one  multicenter
study  (1)  and  higher  prevalence  reported  in  other  smaller
cohorts  (Fig  S9)  (58).  While  the  majority  of  benign  regener-
ative  nodules  and  FNH-like  lesions  have  standard  imaging
features enabling them to be distinguished from HCC, some
have atypical features that muddy the picture.

A single-center study by Wells et al (52) found that 12% (30
of 245) of arterially enhancing nodules that were benign at bi-
opsy or had greater than 2-year stability showed atypical im-
aging  features.  Features  included  heterogeneity,  moderate/
severe  T2-weighted  hyperintensity,  washout  in  the  delayed
phase, and lack of retention of hepatobiliary contrast materi-
al (Figs 16, 17) (52). These atypical and washout characteristics
have  been  partially  attributed  to  abnormal  signal  intensity
and increasing enhancement in the delayed phase of the con-

gested  and  fibrotic  background  liver  (15).  The  same  authors
(52)  found  that  washout  during  the  PVP,  heterogeneity,  and
high  serum  α-fetoprotein  levels  had  high  correlation  with
HCC based on univariate analysis. Ancillary features such as
T2 hyperintensity, diffusion restriction, and growth were also
more  common  among  HCCs  and  helpful  in  distinguishing
them from benign but atypical arterially enhancing lesions.

MRI,  especially  with  hepatobiliary  contrast  material,  is
often  a  troubleshooting  strategy  for  distinguishing  regener-
ative  nodules  and  FNH-like  lesions  from  premalignant  and
malignant arterially enhancing lesions. At this time, there is
no systematic review of the characteristics of FNH-like nod-
ules  during  the  HBP.  They  are  presumed,  in  part  based  on
the  authors’  experience,  to  behave  like  FNHs  in  the  normal
liver:  isointense  or  hyperintense  relative  to  the  background
liver  due  to  abnormal  biliary  drainage  and  overexpression
of OATP1B3 (Fig 18). However, this feature is not perfect. The
retention of contrast material during the HBP is not specific
to benign lesions and can be seen with a subset of adenomas

Conclusion
Different  causes  of  impaired  venous  outflow  can  result  in
liver  congestion.  Imaging  findings  reflect  diffuse  and  focal
changes in liver parenchyma as a consequence of altered he-
patic microcirculation. Focal hepatic lesions are commonly
encountered  in  patients  with  liver  congestion  and  the  dif-
ferentiation of benign from malignant lesions based on im-
aging findings alone can be difficult. Furthermore, LI-RADS
cannot be used in this patient population. Knowledge of the
pathophysiology of these lesions can improve the interpre-
tation of imaging findings. The management of challenging
lesions  (including  imaging  follow-up,  biopsy,  or  definitive
treatment) should be based on multidisciplinary discussion.
