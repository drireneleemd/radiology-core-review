The abdominal mesentery consists of a double-layered peri-
toneal reflection that suspends the bowel from the posterior
abdominal  wall  and  encases  the  mesenteric  vasculature,
the lymphatics, and a variable amount of fat. The differen-
tial diagnosis for mesenteric pathologic conditions is broad,
ranging  from  benign  and  purely  incidental  causes,  such  as
mesenteric panniculitis and retractile mesenteritis, to malig-
nant and aggressive causes, such as neuroendocrine tumors,
lymphoma, and metastatic disease.

Overlapping  imaging  features  of  mesenteric  pathologic
conditions  present  a  diagnostic  dilemma  for  radiologists.
When present, clinical history and symptoms can aid in dif-
ferentiation.  However,  many  mesenteric  pathologic  condi-
tions are often discovered incidentally. Both factors contrib-
ute  to  the  frustration  felt  by  many  radiologists  when  faced
with  determining  the  potential  diagnosis  and/or  providing
the  most  appropriate  recommendations  for  further  assess-
ment of these lesions.

Sclerosing  mesenteritis  is  an  umbrella  term  used  by  pa-
thologists  to  define  nonspecific  inflammatory  changes  of
the  mesentery;  the  disease  spectrum  varies  from  mesen-
teric  panniculitis  to  retractile  mesenteritis.  The  histologic
features  underscore  the  radiologic  findings  in  that  the  lip-
id-laden macrophages and areas of fat necrosis seen in mes-
enteric panniculitis correlate with hazy fat stranding with a
thin pseudocapsule and small mesenteric lymph nodes that
typically  measure  less  than  1  cm.  Patients  with  sclerosing
mesenteritis are typically asymptomatic. The presence of ab-
dominal symptoms warrants close inspection for acute pro-
cesses, such as enteritis or trauma.

Retractile mesenteritis is at the other end of the spectrum,
with  pathologic  features  of  dense  masslike  fibrosis  and  in-
flammation.  At  imaging,  retractile  mesenteritis  can  appear
masslike with calcifications but without lymphadenopathy.
When lymphadenopathy is present, alternative diagnoses to
sclerosing mesenteritis should be considered, such as Myco-
bacterium avium complex infection or neoplastic processes
(ie, lymphoma).

While  many  mesenteric  pathologic  conditions  will  ulti-
mately require tissue sampling for definitive diagnosis, there
occasionally are clues gleaned through the patient’s history
and the radiologic features of the lesion that can help nar-
row the differential diagnosis. The slide presentation uses a
quiz-style case-based approach to provide radiologists with a

framework for approaching solid mesenteric pathologic con-
ditions. Additionally, we provide an algorithm that learners
can use in their evaluation of mesenteric lesions that would
lead  to  a  favored  diagnosis  based  on  the  patient’s  history
of malignancy, presence of mesenteric stranding (Fig 1) or
a  soft-tissue  mass  (Fig  2),  presence  or  absence  of  enlarged
lymph nodes, and associated symptoms. The series of cases
comprise  clinical,  radiologic,  and  pathologic  correlations
across a wide variety of commonly encountered mesenteric
abnormalities, in addition to some of the less commonly en-
countered entities so that radiologists will have a familiarity
with  these  as  well.  When  possible,  we  provide  illustrative
examples  of  the  underlying  histologic  features  for  select
pathologic  conditions  and  discuss  how  the  underlying  tis-
sue  structure  influences  the  appearances  demonstrated  at
imaging.

The slide presentation will help radiologists better under-
stand the mesentery and its more common pathologic con-
ditions  as  well  as  how  to  make  more  informed  differential
diagnoses and follow-up recommendations when these con-
ditions are encountered at imaging.

TEACHING POINTS
	 Sclerosing mesenteritis can be pathologically divided into two subgroups that
correlate with imaging findings. Mesenteric panniculitis manifests with mesen-
teric haziness with small lymph nodes, while retractile mesenteritis manifests
with a focal mesenteric mass with calcifications.

	 Metastatic carcinoid tumor can manifest similarly to retractile mesenteritis with
a calcified mesenteric mass and fibrosis. Somatostatin imaging or tissue sam-
pling is frequently needed to differentiate these entities.

	 Enlarged mesenteric lymph nodes may be seen in both benign and malignant
conditions, but a short-axis diameter greater than 1 cm is more concerning for
malignancy and should prompt further workup or tissue sampling.
