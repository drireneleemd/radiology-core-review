Hepatocellular Adenomas: Molecular Basis and
Multimodality Imaging Update

Justin R. Tse, MD • Ely R. Felker, MD • Bita V. Naini, MD • Luyao Shen, MD • Jody Shen, MD • David S. K. Lu, MD • Aya Kamaya, MD •
Steven S. Raman, MD

Author affiliations, funding, and conflicts of interest are listed at the end of this article.

Hepatocellular adenomas (HCAs) are a family of liver tumors that are associated with variable prognoses. Since the initial de-
scription of these tumors, the classification of HCAs has expanded and now includes eight distinct genotypic subtypes based on
molecular analysis findings. These genotypic subtypes have unique derangements in their cellular biologic makeup that determine
their clinical course and may allow noninvasive identification of certain subtypes. Multiphasic MRI performed with hepatobiliary
contrast agents remains the best method to noninvasively detect, characterize, and monitor HCAs. HCAs are generally hypointense
during the hepatobiliary phase; the β-catenin–mutated exon 3 subtype and up to a third of inflammatory HCAs are the exception
to this characterization. It is important to understand the appearances of HCAs beyond their depictions at MRI, as these tumors
are typically identified with other imaging modalities first. The two most feared related complications are bleeding and malignant
transformation to hepatocellular carcinoma, although the risk of these complications depends on tumor size, subtype, and clinical
factors. Elective surgical resection is recommended for HCAs that are persistently larger than 5 cm, adenomas of any size in men,
and all β-catenin–mutated exon 3 HCAs. Thermal ablation and transarterial embolization are potential alternatives to surgical
resection. In the acute setting of a ruptured HCA, patients typically undergo transarterial embolization with or without delayed
surgical resection. This update on HCAs includes a review of radiologic-pathologic correlations by subtype and imaging modality,
related complications, and management recommendations.

Introduction
Hepatocellular  adenomas  (HCAs)  are  a  heterogeneous  group
of liver tumors associated with variable prognoses. When first
discovered in the 1970s, HCAs were believed to reflect a sin-
gle entity (1). Over time, these tumors have been stratified into
an  increasing  number  of  unique  genotypic  subtypes  based
on molecular analysis findings. The first major update was in
2006, when the Bourdeaux group described four HCA subtypes
(2,3):  inflammatory,  hepatocyte  nuclear  factor  1α  inactivated
(HNF-1α),  β-catenin–mutated  exon  3,  and  unclassified.  The
second  major  update  occurred  in  2017,  when  four  additional
groups were added: sonic hedgehog, β-catenin–mutated exon
7/8, mixed inflammatory and β-catenin–mutated exon 3, and
mixed inflammatory and β-catenin–mutated exon 7/8 (4). With
the  exception  of  mixed  inflammatory  and  β-catenin-mutated
subtypes, HCA subtypes develop from mutually exclusive mo-
lecular pathways, with distinct risk factors and prognoses (5).

HCAs classically occur in women of childbearing age who
have engaged in long-term use of oral contraceptives (OCs),
the latter of which increases the incidence of these tumors 30-
to  40-fold  (6).  All  HCA  subtypes  have  a  female  predilection
(60%–100%),  and  median  OC  use  ranges  from  8  to  22  years
(4).  Other  risk  factors  include  anabolic  androgenic  steroid
use,  obesity,  alcohol  consumption,  and  certain  genetic  syn-
dromes. Although HCAs were initially believed to be rare, the
incidence of these tumors has increased over time. This may
be  due  to  a  combination  of  increased  use  of  cross-sectional
imaging,  increasing  rates  of  obesity,  and  increased  anabolic
substance  use  (6,7).  HCAs  can  be  solitary  or  multiple,  and
adenomatosis  (more  than  10  adenomas)  has  been  described
with all subtypes (4,8). In patients with multiple HCAs, the

molecular subtypes tend to be the same; however, the muta-
tions at the codon level are not (4).

Rates of complications, which include bleeding and malig-
nant transformation, depend on the HCA subtype (4,5). Com-
mon  HCA  subtypes  have  variable  imaging  appearances  and
may mimic other liver neoplasms (6,9,10). Thus, it is import-
ant to accurately identify, characterize, and distinguish HCAs
when they are encountered in clinical practice. The reference
standard  for  determining  the  HCA  subtype  is  either  immu-
nohistochemical stain analysis or rarely, genomic sequencing
with use of a surgical pathologic specimen (4,5). However, such
tests may be unavailable outside of a tertiary liver center or un-
familiar to the pathologist. Also, it is impractical to resect or bi-
opsy every suspected HCA lesion, particularly if the patient has
adenomatosis. The HCA subtype can often be suggested by the
imaging findings, obviating the need for an invasive procedure.
In this article, we review the clinical-epidemiologic, im-
aging,  and  histopathologic  features;  complications;  and
management of HCA subtypes on the basis of the most re-
cent molecular classification systems.

Radiologic-Pathologic Correlation by Molecular
Subtype

Inflammatory HCA
Inflammatory  HCA  is  tied  with  HNF-1α  HCA  as  the  most
common  subtype  (30%–35%  of  HCAs)  and  is  strongly  asso-
ciated with higher body mass index and OC use. In patients
with inflammatory HCA, the median body mass index is 25
kg/m2 and the median length of OC use is 20 years (4). Other
risk  factors  include  alcohol  use,  a  fatty  liver,  and  glycogen
storage disorders. Patients may have inflammatory syndrome
(fever,  elevated  white  blood  cell  count,  elevated  fibrinogen
level, and elevated C-reactive protein level) or abnormal liver
test  results  (commonly  elevated  alkaline  phosphatase  and
γ-glutamyl transferase concentrations) (2,4–6,11).

At the molecular level, inflammatory HCAs have sustained
activation of the interleukin 6/Janus kinase/signal transducer
and activator of transcription (STAT) pathway through somatic
gain-of-function  mutations  (11).  At  the  histologic  level,  these
HCAs  can  exhibit  patchy  inflammatory  infiltration,  ductular
reaction,  sinusoidal  dilatation,  and  dystrophic  blood  vessels
(pseudoportal  tracks  with  thick-walled  arteries  and  lack  of
veins or bile ducts). Inflammatory infiltration is responsible for
diffuse strong serum amyloid A and C-reactive protein immu-
nohistochemical  staining,  a  hallmark  of  inflammatory  HCAs
at immunohistochemical analysis (5,11–13). Sinusoidal dilata-
tion and dystrophic blood vessels are responsible for persistent
enhancement  during  the  portal  venous  phase  of  iodinated  or
gadolinium-based  contrast  agent–enhanced  CT  or  MRI,  re-
spectively. Dilated sinusoids are also believed to be responsible
for the “atoll” sign and T2 hyperintensity (14–16); thus, these
HCAs were once termed telangiectatic focal nodular hyperpla-
sia  (FNH)  until  they  were  reclassified  as  inflammatory  HCA
by  the  World  Health  Organization  (5,16).  At  histopathologic
analysis, the surrounding liver can also exhibit sinusoidal dila-
tation and steatosis, which are present in up to 40% of patients
(4,5,13,16,17).

Inflammatory  HCAs  have  variable  T1  signal  intensity
relative  to  the  background  liver,  which  may  depend  on
background hepatic steatosis. During dynamic postcontrast
imaging,  these  tumors  have  avid  and  peak  enhancement
during the arterial phase that persists above the background
liver  during  the  portal  venous  phase.  In  the  hepatobiliary
phase,  most  inflammatory  HCAs  are  hypointense  to  the
background liver (18,20,21). Figure 1 shows a large (>5 cm)
typical  inflammatory  HCA  with  an  atoll  sign  and  subse-
quent rupture. However, it is recognized that a small propor-
tion of the tumor may be iso- or hyperintense relative to the
liver during the hepatobiliary phase (10). In some cases, the
increased  HCA  signal  intensity  in  the  hepatobiliary  phase
can  be  attributed  to  preserved  or  increased  organic  anion
transporting  polypeptide  (OATP)  expression  (20,22),  while
in other cases, it may be due to background hepatic steato-
sis causing relative HCA hyperintensity after fat suppression
(23). Inflammatory HCAs with true hepatobiliary phase up-
take (ie, tumor isointensity to hyperintensity [relative to the
liver] that is not attributed to background steatosis) are a po-
tential pitfall in distinguishing this HCA subtype from other
liver  tumors  (9,10).  Figure  2  shows  an  inflammatory  HCA
with hepatobiliary phase isointensity.

At  US,  the  echogenicity  of  inflammatory  HCAs  relative
to the liver varies and may depend on the degree of hepatic
steatosis  (19,24–26).  Figure  3  shows  how  an  HCA  may  ap-
pear  hypoechoic  when  the  liver  has  background  steatosis.
With  contrast-enhanced  imaging,  venous  washout  is  more
commonly  observed  at  contrast-enhanced  US  than  at  con-
trast-enhanced  MRI  or  CT  (19,26,27).  This  discrepancy  can
be attributed to the diffusion of gadolinium- or iodine-based
contrast agents into the disordered interstitial spaces, whereas
contrast-enhanced US involves the use of purely intravascular
contrast agents.

HNF-1α HCA
HNF1A is a tumor suppressor gene that also acts as a major he-
patocyte transcription factor. It is responsible for the expression
of liver fatty acid–binding protein (L-FABP) and organic anionic
transporter  proteins  (5,27,28).  L-FABP  is  responsible  for  fatty
acid trafficking, and the downregulation of this protein leads to
accumulation of intracellular fat. At histopathologic analysis,
macrovesicular  fat  and  lack  of  L-FABP  staining  at  immuno-
histochemical analysis are hallmarks of HNF-1α HCA (5,12).

Decreased L-FABP (leading to intracellular fat) and decreased
OATP1B1/3 expression in HNF-1α HCAs cause decreased signal
intensity at T1-weighted opposed-phase and hepatobiliary phase
MRI, respectively. Most (90%) HNF-1α HCAs have acquired so-
matic mutations, while the remaining 10% of these tumors have
germline  and  somatic  mutations.  For  example,  patients  with
maturity-onset diabetes of the young–3 (MODY 3) already have
at least one congenital preexisting mutation and thus are at a
higher risk for HNF-1α HCA (5). A patient may have hepato-
cellular adenomatosis, and the HCAs within the same patient
can have either shared or separate independent mutations (4).
The association between HNF-1α HCA and either OC use or
obesity is not as strong as that between inflammatory HCA and
these factors. In patients with HNF-1α HCA, the median length
of OC use is 12 years and the median body mass index is 21 kg/
m2 (4). HNF-1α HCAs have not been reported among patients
with glycogen storage diseases (29,30). Background fatty liver is
less common with this HCA subtype (3,4,14,20).

At MRI, HNF-1α HCAs are readily identified by a loss of
signal  at T1-weighted  opposed-phase  imaging,  which  is  due
to intracellular microscopic fat (14,16,18,20,21). The presence
of microscopic fat is highly sensitive and specific for HNF-1α
HCA, with a contemporary series (18) reporting a sensitivity
of  95%,  specificity  of  85%,  and  area  under  the  receiver  op-
erating  characteristic  curve  of  90%.  Intratumoral  fat  can  be
quantified,  and  HNF-1α  HCAs  have  an  average  fat  fraction
of 24% (21). HNF-1α HCAs have variable signal intensity at
unenhanced T1-weighted fat-suppressed MRI (14,16) but may
appear hypointense relative to the liver owing to the presence
of fat (3,21). Like other HCA subtypes, HNF-1α HCAs show
peak  enhancement  during  the  arterial  phase  (14,16,18–22).
However, unlike other HCA subtypes, they do not show hy-
perenhancement  during  the  venous  phase  (16)  and  may  be
hypoenhancing,  giving  the  appearance  of  washout  (20,21).
In the hepatobiliary phase, HNF-1α HCAs are markedly hy-
pointense and, compared with other subtypes, have the low-
est signal intensity relative to the background liver owing to
reduced  OATP1B1/3  expression  (18,20–22).  Figure  4  shows
typical MRI and US images depicting HNF-1α HCA.

At CT, HNF-1α HCAs can have appearances similar to their
appearances on dynamic MR images, including hypoattenua-
tion in the unenhanced phase and peak enhancement in the
arterial phase. While MRI is much more sensitive for the pres-
ence  of  intratumoral  fat,  unenhanced  CT  may  occasionally
show areas of low attenuation corresponding to fat (Fig 5). In-
vestigators in one study of unsubtyped HCAs (31) identified fat
in 7% of cases, while the prevalence of HNF-1α HCAs among
the recent above-mentioned research cohorts is closer to 30%,
suggesting that CT would underestimate HNF-1α HCAs.

Bleeding (Fig 5) and malignant transformation (Fig 6) are
rare but well-recognized complications of HNF-1α HCA, par-
ticularly when these tumors are large (>5 cm) (4). The mo-
lecular pathogenesis to hepatocelluar carcinoma (HCC) is not
known, but it is not related to the β-catenin pathway, which
drives HCC transformation in β-catenin–mutated HCAs (5).
Malignant  transformation  occurs  in  up  to  4%  of  HNF-1α
HCAs (4). HNF-1α HCA is the subtype most likely to progress
in size or number despite the cessation of OC use and is over-
represented  in  postmenopausal  patients  older  than  60  years
(Fig 6) (37,38). Some of these cases may reflect a newer “myx-
oid  subtype,”  a  histologic  descriptor  based  on  extracellular
myxoid material within the tumor. This myxoid subtype can
be considered a subcategory within the HNF-1α HCA subtype
that is based on loss of L-FABP expression (12) and is associ-
ated with older age and malignant transformation (39,40). It
is possible that some HNF-1α HCAs are hormonally indepen-

weakest association with OC use and obesity.

β-Catenin–mutated HCA
β-catenin–mutated  HCAs  are  a  family  of  HCAs  that  share
a  missense  mutation  in  the  cadherin-associated  protein  β1
gene  (CTNNB1)  at  exon  3  or  exon  7/8  (4,5).  This  mutation
leads  to  variably  increased  activation  of  the  wingless -type
integration  site  protein  ligand/β-catenin  pathway,  which
regulates  multiple  cellular  processes  such  as  proliferation
and differentiation (41). Aberrant activation of this pathway
is shared among malignancies and is implicated in up to 35%
of  HCCs  (42).  CTNNB1  mutations  at  exon  3  lead  to  strong
pathway  activation,  and,  thus,  this  β-catenin–mutated  HCA
subtype has the highest risk (as high as 40%) of HCC transfor-
mation (3,4). Exon 3 mutations are also associated with ana-
bolic androgenic steroid use, vascular liver disease, and male
sex, with 30%–40% of these mutations occurring in men (4).

On  the  other  hand,  exon  7/8  mutations  involve  a  negli-
gible (though nonzero) risk of HCC transformation and are
highly associated, just after sonic hedgehog HCAs, with his-
tologic and symptomatic bleeding (4,5,43). Exon 7/8 muta-
tions were discovered relatively recently (4) and before the
associated HCAs were included in the unclassified subtype
(7,44). In one study (4), β-catenin–mutated exon 7/8 HCAs

A  small  proportion  of  inflammatory  HCAs  also  harbor
CTNNB1 mutations at exon 3 or exon 7/8, resulting in four
HCA subtypes that share this mutation: β-catenin–mutated
exon  3,  β-catenin–mutated  exon  7/8,  mixed  inflammatory
and  β-catenin–mutated  exon  3,  and  mixed  inflammatory
and  β-catenin–mutated  exon  7/8,  representing  8%,  4%,  8%,
and 5% of HCAs, respectively (4). Mixed inflammatory and
β-catenin–mutated  HCAs  are  primarily  inflammatory,  as
they are histologically more similar to inflammatory HCAs,
and  CTNNB1  mutation  is  believed  to  be  a  second  genetic
event  (Fig  S2,  Fig  7)  (5).  Additional  immunohistochemical
staining  is  required  in  all  cases  of  suspected  inflammatory
HCAs to exclude superimposed CTNNB1 mutations. Similar
to  “pure”  β-catenin–mutated  exon  3  HCAs,  mixed  inflam-
matory and β-catenin–mutated exon 3 HCAs are associated
with higher rates of HCC transformation (4).

β-catenin–mutated HCAs may show an increased number
of  atypical  cells  and  expression  of  nuclear  β-catenin  and/
or  glutamine  synthetase  at  immunohistochemical  analysis.
Glutamine synthetase is a downstream target of the wingless-
type  integration  site  protein  ligand/β-catenin  pathway  and
shows diffuse staining in some exon 3 mutations (non-S45) but
is patchy and faint in exon 7/8 mutations. Glutamine synthetase
staining  is  more  sensitive  than  nuclear  β-catenin  staining,
which often yields negative findings in exon 7/8 mutations (5).

At  MRI,  β-catenin–mutated  exon  3  HCAs  may  have  a
T2-hyperintense  central  scar  and  show  peak  enhancement
in  the  arterial  phase  that  may  persist  to  the  portal  venous
phase (14,16,18,20–21). An important distinguishing feature
of β-catenin–mutated exon 3 HCAs is that most (>80%) of
them are iso- or hyperintense during the hepatobiliary phase
(45,46),  corresponding  to  increased  OATP1B1/3  expression
(Fig  8)  (20,22,23,47,48).  At  the  molecular  level,  increased

The  FDG  PET/CT  findings  of  β-catenin–mutated  exon

portal  venous  washout,  while  the  HCA  in  the  other  study
(19) was isoechoic.

Unclassified HCA
The  remaining  7%  of  HCAs  are  unclassified  and  cannot  be
categorized  into  one  of  the  described  subtypes  (inflamma-
tory, HNF-1α, β-catenin–mutated exon 3, β-catenin–mutated
exon 7/8, mixed inflammatory and β-catenin–mutated exon 3,
mixed inflammatory and β-catenin–mutated exon 7/8, sonic
hedgehog) on the basis of molecular or immunohistochemi-
cal analysis findings. No discerning clinical or histopathologic
features  have  been  established  for  this  group.  The  imaging
findings  of  unclassified  HCAs  are  not  well  established  and
are further confounding because they have probably included
sonic  hedgehog  and  β-catenin–mutated  exon  7/8  HCAs  in
prior series (14–16,18,20,21). On the basis of the Bourdeaux
classification system (29,30), up to 20% of the HCAs in glyco-
gen storage disorders are unclassified. However, no cases of
glycogen storage disorders were identified among unclassified
HCAs in the 2017 Bourdeaux update (4), suggesting that they
were redistributed to other subtypes that were previously con-
sidered to be unclassified.

Management

Initial Diagnosis
All patients suspected of having HCA should undergo con-
trast-enhanced  MRI  (6).  MRI  can  readily  depict  the  most
common  HCA  subtypes,  which  account  for  70%–80%  of
these tumors. For example, the presence of microscopic fat
has  a  sensitivity  of  95%  and  specificity  of  85%  for  HNF-1α
HCAs  (18).  Moderate  T2  hyperintensity  and  persistent  en-
hancement  in  the  venous  phase  have  a  sensitivity  of  85%–
88% and specificity of 88%–100% for inflammatory HCAs (6).
The atoll sign has fair sensitivity (60%) but high specificity
(97%)  for  inflammatory  HCAs.  A  T2-hyperintense  central
scar has a sensitivity of 80% and specificity of 91% for β-cat-
enin–mutated exon 3 HCAs (18).

MRI  can  be  performed  with  extracellular  or  hepatobili-
ary  contrast  agents  (gadobenate  dimeglumine  or  gadoxetic
acid). HCA subtypes show varying degrees of signal intensity
in  the  hepatobiliary  phase,  which  can  improve  diagnostic
performance.  For  example,  HNF-1α  HCAs  have  the  lowest
signal  intensity  during  the  hepatobiliary  phase  (18,20–22),
and  the  inclusion  of  this  criterion  improves  specificity  to
96% (18). HCAs generally are hypointense on hepatobiliary
phase  MR  images;  the  exceptions  to  this  feature  are  β-cat-
enin–mutated exon 3 subtypes and up to 30% of inflamma-
tory  HCAs,  which  may  be  iso-  to  hyperintense  during  the
hepatobiliary phase (10,18,20–22,45,46).

Distinguishing  inflammatory  HCAs  with  hepatobiliary
phase uptake from FNHs can be challenging, although quan-
titative MRI assessment of the hepatobiliary phase with both
gadobenate dimeglumine and gadoxetic acid has been shown
to have strong discriminatory power, with FNHs having sig-
nificantly  higher  signal  intensity  ratios  (60,61).  Other  ancil-
lary  features  such  as  a  lobulated  morphology  and  a  central
scar are more common in FNHs (61). Both FNHs and HCAs
can  restrict  diffusion;  however,  in  one  study  (62),  the  mean
apparent  diffusion  coefficient  (ADC)  value  of  HCAs  was

lower than that of FNHs; no differences in ADC values were
identified among the HCA subtypes themselves.

Rarer  HCA  subtypes  such  as  sonic  hedgehog  and  β-cat-
enin–mutated exon 7/8, which are associated with the highest
bleeding risk, may be challenging to diagnose by using imaging
alone, as they have no specific imaging findings. When the di-
agnosis or a definitively indolent subtype cannot be established
with  imaging,  biopsy  may  be  necessary  (43,60).  Ultimately,
there is no consensus regarding specific indications for biopsy,
and lesions suspicious for HCA may require a customized mul-
tidisciplinary discussion at a liver referral center (63).

Initial Management
When  HCA  is  diagnosed  in  men,  surgical  resection  is  uni-
formly recommended because of the substantially higher risk
of malignancy, regardless of tumor subtype and size. It is also
recommended  that  patients  with  β-catenin–mutated  exon  3
HCA  undergo  surgical  resection,  regardless  of  tumor  size,
owing to the higher risk of malignancy (6). For women, risk
factor  reduction  measures  such  as  cessation  of  OC  use  and
weight  loss  are  recommended  first,  followed  by  repeat  MRI
for assessment of size change. If the HCA grows (20% diame-
ter increase, adopted from RECIST [Response Evaluation Cri-
teria in Solid Tumors] criteria for “progression”) or remains
larger than 5 cm, surgical resection is recommended owing to
the increased risk of bleeding and malignant transformation.
Transarterial  embolization  and  ablation  are  considered  sec-
ond-line treatment alternatives according to European Asso-
ciation for the Study of the Liver guidelines and are reserved
for  patients  who  are  not  surgical  candidates  (6).  However,
recent studies have found success with use of local-regional
treatment only and are discussed later.

Surveillance
If  the  HCA  has  a  stable  or  small  size  and  has  remained
smaller  than  5  cm  for  a  considerable  period,  conservative
management  with  annual  surveillance  imaging  is  recom-
mended.  MRI  is  generally  used  for  serial  follow-up  exam-
inations  (37).  Unlike  CT,  MRI  does  not  involve  the  use  of
ionizing  radiation,  and  most  patients  with  HCAs  are  rela-
tively young individuals who are expected to undergo mul-
tiple  imaging  examinations.  Furthermore,  the  criterion  for
progression  is  adopted  from  RECIST,  in  which  axial  CT  or
MRI  is  used  for  size  measurements  (6).  Nevertheless,  fol-
low-up  with  US  has  been  proposed  as  an  alternative  in  se-
lect  circumstances,  as  it  may  be  more  cost  effective  (6).  It
is unclear whether all follow-up MRI examinations require
hepatobiliary  contrast  agents.  Hepatobiliary  agents  may  be
more sensitive for detecting small new lesions and theoret-
ically may be used to detect gain of β-catenin mutations in
an  inflammatory  adenoma,  which  is  presumed  to  reflect  a
second genetic event (5).

stringent follow-up advocated by one group involves annual
follow-up  until  menopause  in  women  (60).  In  one  study  of
postmenopausal patients with HCAs, 44% of tumors were un-

Local-Regional and Surgical Treatment
Treatment  of  ruptured  or  actively  bleeding  HCAs  typically
involves fluid resuscitation followed by transarterial emboli-
zation to achieve hemodynamic stability (Fig 10) (43,65,66).
In rare cases, laparotomy with packing may be necessary to
control  the  bleeding.  Immediate  surgical  resection  of  the
HCA is associated with increased morbidity and thus is typ-
ically delayed for several months. In the interim, the blood
products  may  resorb,  the  inflammation  may  decrease,  and
the culprit HCA may be easier to identify. Some patients with
bleeding HCAs can be treated with transarterial embolization
only,  without  delayed  surgical  resection,  particularly  if  the
HCAs shrink or no longer enhance (43,65–68). Investigators
in one relatively recent study (66) found that complete ne-

crosis could be achieved in 12 of 18 bleeding HCAs by using
transarterial  embolization.  At  follow-up,  the  authors  pro-
posed  conservative  management  for  those  women  whose
HCAs  had  reduced  to  smaller  than  5  cm  or  had  no  viable
tissue  (66);  in  such  cases,  surgery  can  be  avoided  in  up  to
40% of patients with use of this approach. Men would still
require  surgical  resection  because  of  the  high  risk  of  HCC
transformation;  if  and  how  transarterial  embolization  af-
fects malignancy risk are still unknown.

For  elective  treatment  of  growing  HCAs  or  HCAs  that
remain larger than 5 cm, guidelines of the European Asso-
ciation  for  the  Study  of  the  Liver  recommend  surgical  re-
section. Surgical resection can be performed with a laparo-
scopic or open technique (65). Large HCAs or adenomatosis
may require a staged approach (Fig S3). Liver transplant is
rarely necessary and is reserved for patients with malignant
transformation  or  those  with  chronic  liver  disease  such  as
glycogen storage disease or vascular liver disease (43). Liver
adenomatosis by itself (without chronic liver disease) is not
an indication for transplant (65).

the emergent setting, it is increasingly being used in elective

Pregnancy
Current  guidelines  recommend  periodic  sonographic  moni-
toring  for  tumor  growth  every  6–12  weeks  for  HCAs  of  any
size in pregnant women (6). For small (<5 cm) HCAs, the risk
is  minimal,  although  about  25%  of  these  tumors  will  grow
during pregnancy (74). Another 25% will regress, and the rest
will  remain  stable.  Bleeding  during  pregnancy  has  been  re-
ported in only HCAs 6.5 cm or larger, mostly during the third
trimester,  and  it  has  not  been  reported  in  the  first  trimester
(75). The true rate of bleeding during pregnancy is unknown,
given  selection  bias  and  the  probable  underreporting  of  as-
ymptomatic HCAs during uncomplicated pregnancies. How-
ever, when bleeding occurs, it potentially can be fatal to both
the mother and the fetus (43,75). Treatment options include
transarterial  embolization  or  surgery,  which  would  require
multidisciplinary  evaluation  by  surgeons,  interventional  ra-
diologists,  and  obstetricians,  particularly  when  evaluating
risk to both the fetus and the mother (6,43,75).

Conclusion
New research on the genetic underpinnings of HCAs in the
past decade have significantly improved our understanding of

the biologic features of these tumors. These unique molecular
pathways  affect  the  imaging  appearances  and  prognoses  as-
sociated with HCAs, which in turn guide management. Mul-
tiphasic MRI with hepatobiliary contrast agents remains the
best method to noninvasively detect, characterize, and mon-
itor HCAs. Nevertheless, it is important to understand their
appearance  at  imaging  with  other  modalities  as  well.  HCAs
may  mimic  other  neoplasms  or  require  imaging  with  other
modalities according to the clinical setting, such as pregnancy
or suspected rupture. Bleeding and malignant transformation
are the two major complications and depend on the size, clin-
ical-demographic  features,  and  subtype  of  the  HCA.  These
complications  are  the  basis  for  surgical  resection  of  HCAs
larger  than  5  cm,  growing  HCAs,  HCAs  of  any  size  among
men,  and  β-catenin–mutated  HCAs  of  any  size.  Promising
emerging  data  and  results  from  transarterial  embolization
and  thermal  ablation  suggest  that  these  techniques  may  be
alternatives to surgical resection.
