The use of standardized terms in assessing and reporting disease processes has well-established benefits, such as clear communica-
tion between radiologists and other health care providers, improved diagnostic accuracy and reproducibility, and the enhancement
and facilitation of research. Recently, the Liver Imaging Reporting and Data System (LI-RADS) Steering Committee released a uni-
versal liver imaging lexicon. The current version of the lexicon includes 81 vetted and precisely defined terms that are relevant to
acquisition of images using all major liver imaging modalities and contrast agents, as well as lesion- and organ-level features. Most
terms in the lexicon are applicable to all patients undergoing imaging of the liver, and only a minority of the terms are strictly in-
tended to be used for patients with high risk factors for hepatocellular carcinoma. This pictorial atlas familiarizes readers with the
liver imaging lexicon and includes discussion of general concepts, providing sample definitions, schematics, and clinical examples
for a subset of the terms in the liver imaging lexicon. The authors discuss general, technical, and imaging feature terms used com-
monly in liver imaging, with the goal of illustrating their use for clinical and research applications.

Introduction
The Liver Imaging Reporting and Data System (LI-RADS) is
a  comprehensive  system  for  standardizing  the  terminology,
techniques,  interpretation,  reporting,  and  data  collection  on
observations in patients who have or are at increased risk for
hepatocellular  carcinoma  (HCC)  (1).  LI-RADS  algorithms
have  been  developed  as  a  necessary  response  to  an  urgent
need to standardize the imaging features, criteria, and treat-
ment of patients at risk for or with HCC. The benefits of stan-
dardized reporting are clear across the field of radiology, with
documented improvements in communication (2), data min-
ing and auditing (3), adherence to clinical guidelines (4), and
diagnostic  accuracy  (5–7).  The  development  of  a  universal,

precise vocabulary or lexicon is integral to creating standard-
ized, structured, or templated reports. A standardized lexicon
permits clear communication between radiologists and other
members of the health care team, facilitates clinical adoption
of terms, improves diagnostic accuracy, and promotes agree-
ment  among  radiologists  (8).  Standardized  terms  exist  for
many organs and disease processes, including prostate cancer,
breast cancer, thyroid nodules, ovarian masses, and lung can-
cer, but not—until recently—for liver imaging applicable for
the general population (6,8–20).

versally  adopted  lexicon  is  needed  for  research.  Scientific

Developing a standardized lexicon for all of liver imaging is
a critical step toward harnessing the future potential of “‘big
data” comprising our imaging studies and for developing ac-
curate population-based models for diagnosis and prognosis.
In an effort to standardize communication in the clinical set-
ting and the scientific literature, the LI-RADS Steering Com-
mittee recently released a liver imaging lexicon (24). The cur-
rent version of the lexicon comprises 81 vetted and precisely
defined terms relevant to imaging with all major liver imaging
modalities  and  contrast  agents  and  includes  lesion-  and  or-
gan-level  features.  Most  terms  are  applicable  to  all  patients
undergoing  liver  imaging,  although  a  minority  of  terms  are
intended for imaging of patients at a high risk for HCC. The
lexicon is available free of charge on the American College of
Radiology (ACR) website (https://www.acr.org/-/media/ACR/
Files/RADS/LI-RADS/LIRADS-Lexicon-Table.pdf) (24).

The  purpose  of  this  article  is  to  provide  a  pictorial  atlas
of  this  universal  liver  imaging  lexicon.  We  aim  to  familiar-
ize  readers  with  the  lexicon  by  discussing  general  concepts,
reviewing sample definitions, and providing schematics and
clinical examples to illustrate the use of terms for clinical and
research applications.

Lexicon Development
Multiple  terms  and  definitions  have  been  developed  and  re-
fined since the LI-RADS inception in 2008 and have been pub-
lished  in  each  released  version  of  LI-RADS.  The  terms  and
their  definitions  were  revised  as  evidence  and  user  feedback
accumulated.  For  example,  the  term  rim APHE  was  formally
introduced in the 2017 version, when the scientific evidence for
the role of APHE morphology in the differentiation of intrahe-
patic cholangiocarcinoma from HCC became available (22,23).
Clear definition of imaging features of non-HCC malignancy
from  those  of  HCC  allows  high  specificity  in  distinguish-
ing between the conditions and for achieving a 95% positive
predictive value for diagnosis of LI-RADS-5 (definite HCC)
(25,26).

In 2019, the process of development and approval of the
lexicon  was  formalized,  and  the  Lexicon  Working  Group
was convened. The members of the Lexicon Working Group
extracted relevant terms and their definitions from the ex-
isting  LI-RADS  documents  and  relevant  literature.  After
the  Lexicon  Working  Group  created  and  reviewed  multi-
ple iterations of the lexicon terms and their definitions, the
entire  34-member  LI-RADS  Steering  Committee  reviewed
and eventually approved the lexicon, with each term and its
definition requiring greater than 90% approval. Ultimately,
81  terms  were  approved  and  included  in  the  most  recent
version of the lexicon, which was released in June 2021.

Lexicon Structure
For  each  included  term,  the  LI-RADS  lexicon  provides  the
term name, definition, context of use (COU), applicable mo-
dalities,  general  comments,  LI-RADS–specific  comments,
synonyms, and the type of term. COU refers to the condition
under  which  a  given  term  should  be  used  in  clinical  prac-
tice or research. Terms with a broad COU are applicable for
all  patients  undergoing  liver  imaging,  while  those  with  a

LI-RADS–specific  COU  are  applicable  mainly  for  patients
with or at high risk for HCC. Of the 81 terms included in the
latest version, 69 have a broad COU, and 12 have a LI-RADS–
specific COU. Although terms with a broad COU may be used
for  all  patients  undergoing  liver  imaging,  the  implications
may differ for patients with and without a risk for HCC. For
instance, the feature “fat in mass, more than adjacent liver”
favors the diagnosis of HCC when it is present in a patient at
high risk. However, the same feature in a young woman with-
out background liver disease is more suggestive of a hepato-
cellular  adenoma. To  keep  the  definitions  short,  the  lexicon
includes  comments  with  clarifying  information  that  can  be
general or LI-RADS–specific.

The lexicon is subdivided into broad categories of terms
including  general,  technical,  and  imaging  feature  terms.
The following sections provide a sample of terms from each
broad category. We present the terms and their definitions
as they appear in the lexicon.

General Terms
General  terms  are  those  that  refer  to  focal  abnormalities.
They  have  a  broad  COU  and  are  applicable  to  all  modal-
ities  (ie,  CT,  MRI,  gray-scale  US,  and  contrast-enhanced
US [CEUS]). The lexicon standardized these terms and es-
tablished  a  hierarchical  relationship  (Fig  1).  For  example,
observation  is  defined  as  an  area  that  is  distinctive  when
compared  with  the  background  liver  at  imaging.  Observa-
tions  may  be  true  lesions  (ie,  if  it  corresponds  to  a  patho-
logic  abnormality)  or  pseudolesions  (if  it  does  not  corre-
spond to a pathologic abnormality). If there is uncertainty
about  whether  an  observation  represents  a  true  lesion  or
a  pseudolesion,  the  term  observation  is  preferred  over  the
term  lesion.  Lesions  are  further  subdivided  into  nonmass-
like lesions (ie, those not distorting or destroying the paren-
chyma or other anatomic structures) and masses (ie, those
distorting or destroying the parenchyma or other anatomic
structures).  Ovoid  or  rounded  noncystic  lesions  measur-
ing  2  cm  or  smaller  can  be  referred  to  as  nodules.  While
pseudolesions  can  be  masslike  (ie,  hypertrophic  pseudo-
mass) or nonmasslike (ie, perfusional anomalies), there are
no terms applicable to such subgroups.

Technical Terms
Technical terms are those that refer to imaging, and they have
a  broad  COU.  The  lexicon  contains  technical  terms  for  CT,
MRI, gray-scale US, and CEUS. Among the technical terms,
imaging phases are particularly important to define because
they  have  profound  implications  for  the  detection,  appear-
ance,  and  interpretation  of  liver  observations. The  next  sec-
tion includes definitions for the various phases and a few key
clarifying comments from the lexicon. The AP and the portal
venous phase (PVP) are applicable to CT, MRI with extracel-
lular contrast agents, MRI with hepatobiliary contrast agents,
and  CEUS.  The  transitional  phase  (TP)  and  hepatobiliary
phase  (HBP)  are  only  applicable  to  MRI  with  hepatobiliary
contrast  agents,  while  the  delayed  phase  (DP)  is  applicable
only to MRI with extracellular contrast agents and MRI with
gadobenate. The late phase (LP) is applicable only to CEUS.

Imaging Phases

Arterial Phase.—The AP is the contrast-enhanced phase when
the hepatic artery and branches are fully enhancing and the
hepatic  veins  are  not  enhancing  more  than  the  liver  by  an-
tegrade  flow.  At  CEUS,  the  AP  usually  starts  approximately
10–15 seconds after injection of contrast material and lasts for
10–20  seconds.  At  CT  and  MRI,  the  AP  is  divided  into  two
temporal subtypes: early AP, when the portal vein is not en-
hancing or is enhancing less than the liver, and late AP, when
the portal vein is enhancing more than the liver. The AP is es-
sential for characterization of lesions with preferential arterial
blood supply (27,28). In general, the late AP is preferable to
the early AP because the former demonstrates nonrim APHE,
which is an imaging feature that is required for noninvasive
diagnosis of HCC, better than the early AP (1,29) (Figs 2–4).

Portal  Venous  Phase.—The  PVP  is  the  phase  after  the  AP
that occurs no more than 2 minutes after injection of a con-
trast agent, when the portal and hepatic veins are enhancing
more than the liver. At CEUS, the PVP usually starts approx-
imately  30–45  seconds  after  injection,  lasts  for  90–100  sec-
onds,  and  ends  at  approximately  2  minutes  after  injection.
At CT and MRI, the PVP images are acquired approximately
60–80 seconds after start of injection.

Late Phase.—The LP is the phase of CEUS after the AP and
the  PVP  when  the  portal  and  hepatic  veins  are  enhancing
but less so than they are during the PVP. The LP lasts from
the end of the PVP until there is clearance of microbubbles
from  the  circulation  at  approximately  4–6  minutes.  In  the
LP, the liver parenchyma is enhancing, but usually less than
it does in the PVP.

Delayed Phase.—The DP is the phase after the AP that occurs
at least 2 minutes after injection of an extracellular agent or
gadobenate dimeglumine, when the portal and hepatic veins
are  enhancing  more  than  the  liver.  The  DP  applies  to  CT,
extracellular  contrast
enhanced  MRI,  and  MRI  with  gado-
benate  dimeglumine  enhancement.  DP  images  are  typically
acquired 2–5 minutes after injection of contrast material. The
term DP does not apply to gadoxetate-enhanced MRI.

Transitional Phase.—The TP is the phase after the AP when im-
ages are acquired with an intravenous hepatobiliary contrast
agent, when the liver vessels and the hepatic parenchyma are
of similar signal intensity, which occurs between the PVP and
the HBP. The term applies to MRI with gadoxetate; although
the TP  does  occur  when  gadobenate  is  used, TP  images  are
usually not acquired with this agent. The TP typically occurs
2–5  minutes  after  injection  of  gadoxetate,  when  the  liver  is
enhancing because of both extracellular and intracellular dis-
tribution of the contrast agent. The onset of the TP may occur
less than 2 minutes or more than 5 minutes after injection in
a minority of patients.

Terms for Imaging Features
The terms for imaging features can be subdivided into those
that  have  a  broad  COU  and  those  with  a  LI-RADS–specific
COU. For instance, threshold growth is a term that is specific
to the LI-RADS and would not be applicable for use in a broad
COU. Although terms with a broad COU can be used in the
general population or in patients at high risk for HCC, some
of  these  terms  (eg,  washout)  have  specific  diagnostic  impli-
cations  in  the  LI-RADS,  because  they  are  used  in  assigning
LI-RADS  categories.  Care  should  be  taken  to  reserve  LI-
RADS–specific diagnostic implications to patients who meet
the  criteria  for  application  of  the  LI-RADS  (eg,  those  with
cirrhosis, chronic hepatitis B viral infection, or those with a
personal history of HCC).

Washout
Washout  is  defined  as  a  reduction  in  enhancement  from  an
earlier  to  a  later  phase,  resulting  in  hypoenhancement  rela-
tive to the liver (Fig 13). At CT or MRI, washout can manifest
as a change from hyperenhancing to hypoenhancing (Fig 14)
or  from  isoenhancing  to  hypoenhancing  (Fig  15).  At  CEUS,
washout manifests as one of the following: hyper- to hypoen-
hancement  (Fig  E2),  isoenhancement  to  hypoenhancement
(Fig  E3),  or  hypoenhancement  to  unequivocally  more  hy-
poenhancement (Fig 16).

Washout can be assessed only if images are obtained during
at least two contrast-enhanced phases (eg, AP followed by one
or more phases after the AP) so that the reduction in enhance-
ment over time can be assessed. Consequently, washout cannot
be assessed in an examination with a single contrast-enhanced
phase.  Washout  can  be  assessed  only  during  an  extracellular
phase after the AP, which is the PVP or the DP at CT, extracel-
lular contrast–enhanced MRI, and gadobenate-enhanced MRI
and only the PVP at gadoxetate-enhanced MRI. TP and HBP

the time of onset and degree. A time of onset of less than 60
seconds after injection of contrast material is considered early
washout,  and  a  time  of  onset  of  60  seconds  or  longer  after
injection is considered late washout. The degree of washout
can be mild or marked. Mild washout is less enhancing than
the liver but is not devoid of enhancement (ie, some enhance-
ment  persists)  (Fig  16).  If  mild  washout  becomes  marked
more than 2 minutes after injection of the contrast agent, it is
still characterized as mild. Marked enhancement is virtually
devoid of enhancement (ie, a “punched out” appearance) by 2
minutes after injection of the contrast agent (Fig 17).

The term washout and its subtypes have a broad COU. In
the context of the LI-RADS CT and MRI diagnostic algorithm,
peripheral  washout  is  an  LR-M  feature  and  nonperipheral
washout is a major feature of HCC. In the context of the LI-
RADS CEUS diagnostic algorithm, early or marked washout
is an LR-M feature, whereas late and mild washout is a major
feature of HCC.

Capsule
A capsule is defined as a smooth, uniform, sharp border at CT
or MRI that encloses most or all of an observation (Figs 18, E4).
This term has a LI-RADS–specific COU. Capsules are of one of
two subtypes: an enhancing capsule, which is a major feature
of  HCC,  and  a  nonenhancing  capsule,  which  is  an  ancillary
feature that favors diagnosis of HCC. An enhancing capsule is
visible as an enhancing rim during the PVP, DP, or TP, whereas
a nonenhancing capsule does not show enhancement during
any postcontrast phases (Fig E5). If a capsule is visible as an
enhancing rim during one phase and as a nonenhancing rim
during a different phase, it should be characterized as an en-
hancing capsule. The imaging feature capsule refers to the im-
aging appearance. The feature may correspond to a true tumor
capsule or a pseudocapsule, and the distinction between the
two  can  be  made  only  at  pathologic  examination.  A  capsule

Targetoid Appearance
Targetoid is defined as a target-like appearance at CT or MRI,
where the center and periphery of a mass have different im-
aging characteristics. The term has a broad COU. The pres-
ence  of  a  targetoid  appearance  suggests  intrahepatic  chol-
angiocarcinoma or other non-HCC malignancies, including
combined  HCC-cholangiocarcinoma  and  metastases  to  the
liver  from  an  extrahepatic  primary  tumor,  but  it  does  not
exclude HCC. There are five subtypes of targetoid morphol-
ogy,  including  rim  APHE  and  peripheral  washout,  which
were described in previous sections of this article. The third
subtype is delayed central enhancement (Fig 6), which is a
post-AP pattern in which the inner part of the observation
is more enhancing than is the periphery. This feature does
not apply to a central scar with delayed enhancement or to
observations  that  can  be  confidently  diagnosed  as  heman-
giomas  on  the  basis  of  other  features.  The  fourth  subtype
is targetoid diffusion restriction (Fig 20), which appears the
greatest in the periphery of the observation periphery. The
fifth subtype is a targetoid appearance during the TP or HBP
(Fig 21), in which the periphery of the observation is more
hypointense than the center. In the context of LI-RADS pop-
ulation, the presence of any of the targetoid features results
in categorization as LR-M.

Peripheral Discontinuous Nodular Enhancement
Peripheral discontinuous nodular enhancement is defined as ar-
eas of enhancement that are round or globular in shape and dis-
tributed discontinuously along the periphery of a lesion in the
early postcontrast phases and expand and appear approximately
the  same  as  the  blood  pool  in  intensity  in  subsequent  phases
(Fig 27). The term has a broad COU; is applicable to CEUS, CT,
and MRI; and is a diagnostic imaging feature of nonsclerosing
hemangiomas. This peripheral discontinuous nodular enhance-
ment  feature  reflects  a  temporal  enhancement  pattern;  there-
fore, its strict assessment requires acquisition of images during
two or more phases. However, a diagnosis of hemangioma can
be  made  on  the  basis  of  imaging  during  a  single  postcontrast
phase if the imaging features are sufficiently characteristic. In
such cases, the temporal pattern is inferred.

The  enhancing  areas  approximate  the  appearance  of  the
blood pool in intensity on images from all postcontrast phases.
If a hepatobiliary agent is administered, the enhancing areas
usually  become  isointense  and  then  hypointense  relative  to
the liver in the TP and HBP, similar to the appearance of the
vessels.  As  the  areas  of  enhancement  expand,  they  may  co-
alesce  to  become  continuous  or  may  fill  the  lesion  entirely,
and they may no longer appear round or globular.

Fade
Fade is defined as a reduction in enhancement relative to that
of  the  liver  from  hyperenhancement  in  an  earlier  phase  to
isoenhancement  or  minimal  hyperenhancement  in  all  later
phases. The term has a broad COU and is applicable to CEUS,
CT, and MRI (Figs 28–30). Fade can be assessed only if images
are  obtained  during  at  least  two  contrast-enhanced  phases
(eg, the AP followed by one or more phases after the AP), so
that the reduction in enhancement over time can be assessed.
Fade  can  appear  as  one  of  two  patterns:  (a)  hyperen-
hancement during the AP and isoenhancement to minimal
hyperenhancement  during  all  later  phases  or  (b)  hyperen-
hancement during the PVP and isoenhancement to minimal
hyperenhancement during all later phases (Fig 13).

When  hypoenhancement  relative  to  the  liver  is  seen
during  any  phase  after  the  AP,  the  pattern  should  not  be
characterized as fade.

Future Directions
The  latest  version  of  the  LI-RADS  lexicon  was  released  in
June 2021. The authors of the lexicon and this article recog-
nize  that  a  perfect  consensus  by  all  users  for  every  term  in
the lexicon is not possible, and some readers may dislike or
disagree  with  a  particular  term  or  definition.  Nevertheless,

adoption of the standardized lexicon is crucial to promoting
clarity and consistency of communication in clinical practice
and scientific literature (8). Over time, the lexicon will be im-
proved,  and  additional  terms  will  be  added  as  the  evidence
accumulates,  expert  consensus  matures,  and  user  feedback
is compiled.

The current lexicon focuses on subjective imaging features
and descriptions. As data on quantitative imaging biomark-
ers  are  compiled,  terms  related  to  quantitative  images  may
be  added.  Similarly,  as  evidence  accrues  and  matures,  the
lexicon may be expanded to incorporate prognostic imaging
terms and their implications, such as such features that may
help to predict long-term outcomes in patients with HCC.
