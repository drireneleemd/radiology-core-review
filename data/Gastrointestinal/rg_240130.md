Acute pancreatitis is a dynamic and complex pathologic con-
dition with a variable course and a wide spectrum of clinical
and imaging manifestations. The accompanying slide presen-
tation  discusses  the  clinical  and  morphologic  classifications
of acute pancreatitis.

The 2012 revised Atlanta classification, developed by the
Acute Pancreatitis Classification Working Group after a com-
prehensive review and modification of the original 1992 sys-
tem, stands as the most widely used clinically based classi-
fication  system  among  clinical  physicians  and  radiologists.
The goal of the revision was to integrate the latest concepts to
understand the disease and mitigate ambiguity and confu-
sion surrounding certain terms in the original classification.
The revision established a clinical and radiologic nomencla-
ture with more precise definitions and standardized termi-
nology  for  describing  and  categorizing  the  manifestations
associated with acute pancreatitis. The use of standardized
and disease-specific language is fundamental because it al-

lows effective, precise, and objective communication among
members of a multidisciplinary team.

The  consensus  classification  defined  diagnostic  criteria
for  acute  pancreatitis  and  subdivided  the  clinical  course  of
the disease into an early phase (<1 week) and a late phase (>1
week), as well as categorized its severity into mild, moderate,
and severe, based on the presence and duration of multiorgan
failure and other local and systemic complications.

Given  the  increasing  importance  of  imaging  evaluation
for the diagnosis and treatment of acute pancreatitis, the re-
vised  Atlanta  classification  primarily  relies  on  morphologic
imaging findings to define the various manifestations of the
disease  (Fig  1),  predominantly  through  contrast-enhanced
CT. This method is chosen as the primary tool for assessing
the imaging-based criteria because of its high precision and
widespread availability, which make it a practical option for
severely ill patients. Thus, acute pancreatitis is classified into

TEACHING POINTS
	 Developed by experts, the revised Atlanta classification is the only sys-
tem accepted by clinicians and radiologists to define and categorize the
manifestations of acute pancreatitis.

	 The morphologic classification of acute pancreatitis and the definition
of  local  collections  depend  on  the  presence  or  absence  of  necrosis.
Thus,  pancreatitis  is  divided  into  interstitial  edematous  pancreatitis
and necrotizing pancreatitis, and collections can be acute peripancre-
atic  fluid  collections  and  pseudocysts,  if  they  contain  only  fluid,  and
acute necrotic collection and walled-off necrosis, if they contain fluid
and necrotic debris.

	 In  addition  to  local  collections,  other  local  complications  should  be
identified, particularly in the context of necrotizing pancreatitis, such
as  infection,  pancreatic-enteric  fistulas,  biliary  obstruction,  vascular
complications, and mass effect.

of necrosis in the pancreatic parenchyma and/or peripancre-
atic tissues: interstitial edematous pancreatitis and necrotiz-
ing pancreatitis, respectively (Fig 2). Moreover, associated lo-
cal collections are classified into four subtypes based on their
composition  and  presence  since  the  onset  of  pancreatitis:
acute  peripancreatic  fluid  collections  and  pseudocysts  that
occur in interstitial edematous pancreatitis and contain only
fluid;  and  acute  necrotic  collections  and  walled-off  necrosis
that  occur  in  necrotizing  pancreatitis  and  contain  varying
amounts of fluid and solid components.

In addition to defining local collections, precise identifica-
tion and description of other local complications (particularly
in cases of necrotizing pancreatitis), such as vascular, gastro-
intestinal, biliary, inflammatory, or infectious complications,
also  are  essential  because  they  can  exacerbate  the  patient’s
condition and necessitate specific therapeutic management.

Although  the  use  of  standardized  definitions  facilitates
the categorization and description of manifestations associ-
ated with acute pancreatitis, the more complex forms of the
disease may present a formidable interpretive challenge for
radiologists.
