Cholecystectomy is one of the most common surgeries performed
in the United States. Although complications are uncommon, the
high incidence of this surgery means that a radiologist will likely
encounter these complications in practice. Complications may arise
in the immediate postoperative period or can be delayed for weeks,
months, or years after surgery. Vague and nonspecific symptoms
make clinical diagnosis challenging. As a result, multimodality im-
aging is important in postoperative evaluation. US and multidetec-
tor CT are the usual first-line imaging modalities. Hepatobiliary
scintigraphy, SPECT/CT, and MRI with conventional or gadox-
etate hepatobiliary contrast material are important and comple-
mentary modalities that are used for workup. The authors begin
with a brief discussion of surgical technique and expected postop-
erative findings and then describe complications organized into four
groups: (a) biliary complications, (b) stone-related complications,
(c) iatrogenic complications, and (d) gallbladder complications.
Biliary complications include bile leaks and bilomas, acute bili-
ary obstruction, and biliary stricture. Stone-related complications
include retained and recurrent stones and spillage of stones into
the peritoneum. Iatrogenic complications include hemorrhage,
vasculobiliary injury, arterial pseudoaneurysms, duodenal injury,
and migration of clips. Gallbladder complications include recurrent
cholecystitis after subtotal reconstituting cholecystectomy and un-
expected gallbladder cancer.

Introduction

Cholecystectomy is indicated for symptomatic diseases of the gall-
bladder and biliary tree, including symptomatic cholelithiasis, acute
cholecystitis, choledocholithiasis, and biliary dyskinesia. Relative
contraindications include untreated coagulopathy or advanced cir-
rhosis (1,2). Laparoscopic cholecystectomy is considered the stan-
dard of care for the treatment of gallstones. After this technique was
developed by European surgeons in the 1980s, it was introduced
to the broader general surgical community in the early 1990s and
was quickly incorporated into general surgical practice within 2–3
years. The rapid adoption of this technique as a routine surgical
procedure was unparalleled in surgical history and heralded the era
of minimally invasive surgery. The success of this technique is owed
to its demonstrated reduction in postoperative pain and length of
hospital stay, rapid recovery, minimal scarring, and overall decreased
medical costs (3,4). More than 750 000 cholecystectomies are now
performed annually in the United States, the majority of which are

The overall complication rate of laparoscopic

cholecystectomy is 1.6%–5.3% (8). The most
common complications (eg, wound infection, uri-
nary retention, and postoperative bleeding) are not
unique to cholecystectomy and typically do not re-

retained common bile duct stones, occurring in

0.32%–0.52% and 0.5% of cases, respectively (8).
Although complications of cholecystectomy are
rare, the high frequency of this procedure means
that a radiologist is likely to encounter them in
practice. Complications at multimodality imag-
ing may manifest in the immediate postoperative
period or be delayed for weeks, months, or even
years. Prompt recognition is important, because
when they are unrecognized, these complications
can result in severe morbidity or mortality (8).
The complications can be conceptually organized
into four groups: (a) biliary complications (eg, bile
leakage and bilomas, acute biliary obstruction, and
biliary stricture), (b) stone-related complications
(eg, retained and recurrent stones and spillage of
stones into the peritoneum), (c) iatrogenic compli-
cations (eg, hemorrhage, arterial pseudoaneurysm,
duodenal injury, and migration of clips), and
(d) gallbladder complications (eg, recurrent cho-
lecystitis after subtotal reconstituting cholecystec-
tomy and unexpected gallbladder cancer).

Laparoscopic Cholecystectomy
Overview and Technique
After insufflating the abdomen and inserting the
laparoscopic ports, the surgeon identifies the gall-
bladder and dissects it at the hepatocystic triangle
(ie, the triangular space bordered by the cystic
duct laterally, the common hepatic duct medially,
and the inferior edge of the liver superiorly). The
surgeon dissects the surrounding tissue and mobi-
lizes the gallbladder from the cystic plate. The goal
is to identify the critical view of safety, where the
surgeon visualizes only two structures entering the
gallbladder: the cystic duct and the cystic artery. If
the critical view is identified, the surgeon clips the
cystic duct and artery and removes the gallblad-
der. If the critical view of safety is not identi-
fied, the surgeon may convert the laparoscopic
procedure to open surgery, attempt to clarify the
anatomy with intraoperative cholangiography, or
perform a subtotal cholecystectomy (9).

Intraoperative cholangiography is performed by

inserting a cannula in the cystic duct and inject-
ing contrast material under fluoroscopic guidance.
This procedure can help to clarify the biliary
anatomy and identify cystic duct or common bile
duct stones and biliary injuries intraoperatively
(2). However, routine use is controversial, and ap-
plication of this procedure varies by institution.

A subtotal cholecystectomy is a well-described

alternative (ie, “bail-out”) procedure used when
the critical view of safety is not identified (10).
Other indications for subtotal cholecystectomy are
also related to factors that prevent safe removal of
the total gallbladder, including severe cholecys-
titis (eg, gangrenous or perforated cholecystitis)
and cirrhosis and portal hypertension (11). The

Biliary Complications

Biliary injuries are the most common complica-
tions after cholecystectomy. Bile leaks, bilomas,
or acute biliary obstruction usually manifest soon
after surgery, while biliary strictures often manifest
months or years after surgery.

Biliary injuries are more common with laparo-
scopic cholecystectomy than with open cholecys-
tectomy, occurring in 0.3%–0.5% of laparoscopic
surgical procedures, and therefore, rates of injury
have increased over time because of increasing
adoption of a laparoscopic approach (8). The signs
and symptoms of biliary injuries are often non-
specific, such as pain and fever with or without
jaundice and pruritis. Elevated serum bilirubin or
alkaline phosphatase levels are neither sensitive
nor specific for a biliary injury (9). Thus, recogniz-
ing that many bile injuries may manifest without
biliary ductal dilatation is important (14,15). Risk
factors include severe gallbladder inflammation or
gallbladder fossa adhesions, chronic cholecystitis,
and variant biliary anatomy, which may obscure or
alter the critical view of safety.

Conventional biliary anatomy is seen in only

60% of the population; however, many biliary
variants are unimportant to the performance of
cholecystectomy (16). One of the most common
surgically important variants is the right posterior
hepatic duct draining into the common hepatic
duct or into the cystic duct (Fig 3). In one study
of 73 cadavers, these variants were found to oc-
cur in 2.7% and 1.4% of individuals, respectively
(17). Another important variant, a subvesical bile
duct (previously termed the duct of Luschka) is
prone to injury because of its close proximity to
the surgical field (Fig 4). The precise definition of
the subvesical duct is debated, but it can be con-
sidered a bile duct that runs in close proximity to
the gallbladder fossa, either a superficial intra-
hepatic duct draining to the right hepatic duct,
a bile duct draining directly into the gallbladder,
or multiple small ducts in the connective tissue
between the liver and gallbladder. The prevalence
of a subvesical duct is reported to be 4%–10%
of the population; however, these are rarely seen
at imaging, presumably because of their small
size (18). Congenital anatomic variants of the
cystic duct occur in 18%–23% of the population
and increase the risk of bile duct injury during

Bile Leaks and Bilomas
Bile leaks occur in the immediate postoperative
period and most often arise from the cystic duct
and the subvesical bile duct (9). The leak may
occur because of a slipped cystic duct clip or
a dissection injury secondary to variant biliary
anatomy (15,16,18). Most patients present with
abdominal pain, and only a minority present
with biliary peritonitis (15,23). If a bile leak is
contained as a discreet collection of fluid, it is
termed a biloma. Bilomas are usually located
near the site of injury, classically in the gallblad-
der fossa, perihepatic space, or subcapsular
space of the liver. These fluid collections have a

gadoxetate dose is excreted in the biliary system,
which results in T1-weighted shortening of fluid in

MRI. The hepatobiliary phase occurs 10–120 min-

confirmatory data have involved imaging during
the hepatobiliary phase after a 20-minute delay,
which is usually sufficient for visualization of the
bile duct (27,28). However, a standard 20-minute
delay may be inadequate for a variety of reasons.
First, biliary excretion of gadoxetate is related to
hepatocyte function, so further delayed images
should be considered in patients with abnormal
liver function test results. Second, a bile leak may
not be evident with a 20-minute delay, even if the
intrahepatic bile ducts are opacified (14,29).

Biliary Stricture
Biliary strictures are a rare delayed complica-
tion of cholecystectomy that manifest months or
years after surgery (9,34,35). Patients can pres-

ent with nonspecific pain, jaundice, pruritis, or
infection. Unrecognized and untreated central
biliary strictures can result in secondary biliary
cirrhosis (34). Similarly, segmental strictures
may result in atrophy of a portion of the liver.
Strictures develop because of fibrosis adjacent to
surgical clips, after direct injury, or after thermal
injury during dissection. Like acute injuries,
variant biliary anatomy is a risk factor for biliary
stricture because of inadvertent injury or liga-
tion of an anomalous duct.

One of the most commonly encountered bili-
ary strictures after cholecystectomy is aberrant
stricturing of the right posterior hepatic duct.
This duct is prone to injury because it often
inserts on either the cystic duct or the common
duct near the cystic duct, leading to direct clip-
ping or injury. This injury has a specific pattern
on cross-sectional images, with segmental dilata-
tion of posterior right intrahepatic ducts, and the
dilated aberrant duct often can be traced to its
low insertion on the common duct (Fig 9). If the

Gallbladder perforation and subsequent gall-

should attempt to retrieve the stones and irrigate
the abdomen (45). The incidence of unretrieved

Vasculobiliary Injury
Vasculobiliary injury represents a concomitant
injury to a bile duct and a hepatic artery and/or
a portal vein (50). The most common injury is
to the right hepatic artery (Fig E2) because of its
anatomic proximity to the common hepatic duct,
which is mistakenly divided during cholecystec-
tomy. The true incidence of this injury is unknown
because routine angiography is not performed.
One review (50) reported that 12%–61% of
patients with a bile duct injury had a concur-
rent right hepatic artery injury. Isolated right
hepatic artery occlusion during cholecystectomy
is thought to occur frequently, but because of col-
lateral flow along the bile duct, patients are usually
asymptomatic, without clinically significant biliary
ischemia (50). However, concurrent biliary injury
affects this collateral arterial flow, most severely
with hilar injuries, which may result in bile duct
necrosis occurring over several months. In approx-
imately 10% of cases, slow infarction of the right
hepatic lobe occurs over several weeks, resulting
in areas of necrosis or eventual atrophy. In rare
cases, injury to the portal vein, common hepatic
artery, or proper hepatic artery occurs, which may
result in more rapid infarction (Fig E3), requir-
ing urgent right hepatectomy or a liver transplant
(50). Because of this high association of injuries,
multiphasic liver imaging is often used to evalu-
ate for vascular injury whenever a biliary injury is
suspected.

Arterial Pseudoaneurysm
Cystic or hepatic artery pseudoaneurysm is a rare
complication after cholecystectomy and may man-
ifest in the early postoperative period or be delayed

for multiple months. Patients may present with
the Quincke triad: jaundice, right upper quadrant
pain, and upper gastrointestinal bleeding, although
the clinical presentation can vary (51–53). The
underlying mechanism for formation of a pseu-
doaneurysm is uncertain, but it is hypothesized
that local inflammation and fibrosis combined
with electrocauterization during surgery injures
the arterial wall, leading to a pseudoaneurysm
(51,52). Bile leak is a risk factor for formation of
a pseudoaneurysm, because bile can damage the
vascular wall and predispose patients to formation
of a pseudoaneurysm (54).

At CT angiography, a pseudoaneurysm shows
a rounded, often heterogeneous mass in the porta
hepatis that enhances avidly during the arterial
phase and follows the blood pool in later phases
of imaging. MRCP may be performed because
of jaundice and suspicion for a retained stone.
The pseudoaneurysm may be seen as a signal
void in the hilum. T2-weighted MR images have
a variable appearance, depending on the size of
the pseudoaneurysm and whether flow voids or
a mural thrombus is present. Contrast-enhanced
T1-weighted MR images show a variable degree of
central enhancement, which is most intense dur-
ing the arterial phase. The wall is often of variable
thickness and may enhance during the delayed
phase. A mural thrombus may be seen, depend-
ing on the age of the pseudoaneurysm (Fig 18).
Treatment usually involves transarterial emboli-
zation or surgical ligation (51,52).

Clip Migration
Clip migration is a rare complication with fewer
than 100 cases reported between 1978 and 2008
(58). The underlying mechanism for clip migration
is poorly understood. One proposed mechanism
is that cystic duct necrosis results in erosion of the
clip into the lower-pressure biliary tree (59). Risk
factors for clip migration include the use of four
or more clips, local inflammation in the clipped
area, or a subtotal cholecystectomy (60). Patient

presentation with clip migration is usually delayed,
with a median time to presentation of 2 years (58).
The most common location to detect a migrated
clip is in the common bile duct, where it can cause
biliary obstruction or act as a nidus for formation
of choledocholithiasis (38,59).

At imaging, radiographs show a clip displaced

inferiorly, away from the other clips in the liver
hilum. CT shows a metallic filling defect in the
common bile duct. MRI findings are generally
indistinguishable from those of a stone, includ-
ing a T2-hypointense filling defect and associ-
ated susceptibility artifact (Fig 20). Comparing
the number and location of the clips to those in
prior examinations is often helpful. Treatment
usually involves ERCP with cannulation and
sweeping of the common duct, which is similar
to the treatment for choledocholithiasis.

Conclusion
Cholecystectomy is associated with several early
and delayed complications. Many of these com-
plications have vague and nonspecific clinical
signs and symptoms and can result in morbidity
and mortality if unrecognized. Consequently,
multimodality imaging is important in the evalu-
ation of these complications. Familiarity with the
imaging manifestations can assist the referring
providers with accurate diagnosis and treatment.
