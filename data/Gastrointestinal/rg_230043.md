Pitfalls for Diagnosis of Focal Benign and

Cirrhosis is the end stage of chronic liver disease and causes architectural distortion and perfusional anomalies. It is a major risk
factor for developing hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC). Common disease entities in noncirrhotic livers, such as hemangiomas, can
be rare in cirrhotic livers, and benign entities such as confluent hepatic fibrosis and focal nodular hyperplasia–like lesions may
mimic the appearance of malignancies,. HCC usually has typical imaging characteristics, such as the major features established
by the Liver Imaging Reporting and Data System. However, HCC can also have a spectrum of atypical or uncommon appearanc-
es, such as cystic HCC, hypovascular HCC, or macroscopic fat–containing HCC. HCCs with certain genetic mutations such as
CTNNB-1–mutated HCC can harbor unique imaging features not seen in other types of HCC. In addition, malignancies that are
less common than HCC, such as cholangiocarcinoma and metastases, which can be difficult to differentiate, can still occur in cir-
rhotic livers. Atypical imaging features of benign and malignant lesions can be challenging to accurately diagnose. Therefore, fa-
miliarity with these features and an understanding of the prevalence of disease entities in cirrhotic livers are key in the daily prac-
tice of radiologists for evaluation of cirrhotic livers. The authors illustrate the typical and atypical features of benign and malignant
lesions in cirrhosis and discuss the technical pitfalls and unique advantages associated with various imaging modalities in assessing
cirrhotic livers, including noncontrast and contrast-enhanced US, CT, and MRI.

agent injection) with mild washout (liver nodule is less enhanced than the sur-

mimic the enhancement characteristics of HCC with APHE and washout.

es  overlying  capsular  retraction  most  commonly  at  the  anterior  and  medial

Introduction
Cirrhosis  is  the  advanced  irreversible  stage  of  progressive
hepatic fibrosis, characterized by distortion of hepatic archi-
tecture and formation of regenerative nodules (1). Cirrhosis
is the 11th leading cause of death globally (2) and the ninth
most common cause of years of life lost for males (2,3). The
leading risk factors in developed countries include chronic vi-
ral hepatitis (hepatitis B and C) infection, alcohol-associated
liver  disease,  non–alcohol-associated  fatty  liver  disease,  and
hemochromatosis (1). Less common causes include autoim-
mune hepatitis, biliary cirrhosis, Wilson disease, and granulo-
matous liver disease. Vascular conditions such as right-sided
heart failure, Budd-Chiari syndrome, and Fontan circulation
can also predispose patients to cirrhosis.

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) is the most common pri-
mary tumor that occurs in cirrhotic livers. Preexisting cirrhosis
is found in more than 80% of people diagnosed with HCC (4).
The  risk  of  developing  HCC  is  10–25-fold  higher  in  patients
with chronic hepatitis B infection and up to 17-fold higher in
patients with hepatitis C–associated cirrhosis (5). According to
the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, in 2020 liver
cancer  was  the  sixth  most  common  cause  of  cancer-related
death in America  (6), of which hepatocellular  cancer makes
up  75%–85%  of  cases  (7). Worldwide,  HCC  is  the  third  most
common cause of cancer-related death according to the World
Health  Organization  (8).  Over  the  past  2  decades,  the  inci-
dence of HCC in the United States has tripled, while the 5-year

survival rate has remained below 12% (9). Although hepatitis
B vaccinations are expected to reduce the number of cases of
hepatitis B infection and therefore of HCC, studies predict that
the incidence of HCC will continue to rise until 2030 (10,11).

Despite  the  typical  imaging  characteristics,  HCC  can  have
a  spectrum  of  atypical  appearances.  Cystic  appearance,  mac-
roscopic fat content, and arterial hypovascularity have all been
observed in HCC determined at histopathologic examination.
With the recent advancement in next-generation sequencing,
the genomic pathogenesis of HCC has been better understood.
Certain  genetic  HCC  subtypes  have  unique  atypical  imaging
features. In addition, malignancies that are less common than
HCC, such as cholangiocarcinoma, can still occur in cirrhotic
livers and can be difficult to differentiate. Familiarization with
the typical and atypical appearances of HCC and non-HCC ma-
lignancies is key for accurate diagnosis and aids in genetic and
molecular subtyping of HCC for clinical management. Benign
focal  abnormalities  that  mimic  malignancies  in  cirrhotic  liv-
ers, including confluent fibrosis and focal nodular hyperplasia
(FNH)–like lesions, are also discussed in this article.

The most common imaging modalities used to assess cir-
rhotic livers are US, contrast-enhanced US (CEUS), CT, and
MRI for HCC surveillance and focal observation characteriza-
tion. We discuss the technical pitfalls and unique advantages
associated with these different imaging modalities.

Typical Imaging Findings of HCC
Typical HCC features have been established as major features
in  the  Liver  Imaging  Reporting  and  Data  System  (LI-RADS),
a classification system used to reflect the probability of malig-
nancy and HCC at imaging. LI-RADS applies to patients at high
risk for HCC, such as those with cirrhosis, chronic hepatitis B
infection without cirrhosis, and current or prior HCC. It is not
applicable to patients younger than 18 years or patients with
cirrhosis  secondary  to  congenital  hepatic  fibrosis  or  vascular
disorders such as Budd-Chiari syndrome, chronic portal vein
occlusion,  or  cardiac  congestion  (11).  The  major  features  in
LI-RADS  include  nonrim  arterial  phase  hyperenhancement
(APHE),  nonperipheral  washout,  enhancing  capsule,  and
threshold growth (Fig 1). Ancillary LI-RADS features that favor
HCC include a nodule-in-nodule appearance, more fat in the
mass than in the adjacent liver, nonenhancing capsule, mosaic
architecture, and blood products in a mass.

useful as a screening tool owing to its low cost, ease of access,
and lack of radiation. A meta-analysis that involved US with-

pooled sensitivity of 78% and a specificity of 89% (12).

Contrast-enhanced US

Advantages.—CEUS contrast agents remain purely intravas-
cular without the interstitial leakage seen with contrast agents
used at CT and MRI (13). The purely intravascular nature of

CEUS contrast agents allows a more sensitive detection of the
washout pattern compared with that of CT and MR (Fig S1,

favoring  HCC.  The  timing  and  degree  of  washout  in  CEUS
provide  additional  information  to  differentiate  HCC  from
non-HCC malignancies (ie, differentiation between the CEUS
LI-RADS 5 [LR-5] and LI-RADS M [LR-M] categories). Non-
HCC malignancies, such as intrahepatic cholangiocarcinoma
(iCCA) or metastases, usually have early-onset washout (de-
fined as washout first detected under 60 seconds) with marked
washout (virtually devoid of contrast agent within 2 minutes
of  injection).  Comparatively,  HCC  will  usually  demonstrate
late-onset washout (first detected later than 60 seconds after
contrast agent injection) with mild washout (liver nodule is
less enhanced than the surrounding liver, with some degree
of  persistent  contrast  enhancement)  (14)  (Fig  2).  In  a  study
in which 50 indeterminate nodules at MRI were assessed by
using CEUS, 14 were reclassified as HCC and two as cholan-
giocarcinoma (15). Thus, CEUS provides additional value to
CT  and  MRI  in  differentiating  between  HCC  and  non-HCC
malignancies (CEUS LR-M).

HCC or other hypervascular malignancies may be missed at

portal venous flow in patients with cirrhosis. CEUS involves

Pitfalls and Disadvantages.—The limitations of CEUS are sim-
ilar  to  those  of  gray-scale  US.  It  is  difficult  to  perform  CEUS

Assessment of washout in a severely fatty liver is challenging
because the highly echogenic background can result in a false
perception of washout (13). In addition, CEUS is typically lim-
ited to characterizing one observation per injection. A contra-
indication to CEUS is patient hypersensitivity to microspheres
or to any of the inactive ingredients in the contrast agent. CEUS
is highly dependent on operator experience and familiarity and
therefore has not been universally used in all institutions.

Computed Tomography

Advantages.—Multiphase  CT  is  routinely  used  owing  to  its
ease of access and high spatial resolution. It is applicable to
patients with a large body habitus, hepatosteatosis, heteroge-
neous  liver  parenchyma,  or  deep  focal  lesions,  where  US  is
limited  for  assessment.  In  comparison  with  MRI,  CT  is  less
affected by motion artifact or large-volume ascites.

Pitfalls  and  Disadvantages.—Deposition  of  fat  in  the  liver
can cause a diagnostic challenge when confluent or nodular,
as it may appear infiltrative or masslike (Fig S2). Globally, a
quarter of the adult population have nonalcoholic fatty liver
disease.  In  this  population,  the  disease  will  progress  to  ste-
atohepatitis (nonalcoholic steatohepatitis) in a small percent-
age, which is estimated to be 3%–5% of the population (18), of
which 10%–15% will develop cirrhosis (19). Typically, focal fat
tends to have a geographic configuration, with an absence of
mass effect, and may have vessels traversing the fat. If focal
fat in the liver represents a diagnostic dilemma, MRI can be
performed, as the loss of signal intensity in the out-of-phase
sequence compared with the in-phase sequence can help con-
firm the diagnosis (Fig S2).

Atypical and Less Common Appearances of HCC

Magnetic Resonance Imaging

Advantages.—MRI  has  superior  contrast  resolution  com-
pared  with  that  of  CT.  Systematic  reviews  that  compare  CT
with  MRI  have  shown  that  MRI  has  a  higher  sensitivity  for
detecting HCC (21). The use of multiple MRI sequences, such
as diffusion-weighted imaging, in-phase and out-of-phase im-
aging,  precontrast  T1-weighted  and  T2-weighted  sequences,
and hepatobiliary phase (HBP) imaging, allow radiologists to
assess ancillary features to diagnose HCC and to differentiate
malignant from benign lesions.

Pitfalls and Disadvantages.—The dielectric effect is an artifact
that is more pronounced in patients with ascites, a common
occurrence  in  patients  with  cirrhosis.  This  artifact  occurs
when the wavelength of the radiofrequency transmission field
is close to the size of the patient, producing areas of increased
and  decreased  signal  intensity  (22)  that  usually  manifest  as
darkening in the center of the image (Fig S4). The wavelength
of radiofrequency transmission at 3 T is approximately 26 cm
in soft tissue, which is close to the cross-sectional diameter of
the abdomen. In contrast, the wavelength at 1.5 T is 52 cm.
Therefore,  this  artifact  is  more  commonly  seen  with  a  3-T
magnet than with a 1.5 T magnet. Techniques to assuage this
artifact include imaging at 1.5 T instead of 3 T, imaging after
paracentesis, and use of dielectric pads.

When  a  hepatobiliary  contrast  agent  is  used,  the  transi-
tional phase typically refers to the phase acquired 2–5 minutes
after contrast agent injection, in which the liver vessels and he-
patic parenchyma have a similar signal intensity. During this
phase, the intra- and extracellular pools of contrast agent con-
tribute to parenchymal enhancement. Owing to the decrease
in functional hepatocytes, cirrhotic livers may demonstrate a
prolonged transitional phase (23). A pitfall when using a hepa-
tobiliary contrast agent is to characterize an observation with
washout when it occurs during the transitional phase and not
during the portal venous phase. This may not be a true wash-
out, as hypoenhancement of an observation in this phase may
be due to the relative hyperenhancement of the adjacent liver
parenchyma rather than to contrast enhancement washout of
the lesion, which is termed pseudo-washout.

With a decrease in hepatic function, fibrosis, and portosys-
temic  shunts  in  cirrhosis,  there  is  a  reduction  of  background
liver enhancement in the HBP, which can impair detection and
characterization of lesions. To ensure an adequate HBP, the sig-
nal intensity of background liver should be higher than that of
the portal vein and kidney (23). The HBP is usually acquired 20
minutes after administration of HCA. However, a further de-
lay may be required to allow time for the hepatocytes to uptake
contrast agent from the extracellular space.

CTNNB-1 (β-catenin)–mutated HCC
HBP  isointensity  on  gadoxetate-enhanced  MR  images  is  a
LI-RADS  ancillary  feature  that  favors  benignity.  However,
10%–15%  of  HCCs  are  hyperintense  in  the  HBP  (24,25).  The
uptake of contrast agent in focal liver lesions during the HBP
depends on their stage of differentiation, degree of functioning
hepatocytes,  and  organic  transporter–anion  polypeptide  1B3
(OATP1B3)  expression.  Generally,  HCCs  do  not  demonstrate
uptake  of  contrast  agent  in  the  HBP  owing  to  their  dediffer-
entiation and lack of normal hepatocytes. However, there are
subtypes of HCC that are more likely to demonstrate contrast
agent uptake during HBP. In approximately 16%–40% of cases
of  HCC,  a  CTNNB-1  mutation  that  encodes  aberrant  β-cat-
enin protein promotes OATP1B3 expression and is associated
with retention of gadoxetate contrast agent in the HBP (Fig 4)
(26,27). CTNNB-1–mutated HCC is less aggressive and associ-
ated with a better prognosis compared with that of CTNNB-1
wild-type HCC (28) and therefore is important to distinguish
clinically. Helpful imaging features associated with CTNNB-1–
mutated HCC also include peritumoral enhancement, defined
as focal or patchy enhancement contiguous with the tumor that
is  greater  than  that  of  the  background  liver  parenchyma  and
persists from the later arterial to delayed phase (29).

Cystic HCC
Although rare, cystic degeneration of HCC has been reported
in a few studies (30–33). The pathogenesis of this phenomenon
has not been elucidated, with proposed possible mechanisms
related  to  arterial  thrombosis,  inflammation,  rapid  tumor
growth,  and  immune  cell–mediated  tumor  necrosis  (34,35).
Owing to the cystic appearance of these HCCs, determining a
diagnosis can be challenging, especially at initial patient pre-
sentation, and possible misdiagnoses include a mucinous cys-
tic neoplasm, cystic metastasis, or cholangiocarcinoma. Cystic
degeneration  of  HCC  usually  manifests  as  a  hypoattenuating
lesion  with  peripheral  rim  enhancement  (Fig  5)  (36),  similar
to mucinous cystic neoplasm of the liver. Radiologists need to
be familiar with this phenomenon, as HCC with these atypical
findings can be difficult to diagnose solely at imaging. Certain
features  can  be  helpful  to  differentiate  HCC  from  mucinous
cystic neoplasm. The presence of fine septa and calcifications
strongly favors mucinous cystic neoplasm over cystic HCC (37).
In addition, mucinous cystic neoplasm is rare, and cirrhosis is
not a predisposing factor for mucinous cystic neoplasm, with
a small study demonstrating background cirrhosis in only 2%
of  patients  with  a  mucinous  cystic  neoplasm  (38).  Common
cystic-appearing  liver  metastases  include  neuroendocrine  tu-
mors,  gastrointestinal  stromal  tumors,  colorectal  cancer,  and
lung cancer (38). However, these patients often have a history
of previous malignancy.

HCC with Fat Content
Fatty change can be found in up to 20%–35% of HCCs. Small
and well-differentiated HCCs are more likely to have diffuse
fat,  and  larger  lesions  are  more  likely  to  have  focal  regions
of fat (39). Fat in a mass, more than that found in the adja-
cent liver, is a LI-RADS ancillary feature that favors HCC. The
mechanism  for  fat  development  in  HCC  is  not  fully  under-
stood, but some hypotheses exist. For example, as a dysplastic
nodule  develops  into  HCC,  there  is  an  associated  transition
from  the  portal  venous  supply  to  the  arterial  supply,  which
may  cause  relative  ischemia  that  leads  to  fatty  change  (40).
Fat-containing HCC tends to have fewer intralesional arteries
(40)  compared  with  non–fat-containing  HCC  and  therefore
may not demonstrate typical APHE (Fig 6).

Fat in HCC can be intravoxel (ie, microscopic), macroscopic,
or both. Typically, fat content in HCC manifests as intravoxel
fat,  as  demonstrated  by  decreased  signal  intensity  on  op-
posed-phase T1 images compared with on the in-phase images.
This  may  be  misinterpreted  as  an  adenoma  because  certain
subtypes of adenoma can contain intravoxel fat. HCC may also
show  macroscopic  fat,  suggested  by  attenuation  between  10
and 100 HU on nonenhanced CT images (Fig 6). This may be
misinterpreted as an angiomyolipoma or lipoma. A clue to dif-
ferentiate fat-containing HCC from hepatic angiomyolipoma is
that an angiomyolipoma will have more enhancement of the
fatty component, whereas the fat component of HCC will have
poor enhancement, as it is minimally vascular (41).

Hypovascular HCC
Most HCCs demonstrate arterial phase hypervascularity. How-
ever, 10%–18% are hypovascular and enhance equal to or less

than the surrounding parenchyma in the arterial phase (Fig 7)
(42). These are typically smaller and highly well-differentiated
tumors (42) with a better prognosis. They are usually less ag-
gressive  than  hypervascular  HCC,  with  a  lower  α-fetoprotein
level and low incidence of microvascular invasion (43). As the
tumor increases in size, it is more likely to become hypervascu-
lar, particularly when it is larger than 1.5 cm (43). While these
tumors remain hypovascular in the arterial phase, clues to the
diagnosis  are  that  these  lesions  may  show  further  washout
and develop a pseudocapsule in the delayed phase. Diffusion
restriction  is  also  strongly  associated  with  progression  to  hy-
pervascular HCC (44). Hypovascularity can also be observed in
poorly differentiated HCC owing to the loss of intact sinusoidal
structures and the inability to take up a visible amount of con-
trast agent in the arterial phase (45). Diffusion-weighted imag-
ing and CEUS have been shown to be helpful for distinguishing
poorly differentiated from well-differentiated HCC (45,46).

Nodule-in-Nodule HCC
LI-RADS  uses  nodule-in-nodule  architecture  as  an  ancillary
feature  that  favors  HCC.  The  nodule-in-nodule  appearance  is
defined as the presence of a smaller inner nodule that has dif-
ferent imaging features than the larger outer nodule (Fig S5)
(47,48).  The  pathogenesis  is  due  to  the  clonal  expansion  of
highly dedifferentiated cells that are more advanced along the
hepatocarcinogenesis  pathway,  within  the  outer  background
of  the  regenerative  or  dysplastic  nodule,  or  poorly  differenti-
ated  HCC  within  the  background  of  well-differentiated  HCC
(47). On CT and MR images, the inner nodule can show HCC-
like  features  with  arterial  hyperenhancement  and  washout,

Other Malignancies Mimicking HCC

Cholangiocarcinoma
iCCA  is  the  second  most  common  primary  hepatic  malig-
nancy in cirrhotic livers after HCC. iCCAs can mimic HCCs,
particularly  when  they  are  small  (<3  cm),  as  iCCAs  can  be
hypervascular (50). When the tumor is larger than 3 cm, iCCA
usually  has  rim  enhancement  in  the  arterial  phase  (Fig  8).
Other helpful differentiating factors include peripheral wash-
out and a targetoid appearance on diffusion-weighted images
and HBP images, capsular retraction, and segmental bile duct
dilatation (51–54). CEUS  is a useful modality to help differ-
entiate  HCC  from  non-HCC  malignancy  owing  to  the  early
and marked washout seen in non-HCC malignancy. Ancillary
findings  that  favor  HCC  over  iCCA  include  intralesional  fat
and intrinsic T1 hyperintensity.

Metastases
Metastases are the most common malignancy in noncirrhotic
livers but are rare in cirrhotic livers, which may be partially

attributed to portal hypertension or hepatofugal portal venous
flow  associated  with  advanced  cirrhosis  preventing  cancer
cells from seeding the liver (55,56). Another hypothesis sug-
gests that Kupffer cell activation in cirrhosis sensitizes meta-
static colon cancer cells to immune-mediated apoptosis (57).
Metastases in cirrhotic livers have imaging features similar to
those in noncirrhotic livers, including mild hyperintensity on
T2-weighted  images  and  rim  enhancement  on  postcontrast
images.  Hypervascular  metastases,  such  as  from  a  neuroen-
docrine tumor (Fig 9) or renal cell carcinoma, should be con-
sidered  in  the  relevant  clinical  context,  as  these  may  mimic
the  enhancement  characteristics  of  HCC  with  APHE  and
washout.  When  a  malignant-appearing  lesion  in  a  cirrhotic
liver  does  not  have  the  classic  features  of  HCC,  tissue  sam-
pling serves as the standard method for diagnosis. In addition,
CEUS can provide additional clues to differentiate HCC from
non-HCC  malignancies.  The  washout  of  non-HCC  malig-
nancies such as metastasis and cholangiocarcinoma is rapid
(30–50 seconds) and marked at CEUS, whereas the washout
of HCC mostly occurs much later and is milder (13,58).

Sclerosing  hemangiomas  (SHs)  have  different  radiologic

owing to its real-time nature (14).

Other Considerations in Cirrhotic Liver

Confluent Hepatic Fibrosis
Confluent  hepatic  fibrosis  (CHF)  occurs  owing  to  replace-
ment  of  hepatic  parenchyma  with  fibrotic  tissue,  which  oc-
curs  in  long-standing  cirrhosis.  The  typical  appearance  of
CHF includes a wedge-shaped area with a wide base toward
the liver capsule (68), which radiates from the porta hepatis
and  causes  overlying  capsular  retraction  most  commonly  at
the anterior and medial segments (69). There is often associ-
ated progressive contraction over time (49,69) (Fig 12). CHF
can present a diagnostic challenge, as it may mimic cholan-
giocarcinoma, the second most common form of malignancy
in the cirrhotic liver. Imaging features shared by the two en-
tities  include  mild  to  moderate  T2  hyperintensity,  capsular
retraction,  and  lack  of  contrast  agent  retention  in  the  HBP.
Distinguishing features include the location, shape, presence
of  biliary  dilatation,  and  enhancement  characteristics.  CHF
typically involves subcapsular segments IV, VII, or VIII with
wedge-shaped  or  well-delineated  borders,  while  cholangio-
carcinoma can manifest in any segment as a lobulated lesion.
CHF is not associated with biliary dilatation and lacks re-
striction,  whereas  biliary  dilatation  is  more  commonly  seen
in  cholangiocarcinoma.  CHF  demonstrates  progressive  en-
hancement  in  the  delayed  phase,  similar  to  that  of  fibrotic
lesions. Occasionally, mild arterial hyperenhancement can be
observed in CHF, secondary to the presence of inflammation
(70) (Fig 12). Cholangiocarcinoma typically demonstrates ar-

terial rim enhancement with progressive central filling in the
portal venous and delayed phases.

Peribiliary Cysts in Cirrhosis
Peribiliary cysts have the same characteristics as parenchymal
cysts. However, they manifest in a characteristic location, with
clustering in the peribiliary regions and on both sides of the
portal vein, as opposed to biliary dilatation, which is located
on only one side of the portal vein (Fig S6) (50,68). Peribiliary
cysts are usually small (<2 cm) and do not communicate with
the biliary tree (71). The mechanism is thought to result from
peribiliary gland obstruction and is present in 9% of patients
with  cirrhosis,  more  commonly  in  cirrhosis  due  to  fibrocys-
tic disease or alcohol. These can be mistaken for dilated bile
ducts. Occasionally, they can be clustered and resemble mul-
tilocular  malignancies  or  cause  mass  effect  on  adjacent  bile
ducts, resulting in cholestasis. A clue to accurate diagnosis is
their characteristic imaging features similar to those of simple
cysts, with thin walls and no internal enhancement.

Perfusion Anomalies
When typical in appearance, perfusion anomalies (PAs) do not
pose  a  diagnostic  dilemma. They  usually  appear  as  a  wedge-
shaped subcapsular observation with APHE that fades in the
portal venous and delayed phases. They are isointense on T1-
and T2-weighted noncontrast MR images. Given that these are
not  true  masses,  there  should  be  no  mass  effect,  and  vessels
may traverse PAs. Sometimes PAs can be more focal and round,
therefore mimicking a true lesion with arterial hyperenhance-
ment  (Fig  13A),  especially  when  new.  Noncontrast  US  and
contrast-enhanced CEUS are useful modalities to differentiate
a  true  lesion  from  PAs.  Also,  stability  over  time  at  follow-up
imaging helps confirm the benignity of these findings (Fig 13).
