The  mammographic  imaging  features  of  architectural  dis-
tortion  (AD)  are  linear  spiculations  that  radiate  from  a
central  point  within  the  tissue,  appearing  as  delicate  lin-
ear strands extending outward (Fig 1). At US, AD may man-
ifest  as  an  irregular  mass  with  associated  distortion  of  the
surrounding  parenchymal  tissue,  such  as  straightening  or
bending of the Cooper ligaments (Fig 2). Focal retraction or
distortion of the breast tissue that creates an irregular con-
tour  represents  another  distinct  feature  seen  at  MRI  as  an
enhancing or nonenhancing finding.

AD  is  seen  in  4%  of  breast  cancers  and  found  on  6%  of
abnormal  screening  mammograms.  Postsurgical  scars  are
by  far  the  most  common  cause  of  AD.  A  study  by  Sickles,
performed  before  the  advent  of  digital  breast  tomosynthe-
sis  (DBT),  highlighted  the  prevalence  of  AD  on  over  80%
of  mammograms  obtained  up  to  6  months  after  surgical
biopsy,  with  its  appearance  decreasing  over  time.  Benign
causes of AD can include radial scars and complex scleros-
ing lesions, sclerosing adenosis, and fat necrosis. The most
common malignant causes of AD are invasive ductal carci-

TEACHING POINTS
	 Recognizing architectural distortion, which is present on 6% of screen-
ing  mammograms  and  characterized  by  linear  spiculations  without  a
visible mass, is crucial for diagnosing breast cancer.

	 Architectural distortion, which can be most commonly caused by postsur-
gical scars, encompasses a spectrum of causes, from benign lesions such
as radial scars to malignant tumors such as invasive ductal carcinoma.
	 Subtle distortion, which is pronounced at DBT, is notorious for being a
missed sign of breast cancer and necessitates a systematic correlation
of multimodality imaging findings with clinical and pathologic findings.
	 DBT  has  been  shown  to  have  better  sensitivity  for  subtle  architectural
distortion when compared with that of two-dimensional mammography.

There  are  several  pitfalls  associated  with  identification
of AD. Heterogeneous or extremely dense breast tissue may
limit AD visibility at mammography, as the overlapping tis-
sue may obscure AD. Further, AD that appears on only one
imaging view may occur due to the orientation of the AD rel-
ative to the imaging plane and may lead to underestimation
of its significance.

The  accompanying  slide  presentation  provides  multi-
modal  examples  of  AD  with  pathologic  correlation  and  rec-
ommended  management.  The  aim  is  to  help  the  radiologist
understand the differential diagnosis of AD, suggest suitable
management,  and  correlate  radiologic  findings  with  the  re-
sults  of  pathologic  analysis.  AD  requires  a  thorough  clinical
and imaging evaluation.
