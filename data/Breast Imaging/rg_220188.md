Update on Lobular Neoplasia

Samantha L. Heller, PhD, MD • Yiming Gao, MD

Author affiliations, funding, and conflicts of interest are listed at the end of this article.

Lobular neoplasia (LN) is a histopathologic entity that encompasses both lobular carcinoma in situ (LCIS) and atypical lobular
hyperplasia (ALH). Management of LN is known to be variable and institutionally dependent. The variability in approach after a
diagnosis of LN at percutaneous breast biopsy derives in part from heterogeneity in the literature, resulting in a range of reported
upgrade rates to malignancy after initial identification at percutaneous biopsy, and also from historical shifts in understanding of
the natural history of LN. It has become increasingly recognized that not all LN is the same and that distinct variants of LN such
as pleomorphic LCIS and florid LCIS have distinct natural histories and distinct likelihoods of upgrade to malignancy. In addition,
it is also increasingly understood that appropriate management of LN relies on scrupulous radiologic-pathologic correlation. This
review details the imaging features and histopathologic nature of ALH, classic-type LCIS, and the LCIS variants; addresses changes
in the historical understanding of this entity contributing to confusion regarding its management; and discusses the importance
of performing radiologic-pathologic correlation after percutaneous biopsy to help guide appropriate management steps when LN
is encountered. In addition to the short-term implications of an LN diagnosis in terms of upgrade and surgical outcomes, the long-
term implications of an LN diagnosis regarding risk of developing a later breast cancer are examined.

Introduction
Lobular  neoplasia  (LN)  is  a  histopathologic  entity  that  en-
compasses both lobular carcinoma in situ (LCIS) and atypi-
cal lobular hyperplasia (ALH). Management of LN is known
to be variable and institutionally dependent. A 2019 survey
of academic imaging centers in the United States found that
71% of respondents thought that LCIS should be excised and
61% thought that ALH should be excised. However, a sizable
minority  opted  for  surveillance  of  both  (1).  Another  case-
based scenario survey from Canada also demonstrated simi-

lar variability in recommendations for surveillance, risk-re-
duction  management,  and  surgical  excision  after  a  classic
LCIS diagnosis (2).

The  variability  in  management  approaches  after  LN  is
identified at percutaneous breast biopsy has several causes.
First,  the  literature  evaluating  upgrade  to  either  in  situ  or
invasive cancer in the setting of an LN percutaneous biopsy
result is heterogeneous. Studies are often small, single-insti-
tution, and performed with variable biopsy devices and vary-

Historical Shifts in Understanding LN
Historical changes in understanding of LN have contributed
to confusion regarding its management. In 1919, Ewing (3)
referred to LN as an “atypical proliferation of acinar cells.”
In 1941, Foote and Stewart (4) described LCIS as an invasive
cancer precursor for which mastectomy was considered to be
appropriate. Then in 1978, Haagensen et al (5) observed that
LCIS had only low rates of progression to cancer. The authors
opined that LN was a risk marker for later cancer develop-
ment but not a likely precursor of malignancy, and therefore

recommended surveillance with clinical breast palpation to
be performed every 4 months instead of mastectomy.

In  1992,  Eusebi  et  al  (6)  realized  that  not  all  LCIS  was
the same. Through their description and recognition of the
variant pleomorphic LCIS, Eusebi et al (6) opened the door
to  understanding  that  LCIS  is  a  heterogeneous  entity  and
that distinct management strategies for distinct types of LN
might be appropriate. Today, LN is understood to be both a
risk  marker  for  later  development  of  breast  cancer  and,  in
some  forms,  a  nonobligate  precursor.  This  recognition  has
further shaped management approaches.

Evidence That LN Is a Nonobligate Cancer
Precursor
How do we know that LN may sometimes lead to cancer? In
women with a history of LN, there is a greater lifetime risk
of cancer (7,8). Although future malignancy may be ductal
or lobular and may occur in either breast, later cancers are
more likely to occur in the ispsilateral breast and are more
likely to be lobular (9–11).

In  addition,  genetic  and  molecular  studies  have  demon-
strated  that  LN  and  invasive  lobular  carcinoma  (ILC)  are
likely to be found in association with each other and are phe-
notypically and genetically similar. Both usually demonstrate
loss  of  chromosome  1q,  recurrent  deletions  of  16q,  and  loss
of  E-cadherin  (due  to  mutations  of  the  CDH1  gene  on  16q)
(12). Shared genetic changes and clonal relationships are fre-
quently observed among associated LN and ILC (13–15). One
study that evaluated mastectomy specimens in 50 cases with
copy number profiling and whole exome sequencing demon-
strated  high  numbers  of  clonal  relationships  between  LCIS
and ILC in the same specimen. Specifically, the authors found
concordant  gene  copy  number  changes  and  identical  muta-
tions in LCIS and ILC within the same patient, again suggest-
ing that LCIS serves as a precursor lesion.

LN Types and Upgrade Rates
It is critical to understand that LN is a heterogeneous disease.
Appropriate management—in practice deciding between sur-
veillance  versus  surgical  referral  or  excision—has  been  insti-
tutionally variable and controversial based on a wide range of
reported upgrade rates to either in situ or invasive malignancy
of 0%–43% for excised LCIS and 0%–67% for excised ALH (16).
However, once it is understood that there are variant subtypes
of LN with distinct natural histories and that certain types of
LN are more likely to be associated with malignancy, the broad
upgrade  rate  ranges  can  be  broken  down  and  management
approaches can be more evidence based. With this in mind, it
is important to delve more deeply into the imaging and histo-
pathologic features of LN.

hesion protein. Two cell types may be seen: A cells, which are
small with minimal cytoplasm and homogeneous nuclei; and B

is often bilateral and most commonly asymptomatic. Nonvari-

not the imaging-targeted entity but that it just happened to be
seen by the pathologist in histopathologic samples (Fig 1) (17).
However,  it  may  rarely  (<2%  of  the  time)  be  associated  with
imaging findings, including calcifications (18,19).

ALH and classic LCIS would not be expected to appear as
mass lesions, although they may be found in association with
mass  lesions  such  as  fibroadenomas. They  may  also  be  asso-

mens and in 0.04%–1.2% of breast reduction tissue specimens,
but its true natural incidence is unknown precisely because it is
often undetected (19). However, the incidence is noted to have
increased in the 1980s, especially in women over age 50 years,
a finding attributed in large part to the rise of screening (20).

The Variants (Nonclassic LN)
The LN variants are relatively rare and, per the World Health
Organization (WHO) classification, include pleomorphic LCIS
and florid LCIS. The latter was added in 2019. LCIS described
as necrotic should also be placed in the variant category.

In contrast to classic LCIS and ALH, the variants are more
commonly  found  in  postmenopausal  women  (10,22).  In  a
study  that  evaluated  179  women  with  pleomorphic  LCIS,
the mean age at diagnosis was 60 years (23). In contrast to
classic LCIS, the variants are more likely to be noninciden-
tal findings, meaning that the pathologic entity is associated
with the imaging-targeted finding (most commonly  calcifi-
cations at mammography) (Figs 3–5) (10,24).

The  variants  are  also  more  likely  to  be  upgraded  to  and
associated  with  malignancy.  Upgrade  rates  to  malignancy
for  pleomorphic  LCIS  and  florid  LCIS  are  reported  to  be
25%–60% (25). Classic LCIS and the variants may be found
together in the same biopsy or surgical specimen (17).

Pleomorphic  LCIS.—At  histopathologic  analysis,  pleomor-
phic  LCIS  is  composed  of  large  pleomorphic  discohesive
cells  with  marked  nuclear  atypia,  akin  to  the  high-grade
nuclei  seen  in  ductal  carcinoma  in  situ  (DCIS)  (26).  Sig-
net-ring cell morphology may be seen, as in classic LCIS (Fig
3).  High-grade  cytomorphology,  massive  acinar  distention,
comedo necrosis, and microcalcification are commonly seen
(Figs 3, 4), although necrosis and calcification are not man-
dated  for  diagnosis  (17).  Loss  of  E-cadherin  expression  is
typical. While classic LCIS is usually estrogen receptor (ER)
and progesterone receptor (PR) positive and human epider-
mal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) negative, pleomorphic
LCIS is more varied in its receptor profile and while typically
HER2 negative may also be HER2 positive (19,27).

At imaging, pleomorphic LCIS most commonly manifests
as calcifications at mammography, but it may also manifest as
a mass lesion or distortion and may be noncalcified. A retro-
spective review of pleomorphic LCIS found that of 16 cases of
nonupgraded pleomorphic LCIS, 13 (81%) manifested as fine
pleomorphic calcifications, one (6%) as distortion with calci-
fications, one (6%) as a mass lesion, and one (6%) as nonmass
enhancement at MRI. The median imaging size was 11 mm,
with a mean of 14 mm and range of 3–47 mm (24). Another

study  that  reported  on  37  cases  of  pleomorphic  LCIS  found
that 31 cases (84%) manifested as calcifications, although fur-
ther descriptors were not reported (24,28).

Florid LCIS.—Florid LCIS demonstrates nuclear features akin
to  those  of  classic  LCIS.  Like  classic  LCIS,  florid  LCIS  may
have  type  A  and/or  type  B  cells.  However,  the  architectural
features of florid LCIS are distinct from those of classic LCIS,
with  mass-forming  marked  distention  of  the  acini  or  ducts,
which are filled with fairly bland cells and very little stroma
(Fig 5) (25). Importantly, florid LCIS is not synonymous with
extensive LCIS, a term denoting widespread terminal duct lob-
ular unit (TDLU) involvement without acinar distention (19).
As with pleomorphic LCIS, comedo necrosis and calcifica-
tions  are  commonly  present.  Like  pleomorphic  LCIS,  florid
LCIS is typically associated with calcifications at imaging, but
it may also appear as a mass lesion or distortion and may be
noncalcified. As with classic LCIS and pleomorphic LCIS, loss
of E-cadherin expression is typical. Florid LCIS is typically ER
and PR positive, although it may also be HER2 positive.

Management of LCIS
To determine appropriate management of LN, the histopatho-
logic type of LN must be identified and radiologic- pathologic
correlation with assessment of any discordance must be per-
formed.  The  high  upgrade  rates  for  pleomorphic  LCIS  and
florid LCIS lie behind recommendations to treat these lesions
with  surgical  excision  (26).  The  WHO  Classification  of  Tu-
mours  (fifth  edition)  specifically  recommends  excision  for
both pleomorphic LCIS and florid LCIS (10).

However, it should be recognized that in 2018, the eighth
edition  of  the  American  Joint  Committee  on  Cancer  TNM
staging  revised  its  guidelines  for  LCIS.  In  this  edition,  LCIS
was removed from the staging system and considered benign.
There was debate about whether to include pleomorphic LCIS
in the staging system as pTis, but the committee decided that
there were insufficient outcomes data to do so (26,31). A dis-
cussion  of  treatment  of  the  variants  is  beyond  the  scope  of
this review.

Radiologic-Pathologic Correlation and Evaluation for
Discordance
As stated in the American College of Radiology (ACR) prac-
tice parameters for breast biopsies, the radiologist must eval-
uate  biopsy  results  for  discordance  and  thereafter  make  an
appropriate management recommendation (32,33). Assessing
discordance  and  sampling  entails  review  of  the  nature  and
level of suspicion of the imaging finding and assessment of ap-
propriate sampling of the targeted lesion. In general, the needle
gauge used, number of cores, and use of vacuum-assisted de-
vices have been shown to impact upgrade rates. Smaller needle
gauge and nonvacuum technique are more likely to lead to up-
grade owing to greater chance of undersampling (34), although
not  all  studies  have  demonstrated  such  an  association  for  LN
specifically (35,36).

Regardless  of  the  needle  gauge  or  device  used,  the  histo-
pathologic result must be evaluated to assess whether it explains
the imaging target. A histopathologic result of ALH alone would
not appropriately explain a mass lesion seen at US (Fig 6). If a
result of LN does not explain the imaging finding or match the
level of suspicion, the result should be termed discordant, and
this should be recorded as an addendum to the biopsy report.
Further tissue should be obtained with repeat biopsy or surgical
excision (Fig 7). In general, a multidisciplinary team approach
including discussion as to histopathologic findings with the pa-

thologist is critical and will allow the most optimal evaluation of
concordance and recommendations for management.

Evidence from the Literature
The  importance  of  identifying  LCIS  variants  and  performing
radiologic-pathologic  correlation  for  assessment  of  concor-
dance  in  determining  appropriate  LN  management  has  been
increasingly  recognized.  Multiple  studies  have  shown  that
incidental classic LCIS and ALH have very low upgrade rates
(30,37–39). In contrast to classic LCIS and ALH, discordant le-
sions  and  the  variants  have  higher  upgrade  rates,  warranting
excision (30,37,39).

A recent systematic review and meta-analysis that evaluated
LN upgrade risk to malignancy illustrates this (16). The authors
included  articles  published  from  1998  to  2020  that  reported
rates  of  upgrade  to  malignancy  for  classic  LN,  identifying  65
studies. They determined which studies reported on exclusion
of variant LCIS, imaging-discordant lesions, and outcomes for
at least 70% of lesions. A separate analysis was then performed
for the 16 studies that met all three criteria, and pooled upgrade
rates were estimated using random-effects models.

Upgrade  rates  in  all  65  studies  included  in  the  system-
atic  review  were  0%–67%  for  excised  ALH  and  0%–43%  for
excised LCIS (16). However, the overall pooled upgrade rate
to invasive or in situ disease in the 16 studies was only 3.1%
(95% CI: 1.8, 5.2). The upgrade rate to invasive disease alone
was  1.3%  (95%  CI:  0.7,  2.4).  Isolated  ALH  demonstrated  a
pooled upgrade rate to all malignancy of 2.5% (95% CI: 1.6,
3.9) and to invasive disease alone of 0.4% (95% CI: 0.0, 4.2).
LCIS demonstrated a pooled upgrade rate to any malignancy
of 5.8% (95% CI: 2.9, 11.3) and to invasive disease alone of
3.5% (95% CI: 2.0, 5.9).

The  optimal  approach  for  assessing  the  literature  and  for
illustrating  the  impact  of  a  radiologic-pathologic–sensitive
approach  can  be  demonstrated  by  focusing  on  a  representa-
tive individual study. In a 2008 retrospective single-institution
study by Hwang and colleagues (30), upgrade rates of LN af-
ter multimodality core biopsy were assessed with attention to
variants and discordance. There were 48 cases of ALH, with an
upgrade rate to malignancy of only 2% (one of 48 cases). How-
ever, nine of 39 cases (23%) of pure LCIS were upgraded, a high
percentage, suggesting that LCIS would not be appropriate for
surveillance.

However,  the  authors  also  categorized  LCIS  cases  by  sub-
type.  After  excluding  the  variants  (described  as  pleomorphic
LCIS and LCIS with necrosis in this particular study), classic
LCIS was found to have a much lower (albeit still high) upgrade
rate of 12% (three of 26 cases). On the other hand, pleomorphic
LCIS and LCIS with necrosis demonstrated persistent high up-
grade rates of 43% (three of seven cases) and 50% (three of six
cases), respectively.

An additional level of review was then applied, evaluating
cases for discordance. When the authors removed discordant
cases (all three classic LCIS cases, two of three pleomorphic
LCIS  cases,  and  one  of  three  necrotic  LCIS  cases),  upgrade
rates for LN variants remained high (20% [one of five cases]
for pleomorphic LCIS and 40% [two of five cases] for necrotic
LCIS). However, the classic LCIS upgrade rate for nonvariant

Nonincidental versus Incidental LCIS
As mentioned earlier, classic LCIS may occasionally be associ-
ated with targeted calcifications; in this case, the LCIS would

be  a  nonincidental  targeted  finding.  Very  few  studies  in  the
literature  specifically  assess  the  upgrade  rates  of  noninciden-

assessment of discordance and exclude variants in analysis do
not necessarily make a distinction between incidental noncal-
cified classic LCIS and nonincidental calcified LCIS in analysis
(16,30).

LN and MRI
There is less definitive evidence regarding how to manage LN
detected  at  breast  MRI  as  opposed  to  LN  identified  at  mam-
mography or US. The literature is limited, with small and ret-
rospective studies, many of which included nonpure LN, often
assessed only relatively few cases of LN as part of a larger as-
sessment  of  multiple  high-risk  lesions  combined  (43),  or  did
not  perform  radiologic-pathologic  evaluation  for  discordance
or  distinguish  between  classic  LCIS  and  the  variant  types
(44,45). This  likely  contributes  to  the  relatively  high  upgrade
rates that have been reported for LN detected at MRI. However,
even several studies that performed more nuanced pathologic
assessments show high upgrade rates above a 2% threshold for
which surveillance might be appropriate (46–48).

In  a  multi-institution  study,  Khoury  and  colleagues  (47)
reviewed  1655  MRI-guided  biopsies,  of  which  63  (4%)  were
LN. In this group, 11 of 34 cases of pure LN (32%), including
three  cases  of  pleomorphic  LCIS,  were  upgraded  to  malig-
nancy. Even excluding the three cases of pleomorphic LCIS,

eight of 31 cases (26%) were upgraded. Of note, the authors
do not comment on whether the non–pleomorphic LCIS cases
included  other  variants,  which  would  have  implications  for
the upgrade rates.

Another recent study described 65 of 943 MRI-guided bi-
opsies  that  resulted  in  LN  as  the  highest-risk  pathologic  le-
sion at core sampling (49). In 32 of these cases, LN was the
dominant  finding  at  pathologic  analysis.  Of  this  group,  one
pleomorphic LCIS case and two classic LCIS cases were up-
graded, for a total upgrade rate of 9.4%. The authors found no
predictors of upgrade, although they observed that LN most
commonly  manifested  as  focal  heterogeneous  nonmass  en-
hancement with low T2 signal intensity.

Khoury  and  colleagues  (47)  found  that  more  of  their  up-
graded LN cases manifested as nonmass enhancement versus
mass-enhancing  lesions,  but  this  difference  was  not  signifi-
cant. More recently, Amitai and colleagues (50) found that up-
grade of LN (11% upgraded cases [eight of 76]) was associated
with both lesion size (mean size of 5.5 cm in upgraded cases vs
1.9 cm in nonupgraded cases) and clumped nonmass enhance-
ment (75% vs 24%, P = .006). However, across the majority of
studies to date, no clinical or imaging characteristics have been
shown to allow statistically significant prediction of upgrade
for LN identified at MRI (47,49,51) (Fig 8).

There have also been a few studies assessing whether the
negative predictive power of MRI can be used to safely identify
high-risk  lesions  that  might  be  followed  rather  than  excised
once  identified  at  percutaneous  biopsy.  Lon dero  and  col-
leagues (52) assessed 227 high-risk lesions and concluded that
lack of contrast enhancement could allow reliable exclusion of
malignancy in this setting (70 of 72 cases; negative predictive
value  [NPV]  =  97%;  95%  CI:  94,  99).  However,  a  closer  look
at the 37 LN lesions with results of final excision pathologic
analysis in the study shows that a high number (12 of 37 cases
[32%]) were upgraded to malignancy. There were five interme-
diate-grade ILCs, one intermediate-grade invasive tubulolob-
ular carcinoma, one intermediate-grade invasive ductal carci-
noma (IDC), and five cases of ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS)
(two low grade, one intermediate grade, and two high grade)
in the upgraded group.

Longer-term Management Considerations

Risk
A  diagnosis  of  LN  is  associated  with  a  greater  than  average
lifetime  risk  of  developing  breast  cancer.  ALH  is  associated
with a four to five times relative risk of developing breast can-
cer, and LCIS is associated with a higher eight to 10 times rel-
ative risk (8). King and colleagues (54) observed a 2% annual
incidence of breast cancer in their cohort of 1004 women with
a diagnosis of LN who underwent surveillance over a 29-year
period (168 cancers in 150 women at a median follow-up of 81
months, range 3–368 months).

The relatively higher risk conferred by a diagnosis of LCIS
compared  with  that  conferred  by  a  diagnosis  of  ALH  is  one
argument  in  favor  of  maintaining  distinct  categorizations  of
these entities despite the potentially subjective nature of sepa-
rating them out at histopathologic analysis, as discussed earlier.
Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results (SEER) data have
shown that among 19 462 women with a history of LCIS, there
was a cumulative incidence of breast malignancy of 11.3% (95%
CI: 10.7, 11.9) at 10 years and 19.8% (95% CI: 18.8, 20.9) at 20
years (Fig 9).

Chemoprevention
Several  studies  have  shown  the  benefits  of  chemopreven-
tion in women with LN. King and colleagues (54) found that
breast cancer incidence was significantly reduced in women
with  a  history  of  LCIS  undergoing  chemoprevention  (10-
year  cumulative  risk:  7%  with  chemoprevention,  21%  with
no  chemoprevention;  P  <  .001).  National  Comprehensive
Cancer Network (NCCN) guidelines recommend risk-reduc-
ing endocrine therapy for individuals with a history of LCIS
and  a  life  expectancy  of  greater  than  or  equal  to  10  years
(55).

Supplemental Screening
Several  recent  guidelines  also  recommend  consideration  of
supplemental annual screening breast MRI in women with a
history of LN. The NCCN 2022 guidelines recommend annual
screening mammography to begin at diagnosis of LN (but not
before age 30 years). These guidelines also suggest consider-
ation of annual breast MRI after age 25 years for women with
LN and a residual lifetime risk of greater than or equal to 20%
(56).  Contrast-enhanced  mammography  and  screening  US

Similarly, the 2023 American College of Radiology (ACR)

cancer recommend that supplemental screening MRI should
be  considered,  in  women  with  a  history  of  atypia  or  LCIS,
“especially if other risk factors are present,” an acknowledg-
ment  that  risk  is  multifactorial  and  that  women  with  a  his-
tory of LN may also have other factors—dense breasts, family
history—that will further compound their risk of developing
later cancer.

Currently,  the  evidence  for  screening  with  breast  MRI  in
the population with LN is based on observed risk in the cohort
with  an  LN  history  as  well  as  extrapolation  from  the  high-
est-risk  patient  population  literature  rather  than  on  robust
imaging-based literature. There are small retrospective stud-
ies that have shown increased cancer detection rate (CDR) at
MRI  versus  mammography  in  women  with  a  history  of  LN
(58,59). A review of supplemental MRI performance in 2637
patients with different levels of risk found that supplemental
screening MRI resulted in a CDR of 15 per 1000 examinations
in women with a history of LN or atypical ductal hyperplasia
(ADH). Overall screening performance in women with a his-
tory of a high-risk lesion was equivalent to outcomes in BRCA
mutation carriers without significant difference in CDR, posi-
tive predictive value (PPV), sensitivity, or specificity (60).

A  study  of  MRI  screening  performance  in  145  women
with a history of atypia or LN also found equivalent PPV1 and
PPV3 rates compared with those of other high-risk groups for
whom MRI supplemental screening is performed (61). How-
ever, a prospective two-arm study comparing supplemental
MRI  to  mammography  alone  showed  no  significant  differ-
ence  in  cancer  detection  (62).  A  more  recent  retrospective
study of 542 women with a history of ADH or ALH and 157
women  with  a  history  of  LCIS  who  underwent  either  MRI
and  mammography  or  mammography  alone  found  equiva-
lent  4-year  CDRs  for  breast  cancer  (3.6%  in  both  groups, P
= .89). The cancers in the two groups were all node negative
and  stage  0  or  1.  However,  there  were  significantly  higher
biopsy rates in the MRI group (30.5% vs 12.6%, hazard ratio
(HR) = 2.67, P < .001) (63).

The  mixed  results  in  these  studies  likely  arise  from  the
heterogeneity of included patients, the small sample size, the
overall low numbers of cancers detected in this moderate-risk
population,  and  the  relatively  short  follow-up  periods.  Rec-
ommendations  for  supplemental  MRI  screening  in  women
with a history of LN are based more on lifetime risk observa-
tions, extrapolations from the high-risk patient literature, and
recognition that women may have multiple simultaneous risk
factors,  rather  than  on  robust  data  from  the  screening  MRI
literature.

Consensus and Guidelines
There are several broad recommendation documents by sur-
gical  societies  such  as  the  American  Society  of  Breast  Sur-
geons and by international bodies such as the International
Consensus  Conference  and  the  National  Health  Service

(NHS)  Breast  Screening  Multidisciplinary  Working  Group
(64,65), with recommendations that vary by practice norms
and  tissue-sampling  approaches.  In  the  United  Kingdom,
for example, it is standard to manage classic LN diagnosed
at  14-gauge  or  vacuum-assisted  biopsy  with  percutaneous
vacuum-assisted  excision  if  the  lesion  is  determined  to  be
eligible for this approach after multidisciplinary team eval-
uation (66). Guidelines from the American Society of Breast
Surgeons  state  that  LCIS  and  ALH  may  be  excised  or  fol-
lowed, but that routine excision is no longer routinely rec-
ommended  if  there  is  no  discordance  and  no  additional
high-risk  lesion  in  the  biopsy  sample.  However,  excision
should be performed if there is discordance or limited sam-
pling or if another high-risk lesion is present. Pleomorphic
LCIS  should  be  excised,  as  should  LCIS  with  necrosis  and
other nonclassic lesions (64).

At  this  time,  however,  there  are  no  radiology-based  na-
tional U.S. consensus guidelines for management of high-risk
lesions  and  no  specifically  stated  surveillance  guidelines  for
the appropriate follow-up period and Breast Imaging Report-
ing  and  Data  System  (BI-RADS)  categorization  that  should
be  used  to  follow  high-risk  lesions.  While  short-term  inter-
val surveillance protocols following a BI-RADS 3 model with
6-month,  12-month,  and  24-month  surveillance  for  ALH  or
classic LCIS or both have been suggested (42), there remains
a lack of national guidelines and consensus on how surveil-
lance should be managed.

Conclusion
Appropriate management decisions after LN is encountered
at  biopsy  demand  understanding  of  the  heterogeneity  of
LN, recognition of the LCIS variants, and careful radiologic-
pathologic  assessment  and  evaluation  of  concordance.  For
ALH,  there  is  ample  evidence  to  support  surveillance  if  the
biopsy  results  are  concordant  and  there  are  no  additional
associated  lesions  that  would  warrant  excision.  Incidental
noncalcified classic LCIS also shows very low upgrade rates,
and surveillance is appropriate when scrupulous radiologic-
pathologic correlation is practiced.

There is still a need for consensus and national U.S. radiol-
ogy-based guidelines both overall and specifically in terms of
surveillance intervals and duration of surveillance follow-up.
At this time, there is a need for more studies on the optimal
approach for LN identified at MRI. For all modalities, if there
is  any  uncertainty  about  radiologic-pathologic  correlation,
excision will be the most prudent option. Finally, both ALH
and LCIS confer a greater than average lifetime risk of later
development of breast cancer, warranting referral of patients
for discussion of long-term risk management strategies.
