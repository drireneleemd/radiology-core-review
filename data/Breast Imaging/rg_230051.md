Imaging and Management of Fibroepithelial Lesions
of the Breast: Radiologic-Pathologic Correlation

Meng Zhang, MD • Firouzeh K. Arjmandi, MD • Jessica H. Porembka, MD • Stephen J. Seiler, MD • Sally H. Goudreau, MD
Kanwal Merchant, MD • Helena Hwang, MD • Jody C. Hayes, MD

Author affiliations, funding, and conflicts of interest are listed at the end of this article.

Fibroepithelial lesions (FELs) are among the most
common breast masses encountered by breast
radiologists and pathologists. They encompass
a spectrum of benign and malignant lesions,
including fibroadenomas (FAs) and phyllodes
tumors (PTs). FAs are typically seen in young
premenopausal women, with a peak incidence at
20–30 years of age, and have imaging features of
oval circumscribed hypoechoic masses. Although
some FA variants are especially sensitive to hor-
monal influences and can exhibit rapid growth
(eg, juvenile FA and lactational adenomas), most
simple FAs are slow growing and involute after
menopause. PTs can be benign, borderline, or
malignant and are more common in older women
aged 40–50 years. PTs usually manifest as enlarg-
ing palpable masses and are associated with a
larger size and sometimes with an irregular shape
at imaging compared with FAs. Although FA and
FA variants are typically managed conservatively
unless large and symptomatic, PTs are surgically
excised because of the risk of undersampling at
percutaneous biopsy and the malignant potential
of borderline and malignant PTs. As a result of
the overlap in imaging and histologic appear-
ances, FELs can present a diagnostic challenge
for the radiologist and pathologist. Radiologists
can facilitate accurate diagnosis by supplying
adequate tissue sampling and including critical
information for the pathologist at the time of
biopsy. Understanding the spectrum of FELs can
facilitate and guide appropriate radiologic-patho-
logic correlation and timely diagnosis and man-
agement of PTs.

TEACHING POINTS
	 Fibroepithelial  lesions  (FELs)  of  the  breast  are  a  heterogeneous  group  of
biphasic  neoplasms  characterized  by  proliferation  of  epithelial  and  stromal
components and encompass both benign entities and borderline to malig-
nant phyllodes tumors (PTs).

	 Rapidly  growing  lesions  (>20%  increase  in  largest  dimension  in  a  6-month
period)  should  be  biopsied.  Surgical  excision  is  indicated  for  concomitant
high-risk lesions, lesions with suspicious imaging and/or clinical features, and
lesions with radiologic-pathologic discordance at core biopsy analysis.

	 Although  excision  may  sometimes  be  considered  for  larger  biopsy-proven
FAs or FA variants (ie, >3 cm) to reduce sampling errors and relieve symp-
toms, excision based solely on size is not warranted after a radiologic-patho-
logic–concordant biopsy.

	 Management  of  presumed  FAs  with  associated  calcifications  depends  on
morphologic  features  and  distribution  of  calcifications  mammographically.
Characteristically benign calcifications are managed conservatively (coarse
or popcorn-like calcifications located at eccentric or peripheral sites of the
lesion).

	 PTs may demonstrate intratumoral heterogeneity with FA-like regions, pre-
senting  diagnostic  challenges  to  the  pathologist.  In  large  masses,  biopsy
specimens should be obtained from different regions, with special attention
to grossly heterogeneous areas.

Introduction
Fibroepithelial  lesions  (FELs)  of  the  breast  are  a  heteroge-
neous  group  of  biphasic  neoplasms  characterized  by  prolif-
eration of epithelial and stromal components and encompass
both  benign  entities  and  borderline  to  malignant  phyllodes
tumors  (PTs).  They  are  estimated  to  occur  in  10%–15%  of
women  (1).  Fibroadenomas  (FAs)  are  the  most  common
benign  breast  tumor,  especially  in  women  younger  than  30
years of age (2). The estimated incidence of FAs is 7%–13% in
symptomatic patients presenting to breast clinics (3). Results
of  a  large  cross-sectional  survey  (4)  of  more  than  11 000  as-
ymptomatic  women  who  underwent  screening  mammogra-
phy reported a prevalence of 27.6%. Juvenile and simple FAs
typically occur in adolescents and young adults, respectively.
However, both benign (including FAs and nonmalignant PTs)
and  malignant  (malignant  PTs)  masses  may  also  be  seen  in
women in later reproductive and postmenopausal years (5,6).
FELs may be incidentally detected at imaging or manifest
as palpable mobile masses (7). Most are slow growing; rapid
growth more typically occurs in certain benign lesions sensi-
tive to hormonal changes (juvenile and lactational FAs) and
in PTs (6). The different FELs share radiologic and pathologic
features. In certain instances, definitive diagnosis may be es-
pecially challenging for the pathologist because of lesion het-
erogeneity,  morphologic  overlap,  sampling  limitations,  and
continuous  classification  variables  susceptible  to  interpreta-
tive subjectivity (8). Correct diagnosis of PTs is especially im-
portant because of the aggressive nature of the borderline and
malignant PTs with metastatic potential.

Differences in the clinical behavior and the prognosis of FELs
necessitate accurate diagnosis, underscoring the role of clinical
history and physical examination in guiding appropriate workup
and management. The purposes of this article are to review the
radiologic  and  pathologic  findings  for  the  spectrum  of  benign
and  malignant  FELs,  illustrate  challenging  clinical  scenarios,
and discuss essential aspects of the radiologist’s role in inform-
ing timely and accurate diagnosis and appropriate management.

FA and FA Variants

Fibroadenomatoid Change
Fibroadenomatoid  change,  previously  known  as  fibroadeno-
matoid  hyperplasia,  sclerosing  lobular  hyperplasia,  fibroade-
nomatosis, or fibroadenomatoid mastopathy (9,10), is a benign
entity  usually  detected  at  imaging  because  of  its  association
with  suspicious  calcifications.  The  mean  age  reported  in  the
literature ranges from 32 years to older than 50 years (9–11).
Histologically,  fibroadenomatoid  change  consists  of  a  micro-
scopic  nodule  resembling  an  FA  (often  without  circumscrip-
tion and proliferative stroma typically associated with an FA),
with coarse calcifications (9) (Fig S1).

Simple FA
FA  is  the  most  common  benign  breast  neoplasm  in  young
women, with a peak incidence in the 2nd and 3rd decades of
life  (3,12).  FAs  respond  to  hormonal  changes  and  frequently
undergo atrophic changes in menopause (12). FAs exhibit co-
proliferations of the epithelium and stroma, with intracanalic-
ular variants demonstrating epithelium arranged in clefts (Fig
S2) and pericanalicular variants demonstrating rounded open
ducts (13,14). FAs typically have well-defined borders histolog-
ically, without stromal atypia or overgrowth and with absent or
few stromal mitoses (1,15).

Complex FA
Complex  FAs  typically  occur  in  women  aged  40–50  years  (5),
with  imaging  findings  overlapping  those  of  simple  FAs.  Al-
though  they  were  initially  believed  to  be  associated  with  in-
creased risk of subsequent invasive breast cancer relative to sim-
ple FAs (relative risk, 3.1) (16), results of a study (17) from 2015
suggest that a complex FA is not an independent risk factor for
breast  cancer  after  adjustment  for  other  frequently  associated
risk factors, including proliferative breast disease elsewhere in
the breast. Authors of another study (5) found no malignancy
in patients with complex FAs without a concomitant high-risk
lesion at a mean follow-up of 2 years, whereas only one of 63
(1.6%) complex FAs with a concomitant high-risk lesion at core
needle biopsy was upgraded to malignancy at excision (5).

Complex FAs are characterized by one or more of the fol-
lowing histologic features: cysts larger than 3 mm, sclerosing
adenosis (Fig S3), apocrine metaplasia, and epithelial calcifica-
tions (16). The aforementioned study (5) of 63 patients showed
sclerosing adenosis as the most common feature.

Myxoid  FAs  are  more  common  in  perimenopausal  women
(15)  and  demonstrate  no  distinctive  mammographic  or  US

Hyalinized FA
During menopause, FAs commonly involute because of hor-
monal  withdrawal,  accompanied  by  hyalinization  and/or
calcification of the stroma. Given this natural history, hya-
linized FAs are more commonly seen in older women than
are  other  FA  variants  (6).  Hyalinization  is  less  common  in
premenopausal  women  but  has  been  disproportionately
reported  in  younger  patients  with  Cowden  syndrome  (20).
Authors of one study (7) found that 88% of FAs in patients
younger than 40 years with Cowden syndrome have varying
degrees of stromal hyalinization. At histologic examination,
hyalinized FAs demonstrate epithelial atrophic changes and
decreased  stromal  cellularity,  with  increased  sclerosis  (Fig
S5) (7).

Cellular, Giant, and Juvenile FA
The terms cellular, giant, and juvenile FA describe a relatively
distinct  variant  of  FA  that  demonstrates  hypercellularity  of
glands  and/or  stroma,  may  be  large  (>5  cm),  and  tends  to
occur  in  adolescents  and  young  women  (12).  Cellular  FA  is
characterized by increased stromal cellularity, whereas giant
FA (>5 cm and >500 g) and juvenile FA (seen in patients 10–
18 years of age) are size-related and age-related descriptors,
respectively (21–24).

At histopathologic analysis, cellular FAs are characterized
by uniformly increased stromal cellularity (1), homogeneous
distribution of glands and stroma (Fig S6), and glands retain-
ing  an  intracanalicular  or  pericanalicular  appearance  (15).
Cellular FAs may mimic benign PTs; however, they lack sig-
nificant  stromal  nuclear  atypia,  intratumoral  heterogeneity,
and  well-developed  stromal  fronds,  which  are  seen  in  PTs
(25). Juvenile FAs, a subset of giant FAs occurring in adoles-
cents,  are  characterized  by  both  stromal  and  epithelial  hy-
percellularity (26), with the epithelium often demonstrating
micropapillary usual ductal hyperplasia (15). Stromal mitotic
activity is generally low (<2 mitoses per 10 high-power fields)
(24), contrasting with a mean of 3.1, 10, and 17 mitoses per 10
high-power fields in benign, borderline, and malignant PTs,
respectively (27).

Tubular Adenoma
Tubular  adenomas  are  rare  benign  FELs  predominantly  oc-
curring  in  reproductive-aged  women  (28).  The  reported  in-
cidence  of  tubular  adenomas  among  benign  breast  lesions
is  between  0.13%  and  1.7%  (1,29,30).  Histologically,  tubular
adenomas are sharply demarcated but lack a true capsule and
are composed of densely packed, regular, round tubules with
scant intervening stroma (14) (Fig S7).

Lactational Adenoma
Lactational adenomas occur in late pregnancy or during post-
partum lactation, usually manifesting as a painless mobile mass
(31,32). These masses may grow to a large size under physio-
logic  hormonal  influences  (14).  Most  resolve  spontaneously
after delivery or cessation of lactation (33). Lactational adeno-
mas may reflect a sequela of lactational changes involving an
FA rather than a distinct entity (6). They demonstrate lobules
lined by secreting epithelial cells with an increased vacuolated
cytoplasm  (33)  (Fig  S8),  and  approximately  5%  of  lactational
adenomas exhibit histologic evidence of infarction (34).

Multimodality Imaging Appearances of FAs and
Variants

Appearance at Mammography
Mammographically, FAs classically appear as oval, circum-
scribed,  isodense  masses,  typically  less  than  3  cm  in  size
(35) (Fig 1). Lactating adenomas can manifest as hypodense
masses  and  may  contain  fat.  In  addition,  although  they
are  more  commonly  associated  with  galactoceles,  lactating
adenomas  may,  in  rare  cases,  demonstrate  a  fat-fluid  level
(31,32). At contrast-enhanced mammography, FAs typically
manifest  as  oval  masses  with  homogeneous  enhancement,
with or without nonenhancing septa (36,37), mirroring their
appearance at MRI (Fig 2). Hyalinized FAs may exhibit vari-
able  shapes  and  margins  because  of  degeneration  and  in-
volutional changes (38). In older postmenopausal patients,
tubular  adenomas  may  manifest  as  circumscribed  or  non-
circumscribed  masses  with  microcalcifications,  which  may
resemble malignancy (29,39). Calcifications are common in
hyalinized  and  complex  FAs  and  may  have  variable  initial
morphologic features, with coarsening over time to become
“popcorn-like” (Fig 1) (5,7). Fibroadenomatoid change may
also have associated grouped calcifications, with variation in
shape, size, and density, as well as variable mammographic
appearances, including circumscribed or noncircumscribed
masses and focal asymmetries (Fig 3) (10,11) . In addition,
fibroadenomatoid change can be occult at imaging and can
be found incidentally at the time of biopsy for another im-
aging finding.

Appearance at US
At US, FAs are usually homogeneous, hypoechoic, and hypo-
vascular, with variable posterior acoustic features (35) (Fig 1).
However, compared with simple FAs, complex FAs are more
likely to have suspicious imaging features, such as irregular
shape, noncircumscribed margins (including microlobulated,
indistinct, angular, and spiculated margins), small cystic in-
ternal components, and microcalcifications (40) (Fig 4). Hy-
alinized  FAs  may  also  exhibit  heterogeneous  echogenicity
related to degenerative changes and posterior acoustic shad-
owing from calcifications (7). Cellular, giant, and juvenile FAs
tend to be larger and may demonstrate increased vascularity
(41).  Lactational  adenomas  can  manifest  as  complex  cystic
and solid masses with or without irregular margins (33), po-
tentially indistinguishable from other benign and malignant
entities (33,42,43) (Fig 5).

Phyllodes Tumor
PTs account for less than 1% of all breast neoplasms (35) and
less  than  3%  of  all  FELs  (47).  They  tend  to  occur  in  older
women than do FAs, most commonly in women in their 40s
and 50s (47). Rare cases are reported in patients younger than
25 years of age, including in adolescents (48). Clinically, PTs
typically  manifest  as  enlarging  palpable  masses,  prompting
diagnostic  workup.  Although  older  patient  age,  larger  mass
size, and irregular mass shape at imaging are associated with
increasing aggressiveness (49), standard PT subclassification
along a benign, borderline, and malignant spectrum is based
on various histologic parameters, with significant overlap cre-
ating potential diagnostic pitfalls (1,6).

well-defined  borders,  mild  stromal  cellularity,  absent  stro-

high-power  fields),  and  absent  or  mild  stromal  atypia  (Fig
S9).  Borderline  PTs  demonstrate  well-defined  borders  that
may be focally permeative, moderate stromal cellularity, ab-
sent or focal stromal overgrowth, increased mitoses (5–9 per
10 high-power fields), and mild to moderate stromal atypia
(Fig  S10).  Malignant  PTs  are  characterized  by  infiltrative
borders,  marked  stromal  cellularity,  stromal  overgrowth,
high  mitotic  activity  (>10  per  10  high-power  fields),  and
marked stromal atypia (Figs S11–S13). PTs may contain ma-
lignant heterologous elements, such as rhabdomyosarcoma,
chondrosarcoma,  osteosarcoma,  and  leiomyosarcoma.  The
presence  of  any  of  these  malignant  heterologous  elements
is  diagnostic  of  malignant  PT  (1).  Well-differentiated  lipo-
sarcoma  is  no  longer  considered  a  stand-alone  histologic
criterion  for  malignant  PT,  whereas  pleomorphic  liposar-
coma  is  considered  a  malignant  heterologous  element  and
is associated with less-favorable outcomes, including distant
metastases (51).

Appearance at MRI
MRI has limited utility in differentiating PTs from FAs, poten-
tially related to the selection bias wherein lesions with more
concerning  mammographic  or  US  features  proceed  to  MRI
evaluation (13). In a study (52) examining the MRI features of
81 FAs and 24 PTs, including morphology, margins, signal in-
tensity characteristics, and contrast enhancement, only high
T2  signal  intensity  of  surrounding  breast  tissue,  reflecting
parenchymal edema, was statistically significantly more fre-
quent in PTs than in FAs (Fig 9). Table 1 summarizes demo-
graphic characteristics and multimodality imaging features of
FELs, including FAs, FA variants, and PTs.

Management Considerations

FA and FA Variants
FAs are often managed conservatively. Imaging surveillance
is recommended for up to 2 years for Breast Imaging Report-
ing and Data System (BI-RADS) 3 (probably benign) masses
exhibiting typical benign imaging features and not exceeding
20% change in the largest dimension within any 6-month pe-
riod (53,54). When multiple similar-appearing, bilateral, oval,
circumscribed,  hypoechoic  masses  are  seen  (at  least  two  in
one breast and one in the other breast), these may be regarded
as benign, without additional imaging follow-up unless there
is  a  distinctly  different  mass  or  a  new  palpable  and  larger
mass with any concerning imaging features (Fig 1). Rapidly
growing  lesions  (>20%  increase  in  largest  dimension  in  a
6-month period) should be biopsied (Fig 10). Surgical excision

Multimodality Imaging Appearance of PTs

Appearance at Mammography
Overlapping imaging features between PTs and FAs can make
it challenging to differentiate the two entities. Despite some
similarities,  other  findings  may  be  more  suggestive  of  a  PT
over an FA. Mammographic findings more commonly seen in
PTs include larger size (68% measuring at least 3 cm, vs 15%
of FAs), irregular shape (seen with most PTs, compared with
an  oval  shape  seen  with  most  FAs),  and  noncircumscribed
margins (with most PTs demonstrating microlobulated or in-
distinct margins, compared with circumscribed margins seen
in most FAs) (35) (Figs 7, 8).

FAs are homogeneously hypoechoic (78%), most PTs exhibit
heterogeneous  echogenicity  patterns  (80%),  including  clefts

and  imaging  follow-up  may  be  a  reasonable  alternative  to
