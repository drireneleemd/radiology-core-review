Challenges in Interpretation of US Breast Findings
in the Emergency Setting

Anastasiia Morozova, MD • Claudia Cotes, MD • Shima Aran, MD • Harnoor Singh, MD

Author affiliations, funding, and conflicts of interest are listed at the end of this article.
*A.M. and C.C. contributed equally to this work.

Emergencies in breast imaging are infrequent but not rare. Although infectious conditions such as mastitis and breast abscess are
the most common breast diseases encountered in acute care settings, other entities that may require additional imaging or different
treatment approaches are also seen and include traumatic injury and breast cancer. While mammography is widely available for
breast evaluation in outpatient facilities, most emergency departments do not have mammography units. This makes evaluation of
patients with breast disease incomplete in the acute care setting and emphasizes the role of appropriate US techniques for interpre-
tation. It also highlights the importance of effective sonographer-to-radiologist communication to ensure patient safety and diag-
nostic accuracy, especially in an era of increasing adoption of teleradiology. The authors discuss the challenges in image acquisition
and remote interpretation that are commonly faced by radiologists when they assess breast anomalies in the emergency setting.
They present strategies to overcome these challenges by describing techniques for proper US evaluation, highlighting the impor-
tance of sonographer-radiologist communication, defining the goals of the evaluation, reviewing common differential diagnoses,
and providing appropriate follow-up recommendations.

Introduction
Evaluation of patients with breast symptoms in the emergency
department  (ED)  can  be  a  challenge  for  ED  radiologists,  es-
pecially  those  without  specialized  training  in  breast  imaging.
Infectious conditions such as mastitis and breast abscesses are
the most common breast diseases seen in the acute care setting.
However, other entities such as traumatic injury, postsurgical
complications, and physiologic conditions may also be encoun-

tered.  Some  of  these  conditions  may  have  clinical  manifesta-
tions that overlap with those of breast cancer, highlighting the
importance  of  accurate  characterization  of  imaging  features
and appropriate follow-up recommendations.

Challenges in Evaluation in the Emergency
Setting
Challenges in the evaluation of breast findings in the ED are
multifactorial. These  variables  can  be  categorized  in  terms
of  equipment  availability,  technologist  and  imaging  tech-
nique, technology and telemedicine limitations, clinical and
medical history limitations, limited Breast Imaging Report-
ing and Data System (BI-RADS) utilization, and lack of fol-
low-up and patient navigation systems.

Equipment Availability
US  is  a  noninvasive,  nonionizing,  and  inexpensive  imaging
modality  that  is  well  tolerated  by  patients  with  acute  breast
disease  and  is  readily  available  in  the  emergency  setting.  It
has  high  sensitivity  for  the  diagnosis  of  fluid  collections,

which  are  commonly  encountered  in  the  ED  (1).  However,
compared with US, mammography has higher sensitivity and
specificity  in  the  evaluation  of  suspicious  masses  and  calci-
fications,  especially  in  patients  without  dense  breast  tissue
(2–4). In women younger than 30 years who present with pal-
pable abnormalities or focal pain, US alone has been shown to
have a negative predictive value of 100% in examinations per-
formed  by  breast  imaging  radiologists.  Although  this  result
cannot be extrapolated to the emergency setting, it highlights
the  advantage  that  US  provides  in  ruling  out  malignancy
when it is performed as the primary imaging examination in
this age group (5).

According  to  current  American  College  of  Radiology  ap-
propriateness  criteria  recommendations,  for  symptomatic
patients  aged  40  years  or  older,  mammography  is  the  most
appropriate initial imaging modality. In addition, in patients
aged 30–39 years, mammography can be the initial or comple-
mentary examination performed to evaluate for breast symp-
toms.  The  lack  of  mammography  in  the  emergency  setting
results  in  incomplete  workups  for  patients  within  these  age
ranges who present to the ED (6,7).

Technologists and Imaging Technique
Unlike in most breast imaging centers, where US is performed
by dedicated breast imaging–trained sonographers with direct
supervision by fellowship-trained breast imaging radiologists,
US  in  the  ED  is  commonly  performed  by  general  sonogra-
phers with remote radiologist supervision (8–12). The lack of
subspecialty  expertise  may  be  associated  with  misdiagnoses
and technically suboptimal studies (12,13). In addition, a so-
nographer’s limited experience in breast US may result in in-
adequate image quality due to improper and incomplete im-
age acquisition, incorrect or incomplete image labeling, and
inappropriate patient positioning, among other causes.

Technology and Telemedicine Limitations
During the past 2 decades, teleradiology has evolved to a point
where 85.6% of radiologists in the United States report being en-
gaged in this practice (14). Interpretation of radiologic studies
from the ED is one of the most popular services that radiolo-
gists  provide  through  teleradiology  (14,15).  Lack  of  technolo-
gist proximity has been identified as a major challenge by up to
48.4% of teleradiologists (14). In the emergency setting, in which
study interpretations performed off-site and imaging areas iso-
lated  from  the  reading  rooms  are  common  practice,  the  US
technologist serves as an irreplaceable link between the medical
equipment, patient, and radiologist (16). The synchronous im-
age acquisition model, which enables real-time communication
between  the  technologist  and  the  radiologist,  results  in  better
image quality control and a reduced need for additional images.
However,  use  of  this  model,  as  compared  with  the  asynchro-
nous (ie, store-and-forward) model, in the emergency setting is
technically and logistically challenging (17,18).

Limited communication with referring providers also rep-
resents a major limitation of teleradiology (14,19,20). Verbal
communication facilitates the exchange of complex informa-
tion and appropriate patient prioritization and is more likely
to have a positive impact on clinical decision making (19–22).

Clinical and Medical History Limitations
The  quality  and  clinical  relevance  of  the  radiologic  evalua-
tion for breast disease depend heavily on the patient’s clinical
presentation,  medical  information,  and  current  versus  prior
imaging  findings,  as  well  as  the  radiologist’s  understanding
of  the  patient’s  clinical  concerns  (10).  It  is  not  uncommon
for  radiologists  to  receive  imaging  requisitions  that  have  in-
complete clinical information and lack pertinent physical ex-
amination findings that are necessary for appropriate image
interpretation. These missing data reduce the accuracy of the
radiologic report and subsequently affect patient care (23,24)
(Fig 1).

Limited Utilization of BI-RADS
The use of a standardized reporting system allows better out-
come  monitoring;  prevents  confusion  in  interpretation;  and
facilitates  communication  with  clinicians,  radiologists,  and
patients (25–27). These are the purposes of BI-RADS, which
is widely used to describe breast imaging findings. However,
the use of BI-RADS terms in examinations performed in the
ED  is  not  a  routine  practice,  and,  thus,  those  examinations
cannot be properly audited (9,25).

Lack of Follow-up and Patient Navigation Systems
Although  the  American  College  of  Emergency  Physicians
supports the use of patient navigators in the ED (28), the in-
terventions  aimed  at  accelerating  the  transition  of  patients
from the ED to outpatient care vary broadly across the coun-
try  and  different  health  care  systems.  The  most  described
ED-based  initiatives  are  written  instructions  given  to  the
patient  at  discharge,  scheduling  a  follow-up  appointment
before discharge, telephone contact or automated text mes-
sage  appointment  reminders,  alerting  outpatient  providers,
and more complex care coordination services such as patient
navigation (29).

Patient navigation is a patient-centered approach used by
clinical  professionals  and  nonclinical  health  care  workers
who are integrated into the health care team to improve the
continuity of patient care by identifying and addressing indi-

vidual barriers to care (30,31). Patient navigation is a common
practice in breast imaging (31). It has been shown to be effec-
tive in increasing the screening rate and decreasing the times
to diagnostic follow-up and treatment initiation (31–33). For
patients  with  breast  findings,  the  lack  of  patient  navigators
and patient reminder systems in the ED can result in loss of
follow-up and delays in care, which negatively affect patient
outcomes (33).

Overcoming the Challenges
Several strategies can be implemented to overcome the de-
scribed  challenges  and  include  equipment  and  technical
improvements, technologist training, and system-wide ini-
tiatives (Table 1).

Equipment
US  equipment  must  include  a  matrix  or  linear  transducer
with a wide footprint, and it is recommended that it have a
center frequency of at least 12 MHz, which is suitable for eval-
uation  of  most  breasts  (10,34,35). Transducers  with  a  lower
frequency  and  further  penetration  capability  are  necessary
when evaluating large breasts (>5 cm in thickness) in an ap-
propriately positioned patient (34).

Stand-off acoustic pads play a significant role in the eval-
uation  of  superficial  lesions  by  bringing  them  closer  to  the
transducer focal zone (10). Foam wedges are helpful for trunk
stabilization  while  maintaining  optimal  patient  positioning
during  the  US  examination.  In  addition,  equipping  the  US
room with a dimmer switch allows the lighting adjustments
necessary to decrease ambient light reflections and increase
the contrast sensitivity of the sonographer’s eyes.

environment of the ED.

Spatial compound imaging increases image quality owing
to  the  reduction  of  speckle  noise  and  posterior  acoustic  ar-
tifacts,  allowing  better  identification  of  microcalcifications
and better characterization of lesion borders (34,37). Tissue
harmonics substantially improve clutter noise, eliminate ar-
tifactual  internal  echoes  from  fluid-filled  lesions,  facilitate
definition of the lesion border, and enhance acoustic shadow
conspicuity (34,37). Both spatial compound imaging and tis-
sue harmonics should be routinely used for characterization
of  encountered  abnormalities  (Fig  2).  The  speed  of  sound
may be adjusted, depending  on the  breast density, as speed
propagation varies according to the amount of fat or glandu-

lar tissue in the breast. This enables better depiction of calci-
fications and better characterization of lesion margins (34).

When differentiating solid from cystic lesions, gain and fo-
cal zone optimization, tissue harmonics, spatial compounding,
and color Doppler imaging are beneficial (34–37). Spatial com-
pounding, tissue harmonics, and sound speed adjustments may
be applied for better margin delineation (Fig 3) (34,37).

Positioning and Patient Variables.—Inadequate patient posi-
tioning  may  create  unnecessary  artifacts,  obscure  small  le-
sions, and affect the reproducibility of lesion localization or
measurements.  The  goal  of  optimal  positioning  is  to  mini-
mize the breast thickness in the area being imaged (10). Pa-
tients  are  encouraged  to  lie  supine  with  the  ipsilateral  arm
over the head. For more lateral lesions, a contralateral supine
oblique position, which can be achieved with wedge pillow
support, is recommended (34). The lateral decubitus position
may be necessary for patients with large breasts. Since small
vessels  within  the  breast  are  easily  compressible,  an  appro-
priate amount of pressure must be applied while scanning to
prevent underestimation of blood flow on color Doppler US
images (35).

A satisfactory amount of gel is necessary to ensure a lack of
air between the skin and the probe. Air in the space between
the skin and the probe results in artifacts and limited visual-
ization of structures (Fig 4) (34,36). The application of warm
gel not only enhances patient comfort but also plays a role in
optimizing breast tissue evaluation, especially the subareolar
region, reducing nipple muscular contraction and facilitating
ducts and glandular tissue expansion (Fig 5) (38).

Technologists.—US  technologists  must  have  familiarity  with
the  standardized  protocol  for  breast  US  techniques,  patient
positioning, and image labeling. To ensure the reproducibility
of lesion localization, technologists must add not only the ba-
sic annotations such as laterality and plane (eg, transverse or
longitudinal, radial or antiradial) on images but also the clock-
face position of the lesion and the distance from the nipple in
centimeters. The location of the lesion may also be reported
by  using  a  clock-face  diagram  that  can  be  submitted  to  the
picture archiving and communication system. Lesions should
be  measured  in  three  dimensions  in  two  orthogonal  planes,
and  images  with  and  without  calipers  must  be  recorded,  as
images  without  calipers  allow  complete  margin  assessment
(10). Color Doppler US images of lesions must be obtained for
evaluation of internal vascularity (Fig 6) (35,36).

Radiologist and Sonographer Communication.—The US tech-
nologist’s  communication  of  important  clinical  information
and imaging findings is a crucial component in the evaluation
of  breast-related  emergencies.  Clear  and  consistent  bidirec-
tional  communication  between  the  radiologist  and  sonogra-
pher is  necessary  and must  be achievable around the clock.
To ensure that this communication is faultless, we suggest the
use of a sonographer-standardized form that includes spaces
assigned for patient concerns, pertinent medical history, im-
portant physical observations, study limitations, and US find-
ing annotations on a clock-face breast diagram (Fig 7).

Real-time  scanning  is  an  advantage  when  evaluating  the
breast with US, as it permits evaluation of lesion mobility and
subtle  findings  that  may  not  be  detectable  on  static  images

and aids in differentiating floating debris within fluid collec-
tions  from  fixed  solid  components  within  complex  lesions
(34,39).  In  addition,  dynamic  color  Doppler  US  may  depict
internal vascularity or feeding vessels that are difficult to cap-
ture on static images and thus raise the level of suspicion for
malignancy (39). The lack of this capability for radiologists in
the ED is a limitation. However, the use of US cine loops in
these situations may be beneficial, as investigators in previous
studies (40,41) have reported faster examination performance
by sonographers, more accurate recognition of artifacts by in-
terpreting radiologists, and reduced need for repeat imaging.

Decreased face-to-face communication between radiologists
and  technologists  reduces  opportunities  for  interpersonal
communication,  timely  feedback,  and  education,  impacting
patient  care  (20).  By  reviewing  the  technologist’s  work  and
providing feedback, radiologists can help technologists improve
their  US  skills.  This  feedback  can  be  implemented  regularly
with scheduled meetings, case conferences, and digital forms
of communication. Involving lead technologists in the design
of  the  ED  breast  US  protocol,  implementing  the  protocol
throughout the institution, and implementing quality control
processes ensure effective communication and accurate image
acquisition (42).

Goals of Evaluation and Common Differential
Diagnoses in the ED
Although breast-related emergencies are uncommon, radiolo-
gists in the ED must be familiar with commonly encountered
breast  diseases  and  the  associated  sonographic  findings  and
be able to identify a cause of the patient’s breast-related symp-
toms, recognize entities that require immediate management,
and provide appropriate follow-up recommendations. Just as
important is the role of the ED radiologist to recognize that
these commonly seen entities may have symptoms that over-
lap with those of breast cancer, and if the latter is a possibility,
then immediate specialized assessment is necessary and must
be recommended. Breast-related emergencies can be divided
into  those  that  require  immediate  treatment  and  those  that
are nonurgent but require prompt specialty evaluation or pa-
tient reassurance.

Breast-related Emergencies Requiring Immediate
Treatment
Emergent breast conditions that require immediate treatment
usually fall into one of three categories that are based on the
cause of the entity: infectious and/or inflammatory, postsur-
gical or postprocedural, or trauma (Fig 8).

Infectious and/or Inflammatory Conditions
Infection  and  inflammation  of  the  breast  parenchyma  are
commonly encountered in the acute setting. They comprise a
spectrum of diagnoses, including cellulitis, mastitis, and breast
abscess. Although these conditions are typically seen in young
women in the puerperium, postpartum, and lactation periods,
they can also be nonpuerperal or have noninfectious causes (44).
Risk factors for development of nonpuerperal breast infections
are smoking, diabetes, obesity, and nipple piercing (45–48).

Cellulitis  and  Mastitis.—Cellulitis  is  an  inflammatory-infec-
tious  process  of  the  skin  that  may  extend  into  the  dermis
and  subcutaneous  fat  (Fig  9).  With  mastitis,  the  inflamma-
tory  changes  extend  to  the  breast  parenchyma  (Fig  10).  US
findings  of  cellulitis  and  mastitis  include  skin  thickening,
increased  parenchymal  echogenicity,  edema,  and  increased
vascularity on Doppler images (44,45,49). Owing to the signif-
icant overlap in the clinical and imaging findings of mastitis
and inflammatory breast cancer (IBC), the most lethal form
of breast cancer, close monitoring of symptomatic response to
antibiotic therapy in patients with mastitis is strongly recom-
mended (46,50).

Inflammatory Breast Cancer.—IBC is a highly aggressive inva-
sive cancer that is associated with distant metastases in up to
40% of patients at presentation (50). Common clinical features
include rapid onset of diffuse asymmetric breast enlargement,
skin changes (eg, erythema with or without bruising, warmth,
skin thickening resembling an orange peel or peau d’orange),
and nipple abnormalities (eg, flattening or inversion) (8,50–
52). Typical sonographic features of IBC are skin thickening,
distended  lymphatic  vessels  within  the  dermis,  suspicious
lymphadenopathy, heterogeneous breast parenchyma with or
without solid masses, with diffuse edema, increased vascular-

ity, and lack of focal fluid collections (8,50–52). However, no
clinical or sonographic pathognomonic feature of IBC exists.
For this reason, in addition to clinical monitoring, radiologists
should consider recommending a complete evaluation in the
breast imaging department to document resolution of symp-
toms in patients who have a mastitis-like presentation and are
due  for  screening  mammography.  Prompt  complete  evalua-
tion of patients for whom there is high suspicion for IBC—
namely, women who are of an older age (average age at IBC
diagnosis,  58  years);  are  nonlactating;  have  a  family  or  per-
sonal history of breast cancer; and/or have any or a combina-
tion of regional adenopathy, peau d’orange, nipple flattening

Other Inflammatory Conditions.—Other benign breast condi-

which can also mimic breast abscesses. In these cases, tissue
biopsy  may  be  required  to  rule  out  malignancy  and  make  a
definitive diagnosis (Table 2) (8,44,46,48,52).

Mondor  Disease.—Mondor  disease,  or  superficial  throm-
bophlebitis of the thoracoepigastric vein, is a rare cause of
acute breast pain (49,54). At physical examination, it mani-
fests as a palpable tender erythematous cordlike subcutane-
ous structure extending from the anterior inferior aspect of
the breast to the epigastrium. US is the modality of choice
for detection of this entity and typically shows an anechoic
tubular  structure  with  a  beaded  appearance.  Intraluminal
clots and associated mild surrounding soft-tissue inflamma-
tory changes may be present. Mondor disease is a self-limit-
ing condition, and imaging follow-up is not required (49,54).

Seroma.—Seromas  are  serous  fluid  collections  in  the  post-
surgical cavity that most frequently occur after lumpectomy,
mastectomy, and/or axillary surgery (55). They can be seen
in up to 50% of modified mastectomy cases (55). At US, the
appearance of seromas can vary from anechoic to heteroge-
neous  fluid  collections  (Fig  13).  Although  they  are  usually
self-limited, some cases of seroma may persist for years and
cause  symptoms  such  as  a  palpable  abnormality  and  pain.
Percutaneous  aspiration  may  be  considered,  depending  on
the size and severity of the patient’s symptoms, and patients
may  be  followed  up  clinically  by  their  managing  physi-
cian and with imaging according to their regular screening
schedule (54–56). If clinically warranted, aspiration may be
performed to collect specimens for culture or to allow fast-
er  healing  (55,56).  Rarely,  seromas  can  become  infected  or
evolve into hematomas after percutaneous aspiration or di-
rect trauma (Fig 14) (56).

Hematoma.—Breast hematomas are collections composed of
blood  products  that  are  usually  encountered  following  sur-
gery, percutaneous biopsy, or acute trauma. They may be pres-
ent in 2%–10% of breast surgery cases (55) and at 0.7%–23%
of biopsies performed with stereotactic vacuum-assisted tech-
niques (57).

swelling or palpable lump that is expanding or stable, with or
without  overlying  skin  discoloration  (8,44,49).  The  appear-

with heterogeneous echogenicity. However, if a hematoma un-
dergoes liquefaction, it can appear as anechoic or hypoechoic

Pseudoaneurysms.—Pseudoaneurysms in the breast are rare
and  predominantly  associated  with  a  history  of  biopsy,  sur-
gery, or trauma. They are usually associated with hematomas,
and  a  palpable  pulsatile  mass  is  often  identified  at  physical
examination (44). US evaluation is usually sufficient for the
diagnosis,  demonstrating  a  well-defined  anechoic  structure
with a narrow connection to the adjacent artery and a charac-
teristic bidirectional swirling blood flow pattern at color Dop-
pler imaging (44,60). Treatment options include compression
dressing  and  observation,  US-guided  manual  compression,
thrombin or alcohol injection, coil embolization, and surgical
repair (Fig 16) (44,60).

Implant Rupture.—Breast augmentation is the most common
cosmetic surgical procedure in the United States, with a 45%
increase in the prevalence of this surgery over a decade from
2008 (61). A common implant-related complication that may
be encountered in the ED is implant rupture, which can be
related  to  damage  caused  by  one  or  more  surgical  instru-
ments  during  implant  placement,  trauma,  or  implant  fail-
ure,  with  the  latter  usually  associated  with  implant  aging.
Although  most  ruptures  are  clinically  silent,  acute  symp-
toms such as breast erythema, swelling, and/or hardening;
change  in  breast  shape;  and  painful  axillary  lymph  nodes
may be present (62).

Ruptures  of  saline  implants  are  clinically  evident;  how-
ever, silicone implant ruptures have subtle clinical findings
and may require imaging. Echogenic lines visualized within
the implant lumen that may be contiguous or discontinuous
with the implant envelope are characteristic of the “steplad-
der” sign and suggest an intracapsular silicone implant rup-
ture (Fig 17) (62,63). The “snowstorm” sign, with which free
silicone droplets in the breast parenchyma or axillary lymph
nodes  are  depicted  as  hyperechoic  shadowing  structures
on  US  images,  suggests  an  extracapsular  implant  rupture
(62,63). Patients require treatment for their symptoms, with
reassurance that neither saline leakage nor silicone leakage
poses imminent or significant health risks, and must be re-
ferred to the breast department for further evaluation. Treat-
ment involves observation, implant removal, or implant re-
placement (62,63).

Foreign Bodies.—Common reasons for ED visits include pene-
trating injuries and suspected retained foreign bodies (59). Sur-
gical and biopsy clips and fragments of hook wires are the most
common foreign bodies seen in the breast (64). Pieces of glass,
bullets, needles, and gauze have been also reported (59,64). US
enables  the  detection  of  different  radiolucent  foreign  bodies
and aids in localization of radiolucent and radiopaque foreign
bodies  and  evaluation  of  associated  soft-tissue  complications
(59). In acute settings, foreign bodies usually appear on US im-
ages as echogenic structures with posterior acoustic shadowing
or reverberation artifact and a hypoechoic halo secondary to a
surrounding  inflammatory  reaction  (59).  Long-term  sequelae
such as foreign-body granulomatous reaction and fat necrosis
may appear as a palpable mass with skin dimpling or retraction
and suspicious US features, raising concern for neoplasm, es-
pecially when the history of the previous trauma or surgery is
lacking and a foreign body is not recognized at imaging (49,64).

Nonurgent Breast Conditions in the ED
Reports of breast pain, palpable masses, nipple discharge non-
related  to  trauma,  and  findings  suggestive  of  postoperative
complications  and  infection  also  can  be  encountered  in  the
ED  (9,13,49).  While  these  entities  are  worrisome  for  the  pa-
tient, they may not require immediate intervention in the ED.
However, the radiologist must consider them in the differential
diagnoses to avoid delays in appropriate management and fol-
low-up (Fig 18).

Breast Pain
Breast  pain  is  a  common  clinical  symptom  that  affects  up  to
70% of women during their lifetime (65). Although the proba-
bility of breast cancer in patients with breast pain as the only
symptom  is  low,  imaging  evaluation  may  reassure  these  pa-
tients and help diagnose potentially treatable abnormalities (6).
Patients  who  present  with  diffuse,  bilateral,  cyclical  pain  can
be  safely  referred  for  outpatient  evaluation,  as  this  feature  is
benign and related to hormonal changes (6).

Palpable Masses
Palpable abnormalities require an initial clinical breast exam-
ination by the ordering provider to specify pertinent findings
in  the  study  request.  The  clinical  features  of  masses  can  aid
in  differentiating  benign  versus  malignant  masses,  but  these
features alone cannot exclude malignancy, and, thus, imaging
is indispensable.  Malignant masses may be hard, irregular in
shape, fixed to adjacent tissues, and associated with skin thick-
ening, dimpling, or retraction (7,67). On the other hand, benign
masses are typically soft and mobile, with well-defined margins
(7,67). The sonographer must report these clinical observations
by documenting them on the technologist form (10).

Probably Benign Masses.—Palpable abnormalities with benign
clinical features and US findings consistent with simple cysts,
normal intramammary lymph nodes, or typical sebaceous cysts
may  be  categorized  as  benign,  and  routine  mammographic
screening may be recommended (5,7,67).

When  solid  masses  are  encountered,  features  that  suggest
benignity include oval shape, circumscribed margins, orienta-
tion parallel to the skin surface, no acoustic shadowing, and
homogeneous  isoechoic  or  hypoechoic  internal  echotexture
(Fig 19) (25). These characteristics must be concordant with
benign clinical features at the physical examination. If a prob-
ably benign mass is encountered in the ED, the patient must
be  referred  to  the  breast  imaging  department  for  complete
evaluation and BI-RADS characterization, regardless of his or
her age. Patients who are aged 30 years or older require addi-
tional workup with mammography, while those younger than
30 years require baseline US evaluation to establish the proper
imaging follow-up (25).

Suspicious  Masses.—An  initial  diagnosis  of  breast  cancer
in the ED is uncommon, occurring in fewer than 10% of all
breast  cancer  cases  (68).  However,  it  is  crucial  to  recognize

this  malignancy  in  the  ED,  as  it  is  associated  with  poorer
outcomes compared with breast cancer diagnosed in the out-
patient  setting  and  tends  to  affect  women  with  sociodemo-
graphic inequalities disparately (69).

US features that raise concern for malignancy include ir-
regular shape; antiparallel orientation; spiculated, microlobu-
lated, angular, or indistinct margins; heterogeneous echotex-
ture;  and  posterior  shadowing  (Fig  20)  (25).  If  any  of  these
imaging  characteristics  is  present,  the  abnormality  must  be
categorized  as  suspicious.  In  addition,  suspicious  imaging
findings  take  precedence  over  clinical  findings  in  cases  in
which the latter are not concerning, and urgent evaluation in
the breast imaging department for complete imaging assess-
ment and possible biopsy is necessary for a patient of any age.
Involving the navigation team and scheduling the follow-up
appointment before the patient is discharged from the ED can
aid in timely evaluation and continuity of care (29,33).

Negative Findings
Patients may present to the ED with diverse breast symptoms
and  no  imaging  correlation. These  cases  can  be  approached
according to the patient’s clinical presentation and age. Pa-
tients  of  any  age  who  have  clinical  findings  that  are  con-
cerning  for  malignancy  but  negative  US  findings  must  be
promptly referred to the breast imaging department for com-
plete imaging evaluation and possible biopsy. This includes
patients  with  nipple  discharge  that  demonstrates  at  least
one  of  the  following  suspicious  features:  bloody  or  clear,
arising from a single orifice, initially manifesting as sponta-
neous, and unilateral (70). These patients are often found to
have negative imaging findings at presentation and require
prompt complete imaging workup.

evaluation often have consultation in the ED for breast-related findings. Breast cancer can occur in young and lactating women, despite other diseases such
as infectious and inflammatory entities being more common in these patient groups.
