Human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2/neu or ErbB2)–positive breast cancers comprise 15%–20% of all breast cancers.
The most common manifestation of HER2-positive breast cancer at mammography or US is an irregular mass with spiculated
margins that often contains calcifications; at MRI, HER2-positive breast cancer may appear as a mass or as nonmass enhancement.
HER2-positive breast cancers are often of intermediate to high nuclear grade at histopathologic analysis, with increased risk of lo-
cal recurrence and metastases and poorer overall prognosis. However, treatment with targeted monoclonal antibody therapies such
as trastuzumab and pertuzumab provides better local-regional control and leads to improved survival outcome. With neoadjuvant
treatments, including monoclonal antibodies, taxanes, and anthracyclines, women are now potentially able to undergo breast con-
servation therapy and sentinel lymph node biopsy versus mastectomy and axillary lymph node dissection. Thus, the radiologist’s
role in assessing the extent of local-regional disease and response to neoadjuvant treatment at imaging is important to inform sur-
gical planning and adjuvant treatment. However, assessment of treatment response remains difficult, with the potential for differ-
ent imaging modalities to result in underestimation or overestimation of disease to varying degrees when compared with surgical
pathologic analysis. In particular, the presence of calcifications at mammography is especially difficult to correlate with the results
of pathologic analysis after chemotherapy. Breast MRI findings remain the best predictor of pathologic response. The authors re-
view the initial manifestations of HER2-positive tumors, the varied responses to neoadjuvant chemotherapy, and the challenges in
assessing residual cancer burden through a multimodality imaging review with pathologic correlation.

	 The typical appearance of HER2-positive breast cancer at mammography is an
irregular mass with spiculated margins. The presence of microcalcifications is
highly associated with the HER2-positive subtype.

	 It is important to note that there are no current guidelines for post-NACT imag-
ing and that radiologic assessment often results in underestimation or overesti-
mation of disease. It has been shown that small disease less than 2 cm is missed
with MRI and that remaining disease up to 5 cm is overcalled when there is pCR
at final pathologic analysis. Surgical pathologic analysis remains the standard of

Introduction
Breast  cancer  is  the  most  common  type  of  cancer  in  women
and is diagnosed in approximately 12% of women in the United
States  in  their  lifetime  (1,2).  Breast  cancers  are  commonly
classified  by  immunohistochemical  subtypes,  most  often  by
whether  the  tumor  cells  express  estrogen  receptor  (ER),  pro-
gesterone receptor (PR), and/or human epidermal growth fac-
tor receptor 2 (HER2/neu or ErbB2), which determines therapy
and prognosis. The most common molecular subtype of inva-
sive breast cancer has both ER positivity and PR positivity only,
which together are classified as hormone receptor (HR) posi-
tivity. HR-positive tumors have the highest survival rates (1,2).
HER2-positive  breast  cancers  account  for  an  estimated
15%–20% of breast cancers (1,2) and may or may not demon-
strate  HR  positivity.  HER2  positivity  rates  have  been  re-
ported  to  be  19%  for  patients  aged  49  years  or  younger  and
15% for patients aged 50 years or older (3). Among those with
HER2-positive  breast  cancer,  postmenopausal  women  older
than 65 years are more likely to be diagnosed with HR-positive
and HER2-positive cancer, while women aged 50–64 years are
most likely to be diagnosed with HR-negative and HER2-pos-
itive cancer (1). HER2 positivity varies by ethnicity in women
aged 50 years or older, highest in Hispanics and Asians and
lowest in Black women (3). HER2-positive cancers, regardless
of HR status, tend to be a more aggressive subtype, with lower
survival  rates  compared  with  HR-positive–only  cancers,  but
with a better prognosis than the triple-negative subtype (4–6).

Before  the  advent  of  therapeutic  agents  specifically  tar-
geting  the  HER2  receptor,  women  with  HER2-positive  can-
cers had an overall poor prognosis (4–6). Preoperative use of
targeted agents not only has the potential to downstage from
mastectomy  to  lumpectomy,  but  may  also  limit  the  extent
of  axillary  surgery,  from  axillary  lymph  node  dissection  to
sentinel  lymph  node  biopsy  (7,8).  Furthermore,  response  to
neoadjuvant therapy may determine the need for additional
adjuvant therapy (8). In addition, targeted therapies have led
to improvement in disease-free survival and overall survival.
The  radiologist  plays  a  key  role  in  assessing  imaging  fea-
tures  relevant  to  the  overall  extent  of  disease  and  surgical
management, especially in early-stage HER2-positive tumors
treated with neoadjuvant chemotherapy (NACT). Mammog-
raphy, US, and MRI are important modalities in the evalua-
tion of HER2-positive cancers and response to treatment. In
this article, we review HER2-positive breast cancers, with em-
phasis on imaging features and radiologic-pathologic concor-
dance after NACT.

Pathophysiology of HER2/ErbB2-positive Breast
Cancer
HER2  is  a  transmembrane  receptor  tyrosine  kinase  that  is
overexpressed in HER2-positive breast cancers. HER2 is en-
coded  by  the  ERBB2  gene  located  on  chromosome  17q21.1
(9).  HER2  promotes  cell  growth,  and  its  overexpression  can
encourage  tumor  growth  and  tumor  cell  motility  (9).  There
are  several  methods  for  establishing  HER2  status  in  breast
cancer, including immunohistochemistry, fluorescence in situ
hybridization (FISH), chromogenic in situ hybridization, and
silver-enhanced in situ hybridization.

Immunohistochemistry uses antibodies against the target
HER2  protein,  and  detection  is  related  to  the  intensity  and
completeness  of  membrane  staining  (Fig  1)  (9).  HER2  neg-
ativity is defined as no staining (score = 0) or less than 10%
of tumor cells showing membrane staining (score = 1+) (9).
HER2 positivity is when greater than 10% of cancer cells show
strong  and  complete  membrane  staining  (score  =  3+)  (9).
While it is the fastest and least expensive method, immuno-
histochemistry is limited by lack of standardization in fixation
and antigen retrieval processes and can result in an indeter-
minate score (9,10). When less than 10% of cancer cells show
strong and complete membrane staining or greater than 10%
of  cancer  cells  show  weak  or  moderate  membrane  staining,
HER2 positivity is defined as indeterminate (score = 2+) (9).
FISH  is  highly  sensitive  and  specific  but  is  also  more  ex-
pensive and time intensive. FISH enables evaluation of HER2
gene amplification without or with chromosome 17 amplifi-
cation  by  direct  visualization.  Scoring  is  based  on  direct  vi-
sualization of four or more gene copies per nucleus or when
the ratio of HER2 gene numbers to chromosome 17 copies is
greater than or equal to two (9). FISH is superior to immuno-
histochemistry in the ability to assess treatment response to
trastuzumab (11).

Chromogenic  in  situ  hybridization  (CISH)  and  silver-en-
hanced  in  situ  hybridization  (SISH)  are  similar  to  the  pro-
cedure  used  in  FISH  but  use  different  reagents  to  provide  a
permanent  preparation  (10).  In  situ  hybridization  tests  are

Imaging Findings at Detection
In  the  United  States,  approximately  5.1  cancers  per  1000
mammographic  studies  are  detected  at  screening  mammog-
raphy, and 64.5 cancers per 1000 mammographic studies are
detected  at  diagnostic  mammography  of  a  palpable  finding
(13,14). At presentation, the majority of breast cancers are lo-
cal within the breast (62%), while 31% have spread to regional
lymph nodes and 6% are metastatic, beyond the breast and re-
gional lymph nodes (1). The typical appearance of HER2-pos-
itive breast cancer at mammography is an irregular mass with
spiculated margins (Figs 2, 3A, 3B).

The  presence  of  microcalcifications  is  highly  associated
with  the  HER2-positive  subtype  (Figs  4–6).  Microcalcifica-
tions  often  signify  the  presence  of  ductal  carcinoma  in  situ
(DCIS)  (15).  Calcifications  may  be  present  alone  (Figs  4A,
4C, 5A, 5C) or in association with a mass (Fig 6A–6C), focal
asymmetry,  or  architectural  distortion  with  or  without  ex-
tension  beyond  the  noncalcified  finding.  Microcalcifications
have been reported to be in at least 60.3% of all HER2-positive
cancers and do not significantly differ according to estrogen
receptor and progesterone receptor status (16).

Irregular hypoechoic masses with echogenic foci of micro-
calcifications at US have also been shown to be associated with
HER2-positive  overexpression  (Fig  3D)  (15).  HER2-positive
overexpression  at  MRI  often  manifests  as  an  irregular  mass
with  washout  kinetics  (Fig  7A),  with  or  without  associated
nonmass enhancement (Figs 8A, 9A), and is commonly mul-
tifocal, with a reported overall pooled odds ratio of 2.45 (15).

Neoadjuvant Chemotherapies
NACT is given to patients with locally advanced or inopera-
ble cancers and is more often provided for early-stage disease,
particularly in HER2-positive breast cancers and triple-nega-
tive subtypes (8). One goal is to downstage tumors and axil-
lary metastases in the preoperative setting to achieve a higher
breast  conservation  treatment  (BCT)  rate  (8).  Additional
important  advantages  include  assessing  early  treatment  re-
sponse to potentially alter and individualize treatment plans,
determine the need for adjuvant therapies, and project long-
term outcomes (8).

Targeted monoclonal antibodies have revolutionized treat-
ment  of  HER2-positive  breast  cancers.  Trastuzumab  (Her-
ceptin;  Genentech)  is  the  first  monoclonal  antibody  against
the  extracellular  domain  of  HER2,  often  used  in  smaller
tumors,  and  pertuzumab  is  another  monoclonal  antibody
against the HER2 dimerization domain, often used in larger
tumors  (2).  Common  neoadjuvant  regimens  include  use  of
a  taxane  such  as  docetaxel  or  paclitaxel,  carboplatin,  trastu-
zumab, and possibly addition of pertuzumab (TCH ± P); or
an  anthracycline-based  regimen  such  as  adriamycin/cyclo-
phosphamide  (AC),  a  taxane,  and  targeted  monoclonal  an-
tibody therapy (2,8). Taxanes disrupt cellular mitosis by im-
pairing  the  centrosome,  spindle,  and  microtubule  function
(17). Carboplatin cross-links DNA to disrupt DNA replication
(2).  Anthracyclines  prevent  DNA  replication  by  disrupting
topoisomerase, an enzyme necessary for DNA unwinding be-
fore replication (2,18).

no tumor cells are identified in the surgical specimen after
NACT, is an important marker of both disease-free survival

Radiologic Assessment of NACT Response
The radiologist plays an important role in evaluating treat-
ment response to guide surgical management or use of addi-
tional adjuvant therapies. Currently, there are no standards
for reporting imaging assessment of breast tumor response
to NACT (22). The standardized Response Evaluation Crite-
ria in Solid Tumors (RECIST) method, meant to assess met-

astatic  disease  response  in  all  types  of  cancer,  is  not  appli-
cable to the majority of early local or regional breast cancer
cases in the neoadjuvant setting. RECIST lacks recommen-
dations to use mammography, does not recommend use of
US owing to operator dependence, but does advocate breast
MRI follow-up (23).

Often, the longest tumor diameter and the American Col-
lege of Radiology Breast Imaging Reporting and Data System
(BI-RADS)  descriptors  are  used  to  compare  pre-NACT  and
post-NACT imaging with mammography, US, or MRI at the
discretion of the referring provider (22). The radiologist visu-
ally  assesses  tumor  response  on  the  basis  of  the  modalities
reviewed and assigns complete, partial, stable/unchanged, or
progression imaging response after NACT.

Of note, posttreatment changes including inflammation,
fibrosis, and necrosis can complicate post-NACT measure-
ments (24). It is important to note that there are no current
guidelines  for  post-NACT  imaging  and  that  radiologic  as-
sessment  often  results  in  underestimation  or  overestima-
tion  of  disease  (22,24).  It  has  been  shown  that  small  dis-
ease less than 2 cm is missed with MRI and that remaining
disease up to 5 cm is overcalled when there is pCR at final

In  general,  mammography  enables  prediction  of  patho-

(22,25). For tumors that manifest as a mammographic mass,

the  most  reliable  indication  of  treatment  response  is  a  de-
crease in its size and density (Fig 3) (26).

agents  lapatinib  and  trastuzumab,  mammography  demon-

Calcifications
Mammographic calcifications present a difficult challenge to
the radiologist in assessing imaging response to NACT when
compared with pathologic response. Reported rates of imag-
ing responses after NACT include resolution (4%), decreased
(12.5%–18%), stable/no change (50%–82.8%), increased (7.9%–
29%), and even new calcifications (29–31). Calcifications pose
an  added  element  of  difficulty  when  evaluating  treatment

response  after  NACT,  as  remaining  calcifications  have  been
shown to be an inaccurate reflection of pCR. Remaining post-
treatment  calcifications  may  represent  residual  tumor  (Figs
4C–4E,  6D–6F)  but  can  also  reflect  tumor  necrosis  or  post-
treatment changes (2,29–34).

In  a  study  that  evaluated  90  patients  who  underwent  pre-
NACT  and  post-NACT  mammographic  and  MRI  assessment,
post-NACT  calcification  response  did  not  correlate  with  MRI
response or pCR (31). While loss of MRI enhancement strongly
correlated with pCR and 79% of patients with pCR had no re-
maining enhancement at MRI, MRI response did not accurately
predict  absence  of  tumor  in  the  presence  of  mammographic
calcifications  (31).  Remaining  calcifications  were  associated
with ductal carcinoma in situ (DCIS) or invasive ductal carci-
noma (IDC) 37.8% of the time at final pathologic analysis; 82%
of these cases demonstrated decreased MRI enhancement and
18% demonstrated resolution of MRI enhancement (31).

US and Radiologic-Pathologic Concordance
US has been shown to be more accurate than mammography
for evaluating tumor size after NACT, with overall accuracy
of  approximately  80%  for  pathologic  response  after  NACT
(22,25). However, US has been shown to result in overestima-
tion of remaining tumor size in 20% of all tumors and under-
estimation of remaining tumor size in 17% of all tumors by 1.0
cm or more (35).

Specifically,  a  multicenter  prospective  trial  of  123  patients
who underwent pre-NACT and post-NACT US found that when
compared with pathologic analysis, US resulted in overestima-
tion of tumor size by 1.0 cm or more in 19% of HER2-positive
patients and underestimation of tumor size by 1.0 cm or more in
16% (35). Post-NACT fibrosis and remaining hypoechoic tumor
can be difficult to distinguish (36). In the Neoadjuvant Lapatinib
and/or  Trastuzumab  Treatment  Optimization  (Neo-ALTTO)
study  subset  analysis,  US  showed  that  81%  of  HR-negative
HER2-positive patients and 76% of HR-positive HER2-positive
patients had complete or partial imaging response to treatment
at  the  time  of  surgery  (27).  Complete  US  response  correlated
with pCR 57% of the time (Fig 10E, 10F) (27).

In a recent study of sequential US studies performed at base-
line, week 3, week 6, and the end of NACT, the optimal timing
of US for predicting pCR differed by breast cancer subtype (37).
Assessment  of  HR-positive  HER2-positive  tumors  at  6  weeks
allowed the best prediction of pCR, with accuracy of 66% and
positive  predictive  value  of  39%,  suggesting  that  assessment
could be beneficial at 6 weeks for this subtype (37). Assessment
of HR-negative HER2-positive tumors demonstrated the high-
est accuracy of 66.4% at the end of NACT and positive predic-
tive value of 69.0% at 3 weeks, suggesting that assessment could
be beneficial as early as 3 weeks for this subtype rather than at
6 weeks, as well as at the end of treatment (37). Overall, a 10%
continuous reduction in the length of the lesion over time at 3
and 6 weeks increased the chances of pCR (37).

MRI and Radiologic-Pathologic Concordance
MRI  has  been  demonstrated  to  be  the  best  imaging  modal-
ity for evaluating tumor response and is the best predictor of
pCR. The longest diameter of tumor at MRI is the most clini-
cally applicable feature, accurate in predicting overall remain-
ing tumor size after NACT (38).

The multi-institutional prospective American College of Ra-

MRI and histopathologic tumor measurements had the highest
predictive value for determining pathologic response to NACT
(39). Functional tumor volume (FTV), a semiautomated com-
puter  analysis  of  tumor  volume  based  on  MRI  enhancement
thresholds, was performed at different times during the course
of NACT (39). Although FTV was superior to longest diameter
in predicting tumor response and was shown to be a strong pre-
dictor of recurrence-free survival (39), FTV measurements are
not widely used in clinical practice. Overall, MRI demonstrates
good  predictive  accuracy  for  pCR  of  0.77,  high  sensitivity  of
0.91, and lower specificity of 0.63 (40,41).

At  MRI,  patients  with  HR-negative  HER2-positive  can-
cers  have  been  shown  to  more  often  demonstrate  complete
response  after  NACT  than  patients  with  HR-positive,  tri-
ple-negative, or even HR-positive HER2-positive cancers (Fig
11)  (42).  Among  patients  with  complete  response  at  MRI,
52%–61%  experience  pCR  (40,43,44).  Specifically,  the  neg-
ative  predictive  value  at  which  MRI  enables  correct  predic-
tion of no remaining tumor has been reported to be higher in
HR-negative HER2-positive patients at 73.6%–88% and lower
in HR-positive HER2-positive patients at 30%–57% (40,43,44).

The  positive  predictive  value  of  residual  tumor  at  MRI
with  residual  disease  found  at  pathologic  analysis  has  been
reported to be up to 82.1% in HER2-positive tumors (40). In
a study of 296 patients with 297 tumors, the positive predic-
tive  value  of  MRI  predicting  true-positive  remaining  tumor
with remaining enhancement present after NACT was 78% in
HR-positive HER2-positive tumors and only 50% in HR-nega-
tive HER2-positive tumors (44).

MRI  is  the  least  likely  modality  to  result  in  overestima-
tion or underestimation of remaining tumor size after NACT
when compared with physical examination, mammography,
and  US  (Figs  7,  8)  (24,45).  In  patients  with  radiologic  com-
plete response at MRI that is discordant with pathologic anal-
ysis, mammographic calcifications, multifocal or multicentric
disease, and nonmass enhancement at pre-NACT imaging are
associated with false-negative results (46).

treatment  (BCT)  (Fig  9)  (31,47).  A  study  by  Jochelson  et  al

Future Directions
Given the current limitations of mammography, US, and MRI
in predicting response to NACT, ongoing research is being per-
formed regarding how best to evaluate treatment response with
new innovations. Some are evaluating the use of image-guided
breast  biopsy  after  NACT  to  predict  residual  cancer  (48–51).

In a multicenter trial subset analysis of 76 women with post-
NACT imaging abnormalities less than or equal to 2 cm who
underwent at least six vacuum-assisted biopsies of the remain-
ing tumor bed, the results of these biopsies in predicting pCR
demonstrated a false-negative rate of 3.2% (49). In 20 women
who underwent post-NACT MRI-guided biopsies, MRI-guided
biopsy demonstrated a high negative predictive value of 92.8%
for  demonstrating  no  invasive  disease  and  85.8%  for  demon-
strating no invasive or in situ disease (50). Post-NACT biopsies
may  be  able  to  influence  de-escalation  of  chemotherapies  in
certain responders and eventually lead to omission of surgery
in exceptional responders who achieve pCR with a negative im-
age-guided breast biopsy after NACT (48–51).

Quantitative  functional  imaging  modalities  and  sequences
such  as  contrast-enhanced  mammography  (CEM),  diffu-
sion-weighted MRI, MRI radiomics, PET, and molecular breast
imaging  (MBI)  are  primarily  investigational  in  evaluation  of
NACT response. CEM uses iodinated contrast material in mam-

mography to assess tumor vascularity, similar to MRI, and has
been approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration since
2011 for diagnostic use (52). Small single-institution trials have
shown that the sensitivity of CEM of 76%–100% and specificity
of  66.7%–87.5%  are  comparable  to  those  of  MRI  in  detecting
pCR after NACT, indicating that this emerging modality may
play a role in monitoring treatment response (53–56).

Diffusion-weighted MRI was investigated by the American
College of Radiology Imaging Network (ACRIN) 6698 study,
the first prospective multicenter clinical trial to evaluate alter-
ations in water movement and tumor cellularity after NACT
by measuring the apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) (57).
The  study  found  that  increased  ADC  at  diffusion-weighted
MRI after 12 weeks of chemotherapy, which is indicative of
decreased  tumor  cellularity,  was  moderately  predictive  of
pCR (57). Quantitative textural imaging of HER2-positive tu-
mors and their peritumoral environment via MRI radiomics
may enable differentiation of responders and nonresponders

PET is often used for systemic staging of breast cancer, with
ongoing investigations into NACT response. PET studies with
fluorodeoxyglucose  (FDG),  the  most  commonly  used  molec-
ular imaging agent for evaluating the metabolism of glucose,
show  high  sensitivity  and  specificity  in  evaluating  NACT  re-
sponse and identifying nonresponders (22). However, false neg-
atives due to spatial resolution constraints leading to difficulty
detecting small tumors less than 1 cm and false negatives due to
effective therapy leading to alterations in metabolism limit use
of  PET  (22).  Furthermore,  standardized  uptake  value  (SUV)
cutoffs are not standardized for treatment response (22).

MBI  using  technetium  99m–sestamibi  accumulation
within mitochondria of metabolic cells may potentially help
in  breast  cancer  detection  after  NACT.  A  large  prospective
study  comparing  MBI  and  MRI  showed  comparable  results
in evaluating tumor response, with MBI having higher speci-

ficity and positive predictive value than breast MRI but lower
sensitivity  and  negative  predictive  value,  as  well  as  insuffi-
cient accuracy in predicting pCR (60).

Development and potential use of imaging-guided biopsies
after NACT and functional imaging modalities hold promise
and may provide further insight into appropriate chemother-
apy treatment, surgical management, and overall prognosis.

Conclusion
HER2-positive breast cancers have improved disease-free and
overall  survival  outcomes  with  newer  targeted  chemother-
apy agents, which may be reflected in imaging response and
pathologic response to treatment. Radiologists guide surgical
decision  making  by  determining  the  extent  of  disease  be-
fore  chemotherapy  and  imaging  response  after  chemother-
apy. Difficulties in assessment with mammography, US, and
MRI remain, especially in patients who present with mam-
mographic calcifications.

MRI provides the best evaluation, with high negative pre-
dictive  value  and  correlation  with  pCR  regardless  of  mam-
mographic  manifestation.  Although  MRI  response  with  no
remaining  enhancement  cannot  override  remaining  mam-
mographic calcifications to determine the type of surgery per-
formed, combined mammographic or sonographic and MRI
response may provide insight into the overall prognosis when
compared with the final pathologic response.

Final  pathologic  confirmation  after  surgery  remains  the
standard  of  reference  for  assessing  the  need  for  additional
therapy  and  informing  prognosis.  Innovations,  including
post-NACT  imaging-guided  biopsies  and  tumor  and  peritu-
moral radiomics, may lead to better evaluation of treatment
response and guide future chemotherapy and surgery de-es-
calation research and clinical implementation.
