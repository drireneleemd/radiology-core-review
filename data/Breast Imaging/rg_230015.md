Mucin-containing lesions of the breast encompass a wide range of benign and malignant processes. The spectrum of histologic
findings includes incidental mucus-filled benign cysts or ducts, mucocele-like lesions (MLLs), mucin-producing ductal carcinoma
in situ, and invasive mucinous carcinoma. MLL is characterized by the presence of mucin-containing cysts that are typically asso-
ciated with extravasated stromal mucin. MLL is often benign but can be associated with epithelial atypia or malignancy. Mucinous
carcinoma represents the malignant end of the spectrum of mucinous lesions of the breast. Evidence-based literature supports a
conservative approach for benign MLLs without associated atypia or malignancy, reserving excision for those lesions exhibiting
such pathologic features. The most common imaging finding for MLL is microcalcifications at mammography. No specific imaging
feature is predictive of malignant outcome at surgical excision. Invasive mucinous carcinoma is a heterogeneous breast tumor sub-
type, as defined according to the World Health Organization criteria. Mucinous carcinomas are categorized into pure (>90% mu-
cinous component) or mixed (10%–90% mucinous component) subtypes. Pure mucinous carcinomas are generally associated with
excellent prognosis and survival, with a few exceptions. Mixed mucinous carcinomas do not have the same favorable prognosis and
instead behave similarly to invasive breast carcinomas of no special type. Characteristic diagnostic imaging features can be identi-
fied for mucinous carcinoma based on its mucinous and nonmucinous contents.

Introduction
The spectrum of benign and malignant mucin-containing le-
sions of the breast is broad. While many of these lesions do not
pose  either  a  diagnostic  challenge  or  management  difficulty,
there can be clinical uncertainty regarding the diagnosis and
management  of  mucocele-like  lesions  (MLLs).  Based  on  an
analysis of the current literature and contemporary guidelines,

we present an evidence-based practical approach to the man-

trum  represented  by  invasive  mucinous  carcinoma,  a  tumor
subtype with heterogeneous histopathologic findings. We also

Benign Mucin-containing Lesions
Mucins are a family of membrane-associated and secreted gly-
coproteins  that  are  synthesized  by  epithelial  cells.  They  play
diverse roles ranging from the physical protection of epithelial
surfaces to cellular functions such as modulation of immune
response and cell signaling. In the breast, most secretions are
nonmucinous, but occasionally incidental benign cysts or ducts
may contain mucin. Included in the broad differential diagnosis
of benign mucinous breast lesions are fibrocystic change with
luminal  mucin;  cystic  hypersecretory  lesions;  breast  lesions
containing  stromal  mucin  such  as  nodular  mucinosis;  and  a
variety of lesions with stromal mucinous and myxoid changes
such as myxoma, myxoid fibroepithelial lesions, pleomorphic
adenoma, and nodular fasciitis (1,2). The imaging features for
benign lesions vary and are nonspecific, as the lesions that fall
into this category are many and varied.

Mucocele-like Lesions
The term mucocele-like lesion derives from the lesion’s histo-
logic  analogy  to  a  mucocele—a  mucin-containing  cystic  le-
sion of the minor salivary glands in the oral cavity. MLL of the
breast, first described by Rosen (3) in 1986, is a histologic find-
ing  characterized  by  an  aggregate  of  mucin-containing  cysts
with  rupture  and  mucin  extravasation  into  stroma.  Benign
MLL is often used to refer to an MLL without associated atypia
or carcinoma, and therefore it falls under the broad descriptive

category of benign mucin-containing lesions. The pathophys-
iology  most  likely  reflects  excessive  production  of  mucinous
secretions, leading to cystic distention of glands and eventual
rupture.  MLL  is  a  rare  entity  on  a  spectrum  of  mucin-con-
taining lesions, characterized by a ruptured mucin-filled cyst,
with  extravasation  of  mucin  into  the  surrounding  stroma.  It
frequently  coexists  with  atypical  hyperplasia  or  lesions  with
cytologic atypia. MLL is a descriptive term, and its clinical sig-
nificance is determined by the nature of the epithelium lining
the associated cysts, which may be nonatypical, atypical, or ma-
lignant. Patients with MLL have a mean age of 54 years (range,
27–82 years), and most lesions are less than 2 cm in size (1).

MLL of the breast is rare, with a contemporary retrospec-
tive series reporting an incidence of approximately 2% (3–13),
and it is identified in less than 1% of core biopsies. MLL most
frequently manifests as a mammographic abnormality, but it
occasionally may form a palpable mass or be discovered inci-
dentally in tissue removed for other indications. The majority
of MLLs are benign, a category that encompasses both MLLs
that  are  associated  with  nonatypical  epithelium  and  those
with associated atypical hyperplasia. Approximately 30%–45%
of MLLs are associated with atypical hyperplasia (6,7), which
is  most  often  atypical  ductal  hyperplasia.  MLL  may  also  be
associated  with  other  epithelial  alterations  such  as  flat  epi-
thelial atypia and nonatypical columnar cell lesions. The inci-
dence of malignancy (either ductal carcinoma in situ [DCIS]
or invasive carcinoma) in association with MLL is difficult to
ascertain, as most studies assessing outcomes in patients with
MLL exclude cases with concomitant carcinoma (3–13).

Diagnostic Features at Mammography, US, and MRI
MLL  most  commonly  manifests  as  an  incidental  finding  of
grouped  suspicious  microcalcifications  at  mammography
(fine pleomorphic, coarse heterogeneous, fine linear) (Fig 1),
likely  due  to  the  tendency  of  mucin  to  calcify.  Rarely,  MLL
may manifest as a mass or focal asymmetry with calcifications
(Fig 2) or a mass with or without circumscribed margins (14–
16).  The  spectrum  of  reported  sonographic  findings  spans
from  well-defined  hypoechoic  oval  or  tubular  masses  with
low-level internal echoes to complex cystic and solid masses
and cystic lesions, including grouped cysts (17,18) (Figs 3, 4,
S1). No specific imaging feature is predictive of atypia or ma-
lignant outcome at surgical excision.

The  vast  majority  of  MLLs  are  diagnosed  at  mammogra-
phy and US. Due to the rarity of this diagnosis being made at
MRI, specific MRI features of MLL are not widely reported. In
a limited series of four patients, the reported MRI findings of
MLL include irregular homogeneously enhancing masses and
nonmass enhancement (4,18,19).

extravasated mucin (Fig 5). The epithelial cells lining the cysts

Practical Considerations and Management Options
The  majority  of  pure  MLLs  without  cytologic  atypia  have  a
benign outcome (7–11). For MLLs without atypia diagnosed
at core biopsy, the upgrade rate at surgical excision to DCIS or
invasive carcinoma ranges from 0% to 11%, and for MLLs with
atypia, the upgrade rate ranges from 8% to 33% (6–13). In one
study, no upgrades were found among 14 cases of MLL with
atypia (12). The majority of upgrades are to DCIS. Table 2 sum-
marizes the upgrade rates at surgical excision. Published data
from long-term clinical outcome studies of MLL demonstrate
no significant elevated breast cancer risk attributable to MLL
(20–22). In a contemporary series, Meares et al (20) reported
long-term  clinical  follow-up  in  102  patients  with  MLL  over
14.8 years. Thirteen patients (12.7%) developed breast cancer,
at a similar rate compared with a cohort of women with be-
nign proliferative breast disease; 46% of the subsequent can-
cers were in the contralateral breast to the index MLL.

Radiologic features are not adequate to distinguish between
nonatypical,  atypical,  and  malignant  MLLs.  Historically,  all
MLLs diagnosed with core needle biopsy were surgically ex-
cised. Proponents of routine surgical excision for MLLs diag-
nosed with core needle biopsy tout lesional heterogeneity, the

mends a selective management approach for MLL diagnosed
with  core  needle  biopsy  that  is  based  on  the  presence  or  ab-
sence  of  cytologic  atypia  (23).  Clinical  management  of  MLL
is  contingent  on  the  presence  of  associated  epithelial  atypia
or  malignancy,  with  imaging  surveillance  recommended  for
benign MLL and consideration of surgical excision for lesions
with concurrent pathologic features of atypia or neoplasia. For
MLL with atypia, surgical excision is recommended. For MLL
without atypia, surgical excision is recommended if the pres-
ence  of  atypia  would  alter  patient  management;  atypical  hy-
perplasia is found on excision in 8%–16% of these cases (4,7,8).
Conservative management with imaging surveillance may be
elected  for  those  patients  not  undergoing  excision  (23).  The
presence  of  a  small  focus  of  extravasated  stromal  mucin  not
otherwise defined as MLL, or identified as an incidental find-
ing characterized as minute size, is of uncertain clinical signif-
icance (19). Conservative management may be appropriate for
a small or incidental finding of MLL without atypia. Available

data from long-term clinical follow-up studies (20–22) suggest
the risk of local recurrence, malignant potential, or progres-
sion to invasive carcinoma in MLL without atypia to be quite
low, lending further support for a more selective approach to
management.

Mucinous Carcinoma
Invasive  mucinous  carcinoma,  also  known  as  colloid  carci-
noma, accounts for approximately 2%–4% of all invasive breast
cancers. It occurs over a broad age range but is most common
in postmenopausal women (range, 25–85 years of age) and is
rarely seen in men (24,25). It may manifest as a clinically pal-
pable mass or as a radiographically detected lesion. Tumors are
typically slow growing. Mucinous carcinoma is a special sub-
type of invasive ductal carcinoma that is characterized by clus-
ters and aggregates of tumor cells suspended in mucin pools.
Mucinous carcinomas are subclassified as pure or mixed based
on the percentage of the mucinous component according to the
fifth edition of the World Health Organization (WHO) Classifi-
cation of Breast Tumors (2019) (26).

In  pure  mucinous  carcinoma,  the  mucinous  component
comprises  more  than  90%  of  the  tumor.  Pure  mucinous  car-
cinomas are typically associated with indolent behavior and a
favorable prognosis, with low recurrence rates, low metastatic
risk,  and  infrequent  regional  lymph  node  involvement  (24).
Mixed mucinous carcinomas have a 10%–90% mucin compo-
nent, and the remaining nonmucinous component is typically
invasive breast carcinoma of no special type (IBC-NST), previ-
ously known as invasive ductal carcinoma, not otherwise spec-
ified. Mixed mucinous carcinoma does not have the same fa-
vorable prognosis as pure mucinous carcinoma. The prognosis
is instead similar to that of IBC-NST, and it is associated with
higher rates of nodal metastases than is pure mucinous carci-
noma  (27–30).  There  is  no  specific  category  for  tumors  with
less than 10% mucinous component, and these are diagnosed
descriptively as IBC-NST with focal mucin production and not
considered a form of mucinous carcinoma.

Diagnostic Imaging Features at Mammography

Pure  Mucinous  Carcinoma.—At  mammography,  the  typical
imaging finding is a round or oval low- to high-density mass,
with or without circumscribed margins (31–33) (Figs 7, 8). A
circumscribed or microlobulated margin correlates histologi-
cally to pools of extracellular mucin. Due to their mucin con-
tent, mucinous carcinomas may be mammographically occult
or  manifest  as  nonmass  findings,  such  as  asymmetry,  focal
asymmetry, or a mass with indistinct margins, possibly lead-
ing to delayed diagnosis (Fig 8). Accordingly, these mucin-con-
taining lesions may appear effaced on spot compression views,
and caution is advised when evaluating for the possibility of
such lesions being mucinous carcinoma at mammography.

Mixed Mucinous Carcinoma.—Imaging features may help dis-
tinguish between pure and mixed mucinous carcinomas. Mass
margin  is  an  important  distinguishing  feature  between  pure
and mixed tumor subtypes. Indistinct or spiculated margins are
more  typical  of  mixed  mucinous  carcinomas  (32–36)  (Figs  9,
10), reflective of the higher percentage of conventional nonmu-
cinous carcinoma. Compared with the pure subtype, microcal-
cifications are more commonly seen in mixed lesions and most
likely  correspond  to  the  presence  of  a  conventional  invasive
ductal component or DCIS (37).

Surgical, Medical, and Oncologic Management
A complete discussion of surgical and oncologic management
is outside of the scope of this article. According to the National
Comprehensive  Cancer  Network  Clinical  Practice  Guide-
lines  in  Oncology  (version  4,  2022)  (59),  the  local-regional
treatment  algorithm  for  mucinous  carcinoma,  either  pure
or  mixed,  is  similar  to  that  for  IBC-NST.  Radiation  therapy
is standard for patients undergoing breast-conserving therapy
for mucinous carcinoma but it may sometimes be omitted for
selected low-risk tumors with good prognosis (60). Hormonal
therapy is recommended for most patients, as mucinous car-
cinomas are usually robustly positive for estrogen and proges-
terone receptors. Decisions regarding systemic chemotherapy
are made based on standard considerations used for IBC-NST.
Oncotype  DX  Breast  Recurrence  Score  Test  results  typically
show low or intermediate recurrence scores for both pure and
mixed mucinous carcinomas (61).

There are no specific guidelines for the treatment of ER-
and  PR-positive  and  HER2-negative  mucinous  carcinomas
with  neoadjuvant  chemotherapy,  and  the  decision  is  made
based on standard factors such as tumor stage and ER, PR,
and  HER2  status  (62).  In  patients  undergoing  treatment
with  neoadjuvant  chemotherapy  or  endocrine  therapy,  the
epithelial  component  of  the  tumor  may  undergo  a  robust
or  complete  response,  but  because  the  extracellular  mucin
remains  behind,  persistent  masslike  areas  may  be  seen  at
imaging as well as at gross evaluation. The presence of re-
sidual mucin alone does not preclude a pathologic complete
response, although a diligent search by the pathologist may
be required to detect scattered scant residual tumor cells. If
residual  tumor  cells  are  identified  within  mucin  pools,  the
tumor  is  pathologically  staged  according  to  the  size  of  the
largest discrete area of involvement (2,62).

Other Malignant Mucinous Lesions of the Breast

Solid Papillary Carcinoma
Solid papillary carcinoma is a subtype of papillary carcinoma
that  is  characterized  by  closely  apposed,  expansile,  and  cel-
lular nodules of tumor cells with interspersed delicate fibro-
vascular cores. Solid papillary carcinomas frequently contain
both intra- and extracellular mucin and commonly show neu-
roendocrine  differentiation  (65),  and  they  show  an  associa-
tion  with  type  B  (hypercellular)  mucinous  carcinomas.  The
tumors  are  typically  hormone  receptor  positive  and  HER2
negative.  Solid  papillary  carcinomas  have  indolent  behavior
and a favorable prognosis.

Solid papillary carcinoma is most commonly encountered
in older women (66), and it may manifest as a palpable mass,
with bloody nipple discharge, or as a mass detected at screen-
ing mammography. At imaging, most lesions are detected at
US as hypoechoic masses with irregular margins, intraductal
masses, or complex cystic and solid masses. Mammographic
findings are masses with circumscribed or obscured margins,
and  MRI  findings  include  enhancing  masses  and  linear  or
segmental nonmass enhancement (67).

Mucinous Cystadenocarcinoma
Mucinous  cystadenocarcinoma  is  an  exceedingly  rare  World
Health  Organization  (WHO)–classified  subtype  of  invasive
breast carcinoma that is characterized by cystic spaces lined by
neoplastic columnar epithelium with prominent papillary ar-
chitecture and abundant intra- and extracellular mucin (68,69).
This malignant lesion has been reported mostly in postmeno-
pausal women (68). The tumor morphology resembles that of
pancreatobiliary  or  ovarian  mucinous  cystadenocarcinoma.
Imaging findings have been described as high-density masses
at mammography and hypoechoic masses with circumscribed
or  indistinct  margins  at  US  (70).  Mucinous  cystadenocarci-
noma of the breast is typically negative for ER, PR, and HER2.
Lymph node involvement is uncommon, and the tumors ap-
pear to have a relatively favorable prognosis.

Breast  carcinomas  with  prominent  signet  ring  cells  are  mu-
cin-producing  morphologic  variants  of  either  invasive  ductal
carcinoma  or  invasive  lobular  carcinoma  and  are  no  longer
separately WHO classified. The characteristic histologic feature
is the presence of tumor cells with intracytoplasmic mucinous
vacuoles that compress the nuclei to one side, resembling sig-
net rings (71). When signet ring cells comprise more than 20%
of a tumor, the tumor can be categorized as a signet ring cell
carcinoma.  When  the  signet  ring  cells  are  extensive,  careful
pathologic evaluation is needed to exclude a metastasis from an

Invasive Lobular Carcinoma with Extracellular Mucin
Invasive lobular carcinoma with extracellular mucin is a rare
variant  of  invasive  lobular  carcinoma  (73,74).  The  tumor  is
characterized by the typical growth pattern of lobular carci-
noma but with the additional finding of extracellular mucin
pools. Most tumors are ER and PR positive, and a minority are
HER2 positive. Invasive lobular carcinoma with extracellular
mucin  is  distinguished  from  mixed  mucinous  carcinoma  by
the  lobular  morphology  of  the  tumor.  Mammographic  find-
ings  were  described  as  masses  with  spiculated  or  nodular
margins,  with  or  without  associated  microcalcifications  and
areas of architectural distortion (75).

DCIS with Intraluminal Mucin
DCIS is occasionally associated with conspicuous mucin pro-
duction (Fig 2C). This may be seen in conventional patterns
of DCIS, such as micropapillary or cribriform, or in solid pap-
illary  carcinoma  in  situ.  DCIS  that  is  associated  with  muci-
nous carcinoma frequently contains intraluminal mucin, and
transitional stages between the two lesions can be observed.
The intraluminal mucin in such cases often shows neovascu-
larization (46).

Conclusion
MLL  is  characterized  by  an  aggregate  of  mucin-filled  cysts
that  are  typically  associated  with  mucin  extravasation  into
surrounding stroma. The majority of MLLs without cytologic
atypia that are diagnosed with core biopsy have a benign out-
come and can be managed conservatively with imaging sur-
veillance. When there is associated atypia, surgical excision is
recommended. The  pathologic  hallmark  of  mucinous  carci-
noma is the presence of malignant epithelial cells suspended
in  pools  of  extracellular  mucin.  Pure  mucinous  carcinoma
is  associated  with  a  favorable  prognosis  and  excellent  over-
all survival. It is important to differentiate between pure and
mixed mucinous carcinomas, as the latter are associated with
a worse prognosis. Characteristic imaging findings may help
differentiate pure from mixed forms of mucinous carcinoma.
