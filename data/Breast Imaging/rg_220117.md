Breast masses in children and adolescents are uncommon,
and  the  spectrum  of  pediatric  breast  masses  is  predomi-
nantly benign and different from that in adults. Knowledge
of  the  clinical  presentation  and  imaging  features  of  the
various  stages  of  normal  development  and  mass-forming
lesions in the pediatric breast can guide a tailored imaging
approach and help the radiologist make a definitive diagno-
sis. Breast development begins during fetal gestation along
the embryologic milk lines and continues through puberty
as the breast matures through the Tanner stages of devel-
opment.  Normal  and  developmental  variants  and  benign
neoplastic and nonneoplastic lesions in the pediatric breast
are common causes of concern. Malignant breast masses in
children are rare and are more often due to metastasis than
primary  breast  cancer.  When  clinically  warranted,  US  is
the mainstay for imaging the pediatric breast and requires
careful  correlation  of  sonographic  findings  with  patient
age  and  history.  Breast  MRI  can  be  used  to  further  char-
acterize lesions and evaluate the extent of disease. Biopsy
should be considered only for suspicious findings and must
be weighed against the risk of iatrogenic injury to the de-
veloping  breast.  Given  that  the  majority  of  mass-forming
lesions  in  the  pediatric  breast  are  benign,  the  diagnostic
and  management  approach  should  emphasize  “first  do
no  harm.”  Knowledge  of  the  imaging  appearance  of  nor-
mal  breast  development  and  the  spectrum  of  benign  and
malignant pediatric breast masses is necessary to make the
correct diagnosis.

TEACHING POINTS
	 Given that most pediatric breast lesions are benign, the primary objective in
the evaluation of such lesions is to “first do no harm.” An initial assessment
with  a  thorough  clinical  history  and  physical  examination  is  often  all  that  is
needed to provide reassurance of normalcy.

	 US is preferred for evaluation of the pediatric breast because of its wide avail-
ability, lack of ionizing radiation, sensitivity in dense breast tissue, and ease for
comparison with the contralateral side and because it requires no sedation in
pediatric patients.

	 Normal breast development can be asymmetric, with up to a 2-year difference

in the phase of development between each breast.

	 Pubertal gynecomastia may be bilateral, unilateral, or asymmetric and affects
approximately  30%–60%  of  pubertal  boys.  The  peak  age  of  onset  is  13–14
years, and spontaneous involution typically occurs within 2 years as androgen
levels rise and antagonize the estrogen effect.

	 The US appearance of primary breast cancer is variable and nonspecific. As in
adult patients, breast cancer in pediatric patients commonly manifests sono-
graphically as an irregular hypoechoic mass with suspicious margins, internal
heterogeneity, antiparallel orientation, and variable posterior features.

Introduction
Palpable  breast  masses  in  children  and  adolescents  are  un-
common. Given the high prevalence of breast cancer in adult
patients, a breast lump in a child may be a source of concern
for  patients  and  their  families. The  spectrum  of  mass-form-
ing breast lesions in pediatric patients is different from that
in adults (Table), and the vast majority are benign, with only
0.2%  of  primary  breast  cancers  arising  in  pediatric  patients
(1).  Benign  causes  of  a  breast  lump  in  children  and  adoles-
cents include normal and developmental variants, infection,
trauma,  and  tumors  such  as  fibroadenomas.  Malignant  tu-
mors in the pediatric breast are extremely rare and constitute
only  5%  of  pediatric  breast  masses  (1).  A  malignant  breast
mass in this age group is most often a metastasis rather than a
primary breast cancer.

Given  that  most  pediatric  breast  lesions  are  benign,  the
primary  objective  in  the  evaluation  of  such  lesions  is  to
“first  do  no  harm.”  An  initial  assessment  with  a  thorough
clinical history and physical examination is often all that is
needed to provide reassurance of normalcy. When imaging
is  deemed  clinically  necessary,  a  tailored  approach  should
be  chosen  to  minimize  harm  to  the  patient.  US  is  the  pri-
mary  modality  used  to  evaluate  and  characterize  pediatric
breast masses given its high specificity and lack of ionizing
radiation. As in the adult population, masses in pediatric pa-
tients should be considered suspicious if  they  demonstrate
rapid growth or imaging features such as irregular margins
or posterior shadowing or if there is a patient history of non-
breast primary malignancy or radiation therapy to the chest.
Biopsy and surgery should be reserved for only lesions that
demonstrate  highly  suspicious  features,  given  the  risk  for
iatrogenic injury to the developing breast.

In this article, our objectives are to discuss imaging consid-
erations in the evaluation of pediatric breast concerns, review
normal  breast  embryology  and  development,  and  show  the
spectrum of developmental, benign, and malignant pediatric
breast masses and their most common clinical and radiologic
manifestations.

Radiologic Evaluation of the Pediatric Breast
If diagnostic imaging is deemed clinically necessary to estab-
lish a diagnosis or guide management, US is the primary im-
aging modality performed to evaluate the breast in children
and patients under the age of 30 years, according to the Amer-
ican College of Radiology (ACR) appropriateness criteria (2).
US is preferred for evaluation of the pediatric breast because
of its wide availability, lack of ionizing radiation, sensitivity
in dense breast tissue, and ease for comparison with the con-
tralateral side and because it requires no sedation in pediatric
patients. The success of US for detecting and characterizing
breast lesions is operator dependent and requires knowledge
of  appropriate  scanning  technique  and  the  sonographic  ap-
pearance of normal tissue and specific lesions. High-quality
breast  US  is  performed  with  a  7–15-MHz  linear  transducer,
and  the  highest-frequency  transducer  that  penetrates  the

In contrast to its use in adults, mammography is rarely in-

diation–induced  malignant  changes  in  developing  glandular
tissue, poor visualization in young dense breast tissue, and the
extremely low likelihood of breast cancer (4). However, mam-
mography may be useful to help characterize calcifications in
specific  cases,  such  as  ductal  carcinoma  in  situ  and  invasive
ductal carcinoma.

MRI and CT can be used to evaluate the extent of disease,
chest wall masses, and congenital malformations. They may
also be indicated for problem solving or correlation with US
findings. However, similar to mammography, CT is associated
with radiation risk. While breast MRI has no ionizing radia-
tion, the examination may require sedation, depending on the
age of the child. Both CT and MRI often require intravenous
contrast material administration.

When sampling of a mass is indicated, image-guided per-
cutaneous  biopsy  or  excisional  biopsy  may  be  considered.
While  core-needle  biopsy  (CNB)  and  fine-needle  aspiration
results  may  each  provide  a  diagnosis,  CNB  is  generally  pre-
ferred  given  its  higher  diagnostic  sensitivity  and  ability  to
differentiate  in  situ  and  invasive  disease  (5).  Smaller-gauge
needles  (18–16  gauge)  may  be  used  for  CNB,  rather  than
the  14-gauge  needle  commonly  used  in  adults.  Proceeding
directly  with  excisional  biopsy  may  be  warranted  for  highly
suspicious findings or to obviate a potentially traumatic pro-
cedure experience (6). However, all procedures involving the
pediatric breast, including breast biopsies, carry the potential
risk of injury to the developing breast bud (7–10).

Normal Breast Development

In Utero Breast Development
During  the  4th–6th  weeks  of  fetal  development,  ectodermal
tissue migrates into the underlying mesenchyme, creating pri-
mary mammary ridges or milk lines (Fig 1) that extend along
the  anterior  torso  from  the  axilla  to  the  groin  (7).  Normal
involution of the milk lines occurs around the 10th week of
fetal development, with persistence at the fourth intercostal
interspace giving rise to breast buds. The primary breast buds
evolve  into  secondary  breast  buds  that  subsequently  branch
and  elongate  further  to  form  lactiferous  ducts.  An  external
mammary pit forms at the skin and becomes the nipple are-
olar  complex.  Ductal  and  lobular  structures  arise  from  the
milk  lines,  with  fibrous  and  adipose  tissue  arising  from  the
surrounding mesenchyme (9).

Failure  to  regress  along  the  milk  lines  may  lead  to  poly-
thelia  (accessory  nipple)  or  polymastia  (accessory  breast),
whereas amastia (absence of breast tissue, nipple, and areola)

Prepubertal Breast Development
All newborns have breast buds composed of epithelium-lined
lactiferous ducts supported by stromal connective tissue (8).
During the 1st week of life, the ducts may hypertrophy under
the influence of placental hormones, causing transient physi-
ologic neonatal breast enlargement in up to 70% of newborns.
Galactorrhea may also develop in up to 5% of newborns, his-
torically known as “witch’s milk” (9,12). US images will show
a hypoechoic subareolar mass containing varying amounts of
anechoic tubular ducts (Fig 2) (12). Neonatal breast enlarge-
ment is self-limiting, requires no treatment, and often resolves
by 6 months of age. The normal breast bud may continue to
demonstrate physiologic fluctuations in size and remain pal-
pable until 1–2 years of age and then remains quiescent until
puberty (1,12).

der hormone influence; estrogen stimulates ductal elongation
and  progesterone  induces  lobular  growth.  At  the  conclusion

The  five  Tanner  stages  of  female  breast  development  oc-
cur over 2–3 years (12), with each stage having a classic US
appearance  (Fig  4).  Tanner  stage  I  is  prepubertal  and  ap-

stellate hypoechoic focus corresponding to developing ducts
and can correlate with a palpable breast bud. In Tanner stage
III, the breast enlarges and elevates, with expanding hypere-

nodule  with  more  extensive  ducts  extending  deeper  into
the parenchyma. Tanner stage IV can have transient areolar
mounding at physical examination and demonstrate expand-

mature  breast  contour  and  appears  as  heterogeneous  glan-
dular  tissue  without  central  hypoechogenicity  and  with  in-
creased preglandular and subglandular adipose tissue (4,8).

Normal Variations of Breast Development

Asymmetric Breast Development
Normal breast development can be asymmetric, with up to a
2-year difference in the phase of development between each
breast (4,8,13). This may cause patients to present with a uni-
lateral  subareolar  lump  and  can  be  seen  in  the  prepubertal
stage and after the onset of thelarche (Fig 5) (9). US is helpful
to confirm the presence of normal breast tissue.

Accessory Breast Tissue
Accessory  breast  tissue  arises  when  there  is  incomplete  re-
gression of the embryologic milk line and is most commonly

malignant masses (15). Accessory breast tissue may be asymp-

Gynecomastia
Gynecomastia  is  the  overdevelopment  of  breast  tissue  in
men. It occurs most frequently in puberty, when transiently
elevated estrogen levels cause physiologic ductal and stromal
proliferation, and manifests as a tender firm subareolar lump
or diffuse breast enlargement. Pubertal gynecomastia may be
bilateral, unilateral, or asymmetric and affects approximately
30%–60% of pubertal boys (12). The peak age of onset is 13–14
years,  and  spontaneous  involution  typically  occurs  within  2
years as androgen levels rise and antagonize the estrogen ef-
fect (12,17). Gynecomastia can also be secondary to conditions
associated with increased estrogen levels, such as Klinefelter
syndrome,  testicular  feminization  syndrome,  neurofibroma-
tosis type 1, and tumors such as prolactinomas. Drugs can also
cause gynecomastia, including exogenous estrogens, anabolic
steroids, and marijuana (1). Pseudogynecomastia is enlarge-
ment  of  the  breasts  owing  to  general  obesity  and  increased
breast fat deposition and may mimic true gynecomastia (4).

Benign Pediatric Breast Masses

Vascular Masses

Infection

Mastitis  and  Abscess.—Mastitis  is  an  infection  of  breast  tis-
sue and manifests as a tender, indurated, and erythematous
breast.  In  pediatric  patients,  mastitis  commonly  affects  in-
fants in the first 3 weeks of life (mastitis neonatorum) and is
attributed to ductal obstruction or breaks in the skin surface
that allow bacteria, most commonly Staphylococcus aureus, to
seed the breast tissue (7,19). On US images, mastitis appears
as  increased  parenchymal  heterogeneity  and  echogenicity,

Neuronal Mass
Schwannomas  are  benign  neoplasms  arising  from  the
Schwann cells in neural sheaths. They are the most common
type of peripheral nerve tumor and are commonly found in
patients with neurofibromatosis type II (24). While schwan-
nomas may arise anywhere in the body, they are rarely found
in  the  breast.  Although  most  are  benign,  malignant  trans-
formation is possible (24). US will typically show a circum-
scribed hypoechoic mass but may also demonstrate a targe-
toid appearance with a hyperechoic center (Fig 12) (24–27).
On MR images, schwannomas are typically T1 iso- to hyper-
intense, are T2 hyperintense, and enhance diffusely (28). Bi-
opsy is required to make the diagnosis, and surgical excision
is usually recommended as these are of uncertain malignant
potential (29).

Trauma

Hematoma.—Hematoma  formation  in  the  pediatric  breast
may  be  caused  by  iatrogenic  or  sports-related  trauma.  On
US  images,  hematomas  appear  as  avascular  complex  cys-
tic  masses  with  varying  internal  echogenicity,  which  typ-
ically  evolves  from  a  more  hyperechoic  appearance  in  the
acute setting to anechoic as the clot retracts and  lyses (Fig
13) (8,13,14). At CT, hematomas appear as irregular hyper-
attenuating  masses.  Correlating  the  imaging  findings  with
the clinical history and physical examination results is key
to making the diagnosis, and follow-up US can be performed
to ensure resolution. If there is not a clear cause of injury,
nonaccidental trauma should be considered, and the radiol-
ogist should directly communicate the findings to the refer-
ring physician for further investigation and possible skeletal
survey to evaluate for additional injuries (30).

Fat  Necrosis.—Fat  necrosis  is  a  benign  nonsuppurative  in-
flammatory  process  that  occurs  following  physical  or  iatro-
genic  trauma  (31). While  usually  clinically  silent,  fat  necro-
sis  may  manifest  as  a  palpable  lump.  The  US  appearance
of  fat  necrosis  is  variable  and  may  show  a  hyperechoic  or
mixed-echogenicity mass with internal cystic spaces, a cystic
mass, or a hypoechoic mass with posterior enhancement (Fig
14) (13). A clear history of trauma can help make the diagno-
sis. Short-term follow-up US in 3–6 months can be performed
to ensure resolution.

Foreign  Body.—Penetrating  trauma  may  introduce  foreign
bodies into breast tissue, and patients may present with pain
on palpation over the entry site or poor wound healing. US
can  be  performed  to  evaluate  for  echogenic  foreign  bodies
such  as  vegetative  material  (Fig  15)  (32)  and  can  guide  re-
moval  of  the  foreign  body  versus  removal  in  the  operating
room, which is necessary to reduce the risk of infection and
further injury. If a foreign body is not seen at US, radiogra-
phy or mammography may be helpful if the foreign body is
radiopaque.

Pregnancy

Galactocele.—Galactoceles  are  smooth  epithelial-lined  milk
retention cysts caused by occluded lactiferous ducts and often
manifest as a unilateral or bilateral painless enlarging breast
mass  (33).  While  galactoceles  typically  arise  during  preg-
nancy or lactation, they may be a rare cause of a breast lump
in neonates of either sex owing to the influence of maternal
hormones and in adolescent boys in the absence of endocrine
dysfunction (13,14). US will show a complex cystic mass with
internal echogenicity that varies on the basis of the propor-
tion of fat (hyperechoic) and water (hypoechoic to anechoic)
in  the  fluid  (Fig  16)  (7,8,14,33).  A  classic  layering  fat-fluid
level is sometimes seen. Treatment is often conservative with
sonographic follow-up to ensure resolution. US-guided aspi-
ration of milky fluid can confirm the diagnosis and provide
therapeutic decompression. However, a milk fistula is a rare
complication (33).

Cystic Lesions

Cyst.—Cysts arise from fluid distention of lobular acini owing
to either duct obstruction or an imbalance of fluid secretion
to  absorption  (7,8). While  more  common  in  premenopausal
women, cysts can occur in children. Cysts can arise at any lo-
cation within the breast and may manifest as a palpable lump.

US  will  demonstrate  an  anechoic  mass  with  circumscribed
margins  and  posterior  enhancement,  although  cysts  can  be
complicated with internal debris or thin septa (Fig 18) (4,7).
No treatment is usually required. However, US-guided thera-
peutic aspiration may be performed for symptomatic or large
cysts per patient preference (8).

Fibroepithelial Lesions

Fibroadenoma.—Fibroadenoma is a benign fibroepithelial tu-
mor that constitutes the most common cause of a solid mass
in the pediatric population. The mean patient age at diagnosis
is 15–17 years, and fibroadenomas are multiple in 15%–20%
of patients (1). Because fibroadenomas arise from the stroma
surrounding the terminal duct lobular units, they are almost
exclusively found in female patients, as the male breast tradi-
tionally lacks lobules (14). Fibroadenomas are estrogen sen-
sitive  and  may  grow  rapidly  during  puberty  and  pregnancy
(7,8,14). Patients typically present with a painless, firm, rub-
bery mobile mass 2–3 cm in diameter (7–9,13,14).

At US, fibroadenomas typically appear as parallel oval hy-
poechoic  masses  with  circumscribed  margins  and  variable
posterior  enhancement  (Fig  20)  (7,8,13,14,37).  Rarely,  fibro-
adenomas  appear  heterogeneous  owing  to  necrosis  or  calci-
fication (1). On color Doppler US images, one-third of fibro-
adenomas will demonstrate internal vascularity (4). The MRI
appearance of fibroadenomas is variable, but classically they
appear as T2-hyperintense and enhancing masses with non-
enhancing internal septa (7).

Juvenile (cellular) fibroadenoma is a rare subtype of fibro-
adenoma that can manifest with rapid growth and large size.
Black girls are predominantly affected, and bilateral or multiple
masses are found in 10%–25% of patients (9,14). Masses greater
than 5–10 cm in diameter are termed giant fibroadenomas and
most often are the juvenile variant (Fig 21) (7,8,14,37). The so-
nographic appearance of a juvenile fibroadenoma is similar to
that of a typical fibroadenoma, except for the larger size.

classify on the basis of CNB results are rare, and surgical ex-

ferent clinical behavior and management of these lesions as
discussed previously (46,47).

25% of cases are bilateral (14). Papillomas are uncommon in
children and rare in male patients, and the typical presentation
is spontaneous clear or bloody nipple discharge (1,8,9,14,48).

creased vascularity at color Doppler imaging with associated

Malignant Pediatric Breast Masses

Primary Breast Cancer.—Primary breast cancer is exceedingly
rare in the pediatric breast, accounting for less than 1% of all
breast  masses  (1).  Because  this  population  does  not  undergo
routine screening, patients often present with a firm painless
palpable lump. Primary breast cancer in children and adoles-
cents may be associated with inherited genetic mutations such
as BRCA1 and BRCA2 (1,8,14). Breast cancer is also known to
occur secondary to treatment of childhood malignancies, such
as  in  those  patients  who  receive  high-dose  mantle  radiation
therapy for Hodgkin disease (8,14). The most common subtype
of breast cancer in children is invasive secretory (juvenile) car-
cinoma, which tends to be less aggressive and carry a more fa-
vorable prognosis compared with less common subtypes (1,4,8).

Conclusion
Breast masses are uncommon in the first 2 decades of life, and
the spectrum of mass-forming lesions in the pediatric breast
is  different  from  that  in  adults.  Most  breast  lesions  in  chil-
dren and adolescents are benign and represent normal breast
tissue or fibroadenomas. If imaging is necessary following a
thorough clinical examination, US is the modality of choice to
evaluate a pediatric breast mass, given its lack of ionizing ra-
diation and high sensitivity in dense tissue. Malignant breast
masses are rare in the pediatric breast and are more likely to
be  due  to  metastasis  than  a  primary  breast  cancer.  Conser-
vative management is preferred for the majority of pediatric
breast masses, given the low likelihood of malignancy and the
risk of biopsy causing damage to the developing breast.
