US  emerged  in  the  1950s  and  was  initially  used  in  breast
imaging  to  distinguish  cystic  from  solid  breast  lesions.  As
the  technology  evolved,  it  became  possible  to  accurately
characterize  the  risk  of  malignancy  of  solid  breast  masses
based  on  shape,  orientation,  margin,  echo  patterns,  poste-
rior  features,  and  vascularity.  A  standardized  lexicon  and
classification system developed by the American College of
Radiology known as the Breast Imaging Reporting and Data
System (BI-RADS) improved reporting and tracking of sono-
graphic findings. As US technology evolves, new techniques,
including harmonics and Doppler US, may further improve
evaluation of suspicious US findings.

US  is  an  interactive  and  operator-dependent  method.  It
allows  real-time  evaluation  of  breast  pain  or  areas  of  pal-
pable  concern  and  direct  interaction  with  the  patient.  US-
guided  percutaneous  procedures  are  safe  and  reliable  and
allow precise needle visualization during the procedure.

US  image  quality  depends  on  the  operator’s  technique.
Meticulous  attention  to  detail  is  necessary  to  perform
high-quality imaging. The area of interest should be located
at the apex of the chest wall to allow the breast tissue to fall
to  the  side,  thinning  the  breast  to  facilitate  imaging.  The
supine  position  is  recommended  for  evaluation  of  medial
breast findings, and the lateral decubitus position is recom-
mended to better evaluate the lateral quadrants and axilla,
especially in large breasts.

Evaluation  of  the  breast  must  include  all  layers  of  tissue,
including  the  skin,  subcutaneous  tissue,  fibroglandular  tis-
sue,  retromammary  fat,  and  pectoral  muscle  (Fig  1).  The
depth  must  be  adjusted  to  include  the  posterior  pectoralis
muscle and the focal zone in the anterior third between the
skin and the thoracic wall.

Indications for breast US include evaluation of breast pain,
masses, nipple discharge, and suspicious mammographic and
MRI findings. US can guide percutaneous procedures, includ-
ing biopsies, cyst aspiration, marker placement, preoperative
localizations, and abscess drainage. Additionally, US may be
used as supplementary screening in addition to mammogra-
phy for high-risk patients who may not have access to MRI.

The accompanying slide presentation depicts the correct
use of US techniques and features, such as high-frequency
transducers, harmonics, and color Doppler, to avoid poten-
tial pitfalls (Fig 2).

TEACHING POINTS
	 US is widely available, does not involve ionizing radiation, does not re-
quire intravenous access for contrast material, and is a supplementary
screening option to mammography for high-risk patients who may not
have access to MRI, especially in patients with dense breast tissue.

	 US is an interactive and operator-dependent method that allows real-time

evaluation of suspicious findings and interaction with the patient.

	 Correct  use  of  US  technique  and  features,  such  as  harmonics  and

Doppler US, may help avoid potential pitfalls.
