Various cystic breast lesions are encountered during screening and diagnostic breast imaging. According to the Breast Imaging
Reporting and Data System (BI-RADS) from the American College of Radiology, cystic breast lesions can be classified into the
following categories based on sonographic findings: simple cysts, complicated cysts, clustered microcysts, and complex cystic
and solid masses. With appropriate technique, simple cysts can be diagnosed easily by satisfying the diagnostic criteria, which
include anechoic round or oval lesions with circumscribed margins and posterior enhancement on US images. Simple cysts are
categorized as BI-RADS category 2, benign. Complicated cysts contain debris and satisfy all other sonographic criteria for simple
cysts, except they are not anechoic. Clustered microcysts are defined as lesions comprising a cluster of small anechoic masses
without a solid component. Based on recent investigations, complicated cysts are categorized as BI-RADS category 3, probably
benign, whereas clustered microcysts are categorized as BI-RADS category 2. Complex cystic and solid masses contain fluid and
solid components and include those with a thick wall, thick septations, an intracystic or mural mass, and both cystic and solid
components. They usually are considered BI-RADS category 4, suspicious, and are accompanied by a biopsy recommendation.
Radiologists must evaluate cystic lesions carefully, with meticulous technique, and provide appropriate assessment and man-
agement recommendations, thereby reducing unnecessary follow-up and biopsies while preventing cancers from being missed
or dismissed.

Introduction
Cystic  breast  lesions,  especially  simple  cysts,  are  often  ob-
served at US performed for screening or diagnostic purposes
and may be detected incidentally at breast MRI. These lesions
include  a  wide  spectrum  of  breast  diseases,  from  common
benign simple cysts and papillary lesions to breast carcino-

ma  (1–4).  Appropriate  categorization  by  evaluating  imaging
features  is  important  because  clinical  management  of  each
lesion  category  is  different.  Consequently,  there  are  studies
that have attempted to classify and distinguish between be-
nign and malignant cystic breast lesions by evaluating their
sonographic features (1,2,5).

TEACHING POINTS
	 Complicated  cysts  are  masses  that  otherwise  meet  the  sonographic
criteria for simple cysts, except they are not anechoic on US images. A
lesion  easily  can  be  diagnosed  as  a  complicated  cyst  with  debris  and
categorized as BI-RADS category 2 in cases of mobile internal echoes,
a fluid-debris level, or echogenic reflector scintillating during real-time
US image evaluation.

	 BI-RADS  category  2  is  most  appropriate  for  clustered  microcysts  that
have  a  typical  sonographic  appearance.  The  presence  of  indistinct  or
angular  margins,  discrete  solid  components,  associated  suspicious
calcifications at mammography, or a suspicious change at subsequent
imaging should prompt a BI-RADS category 4 assessment and a recom-
mendation for biopsy.

	 Although echogenicity alone has low specificity in evaluation of a breast
mass, complex cystic and solid masses usually are evaluated as BI-RADS
category 4 and accompanied by a recommendation for biopsy.

	 Simple  cysts  can  be  dismissed  as  benign  (BI-RADS  category  2)  with  a
recommendation  for  routine  screening.  If  the  patient  is  symptomatic
with  pain  or  a  very  large  cyst,  therapeutic  cyst  aspiration  can  be  per-
formed, a seemingly discordant clinical scenario.

	 Core-needle biopsy under US guidance is an effective method for patho-
logic diagnosis. It is important to obtain a sample of the solid compo-
nents  during  biopsy  because  the  cystic  component  could  actually  be
necrosis.

The  established  terminology  for  cystic  breast  lesions,
which  was  provided  in  the  Breast  Imaging  Reporting  and
Data System (BI-RADS) from the American College of Radiol-
ogy  in  2013,  classifies  these  lesions  into  the  following  four
categories:  (a)  simple  cysts,  (b)  complicated  cysts,  (c)  clus-
tered  microcysts,  and  (d)  complex  cystic  and  solid  masses
(6). Simple cysts are the most common type. However, with
the use of high-resolution US equipment, cysts with septa-
tions  or  internal  echoes  are  also  commonly  encountered.
Assessment  and  management  recommendations  for  cystic
breast  lesions,  especially  for  complicated  cysts  with  debris
and  clustered  microcysts,  may  be  confusing,  and  verifica-
tion  through  more  studies  has  been  needed.  Several  stud-
ies have recently reported the outcomes of nonsimple cysts
seen  at  US,  especially  complicated  cysts  and  clustered  mi-
crocysts (7–10).

This article defines the four categories of cystic breast le-
sions;  demonstrates  imaging  findings  on  mammographic,
sonographic, and MR images; and discusses assessment and
management recommendations from published literature.

Cystic Breast Lesions
Breast cysts are the most common nonproliferative disorder
of  the  breast,  with  a  peak  incidence  in  patients  between  35
and  50  years  of  age  (4).  Simple  cysts  are  hormone  sensitive
and therefore have a peak incidence in premenopausal wom-

en.  In  postmenopausal  women,  cysts  are  more  common  in
those receiving hormone replacement therapy (4,11).

Simple  cysts  are  epithelium-lined  fluid-filled  round  or
oval well-circumscribed masses. Several hypothetical mech-
anisms of cyst formation have been suggested. Cystic chang-
es may be caused by obstruction of the terminal duct lobular
unit, continuous secretion with impaired drainage, or alter-
ation of lobules, starting with dilation and then coalescence
of acini, with unfolding or involution of the lobular units. The
epithelium lining the cysts can be flattened or apocrine met-
aplastic  (12).  Biologically  active  compounds  secreted  by  the
apocrine  metaplastic  epithelium  increase  the  intraluminal
pressure and cause adjacent acini to unfold and fuse, forming
apocrine-lined  microcysts.  Smaller  cysts  gradually  enlarge
and fuse, forming larger cystic spaces (Fig 1) (12,13). Cysts with
the apocrine cell lining have a higher risk for recurrence than
those with flat epithelium (14).

It has been suggested that the natural history of cystic dis-
ease is closely related to the cyst type. Type I, a secretory cyst
with a metabolically active apocrine cell lining, may have in-
creased cell proliferation, with apocrine cells prone to atypia,
hyperplasia, or preneoplastic alterations. Type II, a transuda-
tive cyst lined by flattened epithelium without hypertrophic
metabolism, represents a mere retention cyst that is resolved
after aspiration (12).

Evaluating Cystic Breast Lesions with US
US should be performed using a high-frequency transducer
(ie, a broad-bandwidth linear-array transducer, with a center
frequency of at least 10 MHz and a peak frequency of at least
12 MHz), with the appropriate gain and gray scale and focal
zone at the level of the mass adjusted for each patient (4). Gain
settings should be adjusted so that fat at all levels is displayed
as a midlevel gray (15). One criterion for diagnosing a simple
cyst  is  whether  it  is  anechoic  (Fig  2).  Accurate  gain  adjust-
ment,  with  optimal  visualization  of  echogenicity,  is  import-
ant. Setting the gain too high can result in excessive echoge-
nicity, which may obscure some lesions and make some cysts
appear solid. Conversely, setting the gain too low may obscure
details in the image, with hypoechoic solid lesions appearing
anechoic.

Interpretation  of  B-mode  images  may  be  challenging  be-
cause  of  artifactual  echoes  and  difficulty  in  the  evaluation
of  the  lateral  borders  of  breast  lesions.  Spatial  compound-
ing and harmonic imaging may facilitate evaluation of cystic
breast  lesions.  Spatial  compounding  imaging  is  a  technical
approach in which the sonographic image is derived from the
input  of  multiple  off-angle  beams  (Fig  S1).  It  results  in  de-
creased artifactual echoes and enhanced true echoes. Spatial
compounding  imaging  also  can  aid  in  the  evaluation  of  the
lateral  margins  of  the  lesion.  The  improved  signal-to-noise
ratio assists in the recognition of small cysts. However, poste-
rior enhancement is less apparent with spatial compounding,
and the benefits decrease with increasing lesion depth (1,16).
Harmonic imaging, which uses higher harmonic frequen-
cies that originate within tissues during beam propagation to
produce gray-scale images, can be applied to better character-
ize cystic breast lesions (Fig S2). Lower-frequency superficial

Application  of  color  or  power  Doppler  imaging  may  in-
crease diagnostic certainty in the evaluation of cystic breast
lesions.  Although  absence  of  vascularity  cannot  be  used  to
determine whether an anechoic or hypoechoic mass is a cyst,
it may support the diagnosis of a cyst if the mass is anecho-

versely, vascularity within an anechoic mass suggests that the
mass is solid and raises suspicion for malignancy. High-grade
invasive cancer and metastatic lymph nodes occasionally can
appear anechoic, and demonstration of flow within the mass
then requires biopsy (Fig 4) (15).

US elastography can improve specificity in the evaluation
of  cystic  breast  masses  by  measuring  tissue  stiffness  with
strain  or  shear  wave  (Fig  4).  A  “bull’s  eye”  artifact  has  been
described  as  a  characteristic  feature  of  benign  breast  cysts
with  some  strain  elastography  software.  This  can  appear  as

a  round  or  oval  lesion  with  a  stiff  rim  associated  with  two
soft  spots—one  located  centrally  and  the  other  posteriorly
(19). The bull’s eye artifact can reduce the need for biopsies
of  benign  cystic  lesions  (19),  but  it  is  not  observed  with  the
shear-wave  elastography  technique  (20).  Elastography  can
help  differentiate  circumscribed  high-grade  invasive  ductal
carcinomas, which typically show a rim of surrounding stiff-
ness, from complicated cysts, which generally are much softer
than malignant lesions (20).

Simple Cysts
The  criteria  for  diagnosing  simple  cysts  on  US  images  are  a
circumscribed  margin,  an  anechoic  round  or  oval  shape,  a
thin imperceptible wall, and posterior enhancement (Fig 2).
The  presence  of  these  features  establishes  the  diagnosis  of
a simple cyst that can be dismissed as a benign finding (BI-
RADS category 2). Color or power Doppler imaging applied to
the cyst demonstrates no internal vascularity, and flow may
be  seen  in  the  tissue  surrounding  the  cyst.  Thin  septations
may  be  present  in  lobulated  simple  cysts  or  when  several
cysts abut each other. To be defined as a simple cyst, the sep-
tations must be less than 0.5 mm in thickness (Fig 5) (6).

Simple  cysts  are  common  incidental  findings  at  breast

However,  it  is  usually  thin,  uniform,  and  hyperintense  on
T2-weighted images, which confirms the cystic nature (Fig 8).

Complicated Cysts
Complicated  cysts  are  cysts  that  contain  debris,  including
proteins  from  cell  turnover,  milk,  fat,  pus,  and  blood  (4).  A
galactocele is a cystically dilated terminal duct and terminal
ductule filled with milk. It most commonly occurs during lac-
tation  and  occasionally  in  the  third  trimester  of  pregnancy.
The  radiologic  appearance  of  a  galactocele  depends  on  the
amount of fat and protein within the milk (25).

Complicated  cysts  are  masses  that  otherwise  meet  the
sonographic criteria for simple cysts, except they are not an-
echoic  on  US  images.  A  lesion  can  easily  be  diagnosed  as  a
complicated cyst with debris and categorized as BI-RADS cat-
egory 2 in cases of mobile internal echoes, a fluid-debris level,
or echogenic reflector scintillating during real-time US image
evaluation (Figs 9, 10). Otherwise, a complicated cyst should
show  internal  homogeneous  low-level  echogenicity  without
mural  nodules,  thick  septations,  thick  walls,  or  any  other
suggestion of a solid component (Fig 10) (6). The presence of
echoes  that  are  caused  by  debris  or  hemorrhage  associated
with  vascularity  within  a  complicated  cyst,  any  thickening
or irregularity of the wall, or any change in size at follow-up
may require further investigation to exclude malignancy (1,7).

Clustered Microcysts
Clustered  microcysts,  part  of  the  spectrum  of  benign  cystic
changes, are cystic dilatations of individual acini within the
terminal duct lobular unit. Tissue diagnoses associated with
clustered  microcysts  include  fibrocystic  changes,  apocrine
metaplasia, and benign cysts (6,10,34).

Clustered  microcysts  are  defined  by  BI-RADS  as  lesions
comprising  a  cluster  of  anechoic  masses,  each  smaller  than
2–3 mm, with thin (<0.5 mm) septations and no discrete solid
component at breast US (Fig 12) (6). However, some studies in-
cluded lesions with individual microcysts up to 5–7 mm in size
and  suggested  broadening  the  definition  to  include  lesions
with larger cysts (4,9,34). The margin should be circumscribed

The  assessment  and  management  recommendations  for
clustered microcysts may be confusing, even for experienced
radiologists.  In  the  multicenter  ACRIN  6666  protocol,  which
began in 2004, clustered microcysts were included in the pro-
spective criteria for BI-RADS category 3 (35). According to BI-
RADS, clustered microcysts can be assessed as category 2 when
clearly composed of simple cysts (6). However, imaging surveil-
lance and a BI-RADS category 3 assessment may be appropriate
for  smaller  or  deeply  located  clustered  microcysts.  Based  on

prior studies with one malignancy reported among 216 (0.5%)
cases, these lesions have reduced diagnostic certainty. BI-RADS
also states, “The relatively small number of cases studied lim-
its precision in estimating the likelihood of malignancy to be
≤2%; the data would be more convincing if at least 500 cases
were studied” (6). Since then, two additional studies on the out-
comes of clustered microcysts have been published, adding to
the number of cases. Goldbach et al (10) reported the outcomes
of 196 clustered microcysts, which showed no malignancies af-
ter at least 24 months of imaging follow-up or tissue sampling.
Greenwood et al (9) also found no malignancy in 95 clustered
microcysts.  Tanaka  et  al  (8)  reported  outcomes  of  52  lesions
categorized as clustered microcysts, with 8 malignancies out of
52 (8). However, strict criteria of clustered microcysts probably
were not applied because intervening or associated solid com-
ponents were seen in lesions illustrated in the study.

Complex Cystic and Solid Masses
According to the BI-RADS criteria, the echo pattern of “complex
cystic  and  solid”  indicates  a  complex  mass  containing  both
anechoic (cystic or fluid) and echogenic (solid) components at
breast US (6). Lesions found in complex cystic and solid masses
may have one or all of the following characteristics: a thick wall

(≥0.5 mm) (Fig 15), thick internal septations (≥0.5 mm) (Fig 16),
an intracystic or mural mass (Fig 17), and both solid and cystic
components (Fig 18) (4). Although echogenicity alone has low
specificity  in  evaluation  of  a  breast  mass,  complex  cystic  and
solid masses usually are evaluated as BI-RADS category 4 and
accompanied by a recommendation for biopsy.

The reported malignancy rate of complex cystic and solid
masses is 22%–43% (1,4,5). These lesions can be associated
with various benign and malignant breast diseases. Benign
causes  of  thick-walled  cystic  lesions,  with  or  without  thick
internal  septations,  are  ruptured  or  inflamed  cysts  and  fi-
brocystic changes, especially with apocrine metaplasia, ab-
scesses,  fat  necrosis,  seromas,  and  hematomas  (1,4,5).  For
probably benign causes of complex cystic and solid masses,
follow-up US to resolution is recommended to ensure accu-
rate  assessment  and  appropriate  management  while  care-
fully  ruling  out  underlying  malignancy.  The  sonographic
appearances  of  fibrocystic  changes  vary  with  the  relative
degrees  of  cystic  dilatation  and  sclerosis.  When  the  cystic
change  predominates,  sonographic  diagnosis  is  easy.  How-
ever,  fibrocystic  changes  can  manifest  as  a  complex  cystic
and solid appearance when sclerosis and papillary apocrine
metaplasia predominate.

Mastitis causes diffuse changes in the sonographic appear-
ance of breast tissue, such as disrupted normal architecture,
decreased parenchymal echogenicity caused by edema, dilat-
ed and thickened ducts, and skin thickening. With an abscess,
a poorly defined anechoic or hypoechoic fluid-filled space or a
complex cystic lesion can be seen (Fig 15) (37). US findings as-
sociated with clinical symptoms (ie, local heat, swelling, and
redness of the skin overlying the breast) are highly suggestive
of an abscess. A chronic abscess may contain sufficient gran-
ulation tissue and fibrosis, develop into a complex cystic and
solid mass, or have a solid appearance at US.

Fat necrosis and hematoma can manifest as a thick-walled
cystic lesion or a complex cystic and solid mass. A hematoma or
fat necrosis may be suspected in appropriate clinical settings,
such  as  in  cases  of  recent  trauma,  needle  biopsy,  or  surgery.
Correlation with mammography is mandatory, and follow-up
US  can  be  performed  instead  of  needle  biopsy  if  the  mass
shows characteristically benign mammographic findings (Fig
19). After surgery, most seromas appear as simple or complicat-
ed cysts with diffuse low-level internal echoes. A complex cystic
and solid mass at the lumpectomy site can be diagnosed defini-
tively based on US findings because the postoperative fluid col-
lection corresponds directly with the lumpectomy site (Fig S4).

Assessment and Management
Figure 20 summarizes the diagnostic approach and BI-RADS
category assessment of cystic breast lesions at US.

Simple  cysts  can  be  dismissed  as  benign  (BI-RADS  cat-
egory  2)  with  a  recommendation  for  routine  screening.  If
the  patient  is  symptomatic  with  pain  or  a  very  large  cyst,
therapeutic  cyst  aspiration  can  be  performed,  a  seemingly
discordant clinical scenario (6). Aspiration also can also be
performed for symptomatic (ie, tender or palpable) compli-
cated cysts or for diagnostic uncertainty of some complicat-
ed cysts.

Complex  cystic  and  solid  masses  are  usually  considered
suspicious  findings  and  require  biopsy  for  definitive  diag-
nosis. Short-term follow-up US of a thick-walled cystic lesion
can be performed in patients with a history of trauma or signs
of  infection.  However,  if  there  is  any  enlargement  of  such  a
lesion, a biopsy should be recommended because an under-
lying malignancy might bleed from trauma or cause duct ob-
struction and infection (1).

Fine-Needle Aspiration
US-guided  fine-needle  aspiration  is  an  easy  method  to  as-
sess the nature of cyst contents. The color of breast cyst flu-
id ranges from white and yellow through brown and gray to
blue and black (1). The consistency also can vary from thin
and  watery  to  thick  and  viscid  (12).  When  the  cystic  fluid
is  typically  characteristic  of  a  benign  cyst,  such  as  cloudy
yellow or greenish black in color, it can be safely discarded
(Fig  S6).  Routine  cytologic  examination  is  deemed  unnec-
essary  if  the  fluid  obtained  through  radiologically  guided
aspiration is not bloody and if there is complete resolution
of  the  cyst  following  aspiration.  Sending  typical  cyst  fluid

for  cytologic  examination  may  yield  a  false-positive  result,
which potentially leads to unnecessary additional treatment
(4,43). According to a report by Daly et al (31) on complicat-
ed cysts, only one of 184 lesions with nonbloody cystic fluid
showed atypia at cytologic examination and was confirmed
to  be  fibrocystic  disease  after  surgery.  If  bloody,  the  fluid
should  be  sent  for  cytologic  examination.  A  clip  should  be
placed internally at the aspiration site if there is no residual
mass  after  aspiration  (3).  This  ensures  precise  localization
for future diagnostic or surgical procedures. If the aspirate
appears purulent, it should be sent for microbiologic analy-
sis, including culture and staining (1).

US-guided Core-Needle Biopsy
Core-needle biopsy under US guidance is an effective method
for pathologic diagnosis. It is important to obtain a sample of
the solid components during biopsy because the cystic com-
ponent  could  actually  be  necrosis  (Fig  21)  (3).  After  biopsy,
pathologic  findings  should  be  correlated  with  imaging  fea-
tures to determine concordance. Discordant pathologic find-
ings  should  be  discussed  with  the  pathologist,  and  a  repeat
core-needle  biopsy  or  surgical  biopsy  should  be  considered
(44). Although there can be considerable overlap in US find-
ings between benign and malignant complex cystic and solid
masses, the appearance of malignant lesions usually reflects
their  malignant  nature  and  may  involve  angular,  indistinct,
or microlobulated margins or irregular thick walls. The prev-
alence  of  malignancy  is  higher  in  masses  containing  mixed
cystic  and  solid  components  (ie,  a  cystic  portion  occupying
at least 50% of the mass or a predominantly solid mass) than
among masses with a thick wall, thick septations, or cystic le-
sion with discrete solid mural lesions within a cyst (45,46).

Conclusion
Cystic  breast  masses  are  not  uncommon  findings  at  mam-
mography, US, and breast MRI. For accurate classification and
assessment of cystic breast lesions, it is necessary to obtain US
images using a meticulous technique. Simple cysts can be dis-

missed as benign. Complicated cysts with debris and clustered
microcysts that meet strict diagnostic criteria can be considered
benign, BI-RADS category 2. A BI-RADS category 3 assessment is
reasonable in cases of diagnostic uncertainty, even after a thor-
ough US examination. When a cystic lesion shows a thick wall
(≥0.5 mm), thick internal septations (≥0.5 mm), an intracystic
mass, or both cystic and solid components, it should be diag-
nosed as a complex cystic and solid mass. Although follow-up
US could be performed if there is a history of trauma or surgery
with characteristically benign mammographic findings, com-
plex cystic and solid masses are usually assessed as suspicious,
BI-RADS category 4, and require a biopsy. A comprehensive un-
derstanding of the imaging findings and management of cystic
breast lesions is essential to reduce unnecessary imaging fol-
low-up or biopsies so that cancers are not missed.
