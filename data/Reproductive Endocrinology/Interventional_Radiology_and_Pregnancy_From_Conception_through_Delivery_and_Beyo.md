## Abstract

Interventional radiology (IR) plays a unique and often invaluable role in the care of pregnant patients. Special considerations regarding radiation exposure and medication choice must be taken into account when evaluating a pregnant patient for an interventional procedure. In addition, the physiologic changes that occur during pregnancy can pose special challenges for an interventionalist when treating this patient population and should be appropriately recognized. Still, the majority of standard IR procedures can be safely performed in the pregnant population, often with additional precautions and proper patient education about the risks, benefits, and alternatives. In many cases, interventional radiologists can provide lifesaving and fertility-preserving alternatives to more invasive surgical intervention. The authors summarize radiation exposure effects and guidelines and medication choice during pregnancy. The physiologic changes that occur during pregnancy are discussed, with specific interest in the pathologic consequences that can be treated with IR. The authors also describe a wide variety of minimally invasive image-guided procedures offered by IR in pregnant, peripartum, or postpartum patients. The areas where IR can help in the treatment of pregnant patients include venous access, biopsies, genitourinary and biliary interventions, venous thromboembolism treatments, ectopic pregnancy management, aneurysm intervention, and management of trauma patients. IR is also involved in management during the peripartum or postpartum periods, with roles in treatment of invasive placenta spectrum, postpartum hemorrhage after vaginal delivery, and postcesarean delivery complications.

## Introduction

Interventional radiology (IR) plays an important role in managing conditions and complications in pregnant and postpartum patients. Although special considerations must be taken concerning radiation exposure, appropriate medications, and timing of interventions, many IR procedures can be performed safely throughout pregnancy.
IR includes a broad scope of interventions that affect multiple organ systems and can benefit a pregnant patient from conception, throughout pregnancy, and well into the postpartum period. Before performing a procedure in a pregnant patient, it is important for the proceduralist to understand radiation effects on fetal development, know the best practices and techniques to minimize radiation exposure, and be aware of which medications can be safely administered during pregnancy. Conditions unrelated to pregnancy may arise, such as masses that require biopsy or trauma that necessitates immediate intervention. Some standard IR procedures are more likely to take place during pregnancy due to the associated physiologic changes, such as nephrostomy tube placement secondary to hydronephrosis from ureteral compression or thromboembolism management as a result of the hypercoagulable pregnant state. Certain interventions are more specific to the pregnant population, including management of ectopic pregnancies and aneurysms, and can be lifesaving. Furthermore, a wide variety of IR procedures are essential in the postpartum period, such as embolization for uncontrolled bleeding or drainage of postoperative abscesses. The purpose of this article is to educate any physician who performs IR procedures to be able to safely and comprehensively treat a pregnant, postpartum, or peripartum patient should the situation arise.
## The Pregnant Patient and IR: General Concerns

### Radiation Effects on Fetal Development

Because of radiation exposure concerns, the management of a pregnant patient who presents to an IR department can be complex. It is important to balance the needs of the patient and fetus, assessing the risks and benefits of performing a procedure that could expose a fetus to potentially harmful radiation. Any elective procedures that provide minimal benefit should be postponed until at least 6–8 weeks after delivery, which eliminates radiation exposure to the fetus and allows the return of physiologic changes in the patient to a near prepregnancy state (1). In emergent cases, such as hemorrhagic shock, trauma, or sepsis, the primary focus should be on the health and well-being of the pregnant patient. Following this assessment, further attention can then be placed on the fetus, especially when potentially viable. Teaching Point For semi-urgent but not emergent cases, treatment in the second or third trimester is best, if possible, to reduce the risk of radiation to the developing fetus (1).
The potential effects of radiation on an unborn fetus include prenatal death, intellectual disability, intrauterine growth restriction, small head size, organ malformation, and childhood cancer, among others (2). The risk depends on a variety of factors, including the gestational age at which exposure to radiation occurs, the absorbed radiation dose, and the cellular repair mechanisms of the fetus. Two types of radiation effects exist: deterministic and stochastic. Deterministic effects are not seen below a certain radiation threshold. Above that threshold, the severity of the negative impact is dose dependent. Some examples of deterministic effects include spontaneous abortion and teratogenicity. On the other hand, stochastic effects such as carcinogenesis originate from damage to individual cells, and there is no safe level of radiation exposure below which this effect is eliminated. There remain uncertainties in the data across studies regarding the childhood cancer risk related to in utero radiation exposure. Based on available information, a conservative approach estimates the risk of childhood cancer to be approximately 5%–15% per gray, and the cancer most closely associated with intrauterine exposure to ionizing radiation is childhood leukemia (3).
Practice guidelines help better inform physicians about policies for imaging pregnant or potentially pregnant patients. According to the National Council of Radiation Protection and Measurements, radiation exposure to a fetus less than 50 mGy is considered negligible, while levels exceeding 150 mGy increase risk of radiation-induced abnormalities substantially (4,5). Many routine radiologic examinations such as plain film radiography of the pelvis deliver less than 20 mGy and thus pose a low potential risk. Even single-phase CT of the abdomen or pelvis is likely to deliver less than 35 mGy. Fluoroscopic procedures can exceed the threshold for induced malformation (>100 mGy), requiring increased caution (6).
As a result of the potential deleterious effects of radiation exposure to the fetus, an effort is made to reduce exposure time and dosage. Optimally, any use of ionizing radiation should be avoided, so US and MRI procedures should be used whenever possible (7). In many situations, fluoroscopy is necessary for at least part of a procedure. In these cases, there are methods to reduce fetal radiation exposure. These methods include minimizing fluoroscopy time, avoiding magnification, reducing the rate of pulsed fluoroscopy, using the last image hold, minimizing the distance between the patient and receptor, maximizing the distance between the x-ray source and receptor, using collimation, reducing the tube current, and minimizing the use of digital subtraction angiography (1,4,8). In addition, an experienced interventional radiologist should perform the procedure to limit radiation exposure and sedation time. Furthermore, it is imperative to properly monitor and record radiation exposure in the patient chart using estimates including reference point air kerma, kerma-area product, peak skin dose, and fluoroscopy time (4).
Although the use of lead and other forms of shielding has historically been thought to drastically decrease radiation exposure to a fetus, such shielding may be less effective than originally believed. In reality, for many procedures outside of the pelvis or abdomen, the radiation dose that arrives to the fetus is due in large part to internal scatter from the patient (9–11). Still, providing lead shielding to the patient can act as a measure of reassurance and decrease levels of anxiety and concern.
### Medications

Pregnant patients commonly require contrast agents for radiologic studies. In addition, medications can be administered for sedation or comfort. The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) requires that the label of each medication includes a summary and detailed explanation of the risk of use during pregnancy and lactation and pertinent information to help health care providers make informed prescribing decisions and counsel women about the risks and benefits of the drug (12,13).
No evidence exists to suggest that iodinated contrast agents are teratogenic, but iodine-based compounds do have a potential to impact the neonatal thyroid gland that develops at about 3 weeks after conception. The most likely risk is the development of hypothyroidism. As a result, the use of iodinated contrast agents should be reserved for procedures that are essential and after the first trimester, if possible (14,15). Gadolinium has been linked to teratogenicity in animal studies, likely related to its ability to traverse the placenta. Gadolinium-based contrast agents should be considered with extreme caution, and conversations with the ordering physician and patient about risks and benefits must take place (7,16).
Other common medication classes used in IR include sedatives, analgesics, and antiemetics. Propofol is the most widely used general anesthetic for both obstetric and nonobstetric procedures in pregnant patients. Local anesthetics (eg, lidocaine), as used clinically, are not teratogenic. Although rare, the risks of local anesthesia use include allergic reactions and anesthetic toxicity, the latter of which can manifest with neurologic (eg, seizures) or cardiac (eg, myocardial depression, arrhythmia) symptoms. Lipid emulsion therapy should be considered at the first sign of a serious anesthetic toxicity event (17).
Nonopioid analgesic agents should be the first consideration for pain control in most pregnant patients. Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs are not recommended during pregnancy due to risk of premature closure of the ductus arteriosus. Alternatively, acetaminophen can be safely used. Opioid analgesic medication can be given to pregnant patients for adequate pain control when necessary. Morphine, fentanyl, and meperidine are most commonly used and do not appear to be teratogenic when given in small doses. Caution should be taken due to the risk of respiratory depression in the short term and neonatal withdrawal syndrome after chronic long-term exposure during pregnancy.
Benzodiazepines should be avoided if possible, especially during the first trimester of pregnancy. Although concurrent exposure to other toxic drugs and substances may be confounding factors, benzodiazepines have been associated with increased risk of miscarriage, congenital defects, and neurobehavioral effects (1,18). Antiemetic medications are commonly administered during pregnancy. Metoclopramide (Reglan; Pharmaceutical Associates) and ondansetron (Zofran; GlaxoSmithKline) are used for hyperemesis gravidarum and normal pregnancy-related nausea and vomiting, respectively, and do not appear to be teratogenic based on available studies. Prochlorperazine (Compazine; Jubilant Cadista) has isolated reports of causing congenital defects and is likely best to avoid in pregnancy (1,18,19). Specific information regarding the use of medications in breastfeeding patients can be found on the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists website (https://www.acog.org*).
### Mass Effect

There are a myriad of physiologic changes that occur during pregnancy from both hormonal and mechanical factors. Mass effect from the growing fetus and gravid uterus can have ramifications on the renal, cardiovascular, pulmonary, and gastrointestinal systems. It is estimated that up to 90% of female patients have some degree of asymmetric dilatation of the renal calyces (more often on the right) while pregnant, with increased incidence of dilatation and clinically diagnosed hydronephrosis in the second and third trimesters (Fig 1). Although progesterone is thought to play a role in dilatation of the ureters, the strongest evidence for hydronephrosis is the mechanical compression of the ureters themselves by the gravid uterus (20). Significant hydronephrosis may require treatment by an interventional radiologist, with multiple repeat visits for tubing exchanges. Further information about hydronephrosis treatment can be found in the genitourinary section later in this article.

The gravid uterus can also compress the inferior vena cava (IVC) and cause a reduction in venous return to the heart, reducing stroke volume and cardiac output that can further reduce uterine blood flow and placental perfusion (21). Therefore, pregnant patients who require supine positioning for procedures should have the pelvis rotated slightly to drop the uterus to the side of the IVC, if possible (21). Mass effect from the uterus can also lead to decreased diaphragmatic excursion and cause a reduction in lower esophageal sphincter tone, leading to increased respiratory requirements and a heightened risk of aspiration during procedures, so increased attention is warranted to mitigate these risks (22).
## Procedures in the Pregnant Patient

### Venous Access

Pregnant patients may require short- or long-term venous access via peripherally inserted central catheters, tunneled or nontunneled central venous catheters, or implanted ports for a variety of reasons. Common indications for peripherally inserted central catheters include intravenous hydration for hyperemesis gravidarum and antibiotic use for bacterial infections. Central venous catheters may be required for dialysis therapy when appropriate, although an arteriovenous fistula is the best form of access in a pregnant patient (23). While rare, patients with a history of recurrent miscarriages may have anti-PP1Pk antibodies that target antigens of the placenta that appear as early as the 3rd week of gestation (24). Treatment with plasma exchange or double-filtration plasmapheresis with central venous catheters has been shown to lower antibody titers and prevent cytotoxicity leading to miscarriage, allowing successful childbirth (24).
Cancer does occur in approximately one in 1000 pregnancies, with the most common cancers including breast cancer, cervical cancer, lymphoma, ovarian cancer, leukemia, colorectal cancer, and melanoma (25). Many chemotherapeutic agents can be used during pregnancy and are delivered via implanted ports, but it is advised to wait until the second and third trimesters to allow appropriate fetal organogenesis. Venous access can be safely performed with US, and confirmation of catheter placement can be performed with fluoroscopy.
### Biopsy

Biopsies during pregnancy may be required for many reasons, including masses, liver disease, renal failure, or suspected hematologic malignancy. Breast cancer is the most common cancer during pregnancy, and sampling can be done with US-guided core biopsy. Core biopsy or bone marrow biopsy can be safely performed for suspected hematologic malignancy during pregnancy (26). Lung biopsy can also be safely achieved during pregnancy through bronchoscopy with biopsy or with a CT-guided percutaneous technique, if necessary. Although liver disease is uncommon during pregnancy, abnormal clinical and laboratory findings may justify biopsy. Apart from a moderately increased risk of small for gestational age at birth, no other association was seen between liver biopsy findings and adverse pregnancy-related outcomes (eg, stillbirth) (27). The literature surrounding renal biopsies during pregnancy is heterogeneous but suggests that the procedure should only be performed when high suspicion for disease exists and the results will guide urgently needed therapy. The highest rates of bleeding from renal biopsy occur between 23 and 26 weeks gestation (28).
### Genitourinary Interventions

Urinary tract dilatation is common in pregnant patients, more often affecting the right kidney and most apparent in the third trimester (Fig 1). Dilatation can result from any combination of compression from a gravid uterus, stone disease (Fig 2A), ureteral compression from an iliac artery, dilated vein, malignancy, or hormonal effects from progesterone. In some patients, this physiologic dilatation can progress to symptomatic hydronephrosis, which can cause renal failure or infection. Interventional radiologists may be consulted for percutaneous nephrostomy tube placement (Fig 2B) if conservative management with antibiotics or alternative management with cystoscopic retrograde stent placement fails (Fig S1) or is contraindicated (1,29). Multiple studies have shown ureteral stent placement to be as effective as percutaneous nephrostomy for the treatment of hydronephrosis during pregnancy, without the added risks of bleeding and inconvenience of an external drainage bag (30,31). Still, concerns about radiation exposure directly to the pelvis during stent placement and exchange are to be taken into account when making the collective decision of whether to place a stent or a percutaneous nephrostomy tube.

Teaching Point Nephrostomy tubes placed during pregnancy tend to accumulate encrustations more rapidly than those placed in nonpregnant patients, and routine exchanges should be scheduled every 3–4 weeks to avoid difficulty with passing wires during exchange, which would cause an increase in unwanted fluoroscopy time and potential for inability to exchange, necessitating repeat access (29). Percutaneous nephrostomy tube placement can also be beneficial as that access site can be used for definitive stone management following delivery (Fig 2C, 2D).
### Biliary Interventions

Acute cholecystitis is seen in pregnancy secondary to an increase in gallstones and biliary sludge, secondary to a hormonal rise in estrogen and progesterone (21). Management includes conservative medical treatment, open or laparoscopic surgery, and percutaneous cholecystostomy. In most cases, percutaneous cholecystostomy is done solely with US guidance, with the occasional need for minimal fluoroscopy time to confirm placement. Either a transhepatic or transperitoneal approach can be used (32,33). Cholecystectomy is recommended after delivery for definitive treatment.
Other forms of biliary disease, such as pancreatitis (Fig S2), symptomatic choledocholithiasis, or cholangitis can impact pregnant patients and require urgent intervention. The American College of Gastroenterology recommends endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography to be performed in these patients (34). In instances where endoscopic drainage is unsuccessful or contraindicated, percutaneous drainage can be performed.
### Venous Thromboembolism Interventions

**Venous Interventions.—**Pregnancy is a hypercoagulable state, and deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is often seen in this patient population. Pregnant women are up to five times more likely to develop DVT (35). The American Heart Association recommends treatment with low-molecular-weight heparin in stable pregnant patients and intravenous unfractionated heparin in patients with acute pulmonary embolism (PE) or DVT with large clot burden (35). IVC filters can be placed in patients with a contraindication to anticoagulation medication, progression of thrombosis while on anticoagulation medication, or high PE clot burden (36). The FDA recommends removal of retrievable filters as soon as possible once they are no longer needed following childbirth in this population (37). Teaching Point IVC filters are typically placed below the level of the lowest renal vein but are preferably placed within the suprarenal IVC in pregnant patients (Fig 3) as the gravid uterus often compresses the IVC below the renal veins, which increases the risk of filter fracture, migration, or even IVC rupture (36). Suprarenal positioning of a filter can also protect from thromboembolism of an ovarian vein thrombosis (38).

Management of iliofemoral DVT in a pregnant patient may be performed safely. Several techniques have been described, including percutaneous US-guided aspiration thrombectomy and intravascular US-guided catheter-directed mechanical thrombectomy, both of which have shown success without the use of fluoroscopy or tissue plasminogen activator (39,40). Some patients who become pregnant have preexisting iliac vein stents from prior venous intervention that tolerate the gravid uterus well. One retrospective study demonstrated 100% peripartum stent patency without a need for intervention (41).
**Pulmonary Artery Interventions.—**PE is a major cause of mortality in pregnant patients (42). In the clinical setting of submassive or massive PE (Fig 4), systemic thrombolysis can be given, although there is an increased risk of bleeding at the time of delivery and potential for spontaneous hemorrhage within the placenta. Patients who become hemodynamically unstable may require emergent cardiothoracic surgical intervention for open thromboembolectomy. Alternatively, there are endovascular minimally invasive interventions that can be performed to decrease pulmonary arterial clot burden, including pulmonary arterial suction thrombectomy or intra-arterial catheter-directed thrombolysis. These interventions can be performed with initial access in the common femoral vein or internal jugular vein and by advancing a catheter to the pulmonary artery for treatment. Bleeding complications secondary to thrombolysis are much more significant in the postnatal period, likely due to recent delivery or cesarean delivery, and typically manifest as vaginal or intra-abdominal hemorrhage (43). The risk of prenatal bleeding is approximately the same as in nonpregnant patients, around 2%–3% (44).

When considering endovascular thrombectomy or catheter-directed thrombolysis, the radiation dose that will be delivered to the fetus must be considered. Such interventions are advised against within the first trimester, as fetal radiation dose can range from 175 to 245 mGy during percutaneous catheter-directed thrombolysis, which is associated with a 1.3%–2% risk of childhood cancer (44). In emergent cases, the risks and benefits of performing endovascular therapy should be discussed with a multidisciplinary team, including a qualified medical physicist who can help estimate fetal radiation dose.
### Ectopic Pregnancy

Ectopic pregnancy (EP) is a potentially life-threatening condition that occurs when a fertilized egg implants outside of the main inner lining of the uterus, most commonly within the fallopian tubes. The standard for management of tubal EP is treatment with intramuscular methotrexate to disrupt rapidly dividing trophoblastic cells (45). Patients with EP often present with vaginal bleeding and abdominal pain. If symptoms include hypotension or hemodynamic instability, there should be high clinical concern for a ruptured EP. The surgical management of EP is laparoscopic salpingostomy without surgical excision of the fallopian tubes. Although rare, EPs can be occur in locations other than the fallopian tube, including within a cesarean scar (Figs 5A, S3), in the cervix, or within the abdomen, which are more challenging to treat and can benefit from IR intervention.

There are several alternative minimally invasive and fertility-preserving treatments for nontubal EPs described in the literature. However, there is no consensus on the preferred method. An EP gestational sac can be injected percutaneously or transvaginally with 1 mg/kg of methotrexate and/or 1–2 mEq of potassium chloride (46). Potassium chloride may be used to stop fetal cardiac activity (46). Additionally, methotrexate can be injected by using systemic or intra-arterial approaches (46). Close collaboration with the obstetric department is advised to plan the most appropriate intervention.
### Aspiration and Drainage

Pregnant patients may require aspiration of fluid collections, with possible drainage catheter placement depending on the cause. Galactoceles are milk-filled cysts that are formed in the breast by obstruction of the lactiferous duct. These cysts tend to manifest as palpable masses during the third trimester of pregnancy or after delivery during lactation (47). Galactoceles (Fig S4) are diagnosed at US with findings of complex breast cysts without significant vascularity.
US-guided aspiration may be performed for symptomatic relief or in cases of superinfection. Additionally, pregnant patients may have large ovarian cysts (Fig 6), which can cause pain. These cysts may become a fulcrum for potential ovarian torsion and can be treated with aspiration (48). Pregnant patients who develop abdominopelvic abscesses from diverticulitis, appendicitis, or postoperative complications can benefit from image-guided abscess drainage with US or CT guidance. MRI-guided pelvic abscess drainage has been documented in the pregnant population and can be considered to reduce radiation exposure to the fetus (49).

### Abdominal and Cerebral Arterial Disease Management

Aneurysm formation and growth in pregnancy is a documented phenomenon with a risk of rupture, which is potentially life-threatening for both the patient and fetus. Teaching Point During pregnancy, there is an increase in hormones (estrogen, progesterone, relaxin) that have a significant microscopic impact on arterial walls, increasing the risk of medial degeneration and aneurysmal dilatation (50). In addition, the physiologic changes associated with pregnancy, including increased cardiac output and blood volume, put stress on the arterial wall and increase the risk of aneurysmal rupture (50).
Abdominal Aneurysm Intervention.—**Splenic (Fig 7A) and renal artery aneurysms measuring greater than 2 cm should be treated to avoid life-threatening rupture (50,51). Depending on the size and location of the aneurysm, endovascular embolization can be safely performed through radial or common femoral arterial approaches. When possible, the spleen should be preserved during treatment, although complete embolization of the splenic artery is safe. Often, coils are deployed within the aneurysm sac (Fig 7B), and distal splenic perfusion is maintained via collateral vessels (Fig 7C).

Although an aortic aneurysm is uncommon in women of childbearing age, the risk is heightened with a history of hypertension or predisposing connective tissue disorders, such as Marfan syndrome (50). The risk of aneurysm dissection and rupture for pregnant patients increases with gestational age, and almost half of all aneurysm dissections in young women take place during pregnancy. Management for perinatal aortic dissection includes strict blood pressure control, β-blockers, and open surgical management (53,54). Endovascular repair during pregnancy, primarily with the use of intravascular US and transesophageal echocardiography to decrease radiation exposure, has also been used in select cases (55).
**Cerebral Aneurysm Intervention.—**The risk of pregnancy-associated intracranial hemorrhage is rare, at approximately 12.2 cases per 100 000. Often, the underlying pathologic condition is cerebral aneurysm (Figs 9A, 9B, S5) or arteriovenous malformation (AVM). Understanding the management of intracranial hemorrhage in pregnancy is critical given the risk of disability and mortality to both the patient and fetus (56).

The goal of management of aneurysmal subarachnoid hemorrhage is to prevent rebleeding and limit vasospasm-related ischemia (56). Recent studies have demonstrated statistically comparable outcomes for endovascular coiling and open clipping for ruptured or unruptured cerebral aneurysms but lower risk of perioperative ischemic stroke in endovascularly treated patients. In addition, endovascular coiling (Fig 9C) typically requires less anesthesia than does open surgery. Coiling requires systemic heparinization, which may expose the patient and fetus to hemorrhagic complications (57). There are now multiple treatment options available for endovascular aneurysm exclusion, including stent- or balloon-assisted coiling, stent flow diversion, or Woven EndoBridge (MicroVention Terum) placement with or without flow diversion. When the fetus cannot be delivered before treatment, the potential radiation exposure related to the embolization technique should be considered. Additionally, flow diversion techniques require postoperative antiplatelet therapy, which may be undesirable in a pregnant patient (58). Optimally, if the fetus can survive independently, emergent cesarean delivery should be performed before aneurysm treatment (56).
Intracranial AVMs in pregnancy tend to be seen in older patients, White women, and those with a higher prevalence of smoking and hypertension (58). The relative risk of intracranial hemorrhage in women with AVMs is three times greater during pregnancy and postpartum. The optimal treatment strategy for management of intracranial AVMs remains unclear (59). Prior studies have found no significant difference in outcome for patients undergoing surgical treatment of ruptured AVMs during pregnancy compared with those who received no treatment. However, if the lesion is amenable to endovascular treatment, it likely should be pursued for ruptured AVMs (50). While ethylene vinyl alcohol is inert and safe, there is limited data on the safety of dimethyl sulfoxide in pregnant women. N*-butyl cyanoacrylate (NBCA) is safe for use in pregnancy (56). Conversely, unruptured AVMs should be followed conservatively (59). Intraoperative blood pressure management also needs to be carefully evaluated. Lower blood pressure goals are desirable to decrease the risk of hemorrhage but must be weighed against placental perfusion, which requires higher blood pressure (56).
### Trauma

Physical trauma affects one in 12 pregnancies (60). Abdominopelvic CT with intravenous contrast material is often performed to evaluate pregnant patients in cases of high-energy trauma with suspicion for injury. A pregnancy-related complication to trauma includes uterine rupture. In cases of trauma with imaging findings concerning for active bleeding (Fig 10A), angiography can be performed for both diagnostic and therapeutic intervention (Fig 10B) if the patient does not meet the requirements for emergent surgery (61). Subselective embolization may be performed accordingly if active bleeding or pseudoaneurysm is observed during angiography (61). Other sequelae of trauma such as pneumothorax may be managed with image-guided drainage, while bladder rupture may be managed with urinary diversion. Drainage and urinary diversion procedures are covered in other sections in this article.

## Procedures in the Peripartum and Postpartum Periods

### Invasive Placenta Spectrum Management

Invasive placenta refers to the abnormal growth of all or part of the placenta into the uterine wall. Varying degrees of uterine invasion are described, and at times growth can extend into adjacent pelvic organs including the bladder (62). Any degree of invasive placenta can be associated with life-threatening peripartum bleeding, and management often entails cesarean delivery or hysterectomy. Early diagnosis is key for appropriate preoperative planning, as there are procedures that may be performed prophylactically before surgical intervention. Invasive placenta may be diagnosed at prenatal US or MRI. US can demonstrate placental lacunae and abnormal color Doppler flow (Fig 11A) (62). MRI findings include uterine bulging (Fig 11B), a heterogeneous placenta, or placental bands (62).

**Prophylactic Bilateral Internal Iliac Artery Balloon Occlusion.—**Endovascular placement of bilateral internal iliac artery balloons has been shown to be a useful prophylactic measure to decrease perioperative blood loss in patients with invasive placenta disorders undergoing cesarean delivery (63). After sheath placement, the contralateral internal iliac artery is catheterized from each access site and balloon occlusion catheters are deployed. These balloons can then be inflated before surgery to decrease perioperative blood loss. This technique may be used in other patients at high risk for bleeding, such as in those with fibroids or anemia or who cannot receive blood products (Fig 12).

**Prophylactic Resuscitative Endovascular Balloon Occlusion of the Aorta.—**Resuscitative endovascular balloon occlusion of the aorta (REBOA) has been shown to be an effective way of decreasing intraoperative blood loss during elective cesarean delivery in patients with morbidly adherent placenta (64). The REBOA device can be intermittently inflated intraoperatively to temporarily occlude blood flow to the pelvis. The REBOA device can be placed via a common femoral arterial approach and should be placed either in zone 1, between the takeoff of the left subclavian artery and the celiac artery, or zone 3 (Fig 13), between the level of the lowest renal artery and the aortic bifurcation (65). Fluoroscopic guidance during placement is ideal for prophylactic preoperative placement, but placement without fluoroscopy may be performed in the setting of emergent trauma.

### Postpartum Hemorrhage after Vaginal Delivery

Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) is the most common cause of morbidity and mortality during childbirth (66). PPH was previously defined as 500 cc of blood loss after vaginal delivery and 1000 cc of blood loss after cesarean delivery but was redefined as 1000 cc of blood loss within 24 hours of delivery, regardless of the delivery method, with symptoms of hypovolemia (66). Some common causes of PPH include uterine atony, uterine inversion, invasive placenta, retained products of conception (RPOC), genital tract lacerations, and coagulopathy. Once PPH is recognized, rapid initiation of resuscitation and elucidation of the underlying cause are crucial to management.
Prophylactic Uterine Artery Embolization.—**The most common cause of PPH is uterine atony, which is initially managed conservatively with uterotonic agents, transfusion, bimanual massage, and sometimes intrauterine balloon tamponade (66). Teaching Point In cases of persistent bleeding, regardless of the cause, bilateral uterine artery embolization is used as a fertility-preserving alternative to invasive options like hysterectomy. Initial pelvic angiography may be performed after radial or common femoral artery access to evaluate for radiographic evidence of extravasation or pseudoaneurysm (Fig 14). These entities may be addressed with selective embolization, utilizing a permanent agent such as coils or NBCA. If the patient has coagulopathy, then NBCA may be used as it does not require a normally functioning coagulation cascade for proper embolization. Uterine artery pseudoaneurysm (Fig 15) is rare but can be seen after vaginal delivery or cesarean delivery and is diagnosed at US, CT, or angiography. In cases of known uterine artery pseudoaneurysm, dilation and curettage should not be performed before therapeutic embolization (67). If there is no active bleeding, bilateral uterine artery embolization may be performed by selecting the uterine arteries with either a catheter or microcatheter. Temporary embolization agents include gelatin sponge or microfibrillar collagen. Empirical embolization of the bilateral uterine arteries or anterior division of the internal iliac artery may be safely performed (68).

**Retained Products of Conception.—**RPOC refers to persistent intrauterine tissue after either termination of pregnancy or conception. RPOC is often diagnosed with US findings of a thickened endometrial echo complex with increased vascularity (69). Conservative management of RPOC includes expectant management and uterotonic medications. Surgical options include dilation and curettage or hysteroscopic removal. Uterine artery embolization (Fig 16C) with temporary agents can be performed in conjunction with or as an alternative to surgical management. Angiographic features of RPOC include tortuous uterine feeding arteries (Fig 16A) into a focal area of contrast blush (Fig 16B), pseudoaneurysm, early venous return, or active extravasation (69). When tortuous rich vascularity is additionally observed within the myometrium, with peak systolic velocity greater than 20 cm/sec, enhanced myometrial vascularity can be diagnosed. Enhanced myometrial vascularity is often treated with dilation and curettage but may benefit from prophylactic uterine artery embolization in cases of anemia or excessive bleeding (70).

**Uterine AVM.—**Uterine AVMs are defined as an abnormal connection between a uterine artery and a vein, which may be congenital or acquired (such as arteriovenous fistulas) (71). When acquired, they are often secondary to uterine instrumentation such as in dilation and curettage or cesarean delivery. The presence of uterine AVMs can lead to potentially life-threatening PPH, and they should be treated with endovascular embolization. Angiographic findings of a uterine AVM include hypertrophied uterine arteries with dilated accessory arteries feeding a hypervascular uterine mass (Fig 17B) and early venous drainage (Fig 17A). Embolization can be performed with multiple agents, depending on the anatomy or operator preference, including gelatin sponge, NBCA (Fig 17C, 17D), microvascular plugs, coils, or ethylene vinyl alcohol. Recent literature has described additional supplementary techniques to access and embolize a uterine AVM, including both transvaginal and percutaneous methods, which can be useful in cases refractory to endovascular embolization (72).

### Procedures after Cesarean Delivery

**Pelvic Abscess Drainage.—**Pelvic abscesses after cesarean delivery are rare but do occur, often secondary to untreated or undertreated postpartum endometritis. These abscesses tend to occur in the posterior cul-de-sac, between the uterine wall and urinary bladder, or in the broad ligament (73). Depending on the location of the abscess, various image-guided techniques may be performed for minimally invasive drainage. Percutaneous drainage with US or CT guidance may be performed anteriorly or laterally, if there is a safe window (Fig 18). CT-guided transgluteal drainage has been described (74). Alternative options include endocavitary US-guided drainage through the vagina or rectum depending on the location of the abscess. In some cases, there is no safe window for image-guided drainage, and either a laparoscopic surgical washout or an open laparotomy must be performed.

**Urinary Diversion after Ureteral Injury.—**Iatrogenic ureteral injury may occur after cesarean delivery and can be appropriately managed with urinary diversion. Access into the renal collecting system is achieved with US and fluoroscopic guidance, and antegrade pyelography is performed to evaluate the location and type of ureteral injury (Fig 20A). If possible, a wire may be used to traverse the site of injury, and a double-J ureteral stent can be placed between the renal collecting system and the bladder. In this case, an additional nephrostomy tube should be placed to subsequently ensure ureteral patency and repair. A nephroureteral tube may be placed instead, depending on operator preference. If unable to traverse the site of ureteral injury with a wire, a nephrostomy tube should still be placed to promote healing by urinary diversion. At times, there may be complete ureteral occlusion by a surgical suture, which may require nephrostomy tube placement for urinary diversion followed by subsequent antegrade pyelography to assess for ureteral patency for future interventions including balloon dilation (75). Sometimes images depict a urinoma adjacent to a ureteral injury, which may require percutaneous drainage (Fig 20B).

## Conclusion

IR plays an important role in the management of patients during pregnancy and the immediate postpartum period. Interventionalists can offer a myriad of minimally invasive image-guided procedures that span multiple organ systems and disease processes. These interventions are safe and efficacious and often provide life-saving and fertility-sparing alternatives to more invasive surgical intervention. A good understanding of the physiologic changes during pregnancy, current management of pregnancy-related conditions, and ways to minimize radiation dose will help ensure optimal treatment in this patient population.
