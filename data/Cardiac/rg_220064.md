The subclavian artery is an important structure that may be over-
looked at CT of the chest and neck, in part because of its anatomic
location at the periphery of the field of view but also because the
clinical indication for CT examinations infrequently directs atten-
tion specifically to evaluation of the subclavian artery. As with all
arteries, the subclavian artery has the potential to be involved in a
variety of abnormalities, including pseudoaneurysms, dissections,
stenosis or thrombosis, and vasculopathies. In addition, the subcla-
vian artery can be secondarily involved as a collateral pathway be-
cause of an abnormality elsewhere. The subclavian artery may also
be involved in surgical procedures to supply blood to other sites in
the body or as an arterial access site. In these cases, recognizing the
postsurgical appearance of the subclavian artery has become in-
creasingly complex because of the use of the artery in an increasing
number of procedures. Recognizing expected postoperative changes
remains important to avoid mistaking them for abnormalities. The
authors describe the imaging appearance of the normal anatomy of
the subclavian artery and its anatomic variants, related abnormali-
ties, and important postsurgical considerations.

Introduction
As with all arteries, the subclavian artery can be involved in a spec-
trum of abnormalities. The abnormal subclavian artery can manifest
with various imaging findings including stenosis, occlusion, aneu-
rysm, pseudoaneurysm, transection, and mural inflammation. The
underlying cause of these abnormalities is also variable and includes
atherosclerosis, inflammatory and noninflammatory vasculopathies,
and trauma. The subclavian artery can also be secondarily involved
in processes centered elsewhere, particularly when it serves as a col-
lateral pathway. Recognition of involvement of the subclavian artery
in these conditions facilitates understanding of the CT examination
and can aid in differential diagnosis, when necessary.

Despite its involvement in a number of important abnormalities,

the subclavian artery may be overlooked or difficult to evaluate at
CT. There are likely many reasons for this, such as a lower threshold
for concern when the subclavian artery is not the specific reason for
performing the examination or because the anatomic location of the
subclavian artery is at the periphery of the field of view in CT exami-
nations of the neck or chest. Furthermore, evaluation of the subcla-
vian artery can be complicated by technical considerations including
motion, the phase of contrast material administration, and streak
artifacts from an adjacent venous contrast material column or from
nearby metallic objects such as a pacemaker or shoulder arthroplasty.
To avoid missing abnormalities of the subclavian artery, radiologists
must evaluate it in every examination in which it is included.

Anatomy

Normal Anatomy
The right subclavian artery usually branches
from the right brachiocephalic artery, which
arises as the first branch vessel from a left aortic
arch. The left subclavian artery usually arises as
the third and final arch branch vessel (1). There is
some variability in the configuration of the aortic
arch in patients with normal branching patterns.
Most notably, in patients with a bicuspid aortic
valve, the left subclavian artery may have a more
vertical orientation, and the distance between the
origins of the left common carotid and left sub-
clavian arteries may be increased (Fig 1).

The subclavian arteries travel through the supe-
rior thoracic aperture, also known as the thoracic

and middle scalene muscles, and ultimately pass
between the first rib and the clavicle (1,2) (Fig 2).
The subclavian artery can be divided into three
parts according to its relationship to the anterior
scalene muscle. The first part of the subclavian
artery extends from the origin of the subclavian
artery to the medial border of the anterior scalene
muscle. The second part is located posterior to the
anterior scalene muscle, and the third part courses
from the lateral border of the anterior scalene
muscle to the lateral border of the first rib (3).

The superior thoracic aperture is demarcated
posteriorly by the T1 vertebral body, laterally by
the first rib, and anteriorly by the manubrium
(1,2). The subclavian vessels and brachial plexus
are protected by the structures in the thoracic
inlet as they pass through to supply the upper
extremities (4) (Figs 3, 4). Familiarity with the
superior thoracic aperture is critical for accu-
rate evaluation of the lower neck and subclavian
artery. Injuries to one structure should raise
concern for injury to an adjacent structure and
should prompt the radiologist to examine the
surrounding area closely for signs of trauma.

Pseudoaneurysm.—A pseudoaneurysm is defined

vessel wall that is contained by only some of the

with subclavian artery pseudoaneurysms include
eccentric outpouching, an irregular contour, partial
or complete thrombosis, and, in cases of traumatic
injury, hemorrhage adjacent to and in contact with
the damaged artery. In cases of rupture, intrave-
nous contrast extravasation into adjacent tissues
may be seen (13). Infected subclavian artery pseu-
doaneurysms may be associated with lymphade-

Dissection
Dissections are characterized by an intimomedial
defect that allows blood to dissect along the media,
creating an intimomedial flap that separates the
true and false lumina (11). The intimomedial flap
is usually identifiable in subclavian artery dis-
sections (Fig 11). However, because of the small
caliber of the vessel, the false lumen may collapse
or thrombose, giving the appearance of an eccen-
tric smooth occlusion rather than an intimomedial
flap. This appearance, particularly when it is asso-
ciated with an aortic dissection or in patients with
atherosclerosis, should raise concern for a subcla-
vian artery dissection. Subclavian artery dissection
is most commonly seen in patients with an aortic
dissection that extends into one or both subclavian
arteries (5). Isolated subclavian artery dissec-
tion is rare; most cases are associated with arte-
rial thoracic outlet syndrome (TOS), connective
tissue disease, blunt trauma, or iatrogenic injury.
Patients may present with chest or back pain, arm
paresthesia, or nonspecific neurologic symptoms.
Dissection predisposes patients to formation of a
thrombus, which may result in a distal embolism.

compression of the neurovascular bundle that

increases the frequency and degree of compres-
sion of the structures of the thoracic outlet (2).

and subsequent damage of the subclavian artery,

Subclavian Steal Syndrome
The subclavian artery may “steal” blood supply by
means of reversal of flow in a branch vessel when
there is high-grade stenosis in the subclavian artery
proximal to the origin of that branch vessel. Sub-
clavian steal syndrome is defined by the associated
clinical manifestations, which may include limb
ischemia, asymmetric blood pressure in the upper
extremities, and neurologic symptoms (4,5).

The most commonly involved vessel is the ver-
tebral artery. When the vertebral artery is involved,
vertebrobasilar insufficiency may result from rever-
sal of blood flow from the intracranial circulation to
reconstitute the occluded subclavian artery (5). The
diagnosis can be suggested when a focal upstream
stenosis is seen in the subclavian artery in a patient
with appropriate symptoms. Reversal of vertebral
artery flow at Doppler US or phase-contrast MRI
allows confirmation of the diagnosis (15) (Fig 13).
Coronary subclavian steal syndrome is a rare
variation that may be seen when the left internal
mammary artery has been used in a coronary ar-
tery bypass graft procedure (5,17). In these cases,
the subclavian artery receives retrograde blood
flow through the left internal mammary artery (17)
(Fig 14). Patients may present with chest pain and
signs of myocardial ischemia (5). In patients with
left internal mammary bypass grafts, radiologists
should carefully interrogate the graft and the proxi-
mal subclavian artery because stenosis in either
entity can limit flow to the territory of the bypassed
coronary artery.

Penetrating Injury.—Most subclavian artery inju-
ries are due to penetrating trauma, most often from
gunshot wounds and knife injuries (18). Identi-
fication of the wound trajectory is essential; any

trajectory in the vicinity of the subclavian artery
should increase suspicion for injury. Direct findings
of injury include abrupt occlusion, extravasation
of contrast material, outpouching of the vessel, or
formation of a pseudoaneurysm. Of these, contrast
extravasation is the least commonly observed (Fig
15). In some cases, localized tissue disruption can
cause vasospasm and local occlusion that may
simulate an occlusion due to vascular injury.

Blunt Injury.—Blunt traumatic injury to the sub-
clavian artery is estimated to account for only 1%–
3% of acute subclavian artery injuries, which is
likely an underestimate, given the highly fatal na-
ture of the traumatic events that cause these types
of injuries (3). A high-force impact that is strong

contusion, traumatic brain injury, or damage to
the brachial plexus (18). Regardless of the mecha-
nism of injury, prompt diagnosis and intervention
are essential, as subclavian artery injury may be
associated with major complications including
severe hemorrhage, hemothorax, formation of a
pseudoaneurysm, cerebrovascular infarct, perma-

Iatrogenic Injury.—The subclavian artery may
also be involved in iatrogenic injury. Inadvertent
arterial cannulation of the subclavian artery is
rare and occurs in less than 1% of central venous
catheter placements (19). However, when this
does occur, the radiologist plays a vital role in the
patient’s care.

involve a vessel of any size, the two most common
idiopathic large vessel vasculitides are Takayasu
arteritis and giant cell arteritis. Both diseases
are characterized by idiopathic granulomatous
transmural inflammation and arterial wall thick-
ening of the aorta and arch branches, including
the subclavian artery, that often leads to arterial
thrombosis, stenosis, and aneurysm (5). These
findings can be detected at CT and MRI. Fluo-
rine-18 fluorodeoxyglucose PET/CT can also be
used to evaluate inflammatory activity in vascu-
litis before and after treatment, because vessel
walls affected by active large vessel inflammatory
vasculitis are metabolically active (22).

Both Takayasu arteritis and giant cell arteri-
tis have a strong female predilection. However,
Takayasu arteritis is typically seen in a younger
patient population (approximately 10–40 years of
age) and Asian patients, whereas giant cell arteritis
is more often seen in an older patient population
(approximately 70–80 years of age) and in White
patients (5). Takayasu arteritis has a propensity to
involve the subclavian arteries and may result in a
nonpalpable brachial pulse or asymmetric blood
pressure in the upper extremities, hence the moni-
ker “pulseless arteritis” (5,23) (Fig 19). Giant cell
arteritis, on the other hand, is known as temporal
arteritis, given the frequent involvement of the
temporal artery. Patients with giant cell arteritis
may present with headache, jaw pain, unexplained
fever, or visual disturbances (5). In many cases,
distinguishing between Takayasu arteritis and giant
cell arteritis is not possible at imaging or on the

basis of clinical grounds. In these cases, patients
younger than 50 years with large vessel vasculitis
are considered to have Takayasu arteritis, while
those older than 50 years are described as having
giant cell arteritis.

In addition to the idiopathic large vessel vasculi-

tides, secondary large vessel vasculitides such as
those due to chemotherapy or a known systemic
disease, such as sarcoidosis or immunoglobulin
G4–related disease, can produce identical findings
and also involve the subclavian artery (Fig 20).

Fibromuscular Dysplasia
Fibromuscular dysplasia is an idiopathic nonath-
erosclerotic and noninflammatory condition that
predominantly affects small- and medium-sized
arteries, most often the renal, extracranial verte-
bral, and carotid arteries (24,25). The subclavian
arteries can also be involved, either in isolation or
in combination with involvement of other vessels.
The classic finding of fibromuscular dysplasia in-
volves short focal stenoses with alternating areas
of dilatation that produces a “string of beads”
appearance (24) (Fig 21). However, in some
cases, an isolated focal stenosis may be the only
finding. Diagnosis is reliant on the presence of at
least one site of stenosis without another cause.
Arterial tortuosity, dissection, and aneurysm may
also be seen (24).

Connective Tissue Vasculopathies
The subclavian arteries are often involved in con-
nective tissue vasculopathies, either in isolation
or in association with findings in other vessels. A
connective tissue vasculopathy should be sus-
pected when aneurysms or extensive tortuosity of
the subclavian arteries is seen in the absence of
atherosclerosis, particularly in younger patients.
The major syndromic connective tissue vascu-
lopathies are briefly reviewed in the next sections.

Marfan Syndrome.—Marfan syndrome is charac-
terized by impaired formation of elastin, which
predisposes a patient to loss of elasticity, cystic
medial necrosis, fibrosis, and loss of smooth
muscle in artery walls. These underlying histologic
abnormalities manifest as the classic features of
Marfan syndrome, specifically aortic root an-
eurysms involving the sinuses of Valsalva and a
predilection for type A aortic dissections (5). The

subclavian arteries may be involved in isolation,
typically manifesting as an aneurysm or, less com-
monly, as an isolated dissection. More commonly,
the subclavian arteries are secondarily involved by
direct extension from the aorta (11) (Fig 22).

Loeys-Dietz Syndrome.—Loeys-Dietz syndrome is
a rare disease that was initially described in 2005.
Five types have been described and are character-
ized by craniofacial and vascular abnormalities
(5,26). The vascular abnormalities include aneu-
rysms and arterial tortuosity, the latter of which
is a defining feature of Loeys-Dietz syndrome (5)
(Fig 23). Many features of Loeys-Dietz syndrome

results in excessive tissue fragility (5,27). Medium
and large vessels are most often affected, particu-
larly the carotid and vertebral arteries; the subcla-
vian arteries may also be involved (28) (Fig 24).

Role of Subclavian Arteries as
Collateral Vessels

Thoracic Collateral Vessel
The subclavian arteries and their branches are
involved in multiple collateral vascular networks in
the head and neck, upper extremities, and chest.
As such, they have the potential to supply collat-
eral flow in cases of severe arterial stenosis or oc-
clusion, whether from atherosclerotic disease, prior
injury or inflammation, or extrinsic compression
of the vessel. This is of particular importance in
patients with cystic fibrosis, because the subclavian

artery and its branches often provide nonbronchial
systemic arterial and bronchial collateral arterial
flow, especially in cases of upper lobe–predomi-
nant disease (Fig 25). Awareness of these poten-
tial collateral vessels is critical for evaluating and
treating cases of hemoptysis (29,30). The subcla-
vian artery may also serve as a source of collateral
vascular flow in patients with cyanotic congenital
heart disease, most notably in those with repaired
single ventricle morphology or with unrepaired
conotruncal abnormalities. These collateral vessels
often anastomose with pulmonary artery branches.

Aortic Collateral Vessel
Thoracic endovascular aortic repair is often
performed for the management of type B thoracic
aortic dissections and aneurysms. Depending on
the location and extent of disease, the origin of
the left subclavian artery may need to be covered
by the stent, with the proximal landing zone of
the graft located proximal to the origin of the left
subclavian artery (31).

Many surgeons proactively create a subclavian-

to-carotid bypass with or without ligation or
occlusion of the native subclavian artery in efforts
to prevent subclavian steal, vertebrobasilar insuf-
ficiency, upper extremity ischemia, and spinal
cord ischemia (31,32). When the proximal native
subclavian artery is not ligated, the prevention of
an endoleak relies on the formation of a thrombus
at the origin of the vessel (33). However, in some
cases, the artery remains patent and provides
retrograde filling of the excluded aorta, result-
ing in a type II endoleak (Fig 26). If the endoleak
continues to enlarge or causes symptoms, further
intervention is required.

Arteriovenous Malformation
Arteriovenous malformations involving the
subclavian artery are rare and often involve the
vertebral and common carotid arteries in con-
junction with the subclavian and internal jugular
veins (34,35) (Fig 27). Although these arteriove-
nous malformations are typically asymptomatic,
they should be treated to prevent complications
related to high-output heart failure or spontane-
ous hemorrhage. Treatment is usually not cura-
tive, given the complex vascular supply (34).
Surgery poses a high risk of bleeding and injury
to the brachial plexus. Endovascular interven-
tion such as embolization is less invasive and has
low morbidity; however, not all arteriovenous
malformations are amenable to embolization,
and care must be taken to avoid embolizing
arteries that supply blood to other areas of the
body (35).

Mimics of Subclavian Abnormalities

Subclavian Grafts
The subclavian artery is a versatile vessel that may
be used to supply alternative arterial blood flow to
the head and neck, upper extremities, and lower
extremities. Complex repairs of the aortic arch
often require establishing an alternative route of
blood supply to the upper extremities. Carotid to
subclavian artery bypasses are commonly per-
formed when a thoracic endovascular aortic repair
stent covers the origin of the left subclavian artery
(32) (Fig 28). Blood flow to the left upper extrem-
ity is reestablished by bypassing the origin of the
subclavian artery and using a graft to connect the
subclavian artery to the ipsilateral carotid artery
(36). In these cases, the proximal end of the native
subclavian artery may also be ligated or oversewn
to prevent an endoleak.

Arterial Access
Side grafts, which are sometimes referred to as
arterial conduits, are commonly used to secure

temporary arterial access, such as for cardiopul-
monary bypass during aortic arch repair or place-
ment of a temporary ventricular support device
(37). Alternative access is necessary when the
ascending aorta cannot be used because of severe
atherosclerosis, dissection, or aneurysm. Subcla-
vian artery access through side grafts is becom-
ing increasingly common, because this method
has a lower risk of arterial stenosis and injury
compared with direct cannulation. When arte-
rial access is no longer required, the anastomosis
between the graft and artery is left intact and the
end of the graft is oversewn or tied off, creating
the appearance of a stump (36).

bypass and side grafts can simulate pseudoan-
eurysms or contrast material extravasation that
may be seen in vascular injury. Bypass grafts
often have a thickened irregular contour that may
be mistaken for vasculitis or vascular injury. At
noncontrast CT, graft material is typically hyper-
attenuating in comparison with normal vascular
walls. Side branch graft remnants often have a

Conclusion
The subclavian artery is an important structure
that may be involved in a variety of abnor-
malities, may serve as a collateral vessel, or may
have a role in a range of vascular procedures.
Familiarity with normal and variant anatomy
and expected postprocedural findings provides
a foundation for determining if the imaging
appearance is within normal limits and helps
the radiologist to distinguish a true subclavian
artery abnormality from potential mimics.
Knowledge of subclavian artery abnormalities
also helps to narrow the differential diagnosis
and ultimately directly affects patient care and
outcomes.
