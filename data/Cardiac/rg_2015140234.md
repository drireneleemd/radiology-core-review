## Abstract

Cardiac computed tomography (CT) and magnetic resonance (MR) imaging provide clinicians with important insights into cardiac physiology and pathology. However, not all radiologists understand the language and concepts of cardiac physiology that are used daily by cardiologists. This review article covers basic cardiac physiology as it relates to cardiac CT and MR imaging. Topics include a review of the cardiac cycle and left ventricular pressure-volume loops as they relate to different pathologic states, evaluation of cardiac function, and calculation of key parameters such as left ventricular volumes and the ejection fraction. The hemodynamics of cardiac shunts are covered, with an emphasis on factors important to cardiologists, including the ratio of pulmonary flow to systemic flow. Additionally, valvular physiologic function is reexamined, with a focus on understanding pressure gradients within the heart and also the changes associated with valvular pathologic conditions, including measurement of regurgitant fractions in patients with valvular insufficiency. Understanding these basic concepts will help radiologists tailor the reporting of cardiac studies to clinically relevant information.

Figure 2b Pressure-volume relationships of the left ventricle. **(a)** The pressure-volume loop is generated by plotting the left ventricular *(LV)* volume on the x-axis and left ventricular pressure on the y-axis. Completely vertical portions of the curve represent changes in pressure without changes in volume (isovolumetric changes). Horizontal portions of the curve represent changes in volume and occur with some degree of pressure change. The gray area within the curve represents stroke work. Each corner represents a transition, with designations for the aortic valve *(A)* and mitral valve *(M)*. When the valve is closed, the box is shaded red; when the valve is open, the box is shaded green. **(b)** Three-chamber CT image shows that systole begins when the mitral valve closes and isovolumetric contraction occurs, as indicated by the closed aortic valve. Ventricular volume remains at the end-diastolic volume. This combination occurs at the lower right corner of the pressure-volume curve in **a**. **(c)** Three-chamber CT image shows that left ventricular ejection begins when the aortic valve opens (upper right corner of the pressure-volume curve in **a**). **(d)** Three-chamber CT image shows that when the pressure of the left ventricle drops below the systemic pressure, the aortic valve closes, which terminates systole. This step occurs at the upper left corner of the pressure-volume curve in **a**. Diastole begins with isovolumetric relaxation, in which both the aortic and mitral valves are closed and the volume remains at end-systolic volume. **(e)** Three-chamber CT image shows that the drop in left ventricular pressure allows the mitral valve to open (lower left corner of the pressure-volume curve in **a**), and ventricular filling occurs until the onset of systole. *Ao* = aorta, *LA* = left atrium, *RV* = right ventricle.

Figure 3a Dilated cardiomyopathy. **(a)** The left ventricular *(LV)* pressure-volume loop of a heart with dilated cardiomyopathy is shaded blue, with the normal left ventricular pressure-volume loop shaded gray. In a patient with dilated cardiomyopathy, weakened and thinned myocardium is unable to generate a normal contraction, which decreases the stroke volume *(SV)*. This decrease is compensated for by an increased preload according to the Frank-Starling mechanism, which is indicated by a rightward shift of the right edge of the pressure-volume loop. The thinned myocardium cannot compensate, and the end-systolic volume is higher, indicated by a rightward shift of the left edge of the loop. The stroke volume is decreased, as is the stroke work, a finding indicated by the smaller volume of the blue loop. **(b, c)** Three-chamber CT images of a heart with dilated cardiomyopathy obtained at end diastole **(b)** and end systole **(c)**. Note the dilated appearance of the left ventricle in **b** and **c**, as predicted by the pressure-volume loop. The left ventricle was calculated to have an end-diastolic volume of 374 mL, an end-systolic volume of 270 mL, and an ejection fraction of 28%. *Ao* = aorta, *LA* = left atrium, *RV* = right ventricle.

Figure 9a Aortic stenosis. **(a)** The left ventricular *(LV)* pressure-volume loop of a heart with aortic stenosis is shaded blue, with the normal loop shaded gray. In a heart with aortic stenosis, there is an increase in the afterload and end-systolic volume as the left ventricle contracts against the stenotic aortic valve, shifting the left edge of the loop to the right. The increased afterload leads to a decreased stroke volume *(SV)* and a higher end-diastolic volume, shifting the right edge of the loop to the right. Aortic valve closure occurs at higher values of left ventricular pressure and end-systolic volume. This increased preload activates the Frank-Starling mechanism to increase contractility. Some cases of aortic stenosis show asymmetric valve motion, depending on the degree of calcification and fusion of the leaflets. **(e)** For comparison, a short-axis cardiac CT image shows a normal open aortic valve.
