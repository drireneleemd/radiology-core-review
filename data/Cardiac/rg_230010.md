Cardiac and Pericardial Neoplasms in Children:
Radiologic-Pathologic Correlation

Mariangeles Medina Perez, MD • John P. Lichtenberger, MD • Alison R. Huppmann, MD • Mariangela Gomez, MD • Karen I. Ramirez Suarez, MD
Ann Foran, MD • Danish Vaiyani, MD • Ammie M. White, MD • David M. Biko, MD

Primary cardiac and pericardial neoplasms are rare in the pediatric population and can include both benign and malignant lesions.
Rhabdomyomas, teratomas, fibromas, and hemangiomas are the most common benign tumors. The most common primary cardiac
malignancies are soft-tissue sarcomas, including undifferentiated sarcomas, rhabdomyosarcomas, and fibrosarcomas. However,
metastatic lesions are more common than primary cardiac neoplasms. Children with primary cardiac and pericardial tumors may
present with nonspecific cardiovascular symptoms, and their clinical presentation may mimic that of more common nonneoplastic
cardiac disease. The diagnosis of cardiac tumors has recently been facilitated using noninvasive cardiac imaging. Echocardiography
is generally the first-line modality for evaluation. Cardiac MRI and CT are used for tissue characterization and evaluation of tumor
size, extension, and physiologic effect. The varied imaging appearances of primary cardiac neoplasms can be explained by their
underlying abnormality. Treatment of these lesions varies from conservative management, with spontaneous regression of some
lesions such as rhabdomyomas, to surgical resection, particularly in patients with associated heart failure. With adequate imaging
techniques and knowledge of the pathologic basis of the neoplasm, it is often possible to differentiate benign from malignant tu-
mors, which can greatly affect adequate and timely treatment.

Benign Cardiac Tumors

Rhabdomyoma
The majority of primary cardiac neoplasms in children are
rhabdomyomas  (60%),  which  are  congenital  hamartomas.
Up  to  80%  of  cases  are  associated  with  tuberous  sclerosis,
a genetic disease characterized by the presence of multiple
benign  hamartomas  in  several  organs  (1)  (Fig  2).  Usually
they  are  discovered  prenatally  or  in  early  infancy,  with  a
mean patient age at diagnosis of 2 weeks (6,7) (8).

Rhabdomyomas are generally located in the ventricular
myocardium or interventricular septum (9); however, they
may appear in the atria in up to 30% of cases (10,11). They
may be asymptomatic and diagnosed incidentally or may be
clinically symptomatic if they protrude into the lumen and
obstruct blood flow or promote an accessory pathway that
results in cardiac arrhythmia (1,6). Neonates and children
may  present  with  murmurs,  cyanosis,  respiratory  distress,
cardiac  failure,  or  cardiogenic  shock  due  to  myocardial
thickening or ventricular dysfunction (2,7,12,13).

Imaging  Features.—On  echocardiograms,  rhabdomyomas
appear  as  a  homogeneous,  hyperechogenic,  solid  single
mass or as multiple masses of variable sizes (1,6,14). On CT
images,  rhabdomyomas  usually  appear  as  homogeneous,
low-attenuation,  intramural  lesions  (Fig  2)  and  are  com-
monly hypoenhancing after administration of contrast ma-
terial  (10).  They  appear  hyperintense  on  T2-weighted  car-
diac MR images and are isointense to slightly hyperintense
in  comparison  with  the  myocardium  at T1-weighted  MRI.
After intravenous contrast material is administered, they do
not demonstrate significant enhancement (15–17) (Fig 3).

Pathologic  Features.—Rhabdomyomas  are  often  multifo-
cal  (90%)  and  of  variable  size,  with  well-defined  borders
and a yellow to white color, making them distinct from the
surrounding cardiac muscle (Fig 3). They are not encapsu-
lated. Microscopically, these tumors are composed of large
polygonal  cells  with  clear  vacuoles  due  to  glycogen  accu-
mulation.  Spider  cells  are  a  characteristic  feature,  named
because of the radially oriented myofibrils extending from
the nucleus to the cell borders (18).

Treatment and Prognosis.—The majority of rhabdomyomas
regress  spontaneously  in  early  childhood.  Thus,  first-line
management  is  often  conservative,  and  surgical  resection
is reserved for patients with masses that result in hemody-
namic disturbances (2,7,14). Alternative clinical treatments
have  been  studied,  including  everolimus,  a  mammalian
target  of  rapamycin  (mTOR)  inhibitor,  in  patients  with
clinically  significant  cardiac  rhabdomyomas  and  tuberous
sclerosis  disease  (1,19).  Strong  predictors  of  poor  neona-
tal outcomes include large tumors, hydrops, and fetal dys-
rhythmias (20).

Fibroma
Cardiac fibromas are the second most common type of be-
nign cardiac tumors in children after rhabdomyomas (10).
They  are  associated  with  Gorlin  syndrome  in  up  to  3%  of
cases (1,7,10). The onset of symptoms typically occurs later
in  life,  but  nearly  one-third  of  patients  are  diagnosed  in
utero or during the 1st year of life (21). Most fibromas are
solid and solitary and could grow to a size that obliterates
the cavity (2,16). They may cause a variety of symptoms due
to valvular involvement, obstruction of blood flow, and ar-
rhythmias.  Patients  may  be  asymptomatic  in  one-third  of
cases  or  may  present  with  cyanosis,  dyspnea,  or  syncope
and may experience sudden death (6). Pericardial effusion

Pathologic  Features.—Cardiac  fibromas  have  a  predilection
for  the  ventricular  septum,  although  they  may  be  seen  in
other  locations.  The  lesions  grossly  resemble  leiomyomas,
appearing well circumscribed, with a firm white whorled cut
surface (Fig 4). Bland spindle cells (fibroblasts) are present in
a collagenous background at histologic examination. Younger
patients often have more cellular lesions, which decrease with
age in concert with increased collagen. Focal calcification and
elastic fibers may be seen (26).

Treatment and Prognosis.—In general, cardiac fibromas have
a poor prognosis (10). They do not show spontaneous regres-
sion;  thus,  surgical  treatment  is  recommended  to  achieve
better outcomes (27). Location, size, and surgical resectabil-
ity of fibromas determine treatment, which may be complete
surgical resection, incomplete excision, or an orthotopic heart
transplant for large tumors (1,6,10).

Inflammatory Myofibroblastic Tumor
Inflammatory myofibroblastic tumor (IMT) is a rare mesen-
chymal neoplasm predominantly occurring in children, ado-
lescents, and young adults (28). Cardiac involvement is rare,
with only approximately 60 cases reported in the scientific lit-
erature. It usually manifests as one or more endocardial-based
intracavitary masses and most commonly involves the valve
leaflets  (1,29).  These  tumors  may  be  mobile  and  are  at  risk
for extending through the atrioventricular valves or outflow
tracts to the great arteries (29).

Myxoma
Cardiac myxoma is a rare tumor in children, but it deserves
special  attention  given  its  association  with  Carney  complex
and its characteristic imaging appearance. Carney complex is
a rare familial multiple endocrine neoplasia syndrome caused
by mutations of the PRKAR1A gene, and cardiac myxomas are
the  most  common  noncutaneous  diagnostic  criteria  for  this
complex (35,36). When they occur in these patients, cardiac
myxomas are often multicentric and more aggressive and may
be metachronous (36–38). Other tumors associated with this
complex that may be detected at imaging include primary pig-
mented  nodular  adrenocortical  disease,  large-cell  calcifying
Sertoli cell tumors, psammomatous melanotic schwannomas,
and thyroid nodules (36).

Imaging Features.—When sporadic, cardiac myxomas almost
always  occur  in  the  left  atrium  with  a  characteristic  stalk-
like attachment to the interatrial septum near the fossa ova-
lis. They may be mobile and prolapse across the mitral valve
during diastolic filling. However, cardiac myxomas in patients
with Carney complex can occur in any chamber and tend to
be multiple.

diographic  signs  of  valvular  obstruction  such  as  pulmonary
edema.  Echocardiography  shows  a  heterogeneous  mass,  oc-
casionally  with  suggestion  of  calcification.  At  CT,  they  are

depending on the composition of the IMTs. They can be iso-
intense in comparison with the myocardium at T1-weighted
MRI (Fig 5) and hypointense at T2-weighted MRI if there are
relatively more fibrous components, but they may show high
T2 signal intensity if they contain myxoid elements (1,30,31).
There  may  be  persistent  enhancement  during  the  delayed
phase  at  CT  and  intense  delayed  enhancement  at  cardiac
MRI (1).

Pathologic  Features.—IMT  typically  appears  as  a  polypoid
mass originating in the endocardium (Fig 5). The myofibro-
blastic cells of the tumor may be spindled or plump and may
be set in a background containing variable amounts of myx-
oid stroma and chronic inflammatory cells (eg, lymphocytes,
plasma cells, and eosinophils). A thin layer of fibrin may be
present on the surface (33).

Teratoma
Teratoma is a type of germ cell tumor and accounts for less
than 2% of all cardiac tumors in pediatric patients (44). Ter-
atomas are usually extracardiac in location, with the pericar-
dium being the most common location, and they are usually
associated with the great vessels, most often arising from the
root of the ascending aorta (45). They rarely involve the myo-
cardium, manifesting as an intracardiac mass arising from the
atrial or ventricular wall (46). Greater than 75% of cardiac ter-
atomas occur in children aged 15 years or younger (25).

The  patient  presentation  can  be  varied,  with  symptoms
usually attributable to the mass effect from the tumor on the
heart  and  potential  symptoms  including  arrhythmia,  heart
failure, valvular insufficiency, hydrops, or in rare cases, sud-
den death; neonates may die at birth from cardiac tamponade
and cardiac compression (3,47).

Imaging  Features.—Chest  radiography  may  show  a  mass
in  the  aortopulmonary  window  (48),  an  enlarged  cardio-
mediastinal  silhouette,  and  in  rare  cases,  calcification  or
formed  teeth  (21). Teratomas  are  usually  easily  detected  at
fetal  echocardiography  or  postnatal  echocardiography  as  a
heterogeneous  and  encapsulated  cystic  mass,  typically  po-
sitioned on the right side of the heart and frequently asso-
ciated with pericardial effusion (45,48). CT findings are of a
heterogeneous tumor that may show areas of hypoattenua-
tion representing fat or hyperattenuating areas of calcifica-
tions, which can be helpful in supporting the diagnosis (40).
On cardiac MR images, teratomas are described as multiloc-
ular  tumors,  with  cystic  components  and  intervening  solid
areas, typically displacing the heart and rotating it along its
longitudinal axis (25). The tumor is usually intrapericardial
with mass effect on the superior vena cava or right atrium. A
heterogeneous cystic mass, isointense or hypointense to the
myocardium  at  T1-weighted  MRI  and  with  heterogeneous
signal intensity at T2-weighted MRI, is often seen, with areas

typically spherical or ovoid and occasionally lobulated, lower
in attenuation than surrounding myocardium with heteroge-
neous enhancement (Fig 6) (39) (40).

On cardiac MR images, cardiac myxomas are typically hy-
perintense at T2-weighted MRI, show relative hypoperfusion
to myocardium at first-pass perfusion imaging, and show het-
erogeneous enhancement at LGE imaging. Balanced steady-
state free-precession (SSFP) MR imaging may show the mo-
bility of the mass and the contact with the valvular structures
of the outflow tracts (41).

Pathologic  Features.—Myxomas  are  heterogeneous  neo-
plasms of uncertain histogenesis. They can be pedunculated
or  sessile,  with  a  wide  range  in  size. The  firm  to  gelatinous
mass may have focal areas of hemorrhage (Fig 6). Although
myxomas are typically smooth surfaced, excrescences may be
present and are more likely lead to a tumor embolus. Histo-
logically, characteristic myxoma cells, also known as lepidic
cells, contain an eosinophilic cytoplasm and bland round to
oval nuclei, with inconspicuous nucleoli. These cells can be
arranged in nests around vascular channels (ie, perivascular
rings) or in a myxoid background, typically infiltrated by vary-
ing numbers of inflammatory cells. Recent or remote hemor-
rhage is frequently identified (42).

outcomes after surgical resection are excellent.

Malignant Cardiac Tumors
Malignant tumors tend to be larger than benign lesions and
irregularly shaped, and they invade surrounding tissues (54).

Primary Cardiac Sarcoma
Primary cardiac sarcomas represent the most common pri-
mary malignant tumor in adults and children, accounting for
75% of primary malignant cardiac masses (1). Undifferenti-
ated sarcomas, rhabdomyosarcomas, and fibromyosarcomas
are the most common. Nonetheless, giant cell sarcomas, sy-
novial sarcomas, leiyomyosarcomas, myeloid sarcomas, and
osteosarcomas can also be seen (4).

Sarcomas  can  manifest  anywhere  in  the  heart,  but  some
lesions have a higher preference for the left atrium, including
undifferentiated  sarcomas  (9,55)  (25).  Rhabdomyosarcoma
is  the  most  common  primary  cardiac  malignancy  of  child-
hood. Although it has no predilection for any cardiac cham-
ber, it more frequently involves the cardiac valves compared
with  other  sarcomas. The  clinical  presentation  of  sarcomas
is  variable,  with  symptoms  including  recurrent  pericardial

Lymphoma
Primary lymphoma of the heart is extremely rare. Lympho-
mas  are  aggressive  and  usually  of  the  non-Hodgkin  B-cell
type. They commonly involve the right side of the heart, es-
pecially the right atrium, followed by the right ventricle and
left  ventricle  (45).  Patients  can  present  with  dyspnea  and
chest pain; however, specific locations can produce certain
clinical  syndromes  such  as  superior  vena  cava  syndrome,
with  right  atrial  lymphoma.  Arrhythmia,  coronary  artery
obstruction,  tumor  embolism,  and  pericardial  effusion  can
also be seen with tumor infiltration (8).

Imaging  Features.—At  echocardiography,  lymphomas  can
appear  as  localized  wall  thickening  secondary  to  homoge-
neous  infiltrating  masses  or  as  nodular  masses  protruding
into  the  cardiac  chambers.  In  cases  where  the  atrioven-
tricular  groove  is  affected,  there  can  be  encasement  of  the
right coronary artery and invasion of the pericardium, with
resulting  effusion  (9).  Primary  cardiac  lymphoma  typically

slight hypointensity and as isointensity on T2-weighted MR
images  compared  with  the  myocardium.  There  is  minimal
contrast material uptake on LGE MR images (15).

tan, fleshy, lobulated cut surface on gross inspection. Most

Primary  cardiac  lymphomas  are  usually  uniform  tumors.

hemorrhage.

Treatment and Prognosis.—While the prognosis is overall poor,
early diagnosis and institution of chemotherapy may result in
remission of disease and symptom relief, with longer survival
time (21). Radiation therapy and surgical resection can be con-
sidered; however, they are less favorable treatments.

Metastatic Tumors
Metastatic cardiac tumors are more common than primary car-
diac tumors (3). In children, cardiac metastases can occur from
lymphoma,  sarcoma,  testicular  carcinoma,  and Wilms  tumor
(1). Metastatic lesions are generally seen late in the course of
a primary tumor and may arise anywhere in the heart or may
diffusely extend along the epicardial or pericardial surface (Fig
10), with morphology dependent on the primary cell type (12).
Patients usually present with multiple solid nodules associated
with adhesions and hemorrhagic effusions. In addition, intrac-
ardiac lesions can be seen with direct extension of primary ma-
lignancies such as Wilms tumor via the renal veins, through the
inferior vena cava, into the right atrium.

Imaging  studies  are  an  invaluable  tool  in  the  diagnosis  of
cardiac  metastatic  disease.  If  a  patient  with  a  known  malig-

nancy presents with new cardiovascular symptoms, cardiac in-
volvement should be included in the differential diagnosis and
echocardiography should be performed as the initial diagnostic
test. A chest radiograph could show an enlarged cardiac silhou-
ette if a pericardial effusion is present. Cardiac CT and cardiac
MRI can be subsequently performed to evaluate for intramyo-
cardial masses and direct tumor extension.

In  recent  studies  of  adult  cardiac  tumors,  the  presence  of
increased metabolic activity in cardiac lesions at FDG PET/CT
has been associated with an increased likelihood of malignancy
(60). However, nonspecific FDG uptake in the normal myocar-
dium is a common finding that can range from uniform and
intense  to  irregular  or  absent  and  can  represent  a  diagnostic
challenge.

Pericardial Lesions

Lipoma and Lipoblastoma
Fatty  lesions  originating  in  the  pericardium  are  most  com-
monly lipomas. Lipomas are fatty soft-tissue masses encapsu-
lated with a thin layer of fibrous tissue that can occur at any
age, including in a fetus (61). Pericardial lipomas are less cir-
cumscribed than are subcutaneous lipomas, often conforming
to the space that they occupy (62).

Conversely, pericardial lipoblastomas are less common than
lipomas and are composed of fetal adipose tissue. These tumors
most frequently occur in patients younger than 3 years old (63).
Superficial lipoblastomas are usually well encapsulated, but a
more diffuse infiltrative form can be present, which is classified
as lipoblastomatosis (64).

have similar imaging findings. Both are echogenic at echo-

sideration  of  lipoblastoma  (66).  Recurrence  after  resection

complete resection is essential to avoid recurrence. Up to 80%
of incompletely resected lipoblastomas recur (63). Follow-up
imaging after resection of a lipoblastoma for up to 5 years is
recommended (66).

Cardiac Tumor Mimics

Thrombus
The  heart  is  the  most  common  site  for  thrombosis  in  chil-
dren,  representing  approximately  25%–28%  of  cases,  most
commonly associated with indwelling central venous cathe-
ters, followed by locations in the peripheral arteries, central
nervous  system  vessels,  major  intra-abdominal  veins,  and
peripheral veins (68). Intracardiac thrombus is the main con-
sideration in the differential diagnosis when a cardiac mass is
encountered, and it can occur anywhere in the heart.

Echocardiography  is  the  primary  modality  for  evaluation
of a thrombus, which can be seen as a laminar and adherent
mural  mass  or  a  nonpedunculated  and  mobile  intraluminal
mass with heterogeneously increased echogenicity (69). At CT,
thrombi show homogeneous attenuation and can contain cal-
cifications in patients with a chronic thrombus. On cardiac MR
images, the typical appearance is a hypointense nonenhancing
mass. The specific imaging characteristics depend on the age
of the thrombus. An acute thrombus is generally hyperintense
on T1- and T2-weighted MR images; a subacute thrombus is

Myocardial Fatty Foci
Although the classic cardiac manifestation of tuberous sclero-
sis is rhabdomyoma, in recent years there have been reports of
myocardial fatty deposition, predominantly in older children.
There are often multiple fatty deposits, commonly in the ven-
tricular septum, and they can be intramyocardial or epicardial.
At echocardiography, myocardial fatty foci appear as multiple
circumscribed echogenic mass lesions. At CT, they show low
attenuation  with  no  enhancement.  Fatty  deposits  are  hyper-
intense  compared  with  myocardium  at  T1-weighted  and  T2-
weighted MRI and lose signal intensity during fat-suppressed
sequences.  At  cine  SSFP  MRI,  myocardial  fatty  foci  may
demonstrate a rim of chemical shift artifact (24,70).

Differential Diagnosis
The definitive diagnosis of cardiac tumors can only be made
at  histopathologic  evaluation  after  surgical  resection  or  bi-
opsy;  however,  some  imaging  characteristics  may  suggest  a
benign cause. There are usually multiple rhabdomyomas, and
they appear as homogeneous, hyperechoic, lobulated masses.
They  are  often  associated  with  tuberous  sclerosis  that  does
not calcify, and they tend to deform in the opposite direction
of the surrounding myocardium when evaluated with echo-
cardiography.  Rhabdomyoma  is  almost  always  diagnosed
prenatally or in early infancy (71) on the basis of clinical and
imaging features; thus, biopsy is not required for diagnosis. In
comparison,  fibromas  do  not  seem  to  deform  or  contract  in
any direction (10), are usually a single mass, and often show
central calcification (6,15).

Imaging findings are similar for IMTs and myxomas due to
their  pedunculated  appearance  (31).  Myxomas  appear  more
commonly  in  the  left  atrium  with  heterogeneous  delayed
enhancement, whereas the delayed enhancement in an IMT
can be persistent and intense. The possibility of a thrombus
must also be ruled out given the tendency of sporadic cardiac
myxomas to be multicentric and to develop in the left atrium.
However, although chronic thrombi may appear to have het-
erogeneous enhancement at their periphery, thrombi are less
likely to have the typical stalklike attachment to the interatrial
septum and the pedunculated morphology of myxomas.

Teratomas are heterogeneous masses that can contain fat
or calcification on images. A fat-fluid level or calcification in
the mass is diagnostic of a mature teratoma. Intracardiac ter-
atomas can be differentiated from other intracardiac tumors
due to their multilocular appearance. Fibromas may also con-
tain calcification but tend to be homogeneous in appearance
in comparison to the appearance of teratomas. Teratomas can
also be extracardiac tumors and can mimic other pericardial
tumors if they have a high fat content, including lipomas and

lipoblastomas. However, lipomas and lipoblastomas are typi-
cally more homogeneous.

The  differential  diagnoses  for  benign  vascular  masses
(hemangiomas)  include  malignant  vascular  masses  such  as
angiosarcomas and highly vascularized tumors such as para-
gangliomas.  Although  both  paragangliomas  and  hemangio-
mas  are  hyperintense  on T2-weighted  MR  images,  paragan-
gliomas  are  hypointense  on  T1-weighted  MR  images  (72).
Unlike hemangiomas, the majority of angiosarcomas occur in
the right atrium, and the majority of paragangliomas occur in
the roof of the left atrium.

Because  of  their  location,  left  atrial  sarcomas  can  mimic
the appearance of left atrial myxomas. If invasive features are
shown, heterogeneous areas of necrosis and hemorrhage are
noted,  and  no  stalk  is  identified,  a  primary  sarcoma  should
be suspected (9). Primary cardiac lymphomas are usually uni-
form tumors. Necrosis and involvement of the cardiac valves
are atypical and suggest an alternative diagnosis such as an-
giosarcoma, especially if it manifests in the right atrium (8).
Lymphomas are typically homogeneous and centered on the
atrioventricular grove, and they spare the right coronary ar-
tery.  Angiosarcomas  are  usually  centered  on  the  right  atrial
free  wall  and  have  a  heterogeneous  infiltrative  appearance,
with areas of necrosis or hemorrhage.

Mediastinal masses such as teratomas and thymolipomas
are masses that should be considered when evaluating peri-
cardial  lipomas  and  lipoblastomas.  Mediastinal  teratomas
have  fat  but  also  have  fluid-attenuation  cystic  components
and  calcification,  which  are  features  that  can  help  to  differ-
entiate  these  masses  from  lipomas  and  lipoblastomas.  Thy-
molipoma,  or  thymolipomatous  hamartoma,  is  a  fatty  mass
of the thymus that measures an average of 20 cm in diameter
at diagnosis. They have soft-tissue nodules and septa and are
connected to the thymic bed (73).

Diaphragmatic defects with the herniation of omental fat,
such as Morgagni hernias, are also a diagnostic consideration,
but multiplanar imaging and the presence of abdominal vas-
culature should allow differentiation of this diagnosis (62).

Conclusion
Primary cardiac tumors are rare in the pediatric population;
however,  it  is  important  for  radiologists  to  have  an  under-
standing  of  the  most  common  imaging  and  epidemiologic
characteristics. The  most  common  cardiac  mass  in  children
is  rhabdomyoma,  followed  by  fibroma.  Cardiac  tumors  in
children  are  frequently  associated  with  specific  syndromes,
allowing the narrowing of the differential diagnosis. With ad-
equate imaging techniques and knowledge of the pathologic
basis, it is often possible to differentiate between benign and
malignant neoplasms, which can greatly affect adequate and
timely treatment.
