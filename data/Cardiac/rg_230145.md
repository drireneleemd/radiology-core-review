Revisiting Coronary Artery Anomalies

Coronary artery anomalies (CAAs) are uncommon anatomic
variations that have the potential to be life-threatening. They
occur in about 1% of the general population but are often not
diagnosed or even suspected during a person’s lifetime due
to the lack or nonspecificity of symptoms and limitations of
routine diagnostic tests.

The clinical impact of CAAs, a heterogeneous group of con-
genital  disorders  related  to  varying  pathophysiologic  mech-
anisms,  depends  on  the  relationship  between  the  coronary
artery’s  function  and  its  ability  to  provide  adequate  blood
flow to the myocardium. While some forms of CAAs are be-
nign  and  mostly  diagnosed  incidentally,  others  can  cause
severe symptoms and even sudden cardiac death. Therefore,
the assessment of CAAs and prediction of their varied natural
prognoses can be challenging when solely relying on clinical
examination.

The  increasing  adoption  of  coronary  CT  angiography
(CTA)  as  a  valuable  diagnostic  modality  for  patients  with
chest pain has led to a higher incidence of detecting CAAs in
routine  clinical  practice.  In  this  context,  coronary  CTA  has
emerged as a pivotal imaging tool for detecting and charac-
terizing CAAs by providing optimal in vivo anatomic details
on  cross-sectional  and  multiplanar  reconstruction  images,
with  some  advantages  over  invasive  techniques.  Conven-
tionally,  catheter  angiography  for  evaluation  of  anomalous
vessels may not have been able to adequately define the pre-

cise  course  of  the  vessel  due  to  the  complex  three-dimen-
sional  geometry  being  visualized  in  two  dimensions  fluo-
roscopically.  Further,  coronary  CTA  can  delineate  different
anatomic  high-risk  features  that  may  lead  to  physiologic
high-risk consequences, such as interarterial course, slitlike
ostium, acute take-off angle, intramural course, elliptic ves-
sel shape, and proximal vessel narrowing.

More recently, the role of coronary CTA in the assessment
of CAAs has been further cemented by the latest technolog-
ical improvements in CT scanners. These advancements in-
clude  dual-source  technology,  faster  gantry  rotation  times,
new  cardiac  imaging  modalities,  and  the  contemporary  in-
troduction of machine learning–based fractional flow reserve
derived from CT.

In this slide presentation, a practical radiologic approach is
proposed  to  facilitate  understanding  of  CAAs  and  their  com-
mon and uncommon imaging features through the presenta-
tion of sample cases and an assortment of three-dimensional
reconstructions. Of note, a basic principle of coronary classifi-
cation should be that the nature and name of a specific coro-
nary artery are assigned, not according to the site of origin or
proximal course, but according to the dependent territory. The
authors provide a didactic approach to a proposed comprehen-
sive classification scheme for coronary artery anomalies, based
on four catgories: (a) origination and course, (b) intrinsic anato-
my, (c) termination, and (d) anastomotic vessels (Figure).

TEACHING POINTS
	 CAAs are rare and potentially life-threatening variants, rarely diagnosed or even suspected during life, with many differ-

ent entities and natural prognoses.

	 Coronary CTA has emerged as a cornerstone for noninvasive identification and characterization of CAAs and is useful for

catheter-based interventional therapy or surgical planning.

	 Coronary CTA can delineate different anatomic high-risk features that may lead to physiologic high-risk consequences,
such as interarterial course, slitlike ostium, acute take-off angle, intramural course, elliptic vessel shape, and proximal
vessel narrowing.
