Imaging plays a central role in the workup of thromboembolic
events and bleeding complications in patients treated with veno-
arterial extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO) (VA-
ECMO), and radiologists should be familiar with the expected he-
modynamic changes and flow-related artifacts associated with the
VA-ECMO system. VA-ECMO is a form of temporary mechanical
circulatory support for critically ill patients with acute, refrac-
tory cardiac or cardiopulmonary failure. As the use of VA-ECMO
continues to increase, it is important to be aware of associated
hemodynamic changes and challenges at imaging. Patients treated
with VA-ECMO are at high risk for thromboembolic events and
bleeding complications and, thus, often require evaluation with
CT angiography (CTA). VA-ECMO can be implemented by using
central or peripheral cannulation. The peripheral femorofemoral
VA-ECMO circuit in particular alters the sequence and direc-
tion of contrast medium enhancement substantially, resulting in
flow-related artifacts that can mimic or obscure disease at CTA.
Nonopacification can be mistaken for spurious thrombus or simu-
late complete vascular occlusion, while mixing artifacts can mimic
dissections. Misinterpretation of flow-related CTA artifacts can
lead to inappropriate surgical or medical intervention. A methodical
and multiphasic approach should be taken to CTA imaging strate-
gies and interpretation for patients treated with VA-ECMO. There
is no universal CTA protocol for patients on VA-ECMO. Each
protocol must be designed for the study indication, with consider-
ation of the configuration of the ECMO cannulas, contrast material
injection site, region of interest, native cardiac output, and ECMO
flow rate. The authors provide examples of common and unusual
VA-ECMO–related artifacts, with a focus on strategies for optimiz-
ing CTA image acquisition.

ECMO is a type of life support. The two
main ECMO strategies are venovenous ECMO
(VV-ECMO) and venoarterial ECMO (VA-
ECMO) cannulations. With VV-ECMO, blood is
withdrawn from the systemic veins, oxygenated,
and returned to the systemic veins to treat acute
respiratory failure. VA-ECMO is a type of tempo-
rary mechanical circulatory support for patients
with acute, refractory cardiac or cardiopulmonary
failure; it serves as a bridge to recovery, deci-
sion, or transplant (4–6). In addition to providing
gas exchange, VA-ECMO provides circulatory
support and bypasses the heart and lungs in the
setting of cardiogenic shock, with or without
respiratory failure.

While MRI of ECMO has been shown to be
feasible in an animal model (7), ECMO equip-
ment is not yet MRI compatible in clinical prac-
tice. Evaluation of ECMO-related complications—
such as bleeding, thrombosis, thromboembolism,
and extremity ischemia—often necessitates as-
sessment with CT angiography (CTA) (8,9). The
altered hemodynamics of the VA-ECMO circuit,
particularly with a peripheral cannulation strategy,
can substantially change the sequence and direc-
tion of contrast medium enhancement patterns,
resulting in myriad artifacts on CTA images. It is
important to anticipate and recognize these arti-
facts for accurate diagnosis.

Principles of CTA and ECMO
Circulation

VA-ECMO Circuit
The VA-ECMO circuit consists of a venous
inflow cannula, a pump, an oxygenator, and an
arterial outflow cannula. Numerous ECMO
cannulation strategies exist. VA-ECMO can be es-

tablished by means of central or peripheral access,
and peripheral cannulation is further divided into
femorofemoral and “sport” configurations (Figs
1–3) (5). It is important to recognize the different
types of cannulation and the different patterns of
blood flow with the cannulations, which inform
the imaging strategy.

Central VA-ECMO is usually established in
the operating room in a postcardiotomy patient
unable to wean off cardiopulmonary bypass. The
inflow and outflow cannulas are placed directly in
the right atrium and ascending aorta, respectively
(4). In this situation, the direction of reintro-
duced blood flow is antegrade like a physiologic
circuit. Central VA-ECMO is almost exclusively
reserved for patients undergoing cardiac surgery
with postcardiotomy cardiogenic shock, as it is
the most invasive of the cannulation strategies
and requires a sternotomy (6).

Peripheral VA-ECMO in the sport model in-
volves a right internal jugular venous inflow can-
nula and an arterial outflow graft to the right ax-
illary, subclavian, or brachiocephalic artery (10).
Although the cannulation is technically through
a peripheral artery, perfusion in the sport model
circuit is similar to that in central VA-ECMO,
with mainly antegrade flow to the aorta. This
configuration allows the patient to be ambulatory
while undergoing ECMO and maintains cerebral
oxygenation (5).

For peripheral VA-ECMO in the femorofemo-

ral configuration, a large-bore inflow cannula
withdraws blood from the systemic veins, and an
outflow cannula returns oxygenated blood to the
aorta in a retrograde direction (Fig 3). The femo-
ral artery is the most common vascular access site
for VA-ECMO in adults. With peripheral femo-
rofemoral VA-ECMO cannulation, oxygenated
blood flows in a retrograde (ie, caudal-to-cranial)
direction in the aorta. The advantage of this
cannulation strategy is that VA-ECMO can be
initiated outside of the operating room by using a
percutaneous or surgical cut-down approach (5).

To plan an appropriate CTA protocol, it
is important to have a basic understanding of
CTA scanning principles. A detailed overview is
beyond the scope of this article however, publica-
tions by Bae, Fleischmann et al, and Weininger et
al (11–15) provide comprehensive reviews of con-
trast medium pharmacokinetics and CT-related
factors. In the simplest terms, CTA is an orches-

acquisition in an anatomic region of interest. The
underlying principle is related to iodine caus-

results in an increase in CT attenuation. The
degree of contrast enhancement is directly related
to the iodine concentration.

cardiac output, vascular access, injection dura-

scanning delay, contribute to a diagnostic CTA
image. In the setting of peripheral femorofemo-

Pulmonary Artery Evaluation

Unique Challenges
Pulmonary artery evaluation is challenging in all
VA-ECMO cannulation configurations because
blood (and thus injected contrast material) by-
passes the pulmonary arteries, the vessel of inter-
est, by design. Being unaware of the alterations
in the sequence and timing of contrast enhance-
ment in VA-ECMO can lead to nondiagnostic
images (Fig 5).

Strategies for Optimization
Imaging strategies are aimed at minimizing the
diversion of contrast material (16–19). Lee and
Chaturvedi (17) recommended reducing the
pump flow rate to 500 mL/min or temporarily
disabling the pump entirely, if feasible, and con-
sidering manual triggering. This allows contrast
material to pass through the native circulation
and pulmonary arteries, which are otherwise
mainly bypassed by the peripheral femorofemoral
VA-ECMO circuit. Injecting through a central
venous catheter, if available, downstream of the
inflow cannula also can obviate this problem.

The central and peripheral sport models of
VA-ECMO cannulation pose the same scanning

problem owing to the positioning of the outflow
cannula downstream of the vessel of interest.
In these situations, optimal evaluation of the
pulmonary arteries would also involve decreasing
the ECMO flow rate to direct contrast material
through the native circulation.

Changing ECMO parameters should be
coordinated with and performed at the discre-
tion of the perfusionist and clinical team. Risks
include clinical instability, oxygen desaturation,
and thrombus formation. Thus, the ECMO flow
rate should be titrated to balance the diagnostic
quality of the CTA images with perfusion settings
that can be tolerated by the patient (16). ECMO
parameters should be returned to preimaging set-
tings as soon as possible.

In some situations, the patient cannot tolerate

ECMO flow changes. Some have suggested in-
jecting contrast material directly into the ECMO
outflow. However, this strategy would be futile for
pulmonary artery evaluation since the contrast
material would bypass the target vessel (Fig 5B).
A delayed phase relying on recirculation may be
considered, although evaluation of the distal pul-
monary arteries would remain a challenge.

Cardiac Chambers

Unique Challenges
Poor cardiac contractility and low arterial pulsatil-
ity may predispose patients to intracardiac throm-
bus formation due to stasis (5,20). At first-pass
CTA, the cardiac chambers may not be opacified
at the time of scanning if contrast material is pref-
erentially directed to the ECMO circuit, bypassing
the native circulation. This problem applies to all
configurations of VA-ECMO owing to the systemic
arterial positioning of the outflow cannula.

Abdominal Visceral Arteries

Unique Challenges
Evaluation of the abdominal visceral arteries in a
patient on peripheral femorofemoral VA-ECMO
poses the same challenges as evaluation of the
abdominal aorta. The delivery of contrast material
to the region of interest depends on whether the
contrast material will be preferentially directed to
the native circulation or the ECMO circuit, as well
as the location of the watershed area.

Strategies for Optimization
While direct injection of contrast medium into the
ECMO outflow cannula has been proposed (16),
this is not the practice at our institution because of
the risk of air emboli. In most cases, it is advis-
able to decrease the ECMO flow rate and perform
automated bolus tracking in the abdominal aorta
at the level of the region of interest; this will trigger
the scanning independent of its filling direction.

Perfusion CT is difficult to perform in patients

who have been treated with peripheral femoro-
femoral VA-ECMO. The processed perfusion CT
maps in Figure 17 show the apparent transit time
prolongation in tissue supplied by the relatively
less opacified circulation without a corresponding
large-vessel occlusion on CTA images. Perfu-
sion CT maps are composed of pixels calculated
from time-attenuation curves. The arterial input
function (AIF) selection affects the calculation of
mean transit time (MTT) and time-to-maximum
(Tmax) based on the assumption that the AIF
represents the exact input to a given tissue. Thus,
selecting a vessel supplied by ECMO as the AIF
results in artifactual prolongation of the cal-
culated transit time in the right cerebral hemi-
sphere owing to a relatively diminished iodine
concentration.

Strategies for Optimization
The most reliable way of minimizing this pe-
ripheral femorofemoral VA-ECMO artifact is
to decrease the ECMO flow rate to direct more
contrast material antegrade through the heart,
aortic arch, and supra-aortic vessels. Monitoring
in the skull base with a manual trigger is also more
reliable than standard bolus-tracking techniques

with a region of interest in the ascending aorta or
aortic arch (23).

Perfusion CT in the setting of peripheral femo-
rofemoral VA-ECMO is feasible with use of delay-
corrected deconvolution software or the selection
of separate AIFs for time-attenuation curves for
each vascular territory (25). Due to many chal-
lenges, at our institution, perfusion CT is not rou-
tinely performed in patients treated with ECMO.
Perfusion CT is feasible in central and peripheral
sport model VA-ECMO configurations since the
arch vessels have the same antegrade supply.

Extremities

Unique Challenges
One of the complications of peripheral femoro-
femoral VA-ECMO is acute limb ischemia sec-
ondary to hemodynamic instability and arterial
occlusion from the outflow cannula. This ischemia
often necessitates evaluation with CTA (26). The
timing of CTA of the lower extremities in a patient
treated with peripheral femorofemoral VA-ECMO
cannulation is challenging because of the difficulty
in selecting an appropriate trigger. If a region of
interest is placed too distant from the iliofemoral
arteries, the images may be nondiagnostic. An
extreme example is shown in Figure 18A, in which
placement of a region of interest in the descending
thoracic aorta over an intra-aortic balloon pump
caused a false trigger.

CTA of VV-ECMO
Apart from impaired pulmonary oxygenation,
cardiovascular function in the setting of VV-
ECMO is typically normal. In addition, con-
trast material is siphoned from and returned
to the same location (systemic veins) without
bypassing the native circuit. Therefore, CTA of
VV-ECMO can be performed by using routine
protocols.

Conclusion

CTA evaluation of patients treated with VA-
ECMO is logistically and diagnostically challeng-
ing. Awareness of the hemodynamic alterations
is essential for CTA protocol planning and image
interpretation. Each protocol must be designed
for the study indication, with consideration of the
configuration of ECMO cannulas, contrast injec-
tion site, region of interest, native cardiac output,
and ECMO flow rate. In this article, we describe
potential imaging strategies from head to toe, as
CTA may be the last resort for evaluation of a
critically ill patient.
