Cardiac tumors, although rare, carry high morbidity and mortality rates. They are commonly first identified either at echo-
cardiography or incidentally at thoracoabdominal CT performed for noncardiac indications. Multimodality imaging often
helps to determine the cause of these masses. Cardiac tumors comprise a distinct category in the World Health Organization
(WHO) classification of tumors. The updated 2021 WHO classification of tumors of the heart incorporates new entities and
reclassifies others. In the new classification system, papillary fibroelastoma is recognized as the most common primary cardi-
ac neoplasm. Pseudotumors including thrombi and anatomic variants (eg, crista terminalis, accessory papillary muscles, or
coumadin ridge) are the most common intracardiac masses identified at imaging. Cardiac metastases are substantially more
common than primary cardiac tumors. Although echocardiography is usually the first examination, cardiac MRI is the mo-
dality of choice for the identification and characterization of cardiac masses. Cardiac CT serves as an alternative in patients
who cannot tolerate MRI. PET performed with CT or MRI enables metabolic characterization of malignant cardiac masses.
Imaging individualized to a particular tumor type and location is crucial for treatment planning. Tumor terminology changes
as our understanding of tumor biology and behavior evolves. Familiarity with the updated classification system is important
as a guide to radiologic investigation and medical or surgical management.

Introduction
Cardiac  tumors  comprise  a  distinct  category  in  the  World
Health  Organization  (WHO)  2021  classification  system  for
cardiac  tumors  (1).  Primary  cardiac  tumors  are  rare,  with
metastatic involvement of the heart being 20–30 times more
prevalent than primary cardiac tumors (1–4). Approximately
75%–90% of all primary cardiac tumors are benign (3,4). The
most common tumors to metastasize to the heart are thoracic
malignancies, melanomas, extracardiac sarcomas, and renal
cell carcinomas (2). Irrespective of histologic benignity, these
tumors can have devastating clinical consequences due to in-
terference  with  the  mechanical  or  electrical  function  of  the
heart (1). The most common intracardiac “lesions” are throm-
bi or normal anatomic variants that can mimic and should be
distinguished from tumors (5).

Most cardiac tumors are asymptomatic and are often first
identified  with  two-dimensional  transthoracic  echocardi-
ography (TTE). They can also be incidentally seen on thora-
coabdominal CT images obtained for noncardiac indications
such as trauma, acute thoracic or abdominal emergencies, or
staging of known extracardiac tumors. Multimodality imag-
ing is thus often necessary to help determine the cause. Ad-
ditional  imaging  modalities  include  TTE,  transesophageal
echocardiography  (TEE),  electrocardiographic  (ECG)–gat-
ed  contrast-enhanced  CT  angiography,  contrast-enhanced
MRI, and PET. Diagnostic chest CT or MRI can be performed
at the time of a PET scan in a single imaging session (6). This
article discusses the 2021 WHO classification system for tu-
mors of the heart, emphasizing recent changes in terminol-
ogy, and provides an overview of individual tumor entities,
multimodality imaging attributes, and correlative patholog-
ic characteristics (1).

2021 WHO Classification of Cardiac Tumors:
Changes from Prior Edition
Major differences in cardiac tumors between the 2015 WHO
classification system for tumors of the heart and the updat-
ed  2021  WHO  classification  system  are  summarized  in  Ta-
bles S1 and S2 (1,2,7). Under the revised WHO classification
system, papillary fibroelastoma has been recognized as the
most  common  primary  cardiac  neoplasm.  This  change  is
likely due to both increased use of imaging and technologi-
cal advances (1).

New  entities  added  to  the  classification  system  include
hamartomatous  lesions  such  as  lipomatous  hamartoma  of
the atrioventricular valve and mesenchymal cardiac hamar-
toma (1). Histiocytoid cardiomyopathy is also now described
as a conduction system hamartoma.

Advances in molecular techniques have improved our un-
derstanding of certain tumors. A subset of papillary fibroelas-
tomas can have KRAS driver mutations indicative of their neo-
plastic nature. Some cases of lipomatous hypertrophy of the
atrial septum harbor molecular genetic alterations similar to
those of lipomas, suggesting a possible clonal process and not
simply  a  relationship  with  displaced  fat  during  embryogen-
esis. New ancillary tools allow screening for underlying and
associated syndromes, such as for Carney complex in patients
with  cardiac  myxoma.  The  key  clinical  and  pathologic  fea-
tures, treatment, outcomes, and follow-up of these lesions are
summarized in Tables S3 and S4.

Clinical Presentation
The presentation of patients with cardiac tumors depends on
tumor  size,  location,  and  type  (1,3,4,8–10).  Clinical  features
are  often  nonspecific.  Many  patients  are  asymptomatic  and
their tumors are incidentally identified on images. Symptoms
can manifest when tumors interfere physically (eg, arrhyth-
mias from tumors affecting the conduction system). Cardiac
tumors can obstruct outflow and embolize to the systemic or
pulmonary circulation. Some tumors release substances that
can give rise to constitutional symptoms such as fever, mal-
aise, and weight loss.

Evaluating Cardiac Tumors with MRI
Cardiac MRI is the diagnostic test of  choice to characterize
cardiac  masses.  It  provides  information  about  mass  mor-
phology,  location,  extent,  and  functional  effects.  MRI  fea-
tures can help to distinguish benign from malignant tumors
on the basis of morphology, the presence or absence of per-
fusion  and  late  gadolinium  enhancement  (LGE),  T1-  and
T2-weighted  signal  intensity  and  mapping,  fat  saturation,
and the presence of associated pericardial effusion (4). Spe-
cific MRI characteristics can be predictive of a particular di-
agnosis or for differential diagnosis. For example, increased
vascularity  and  hyperenhancement  are  characteristics  of
hypervascular tumors. In addition, ancillary suspicious fea-
tures such as pericardial effusion and lung metastases can
be crucial in establishing the diagnosis of malignant tumors
such as sarcomas (15,16). The common MRI sequences that
are useful in characterizing cardiac tumors are summarized
in Table S7.

Imaging Cardiac Tumors with PET/CT or PET/MRI
Fluorine 18 (18F) fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) PET/CT has been
shown to provide incremental diagnostic information in the
determination of malignancy and staging of cardiac tumors.

FDG  PET/CT  also  assists  in  differentiating  benign  from
malignant  tumors  and  is  useful  for  the  detection  of  distant
metastases  (17).  The  use  of  specific  radiopharmaceuticals
such as gallium 68 (68Ga) DOTATATE PET further allows the
characterization of neuroendocrine tumors such as paragan-
gliomas with a high degree of certainty (18,19).

Integrated PET/MRI has emerged as a hybrid modality with
the advantages of using MRI and PET radiopharmaceuticals.
Its use is promising in the diagnosis and follow-up of cardiac
tumors because it provides a combination of superior charac-
terization  of  tumor  morphology  and  evaluation  of  metabolic
activity. Its diagnostic capability can be enhanced by perform-
ing cardiac MRI at the same time as the PET study (20).

PET/CT, because of its wide availability, is commonly used
in  the  metabolic  evaluation  of  cardiac  neoplasms  and  other
entities such as infectious and inflammatory diseases.

Protocol for Cardiac FDG PET/CT or PET/MRI
Patient preparation is key in FDG cardiac PET/CT to suppress
the normally high physiologic FDG uptake in the myocardium.
A  high-fat,  high-protein,  low-carbohydrate  diet  and  fasting
4–12 hours before  18F-FDG PET is recommended (21). This in-
creases the level of free fatty acids in the bloodstream, reducing

Differentiation of Benign from Malignant Tumors
with Imaging
Imaging is key to differentiating benign from malignant cardi-
ac tumors. Benign lesions are commonly single and small and
often have a narrow attachment to the myocardium, sometimes
a thin stalk, enabling mobility as captured with cine imaging.
The  absence  of  myocardial  infiltration,  pericardial  effusion,
and  signs  of  metastatic  disease  favor  benignancy.  Malignant
lesions tend to be large at the time of diagnosis, have a broad
base, infiltrate the myocardium, are associated with pericardial
effusions,  and  demonstrate  locoregional  or  distant  metasta-
sis.  Yin  et  al  (22)  described  a  higher  maximum  standardized
uptake value in malignant tumors. They suggest a maximum
standardized uptake value cutoff of 3.8 for the diagnosis of car-
diac malignancy (sensitivity, 96.0%; specificity, 93.9%,) at PET/
CT (22).

Biopsy for Pathologic Assessment
Histologic  examination  remains  the  standard  of  care  for
the  definitive  diagnosis  of  a  cardiac  mass.  Multimodality
imaging  allows  detailed  characterization  of  the  mass  and
demonstration  of  metastatic  lesions.  Benign  lesions  such  as
fibroelastomas and myxomas are typically treated surgically.
Extracardiac metastatic lesions may be amenable to CT-guid-
ed percutaneous biopsy. Transvascular biopsy under TTE or
TEE guidance is a useful alternative to obtain tissue when an
extracardiac  percutaneous  biopsy  is  not  feasible.  The  deci-
sion and approach to biopsy are often determined by an insti-
tutional multidisciplinary cancer care board (Fig 1).

Benign Tumors

Papillary Fibroelastomas
Papillary fibroelastoma is the most common benign cardiac
tumor.  This  endocardial  neoplasm  arises  on  the  surface  of
diseased  valves,  most  commonly  the  aortic  valve  (1).  Unlike
vegetations,  fibroelastomas  are  located  downstream  of  the
valve (ie, the aortic side of the aortic valve and the left ventric-
ular side of the mitral valve) (3). Approximately 20% affect the
nonvalvular endocardial surfaces (23). Multifocal lesions are
particularly common after iatrogenic trauma and rheumatic
valve disease (1). Papillary fibroelastoma is a rare complica-
tion of radiation therapy or may arise after long-standing me-
chanical damage or degeneration of native valves (24).

Fibroelastomas range in size from 0.2 to 0.7 cm and are of-
ten attached to the endocardium by a stalk. Patients with fibro-
elastomas may be asymptomatic, may present with heart fail-
ure and embolic symptoms, and may experience sudden death.
At  CT,  fibroelastomas  appear  as  small  lobulated  mobile
masses  attached  to  the  valve,  often  by  a  thin  stalk  (Fig  2A).
Most fibroelastomas are diagnosed with TTE (Fig 2B). While
similar morphology may be seen on CT images, the superior
temporal resolution of TTE has advantages, and CT and MRI
are used for inconclusive or atypical cases (16).

At  MRI,  fibroelastomas  are  round  homogeneous  mass-
es,  sometimes  with  a  small  pedicle  attached  to  the  valve.
T1-weighted  MR  images  show  intermediate  intensity,  and
T2-weighted MR images may show hypointensity (with high
fibrous content) (3) or hyperintensity (16), with variable gado-
linium enhancement (9). Fibroelastomas can have mild FDG
avidity,  although  PET/CT  or  PET/MRI  is  not  routinely  per-
formed for further characterization (17).

Histologically, fibroelastomas consist of narrow avascular
papillary fronds exhibiting complex branching (Fig 2C). The
central collagen and elastic fiber arrangements are similar to
those of the atrioventricular valves, which can be delineated
with an elastic stain.

The differential diagnosis of fibroelastomas includes eval-
uation for valvular vegetation, thrombus, and cardiac myxo-
ma. Surgery is indicated for lesions measuring greater than 1
cm (3). Follow-up imaging is recommended after surgery to
evaluate for residual disease or recurrence (25).

Cardiac Myxomas
Cardiac myxomas are now recognized as the second most com-
mon primary cardiac tumor (1). They are benign intracavitary
endocardial tumors that may occur in all cardiac chambers, but
most commonly the left atrium (80%–90%), attached by a stalk
to  the  fossa  ovalis.  Myxomas  rarely  originate  from  the  valves
(1). Most cardiac myxomas are sporadic (90%), manifesting as
a solitary lesion. A myxoma location outside of the left atrium,
young patient age, and multiplicity of lesions are signs of famil-
ial cardiac myxomas that can manifest as part of a syndrome
(Carney complex, LAMB [lentigines, atrial myxoma, mucocu-
taneous myxomas, blue nevi], or NAME [nevi, atrial myxoma,
myxoid neurofibromas, ephelides, or freckles] syndrome) or as
nonsyndromic familial cardiac myxomas (26).

Multicentric  myxomas  can  be  present  with  Carney  com-
plex (<10% of cardiac myxomas) (1,27). Their size ranges from
less than 1 cm to greater than 15 cm. They may be pedunculat-
ed (arising from a stalk) or sessile and broad based (28).

Cardiac  myxomas  are  often  asymptomatic  and  are  identi-
fied  incidentally  at  imaging  performed  for  other  indications
(1,16,28).  They  can  also  manifest  with  cardiac  symptoms  (or
sudden  death),  embolic  phenomena,  or  constitutional  symp-
toms.

At  TTE  and  TEE,  cardiac  myxomas  are  usually  heteroge-
neous  intracavitary  mobile  masses  with  an  irregular  shape
and  variable  echogenicity  and  may  contain  calcifications
(29,30). At CT (Fig 3A), myxomas have heterogeneous attenua-
tion when compared with the myocardium. They may contain
calcifications (coarse or punctate) (27). MRI cine bright-blood
sequences can show attachment, tumor mobility (Fig 3B), and
prolapse into the atrioventricular valve, which may cause ob-
struction of the valve (16). They are typically heterogeneous,
with intermediate signal intensity on T1-weighted MR images
and hyperintensity on T2-weighted MR images (Fig 3C) (16).
This heterogeneity is due to a combination of calcification and
hemorrhage  (subacute  or  chronic),  necrotic  elements,  and
tumoral tissue (9). LGE MR images show heterogeneous en-
hancement (Fig 3D) (8,9,16). A surface thrombus may be pres-
ent in a cardiac myxoma, with the thrombus demonstrating

Cardiac Fibromas
Cardiac fibroma is the second most common pediatric cardi-
ac tumor (32). It is most commonly encountered in children
younger than 10 years of age (33), with a predilection for pa-
tients  with  Gorlin  syndrome  or  basal  cell  nevus  syndrome.
One-third of cases are asymptomatic. Symptoms, when pres-
ent, range from arrhythmias to sudden death (Table S3).

On  echocardiograms,  cardiac  fibromas  are  solid,  non-
contractile,  and  heterogeneous  masses.  Calcifications  are
a  pathognomonic  feature.  On  MR  images,  tumors  are  in-
tramyocardial,  commonly  located  in  the  ventricular  sep-
tum  or  the  free  ventricular  wall  (an  exception  to  the  nar-
row  base  described  for  other  benign  cardiac  tumors),  with
well-defined  borders.  Tumors  have  heterogeneous  signal
intensity on T1- and T2-weighted MR images (Fig 4A), show
hypointensity on first-pass myocardial perfusion MR imag-

es (Fig S2), and demonstrate delayed gadolinium enhance-

Cardiac Rhabdomyomas
Rhabdomyomas  account  for  more  than  60%  of  all  pediatric
primary cardiac tumors (35). They are benign, are considered
hamartomas, and are strongly associated with tuberous scle-
rosis (36). Their appearance may precede the development of
skin and neurologic signs of tuberous sclerosis by months or
years.  Tumors  are  multiple  in  more  than  60%  of  cases  (37),
especially  when  they  are  associated  with  tuberous  sclerosis
(38).  Most  patients  remain  asymptomatic,  and  spontaneous
regression during childhood is common (39). A small propor-
tion of tumors may manifest with cardiac complications.

Adult cellular rhabdomyoma is a distinct entity in the new
classification system. This benign neoplasm is seen in adults
older than 40 years of age, most commonly in the right atrium.
A  substantial  proportion  of  pediatric  rhabdomyomas  are
diagnosed at prenatal US or fetal echocardiography. On echo-
cardiograms,  rhabdomyomas  are  solid  and  hyperechoic.

Cardiac Lipomas and Lipomatous Hypertrophy of the
Atrial Septum
Cardiac lipomas are benign mesenchymal tumors of mature
adipocytes. They are most often epicardial but can be intra-
cavitary, intramyocardial, or pericardial and can involve any
cardiac  chamber.  They  are  smooth  homogeneously  fatty

masses (Fig S3) that may be sessile or mobile depending on
the location. When they are near the interatrial septum, they
should be differentiated from lipomatous hypertrophy of the
atrial septum. Lipomatous hypertrophy of the atrial septum
has  a  characteristic  dumbbell  shape,  measures  20  mm  in
thickness, and spares the fossa ovalis (Fig 6A).

On CT images, cardiac lipoma demonstrates homogeneous
fat attenuation. On MR images, lipomas demonstrate typical
fat signal intensity, high signal intensity in comparison with
the myocardium on T1- and T2-weighted MR images, and sig-
nal  intensity  decrease  on  fat-saturated  MR  images.  There  is
minimal if any enhancement after administration of contrast
material and no LGE. The presence of enhancing soft tissue in
a fatty mass should raise concern for a complex lipoma or li-
posarcoma (16). Lipomatous hypertrophy of the atrial septum
often  contains  metabolically  active  brown  fat,  which  can  be
FDG-avid on PET images (Fig 6B). Cardiac lipomas and lipo-
matous hypertrophy of the atrial septum require no further
workup.

Other Benign Cardiac Tumors
A hamartoma of mature cardiac myocytes is a benign growth
of differentiated mature striated cardiac myocytes. These can
mimic hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.

Hemangiomas  are  vascular  tumors  that  can  be  capillary,
cavernous,  or  arteriovenous.  Hemangiomas  can  demonstrate
early enhancement and a tumor blush at coronary angiography
(42). Hemangiomas can be intermediate to hyperintense to the
myocardium on T1-weighted MR images and diffusely hyperin-
tense on T2-weighted MR images and can show heterogeneous
contrast enhancement on first-pass perfusion MR images (43).
A conduction system hamartoma consists of a multifocal pro-
liferation of Purkinje or Purkinje-like cells (Fig 7).

Primary Cardiac Paragangliomas
Primary cardiac paragangliomas are neuroendocrine tumors
that  rarely  arise  in  the  heart  (1%–3%  of  primary  cardiac  tu-
mors) (2). They most commonly arise in the left atrium (55%),
followed by the aortic body, interatrial septum (16%), and the
atrioventricular groove or anterior surface of the heart (10%).
Approximately one-third are of familial origin, where there is
an  association  between  succinate  dehydrogenase  deficiency

and  the  epigenome  mutations  in  all  four  subunits  of  succi-
nate  dehydrogenase  (A,  B,  C,  and  D)  (47).  Carney-Stratakis
syndrome (autosomal dominant: paraganglioma, and gastro-
intestinal stromal tumors), and the Carney triad (nonheredi-
tary, more common in female patients: paragangliomas, pul-
monary  chondromas,  and  gastrointestinal  stromal  tumors)
have been described.

Paragangliomas may be well encapsulated or infiltrative at
gross  pathologic  examination.  They  are  highly  vascularized
ovoid or round tumors, often known to parasitize blood flow
from  the  coronary  arteries,  primarily  the  right  coronary  ar-
tery  and  secondarily  the  left  circumflex  artery.  Patients  can
have a wide range of presentations, with symptoms depend-
ing on whether the tumor is secretory (ie, noradrenergic, ad-
renergic, and dopaminergic) or nonsecretory (48). The most
common  presenting  symptoms  are  those  of  catecholamine
excess  (ie,  palpitations,  hypertension,  headaches,  sweating,
and anxiety).

and relatively immobile, with echogenicity depending on the
presence of hemorrhage or cystic changes. On noncontrast CT
images, they are usually heterogeneous hypoattenuating le-
sions  (3);  after  administration  of  contrast  material,  they  are
circumscribed  and  have  heterogeneous  intense  enhance-
ment. Paragangliomas may have ill-defined borders and ext-
racardiac extension. Paragangliomas are typically isointense

Malignant Tumors

Cardiac Angiosarcomas
Angiosarcoma  is  the  most  common  cardiac  sarcoma,  ac-
counting  for  one-third  of  all  primary  cardiac  sarcomas  in
surgical  studies  (1,3).  Angiosarcomas  are  more  common  in

men than they are in women and have a peak incidence in
the  4th  decade  of  life.  They  appear  as  a  well-defined  mass
originating from the anterolateral free wall of the right atri-
um near the atrioventricular groove (16), sparing the inter-
atrial septum, or as a diffusely infiltrative mass that extends
into the adjacent right ventricle, tricuspid valve, or vena cava
or invades the pericardium. Patients present with right-side
heart  failure  or  hemopericardium  and  tamponade.  These
rapidly growing tumors often undergo necrosis and hemor-
rhage and commonly metastasize to the lungs. The cause is
unknown in most cases, but they may be secondary to chest
irradiation  and  may  be  seen  in  families  with  Li-Fraume-
ni–like syndrome. At karyotyping, trisomy 8 and trisomy 17
have been identified.

Angiosarcomas  are  heterogeneously  enhancing  noncalci-
fied masses originating from the free wall of the right atrium.
If  central  cavitation  is  present,  they  can  communicate  with
the  right  atrial  cavity,  forming  a  pseudoaneurysm  (Fig  10A,
Movie 1). Coronary artery encasement is a rare but potentially
life-threatening  complication  of  cardiac  angiosarcomas  (51).
Dual-energy CT with acquisition of delayed phase images is
useful  in  differentiating  an  enhancing  mass  from  a  throm-
bus  formed  in  a  pseudoaneurysm.  Cardiac  angiosarcomas
have  heterogeneous  signal  intensity  on  T1-  and  T2-weight-
ed  MR  images,  with  strong  contrast  enhancement  on  gad-
olinium-enhanced  MR  images  (4)  (Figs  10B,  S6).  Diagnosis
is made on the basis of biopsy results (3). FDG PET has high
sensitivity and accuracy in staging of angiosarcomas and can
help to identify distal metastasis that may be more amenable
to biopsy (Fig 10C).

Hematolymphoid Tumors
Primary cardiac lymphoma primarily affects the heart, with
no  or  minimal  nodal  involvement,  most  commonly  diffuse
large B-cell lymphoma. Primary cardiac lymphomas account
for  approximately  1%  of  primary  cardiac  tumors  and  0.5%
of extranodal lymphomas. They affect men twice as often as
they do women, most frequently in those older than 60 years.
The  most  common  sites  are  the  right  atrium  and  pericardi-
um. Cardiac lymphomas can involve any chamber or multiple
chambers, but they only rarely affect the valves.

Diagnosis  can  be  made  by  sampling  pericardial  fluid  for
cytologic evaluation or by obtaining an endomyocardial biop-
sy if no suspicious extracardiac lesion is present. Most often,
cardiac  involvement  occurs  secondarily  as  a  component  of
systemic disease in up to 20% of patients with lymphoma.

Imaging  findings  are  variable  and  range  from  highly  in-
filtrative myocardial thickening to an intracavitary mass. On
MR images, cardiac lymphomas demonstrate restricted diffu-
sion, various degrees of T2 hyperintensity, and intense con-
trast  enhancement  (Fig  13).  LGE  MR  images  may  show  het-
erogeneous enhancement (16,57). At pathologic examination,
diffuse sheets of neoplastic cells that may be positive for CD20
(Fig  S9)  may  be  seen.  Extranodal  spread  of  lymphoma  may
also involve the heart (Fig S10).

Cardiac Metastases
Cardiac metastases are far more common than primary car-
diac tumors and are found in up to 10% of oncologic patients
at autopsy (2). Any malignancy with metastatic potential may
metastasize to the heart. Approximately one-third of all cases
involve the pericardium, the most common site. Often, peri-
cardial effusion is the only manifestation of cardiac metastat-
ic disease (58).

The most common primary tumors to metastasize to the
heart are lung cancer (accounting for 36%–39% of cardiac me-
tastases). At autopsy, cardiac involvement is present in 10%–
21% of hematologic malignancies, 10%–12% of breast cancers,
and 28%–56% of melanoma cases (Fig 14). Other tumors that

Pseudotumors

Thrombi
Cardiac  tumors  should  be  distinguished  from  thrombi,
which  occur  due  to  thrombotic  disorders,  stasis,  turbulent
flow, or implanted material such as catheters. Thrombi may
form in the left atrium, commonly in the left atrial append-
age in patients with atrial fibrillation or valvular stenosis. A
left ventricular thrombus may form near the scar of a myo-
cardial  infarct  or  near  the  apex  in  dilated  cardiomyopathy.
Catheter-associated  thrombi  can  be  seen  in  the  right  atri-
um  (Fig  17).  Hong  et  al  (14)  used  ECG-gated  dual-energy  CT
and demonstrated that the diagnostic performance of iodine
concentration was higher than that of postcontrast attenua-
tion  measurements  for  distinguishing  cardiac  tumors  from
thrombi. Chronic thrombi may calcify. A thrombus is homo-
geneously hypointense on LGE MR images. A long inversion
time (approximately 550 msec at 1.5 T and 850 msec at 3 T) can
help in the diagnosis of an avascular thrombus (59).

Bandlike Structures
Bandlike structures may be seen in the heart and occasionally
may mimic tumors (60). The crista terminalis is a cord of nor-
mal smooth muscle in the craniocaudal dimension extending
from the superior vena cava to the inferior vena cava (Fig 18).
Aberrant papillary muscle can appear as a filling defect on con-
trast-enhanced MR and CT images (Fig 19), with the same at-
tenuation or signal intensity as that of the myocardium. A cou-
madin ridge, which is formed by the confluence of the long axis
of the left upper pulmonary vein with the roof of the left atrial
appendage, may be misdiagnosed as a mass or a thrombus.

Pseudoaneurysms
Ventricular  pseudoaneurysms  usually  develop  as  a  compli-
cation  of  a  large  myocardial  infarction  with  the  rupture  con-
tained  by  the  pericardium  with  adhesions.  They  manifest  as
cardiac masses on echocardiograms or noncontrast CT images.
CT angiography and MRI allow identification of the pseudoan-
eurysm, its neck, and any thrombus in its lumen (Fig 20).

Conclusion
Cardiac tumors are rare but have high morbidity and mortali-
ty rates. They are identified and characterized at multimodal-
ity imaging. The updated 2021 WHO classification of tumors
of the heart incorporates new entities and reclassifies others.
