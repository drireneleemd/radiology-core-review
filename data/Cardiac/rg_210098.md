Acute aortic syndrome (AAS) is classically attributed to three
underlying pathologic conditions—aortic dissection (AD), intra-
mural hematoma (IMH), and penetrating atherosclerotic ulcer
(PAU). In the majority of cases, the basics of image interpretation
are not difficult and have been extensively reviewed in the litera-
ture. In this article, the authors extend existing imaging overviews
of AAS by highlighting additional factors related to the diagnosis,
classification, and characterization of difficult AAS cases. It has
been well documented that AAS is caused not only by an AD
but by a spectrum of lesions that often have overlap in imaging
features and are not clearly distinguishable. Specifically, phase of
contrast enhancement, flow artifacts, and flapless AD equivalents
can complicate diagnosis and are discussed. While the A/B dichot-
omy of the Stanford system is still used, the authors subsequently
emphasize the Society for Vascular Surgery’s new guidelines for
the description of acute aortic pathologic conditions given the
expanded use of endovascular techniques used in aortic repair. In
the final section, atypical aortic rupture and pitfalls are described.
As examples of pericardial and shared sheath rupture become
more prevalent in the literature, it is important to recognize con-
trast material third-spacing and mediastinal blood as potential
mimics. By understanding these factors related to difficult cases
of AAS, the diagnostic radiologist will be able to accurately refine
CT interpretation and thus provide information that is best suited
to directing management.

Introduction

To facilitate early clinical suspicion for nontraumatic acute aortic
pathologic conditions, the term acute aortic syndrome (AAS) was
introduced in 2001 to describe a group of patients with hypertension
who presented with similar intense, acute, throbbing, and/or migratory
chest or back pain—so-called aortic pain. In this original description,
AAS was attributed to three underlying causes: aortic dissection (AD),
intramural hematoma (IMH), or penetrating atherosclerotic ulcer
(PAU) (1). The term AAS and its association with these conditions has
propagated, and it is now often used to describe the group of patho-
logic conditions themselves rather than the clinical syndrome (2–4).

As a result of its wide availability, rapid ac-

multidetector CT has become the primary means
of evaluation for suspected noncardiac causes of
chest pain in patients presenting to the emergency

sensitivities and specificities near 100% (5).
Despite this data, a recent study of patients with
Medicare claims determined that 4.5% of ADs
were missed in the emergency department (6,7).
While some patients may have presented with
a clinical picture that did not warrant imaging
at initial presentation, others did not receive an
accurate diagnosis despite prompt acquisition of
a CT examination. Indeed, missed acute aortic
pathologic conditions at CT have been observed
in our own practice, both in patients initially
presenting to our institution and in patients trans-
ferred to our quaternary care center. In addition
to the rare completely missed diagnoses, cases of
nearly missed or poorly described acute aortic
pathologic conditions have also been encountered.
To make matters more concerning, we have ob-
served that the majority of these difficult diagno-

ses involve the ascending aorta, in which a missed
or delayed diagnosis is more likely to result in a
deleterious outcome.

Many authors have provided excellent over-
views of imaging of AAS, including recent publi-

purpose of this article is to extend this work and
highlight additional issues encountered in difficult
AAS cases. Broadly, these are divided into factors
related to diagnosis, classification, and rupture.
For diagnosis, important concepts described in-

contrast enhancement, and flow artifacts. Subse-
quently, issues with classification and the need for
precise communication are addressed. In the final
section, difficulties in characterizing aortic rupture
are reviewed on the basis of our own experience
with AAS. We hope that this article will accelerate
the reader’s practice and refine CT interpretation
of difficult AAS cases.

The Difficult AAS Diagnosis

Fundamentally, acute aortic pathologic condi-
tions involve disruption of the aortic media.
Most often, this is due to an intimomedial tear
that occurs on the luminal side of the aortic wall
(10,11). When blood has access to the media, a
false lumen, separated by an intimomedial flap,
can be created. This is the pathophysiologic basis
for AD, the most common acute aortic patho-
logic condition, accounting for between 70% and
95% of cases of AAS (12–14). The best available
data have demonstrated that CT is 98%–100%
sensitive and 98% specific for the diagnosis of
AD (5). CT is particularly well suited to help in
the diagnosis of AD as it can help identify the
false lumen, the intimomedial flap, and the entry
tear, which is the most proximal site of communi-
cation between the true and false lumen.

As has been well documented, however, AAS

is caused not only by AD but rather by a spec-
trum of lesions (6,8,9,15,16). The various types
of intimal tears that can cause aortic pathologic
conditions were further subclassified by Svensson
and later adopted into the multispecialty Ameri-
can Heart Association guidelines in 2010 (15,16)
(Table). Class I–IV intimomedial defects arise in
the absence of a known external force, while class
V lesions are due to iatrogenic (typically vascular
catheterization) or traumatic causes. Class I inti-
momedial defects describe classic AD and result
in the “double-barreled” aortic lumen with true
and false lumina separated by an intimomedial
flap. Class II lesions, known as IMHs, are char-
acterized by hematoma in the aortic wall without
a detectable tear or intraluminal flap (13,15). By
virtue of improved preoperative imaging and the
results of autopsy studies, it is now known that

of IMH are relatively straightforward but may
be subtle and include hyperattenuating typically
crescentic thickening of the aortic wall that is often
most easily appreciated on noncontrast acquisi-

cause of AAS that will certainly be encountered by
the radiologist in practice, the performance of CT
in the diagnosis of IMH is not known. This is in
part owing to its relatively decreased frequency in
comparison with classic AD, as well as the over-

the two conditions (21). It should be known that
in 2021, with the advent of modern CT scan-

acute aortic lesions, and the final determination of
whether a lesion is in class I or class II may not be
satisfactorily resolved either in the operating room
or with pathology specimens (22,23). As such,
essentially all radiology publications on IMH have
focused on its imaging features with the purported
goal of helping radiologists not miss these lesions.
This is of particular importance given that IMH is
typically managed in the same fashion as AD, with
lesions involving the ascending aorta managed
surgically, while lesions involving the aorta distal
to the brachiocephalic artery are managed with
combinations of medical, surgical, and endovas-

cular techniques dependent on patient-specific
factors and the presence of complications (24).

Class IV lesions, otherwise known as PAUs,

involve the ulceration of an atherosclerotic
plaque into the media. This can result in a lo-
cal pseudoaneurysm or longitudinal propaga-
tion in the form of an AD or IMH (10). While
PAUs are lumped together with AD and IMH
as causes of AAS, it is important to recognize
that the overwhelming majority of PAUs do not
result in AAS or clinical symptoms (12). Given
that most patients with PAU are asymptomatic,
it is often impossible to know whether an identi-
fied PAU is the cause of a patient’s symptoms.
While PAUs associated with AD or IMH are
managed more aggressively and on the basis of
the classification of the AD or IMH, the deci-
sion of whether to treat isolated class IV lesions
in patients with suspected AAS often becomes
controversial in real practice, with CT features
potentially playing a role in diagnosis (25). CT
features that can implicate an isolated PAU as
the cause of acute symptoms include interval
development of the lesion from a recent prior
examination, progression of the lesion at short-
term follow-up, large size, deep ulcer depth, and
signs of impending or active rupture (high-at-
tenuating blood in an associated thrombus, ad-
jacent stranding or blood products outside the
PAU) (26,27) (Fig E1). Therefore, in practice,
the complexities of PAU in the setting of AAS
are not related to recognizing the lesion itself,
which is well described and easily understood,
but rather in determining the clinical relevance
of the lesion when it is encountered.

The Flapless Acute Aorta (Limited
Intimal Tear)
Class III lesions, described as intimal tears with-
out mural hematoma, were originally recognized
in the 1970s (28). Largely forgotten in the next
several decades, Svensson rekindled interest in
these lesions in 1999 with his description of nine
patients who each had an acute aortic patho-
logic condition identified at surgery that was
not detected at preoperative imaging (16). More
recently, Chin et al (29) retrospectively identified
24 patients with similar lesions, identifying them
as 4.8% of 497 patients presenting with AAS at
a tertiary care institution. Of note, four of the 24
patients in this series did not have the diagnosis
made at preoperative imaging (29). This has mir-
rored our experience, as we have seen class III
lesions missed at our own institution.

At surgery, class III lesions have been de-
scribed as stellate or linear intimal tears with
exposure of the underlying media or adventitia
but without propagation of a false lumen or
hematoma in the wall (30). Several names have
been used to describe these uncommon lesions,
including “incomplete dissections” and “limited
intimal tears (LITs)” (16,29). CT features of
class III lesions have focused on the identification
of a focal bulge or contour defect in the aortic
wall. This focal bulge may be difficult to iden-
tify on transaxial images, sometimes completely
obscured owing to the curvature of the aorta,
mistaken for pulsation artifact, or appearing as
a focal flaplike luminal defect when one of the
edges is viewed en face. Multiplanar reconstruc-

tions are of value in depicting the focal bulge
often visible in these cases (8,29). This focal
contour bulge should not be associated with
substantial atherosclerosis. The lack of atheroscle-
rosis is a CT feature that allows distinguishing of
class III from class IV lesions, which are far more
common and typically manifest in older patients
in the descending aorta.

Given the subtlety of the focal bulge on CT

images, other findings should trigger careful
scrutiny of the aorta and increase suspicion for a
class III lesion. The first is the presence of signs
of aortic rupture without an immediately identi-
fiable class I or class II lesion (29) (Fig 1). Class
III lesions are more common in the ascending
aorta and can be associated with blood in the
mediastinum, hemopericardium, and rupture
into the shared adventitial sheath with the main
pulmonary artery. Class III lesions also often
result in focal dilatation of the aorta or may
arise in patients with ascending aortic aneu-
rysms. When acute, the focal aortic dilatation
associated with these lesions can result in aortic
insufficiency due to failure of coaptation of the
valve leaflets. In addition to the focal bulge, the
underlying exposed media may produce a wavy
or irregular contour to the inner aortic wall (29).
Indeed, we have observed this wavy or irregular
inner wall in cases in which the focal contour
bulge is identified, often extending beyond the
focal contour bulge and defining the full extent
of the lesion, which is easy to underestimate and
was reported to be up to 7.7 cm in the series
reported by Chen et al (29) (Fig 2).

The Importance of Phase of Contrast
Enhancement
At dissection protocol CT, which most commonly
involves a noncontrast acquisition followed by
a postcontrast acquisition timed in the systemic
arterial phase, identification of class I, II, IV, and
V lesions is almost always straightforward. This
is not to imply that subtle examples of these
pathologic conditions may not be encountered
at CT, but rather that the findings even in subtle
cases are typically identifiable prospectively when
interpreted by experienced readers. Unfortunately,
patients with class I, II, IV, and V lesions can have
variable clinical manifestations. A review of the
International Registry of Aortic Dissections sheds
more light on the variable presentations of pa-
tients with acute aortic pathologic conditions, with
substantial numbers of patients reporting no pain
or denying symptoms typical of aortic pain (32).
In addition, the majority of patients with acute as-
cending aortic pathologic conditions were not hy-
pertensive at presentation (32). Thus, the clinical
presentation of many patients with aortic patho-
logic conditions, particularly those involving the
ascending aorta, will overlap with those of other
conditions, including acute coronary syndrome,
pulmonary embolism (PE), and pneumothorax.
These clinical factors can result in patients
undergoing CT examinations that are not AD
protocols, including PE protocol CT. PE protocol
CT examinations are single-phase examinations
in which opacification of the systemic arter-
ies including the aorta does not reliably occur.
At PE protocol CT examinations obtained in a
phase prior to complete aortic opacification with
contrast material, suspicion for an aortic cause
of the patient’s symptoms often relies on detec-
tion of aortic enlargement, focal contour defects
in the wall of the aorta, or findings suggestive of
potential aortic rupture (33,34). In addition, rou-
tine use of narrow windows should be performed
to evaluate for intimomedial flaps that may be
detectable even with no or weak opacification of
the aorta (Fig E2). When concerning findings are
identified at PE CT, options for definitive charac-
terization include repeating the CT examination
with a standard dissection protocol or, in patients
in whom the additional use of iodinated contrast
material is potentially problematic, performing
transesophageal echocardiography (TEE) or MRI
for further evaluation.

In some cases, there may be opacification of
the aorta, but it will be in an early systemic arte-

rial phase. This may not only occur at PE CT
but also at AD CT protocol examinations on
faster CT scanners or those that are performed in
patients with decreased cardiac function. In the
early systemic arterial phase, there will be limited
organ enhancement and no contrast present in the
portal vein. This has important implications for
the detection of acute aortic pathologic conditions.
In a classic AD, the false lumen will almost always
enhance after the true lumen; therefore, in the
systemic arterial phase, the false lumen may not
be enhancing, may be weakly enhancing, or may
have a gradient of enhancement extending from
the entry tear (Fig 6). In some cases, this gradient
of enhancement in the false lumen can be used to
advantage in identifying the entry tear, which can
be seen as the point where there is equilibration of
enhancement of the true and false lumens.

If the false lumen is thin, it may have low at-
tenuation and thus be difficult to identify in the
systemic arterial phase, in some cases potentially
confused with a pericardial recess, fluid adjacent
to the aorta, or atherosclerotic disease. As with
examinations in which there is no aortic enhance-
ment at all, aortic dilatation, contour defects, and
findings suggestive of rupture can increase con-
fidence that a not yet filling false lumen is pres-
ent. Particularly on a single-phase examination, a
nonfilling false lumen can also lead to confusion
in distinguishing class I from class II lesions. In
almost all of these cases, determining whether a
lesion is class I with a nonfilling false lumen classic
AD or class II with an IMH is not of immedi-
ate clinical importance. Thus, in reporting these
findings, we describe the appearance and commu-
nicate them as aortic dissection equivalents; further
characterization can then be performed with re-
peat CT in a later phase, MRI, or TEE as needed
(Fig 7). Finally, a delayed-filling false lumen can
be confused for a thrombosed false lumen in the
early systemic arterial phase. Therefore, recogniz-
ing the early systemic arterial phase will prevent
confusion with a thrombosed false lumen; this
distinction is important as a thrombosed false lu-
men has been associated with improved outcomes
in class I lesions (35) (Figs 8, E3).

gated with improvements in modern CT scanner
technology including EKG-gating and high-pitch
imaging modes (12). Faster CT scanners, however,
may be responsible for an increased risk of flow

Difficulties in AAS Classification: More
than Type A/B–Terminology and
Communication
The Stanford classification system is most often
used by radiologists when communicating the
anatomic extent of acute aortic pathologic con-
ditions. Pathologic conditions that involve the
ascending aorta are classified as type A, while
those involving the descending aorta distal to the
left subclavian artery are type B (10). This clas-
sification system is based on the general principle
that type A conditions are managed surgically

while uncomplicated type B conditions can be
managed medically. Dissections that begin in the
aortic arch and extend distally are not explicitly
described in the original Stanford classification
system and have been the subject of some contro-
versy, particularly in the last decade. Given that
the majority of uncomplicated dissections begin-
ning in the aortic arch and extending distally are
managed medically, these are best classified as
type B ADs with arch involvement, as has been
described by Lempel et al (39).

With the expanded use of endovascular tech-
niques in aortic repair, the Society for Vascular
Surgery (SVS) has introduced new guidelines for
the description of acute aortic pathologic condi-
tions. While the A/B dichotomy of the Stanford

system is still used, a more detailed anatomic
description of the extent of acute aortic pathologic
conditions using the aortic zones as originally
described by Criado and Ishimaru (40) is encour-
aged (Fig E4). Importantly, the location of the en-
try tear (which is the most proximal discontinuity
in the intimomedial flap in class I lesions) rather
than the extent of the intimomedial flap is used to
determine whether a classic AD is type A or type
B (40) (Fig 6). When the entry tear is not identifi-
able, classification as type I (indeterminate) is sug-
gested, which can later be revised if an entry tear
is discovered at subsequent imaging. This change
is relevant to therapy, as sealing the entry tear is
the primary target of interventions in patients with
class I lesions. As such, an AD with an entry tear
in zone 3 and a false lumen spanning zone 0 to
zone 5 would be described as SVS type B0,5 (40).
The SVS suggests IMH is classified similarly
and based only on the longitudinal extent of the
IMH, with additional notation given to the pres-
ence of associated PAU. The SVS guidelines do
make reference to ulcerlike projections (ULPs), a
term that has been used to describe intimomedial
defects in the setting of IMH, but describe the
terms as interchangeable (40). This is a reflection
of the terminology used to describe intimomedial
defects in the setting of IMH, in which PAU,
ULP, and other terms, including focal intimal
disruption (FID) and tiny intimal disruption
(TID), are used with attempted but inconsistent
definitions among different authors in the litera-
ture and in communication between physicians
(8,13,17,41,42).

While the terminology is inconsistent, intimo-
medial defects in the setting of IMH with larger
communications with the aortic lumen have been
shown to have a negative association with patient
outcomes. In some cases, they can change over
serial examinations and appear to function similar
to the entry tear in a classic AD, with their inter-
val development and enlargement being associ-
ated with increased propagation or the develop-
ment of complications (13,17,43,44). As such,
they may need to be a focus of treatment in IMH
even if they are not located within the thickest
part of the IMH or are located outside zone 0 in a
type A lesion. This is in contrast to focal contrast
material collections in the wall of an IMH that are
isolated or have a small vessel traversing them, so-
called intramural blood pools, which are thought
to be due to avulsed branch vessels and of no
prognostic significance (13,45) (Figs 13, E5).

It is interesting to note that per the SVS writ-
ing committee, the new proposed classification
system “will allow clinicians to conceptually
project a precise image of entry tear location and
proximal and distal dissection extent with one

simple designation,” but that it is “not neces-
sarily the intent” of this system to “completely
replace the current Stanford and DeBakey
systems for everyday clinical use, especially for
practitioners who are not subject matter experts
in aortic disease.” These statements accurately
reflect the difficulty in implementing new and
subtly changing terminology in cases of acute
aortic pathologic conditions, the treatment of
which is continually evolving often on the basis of
personal preferences and experience. Diagnostic
radiologists must acknowledge that cases of acute
aortic pathologic conditions often require direct
communication with treating physicians to ensure
that what is being depicted on images is accu-
rately conveyed. This is particularly relevant given
that the language used to describe these lesions is
not universally agreed upon or used. This is also
of importance in describing some of the difficult
cases discussed previously, including lesions at
CT that cannot be definitively characterized as
class I classic AD or class II IMH owing to phase
of contrast enhancement (which we term AD
equivalents) or the less common class III lesions,
which often have an imaging appearance that may
belie their serious nature and longitudinal extent.

The Unusual Rupture and Pitfalls
In the setting of an acute aortic pathologic condi-
tion, aortic rupture is a potential complication
that may lead to more urgent invasive treatment.
There are several pathways in which the aorta can
rupture. The most commonly seen pathway is rup-
ture through the adventitia into the surrounding
mediastinum or abdomen. CT findings of rupture
through the adventitia include the presence of
hematoma (intermediate- to high-attenuating ma-
terial measuring 40–75 HU) adjacent to an acute
aortic pathologic lesion. In some cases, active ex-
travasation of contrast material may be visible into
the hematoma, a finding which must be identified
as it typically requires immediate management,
while findings of rupture without active extravasa-
tion can be managed in a less urgent fashion in
some stable patients (46) (Fig 14).

The aorta can also rupture into the pericar-
dial and pleural spaces. At CT, the presence of
high-attenuating blood products in these spaces,
particularly when mixed with lower-attenuating
unclotted blood or associated with a hematocrit
sign, is diagnostic of rupture (47). In the case of
rupture into the pericardium, cardiac tamponade
can also occur and may require rapid decompres-
sion. CT findings of cardiac tamponade include
compression of the right heart, deviation of
the interventricular septum to the left, and the
distention of collateral pathways with reflux of
contrast material (48) (Fig 15). In a patient with

Conclusion
As CT has emerged as the first-line test in the
assessment of patients with suspected acute aor-
tic pathologic conditions, interpretation of these
examinations has become a necessary compo-
nent of general radiology practice. Fortunately,
in the majority of cases, the basics of interpreta-
tion are not difficult and have been extensively
reviewed over the past several decades. This
article highlights several important concepts that
extend this previous work with the goal of iden-
tifying nuances and pitfalls that can aid in the
interpretation of difficult cases and findings. We
describe issues pertaining to diagnosis, includ-
ing recognizing the Svensson classes of intimo-
medial tears, findings of flapless AD equivalents,
pitfalls with phase of contrast enhancement,
and flow artifacts. We discuss the classification
of acute aortic pathologic conditions, includ-
ing emerging recommendations from the SVS.
Finally, we present examples and pitfalls involv-
ing the assessment of aortic rupture. These are
all concepts that we have encountered in diffi-
cult cases of acute aortic pathologic conditions.
Ultimately, when used in conjunction with more
widely available material on the acute aorta,
these concepts can be used to add nuance and
value to CT interpretation in this important
patient cohort.
