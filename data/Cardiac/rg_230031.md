Multimodality Imaging of Infective Endocarditis

Sanjeev Bhalla, MD • Eric Williamson, MD • José Carlos Fernández-Camacho, MD • Antonio Luna, MD, PhD

Infective endocarditis (IE) is a complex multisystemic disease resulting from infection of the endocardium, the prosthetic
valves, or an implantable cardiac electronic device. The clinical presentation of patients with IE varies, ranging from acute
and rapidly progressive symptoms to a more chronic disease onset. Because of its severe morbidity and mortality rates, it is
necessary for radiologists to maintain a high degree of suspicion in evaluation of patients for IE. Modified Duke criteria are
used to classify cases as “definite IE,” “possible IE,” or “rejected IE.” However, these criteria are limited in characterizing defi-
nite IE in clinical practice. The use of advanced imaging techniques such as cardiac CT and nuclear imaging has increased
the accuracy of these criteria and has allowed possible IE to be reclassified as definite IE in up to 90% of cases. Cardiac CT
may be the best choice when there is high clinical suspicion for IE that has not been confirmed with other imaging tech-
niques, in cases of IE and perivalvular involvement, and for preoperative treatment planning or excluding concomitant cor-
onary artery disease. Nuclear imaging may have a complementary role in prosthetic IE. The main imaging findings in IE are
classified according to the site of involvement as valvular (eg, abnormal growths [ie, “vegetations”], leaflet perforations, or
pseudoaneurysms), perivalvular (eg, pseudoaneurysms, abscesses, fistulas, or prosthetic dehiscence), or extracardiac embol-
ic phenomena. The differential diagnosis of IE includes evaluation for thrombus, pannus, nonbacterial thrombotic endocar-
ditis, Lambl excrescences, papillary fibroelastoma, and caseous necrosis of the mitral valve. The location of the lesion relative
to the surface of the valve, the presence of a stalk, and calcification or enhancement at contrast-enhanced imaging may offer
useful clues for their differentiation.

Introduction
Infective  endocarditis  (IE)  is  infection  of  the  endocardium,
prosthetic  valves,  or  implantable  cardiac  electronic  devices.
IE is considered the fourth most common life-threatening in-
fectious syndrome (1–5). The clinical presentation of patients
with  IE  varies,  ranging  from  acute  and  rapidly  progressive
symptoms to a more chronic disease onset. Because of its se-
vere morbidity and mortality rates, it is necessary for radiolo-
gists to maintain a high degree of suspicion when evaluating
patients for IE. (2). The main diagnostic challenges in IE are
identification of the infective pathogen, confirmation of val-
vular involvement, and detection of local and embolic compli-
cations, all of which are important for appropriate treatment
selection and prognosis. IE is currently diagnosed on the ba-
sis  of  the  Modified  Duke  Criteria  (6,7).  Echocardiography  is
the first-line imaging modality used in the evaluation of IE.
Advanced imaging modalities such as cardiac CT, fluorine 18
fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) PET/CT, and cardiac MRI are also
increasingly used in diagnosis of IE.

In this article, we review the role of imaging in the diagno-
sis and treatment of patients with IE and describe the key im-
aging findings for accurate diagnosis of valvular and perival-
vular abnormalities as well as the extracardiac findings of IE.

Definition and Epidemiology
IE can be classified as affecting native valves (ie, native valve
endocarditis  [NVE])  or  prosthetic  valves  (ie,  prosthetic  valve
endocarditis  [PVE]),  which  affects  the  diagnostic  algorithm

and treatment strategy. The global incidence of IE is 3–10 cas-
es per 105 people per year. The incidence is higher in older pa-
tients (>70 years), with a slight male predominance, and it is
more common in patients with comorbidities such as degen-
erative valve disease, diabetes, intravenous drug use, or rheu-
matic heart disease (6). NVE has an incidence of 1.9–6.2 cases
per 105 people per year. The prevalence of PVE is 1%–6% of the
population (equally affecting biologic and mechanical valves),
and  it  occurs  more  commonly  during  the  first  5  years  after
valve replacement. The incidence of IE in patients who under-
go transcatheter aortic valve replacement (TAVR) (0.8%–1.4%)
is similar to the incidence for surgical valvular repair (7).

Gram-positive cocci, such as Staphylococcus, Streptococcus,
or Enterococcus species, account for 80%–90% of cases of IE.
Staphylococcus aureus is responsible for acute and destructive
IE. Other microbes, such as HACEK (Haemophilus species, Ag-
gregatibacter species, Cardiobacterium hominis, Eikenella corro-
dens,  and  Kingela  species)  bacteria,  gram-negative  bacteria,
and fungi are less common (1,3). In PVE, early IE (<2 months)
is mainly caused by S aureus and Staphylococcus epidermidis,
whereas late IE is commonly due to Streptococcus species (7).
The mortality rate in PVE can be as high as 40% (8).

Clinical Presentation and Treatment
The clinical presentation of IE is variable, ranging from acute
and  rapidly  progressive  symptoms  to  a  more  subacute  or
chronic onset, with low-grade fever and nonspecific symptoms
(1).  Frequently,  patients  present  with  fever  (90%)  and  heart
murmur (85%). Embolic phenomena can be the initial clinical
finding in up to 30% of cases (Fig 1) (9). Up to 10% of patients
present with culture-negative IE due to fastidious organisms
or preculture empirical antibiotics (10). Atypical presentation
is commonly seen in patients who are elderly and those who
are  immunocompromised.  Therefore,  IE  must  be  suspected
in  any  patient  presenting  with  fever  or  embolic  phenomena
when risk factors are present. High-risk conditions for IE in-
clude rheumatic or degenerative valve disease, diabetes, can-
cer, intravenous drug use, previous IE, prosthetic valves or im-
plantable cardiac devices, and congenital heart disease (1,3).

Clinical  workup  and  treatment  options  depend  on  the
source of infection and whether the affected valve is native or
prosthetic. Successful treatment relies on microbial eradica-
tion with antibiotic drugs and/or removal of infected material
or drainage of an abscess at surgery. Bactericidal drugs, either
as  monotherapy  or  in  combination  for  resistant  microbes,
are the preferred option. Staphylococcal PVE is treated with
an antibiotic regimen that contains rifampin. A longer treat-
ment duration (approximately 6 weeks) is used for PVE than
that used for NVE (2–6 weeks). The duration of treatment is
based  on  the  1st  day  of  effectiveness  (ie,  negative  blood  cul-
ture) (1). Despite improvements in early diagnosis and anti-
biotic  therapy,  surgery  is  indicated  in  up  to  50%  of  patients
with  IE.  Indications  for  surgery  include  heart  failure  (60%),
persistent  sepsis  (40%),  embolic  events  (18%),  large  abnor-
mal  growths  (ie,  “vegetations”  >10  mm;  48%),  perivalvular
complications,  or  a  combination  of  factors  (11,12).  The  main
objectives of surgery in patients with IE are the total remov-
al of infected tissues and restoration of cardiac morphology,

Value of Cross-sectional Imaging in Modified
Duke Criteria
The Modified Duke Criteria allow classification of cases into
definite,  possible,  or  rejected  IE  (Table  1)  (6,7).  However,  up
to 30% of cases proven to be IE are labeled as possible IE (18).
The inclusion of advanced imaging modalities by the 2015 Eu-
ropean Society of Cardiology guidelines has aided in reclassi-
fying those cases of possible IE as definite IE (1).

Transthoracic  echocardiography  (TTE)  is  the  first-line
imaging  modality  for  use  in  patients  suspected  of  having
endocarditis,  owing  to  its  widespread  availability,  low  cost,
portability, and absence of risk. TTE has a class I recommen-
dation according to the American Heart Association and the
American  College  of  Cardiology  and  a  class  IB  recommen-
dation according to the 2015 European Society of Cardiology
guidelines  (Tables  2,  3)  (1,19).  However,  TTE  examinations
should be interpreted with caution in patients with prosthet-
ic valves because acoustic shadowing can obscure vegetations

and paravalvular extension, especially posteriorly located ab-
scesses  (10,20).  Transesophageal  echocardiography  (TEE)  is
recommended  in  cases  with  moderate  to  high  pretest  prob-
ability  and  negative  TTE  results.  TEE  can  also  be  used  for
further evaluation in prosthetic valves or perivalvular exten-
sion. Invasiveness and the limited visualization of anteriorly
located abscesses in PVE are the two major drawbacks of TEE
(1,10,20). Repeating the echocardiography in 5–7 days can be
considered in patients who are highly clinically suspected to
have IE and have an inconclusive result. Electrocardiographi-
cally (ECG) gated coronary CT or FDG PET/CT may be indicat-
ed for further evaluation in selected cases (21).

Coronary  CT  with  ECG  gating  allows  assessment  of  both
valvular  and  perivalvular  IE  lesions,  especially  in  the  aortic
position,  demonstrating  excellent  correlation  with  surgi-
cal  findings  (sensitivity,  96%;  specificity,  97%)  (Tables  2,  3)
(22,23). In the 2015 European Society of Cardiology guidelines,
CT may be considered in patients with possible IE or highly
clinically suspected IE that is not confirmed using other crite-
ria to assess perivalvular disease if the anatomy is not clearly
delineated at echocardiography for preoperative planning or
for ruling out concomitant coronary artery disease (1). One of
the major drawbacks is the inability of CT to allow assessment
of quantitative valvular dysfunction, which may help to iden-
tify the appropriateness of surgical intervention (11).

FDG PET/CT may have a complementary role in IE, espe-
cially in PVE, where abnormal hypermetabolic activity in the
prosthetic  valve  is  considered  a  major  criterion  in  the  2015
European Society of Cardiology guidelines that aids in the re-
classification of “possible IE” into “definite IE” in 90% of cases
(1). Because of its low sensitivity, FDG PET/CT is not routinely

CT Protocol in IE
Retrospective ECG-gated acquisition covering the whole heart
is preferred over prospective image acquisition with ECG gat-
ing in patients with IE (25). Although the radiation dose with
retrospective ECG gating (3–7 msV) was an issue in the past,
methods  such  as  low–tube  voltage  imaging,  iterative  recon-
struction,  and  ECG-based  tube  current  modulation  help  to
lower the dose (5,26). The motion-free thin sections provided
by isotropic high-spatial reconstruction (0.6–0.75 mm) allow
high-quality  reconstructions  in  any  plane.  The  four-dimen-
sional  dataset  obtained  with  reconstruction  at  5%–10%  in-
crements  during  the  cardiac  cycle  allows  evaluation  of  val-
vular  motion,  planimetry,  and  pathologic  conditions  (5,25).
Moreover, blood-pool inversion volume rendering could also
be helpful in evaluation of the structure and function of the
valvular  system  (11).  Noncontrast  imaging  is  helpful  in  as-
sessment  of  calcium  and  in  distinguishing  aneurysms  and
pseudoaneurysms  from  normal  postsurgical  changes  (eg,
high-attenuation pledgets) or intramural hematomas (60–70
HU)  of  the  aortic  wall  (27).  Noncontrast  images  can  also  be
helpful in excluding mimics of IE complications, such as atyp-
ical  mitral  annulus  calcification.  A  multiphasic  approach,
adding  delayed  venous  phase  (at  60–70  seconds)  CT  angio-
graphic  imaging  of  the  chest,  abdomen,  and  pelvis  without
ECG triggering, may help to differentiate vegetations from the
normal  myocardium  or  thrombi  and  to  assess  perivalvular
findings and extracardiac embolic events (22,28).

In  prosthetic  valve  imaging,  beam  hardening  and  metal-
lic artifacts are more prominent, especially with mechanical
prosthetic  valves.  High  tube–potential  (ie,  up  to  140  kV)  or
high-energy  virtual  monoenergetic  dual-energy  CT  images
may be useful in reducing beam-hardening artifacts, which
are more prominent with retrospective ECG gating than with
prospective ECG gating. A smooth tissue reconstruction ker-
nel and four-dimensional CT datasets may demonstrate dys-
function of the valvular leaflets, paravalvular leaks, pseudo-
aneurysms, and abnormal motion of the prosthetic valve (ie,
rocking)  (7).  Care  must  be  taken  in  using  specific  iterative
techniques for metal reduction with cardiac devices because
artifacts with these may simulate thrombi or vegetations.

Patient preparation is identical to that for coronary CT angio-
graphic examinations; however, in patients with IE, premedica-
tion with chronotropic drugs (ie, β-blockers) is not commonly
used  and  may  be  contraindicated.  The  protocol  for  contrast
material injection should be tailored to the suspected site of in-
fection (29,30). To discriminate among the contrast-enhanced
blood pool, surrounding structures, tiny abscesses, and pseu-
doaneurysms,  a  sufficient  rate  (4–6  mL/sec)  of  intravenous
contrast material should be administered, tailored to the fea-
tures  of  the  local  CT  scanner.  Triggered  time-bolus  protocols

Imaging Findings in IE
According to the site of involvement, IE findings can be classi-
fied as valvular (eg, vegetation, leaflet perforations, or leaflet

pseudoaneurysms) and perivalvular (eg, perivalvular dehis-
cence, pseudoaneurysms, abscesses, or fistulas). Extracardiac
findings may be related to embolic phenomena to the central
nervous  system,  intra-abdominal  organs,  and  other  distant
locations (Fig 1) (5,10,11,20,25).

Valvular  Vegetation.—Vegetations  are
soft-tissue lesions of variable sizes and irregular shapes that
are attached to the endocardial surface or prosthetic material
(Fig 2) (1,4). Usually, they are located at the low-pressure side
of cardiac valves, either the ventricular side of the aortic valve
or the atrial side of the mitral valve. Atypical locations include
the chordae tendineae or intracardiac devices (5,11,25). Large

(>10  mm)  and  mobile  vegetations  have  a  higher  risk  of  em-
bolization and are an indication for urgent surgical treatment
(33,34).

At echocardiography, vegetations appear as oscillating or
nonoscillating  echogenic  lesions  similar  to  the  appearance
of  the  myocardium,  with  a  propensity  to  be  more  hypere-
choic with chronicity (Fig 3) (10). Vegetations may manifest
at coronary CT as variably sized low- to intermediate-atten-
uation lesions or as localized focal thickening along a valve,
the endocardium, or a prosthesis (Fig 4, Movie 1). IE causes
inflammation and destruction of the valve, which frequently
leads to regurgitation and stenosis. New valvular regurgita-
tion  may  be  secondary  to  perforation  of  leaflets  and  cusps,
subvalvular involvement, chordae rupture, or malcoaptation
(ie, bulky vegetations) (Fig 3) (5,11,25). Vegetations, particu-
larly nonvalvular vegetations, may be easily overlooked at CT
because the attenuation is similar to that of the myocardium.

The  exact  role  of  cardiac  MRI  remains  unclear.  Some  au-
thors suggest cardiac MRI as an alternative to repeated TEE and
TTE in patients for whom there is high clinical suspicion for IE
(35). When they are identifiable, vegetations are seen as focal
and  mobile  low-signal-intensity  lesions  on  cine  steady-state
free-precession (SSFP) MR images (Movies 2–4). When vegeta-
tions are not conspicuous because of the limited spatial resolu-
tion of cardiac MRI, endothelial late gadolinium-based contrast
enhancement may suggest IE. The extension of late gadolini-
um-based  contrast  enhancement  to  perivalvular  tissues  has
been described as an early sign before the occurrence of peri-
valvular complications (35). A lack of submillimeter resolution
and  the  signal  intensity  void  generated  by  some  prostheses
impair the evaluation of perivalvular endocarditis (25). Recent
advances  such  as  a  four-dimensional  flow  sequence  during
cardiac  MRI  may  be  helpful  in  accurately  assessing  valvular
regurgitation or evaluating intracardiac spread, especially for
patients in whom TTE and TEE have limitations and coronary
CT is contraindicated (Movies 5, 6) (36,37).

IE of the right side of the heart is observed more frequently
in users of intravenous drugs and alcohol and in patients with
an  indwelling  catheter  or  an  implantable  cardiac  electronic
device. The tricuspid valve is more frequently affected than is
the pulmonary valve (Fig 5) (10,20). IE of the right side of the
heart may manifest as extension of an aortic root abscess or
as ventricular septal defects (10). Vegetations are larger in the
right side than in the left side of the heart, and in some cases,
pulmonary embolic events may be seen.

In  prostheses,  vegetations  are  identified  at  coronary  CT  as
low-attenuating lesions on the valve leaflet or near the sewing
ring, usually on the ventricular side of the aortic valve (10,38).

Leaflet  Perforation.—Leaflet  perforations  are  tiny  defects  in
the  valve  tissue  that  permit  retrograde  blood  flow  into  the
preceding  cardiac  chamber  (Movie  7,  Fig  6)  (11).  Doppler  US
assessment  and  three-dimensional  techniques  can  increase
the accuracy of detection with echocardiography (39). Small
perforations  can  be  missed  at  coronary  CT  due  to  limited
temporal resolution. Coronary CT has lower sensitivity (43%
vs  75%)  and  higher  specificity  (89%  vs  70%)  compared  with
TEE for detecting leaflet perforation (40). Although volume–
rendering  techniques  may  be  helpful  for  identifying  leaflet
defects, careful interpretation of two-dimensional datasets is
mandatory  due  to  frequent  volume  averaging,  given  the  in-
credibly thin normal valve leaflets. Adequate selection of the
cardiac phase and acquisition of double oblique multiplanar
reformations  may  be  of  great  value  to  identify  those  leaflet
perforations. Usually, perforations are located at the edge or
the tip of the leaflet, alone or associated with vegetations (22).

Leaflet Pseudoaneurysm.—A leaflet aneurysm is a rare entity
defined as distortion of the leaflet with saccular outpouching
and  loss  of  its  homogeneous  curvature  due  to  infection.  Ex-
cellent agreement (100%) between coronary CT and TEE has
been described for leaflet pseudoaneurysm detection (5,26).

Perivalvular  Abscess  and  Fistula.—A  perivalvular  abscess  is
defined  as  a  perivalvular  cavity  with  necrosis  and  purulent
material that does not communicate with the cardiovascular
lumen. However, with time, an abscess may develop commu-
nication with the lumen and turn into a pseudoaneurysm (1).
At  echocardiography,  an  abscess  appears  as  a  nonhomoge-
neous perivalvular hyperechogenic thickening or irregularly
shaped  hypoechoic  fluid  collection  that  does  not  communi-
cate with the cardiac chambers (Movie 9) (11,25). On coronary
CT  images,  a  low-attenuation  (20–50  HU)  or  heterogeneous
collection, depending on the degree of necrosis, is seen adja-
cent to the valve. Peripheral rim enhancement (ie, a capsule)
is  occasionally  present  and  indicates  active  inflammation.
Hyperenhancement is more conspicuous during the delayed

phase at CT (ie, 70 seconds to 2–3 minutes after contrast ma-
terial  injection)  (25).  Loss  of  normal  periaortic  fatty  tissue
attenuation  (0–100  HU)  and  increased  soft-tissue  periaortic
attenuation (1–30 HU) should always raise the concern for an
early abscess or a phlegmon (Figs 8, 9) (5,25). Adjacent signs
of inflammation, such as fat stranding, extension to the myo-
cardium, or pericardial effusion, may be present (7). Distin-
guishing  perivalvular  abscesses  and  pseudoaneurysms  is
not always possible, and often they may coexist. Both entities
reflect  localized  extension  of  IE,  which  requires  surgical  in-
tervention (5). Performing FDG PET/CT may be necessary to
improve diagnostic confidence due to its high accuracy, espe-
cially in PVE, for which surgery carries a high morbidity rate
(Fig 9) (10,20,47).

Prosthetic  Valve  Dehiscence.—Prosthetic  valve  dehiscence  is
caused  by  infectious  destruction  of  the  valve  ring  leading
to  inadequate  sealing  of  the  prosthetic  valve  annulus  and
a  paravalvular  leak  (1,2).  It  is  more  common  in  mechani-
cal  valves  than  it  is  in  bioprosthetic  valves.  In  mechanical

valves,  the  infection  commonly  spreads  from  the  sewing
ring  or  adjacent  thrombi,  whereas  in  bioprosthetic  valves,
it  spreads  from  leaflet  cusps  and  infrequently  involves  the
sewing  ring  or  paravalvular  region  (7).  The  most  common
cause  of  paravalvular  leaks  is  endocarditis-induced  dehis-
cence (7). Dehiscence with a paravalvular leak is commonly
seen in the immediate postoperative period (5%–20%), with
a prevalence of 7%–17% in the mitral position and 2%–10% in
the aortic position (53). Echocardiographic findings include
a paravalvular regurgitant flow at color Doppler US, with or
without prosthetic rocking motion (1,5). Paravalvular leaks
have variable sizes and morphology and are more often cres-
centic or irregular.

On coronary CT images, a malalignment of the prosthesis,
with  a  tissue  defect  between  the  annulus  and  prosthesis,  is
seen. With four-dimensional CT, a prosthetic annulus motion
of more than 15° is defined as rocking motion (Fig 11; Movies
11, 12) (5,7). A perivalvular channel that connects the two as-
sociated chambers is seen at coronary CT. At cine steady-state
free-precession  (SSFP)  cardiac  MRI,  a  paravalvular  leak  ap-
pears as a dark retrograde jet due to dephasing. Phase-con-
trast imaging may be used to quantify the regurgitant jet (54).
Owing to the difficulty of diagnosis and treatment, coronary
CT and/or cardiac MRI are commonly used with echocardiog-
raphy in defining paravalvular leaks.

There are several potential pitfalls at coronary CT in eval-
uation of patients with a prosthetic paravalvular leak. A true
axial  double-oblique  reconstruction  is  crucial  not  only  for
detection of paravalvular leaks but also for quantification of
the  regurgitant  orifice  (55).  Pledget  material,  beam  harden-
ing, and motion artifacts are common pitfalls. Due to the high
attenuation  of  a  pledget  (383–494  HU)  compared  with  that
of contrast media (202–367 HU), true noncontrast or virtual
noncontrast dual-energy CT images may be useful for its dif-
ferentiation.  A  soft-tissue  kernel  or  iterative  reconstruction
may help to reduce beam-hardening artifacts. Heart rate con-
trol or acquisition of a mid-diastole coronary CT dataset may
be necessary to avoid motion artifacts (7).

Nonvalvular Cardiac Findings
Pericarditis is relatively frequent in IE and is described in up to
7.5% of cases. Pericarditis is an inflammatory response of the
pericardium rather than direct extension. Purulent pericarditis
resulting from direct extension may be difficult to distinguish
at  imaging  from  transudative  pericardial  effusion.  Coronary
CT is an excellent modality for measuring pericardial thickness
and may allow differentiation of transudative pericardial effu-
sions from exudates (cutoff attenuation level, 10 HU). Highly at-
tenuating pericardial effusions (20–60 HU) may be purulent,
malignant,  or  myxedematous.  Cardiac  MRI  may  provide  fur-
ther insight, not only allowing detection of increased thickness
at T1-weighted MRI but also allowing differentiation of active
inflammation  at  T2-weighted  MRI,  late  gadolinium  enhance-
ment, and parametric mapping. In a large multicenter registry
study (10), no association was found between the presence or
size of pericardial effusions and the 1-year mortality rate.

Acute coronary syndrome is an uncommon complication
in patients with IE, with a described incidence of 2.2%, and is
associated  with  poor  outcomes  and  difficult  treatment  (56).
It  may  occur  during  the  first  2  weeks  after  diagnosis.  Coro-

nary  embolisms  constitute  the  most  common  mechanism
and are frequently seen in patients with aortic valve IE, with-
out any vessel predominance. Complete occlusion is seen in
55% of cases. Other described mechanisms include extrinsic
compression  from  a  periaortic  complication,  obstruction  of
the coronary ostia by large vegetations, a steal phenomenon
secondary to severe aortic regurgitation, or thrombosis over a
preexisting stenotic lesion secondary to active inflammation
(56,57). Coronary CT angiography may be used for its diagno-
sis when invasive coronary angiography is too risky or contra-
indicated (57).

Papillary fibroelastoma is the second most common prima-
ry cardiac tumor and constitutes 75% of valvular neoplasms.
Left-sided  valves  are  more  frequently  involved  (aortic  valve
more often than the mitral valve) than are right-sided valves
(tricuspid  valve  more  often  than  the  pulmonary  valve)  (66–
68). Papillary fibroelastomas are small and highly mobile le-
sions that are typically along the downstream surface of the
valve and attached to it by a thin stalk. Papillary fibroelastomas
are echogenic at TTE and TEE; show soft-tissue attenuation at
coronary  CT;  are  hypointense  at  cine  steady-state  free-pre-
cession (SSFP) MRI, and are hypointense at T1-weighted MRI,
with high signal intensity at T2-weighted MRI and uptake at
late gadolinium-enhanced MRI (Fig 13) (5,68).

Nonbacterial  thrombotic  endocarditis  (ie,  marantic  endocar-
ditis) is defined as sterile or aseptic vegetation commonly in-
volving left-sided valves and associated with malignancy and
other autoimmune disorders (ie, Libman-Sacks endocarditis)

(69).  Small  (<10  mm),  irregular,  and  broad-based  echogenic
lesions at TTE and TEE and vegetations with soft-tissue atten-
uation  at  coronary  CT  are  located  mainly  in  left-sided  valves
(Fig 14). Although there are limited descriptions of the appear-
ance of nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis at FDG PET/CT,
mild radiotracer uptake has been described (69). Nonbacterial
thrombotic endocarditis can manifest also with systemic em-
bolic phenomena, mimicking symptoms of left-sided IE (5,70).
Caseous necrosis of the mitral annulus is a rare and benign
process that is present in up to 0.6% of mitral valvular calci-
fications. It is more frequently seen in women and typically
asymptomatic. Caseous calcification is composed of basophil-
ic areas with fibrous tissue around it. A well-demarcated and
homogeneous  mass  is  seen  on  the  mitral  annulus,  hypere-
chogenic on TTE/TEE, with high attenuation at unenhanced
CT and with no enhancement after iodinated contrast media
administration.  A  homogeneous  low-signal-intensity  lesion

Valvular thrombosis is a relatively common complication
of prosthetic valves (0.3%–8%) that is seen frequently during
the first postoperative year (73). Mechanical valves are more
often  involved  than  are  bioprosthetic  valves,  and  valvular
thrombosis is usually related to subtherapeutic anticoagula-
tion  (7).  Tricuspid  valves  are  more  frequently  involved  than
are  mitral  or  aortic  valves.  Higher  morbidity  and  mortality
have  been  related  to  obstruction  or  systemic  embolization
(74). TTE or TEE shows an irregular globular or mobile lesion
that  is  isoechoic  to  the  myocardium  and  is  attached  to  the

high-pressure side of the valve occluder, sewing ring, or both,
with possible signs of obstruction (eg, reduced valve mobili-
ty or effective prosthetic area, increased transvalvular gradi-
ent, abnormal transvalvular flow). Low CT attenuation (<200
HU; <145 HU cutoff ) has been described at coronary CT, with
absence of enhancement at contrast-enhanced imaging (Fig
16). A cutoff value of 145 HU is used to differentiate valvular
thrombosis  (<145  HU)  from  an  inflammatory  pannus  (>145
HU) with high sensitivity (87.5%) and specificity (96%). More-
over, CT may allow prediction of treatment response, because
complete lysis is more likely in thrombi with attenuation of
less than 90 HU than in thrombi with attenuation of 90–145
HU (7). Thrombosis does not show radiotracer uptake at FDG
PET/CT (75).

Hypoattenuating leaflet thickening is a newly described en-
tity occurring in bioprosthetic valves and TAVR that is char-
acterized by thickened and hypoattenuating prosthetic valve
leaflets, with reduced motion that resolves with anticoagula-
tion (78). It is present in 3.6%–40% of bioprosthetic valves and
is higher at 1 year after the valve placement procedure than it
is at discharge, with uneventful hemodynamic and midterm

outcomes  (79).  Hypoattenuating  lesions  at  the  base  and  pe-
riphery of prosthetic leaflets with reduced leaflet mobility is
seen at coronary CT (Fig 18). Typically, no changes in pressure
gradients are registered at TTE or TEE (78,79). Many consider
this to be a prethrombotic condition.
